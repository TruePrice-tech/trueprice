Frequently asked concrete questions

How long before I can drive on new concrete?

Wait 24-48 hours for foot traffic, 7 days for vehicle traffic, and 30 days for full cure. Avoid deicing salt for the first winter. New concrete reaches 90% strength in 7 days and full strength in 28 days.

What is the best time to pour concrete?

Pour when temperatures stay between 50-80F for at least 3 days. Spring and fall are ideal. Avoid pouring in rain, direct hot sun, or below freezing. Cold and heat both compromise cure quality.

How thick should a concrete driveway be?

Residential driveways should be 4 inches thick for cars and 5-6 inches for heavy vehicles like RVs. A 6-inch compacted gravel base underneath is essential. Thickened edges (6 inches) prevent cracking at the perimeter.

Subgrade and mix design: Albuquerque edition

Albuquerque's concrete sub-base splits across three geological zones: stable Rio Grande alluvium in the valley floor (sandy loam and gravel, excellent bearing, standard 4-inch gravel sub-base), expansive adobe clay in the North Valley and South Valley (requires 6-8 inch engineered sub-base with geotextile to prevent heave), and volcanic tuff on the West Mesa (stable, well-drained, supports concrete well but varies in density). The North Valley adobe-clay zone presents the most challenging concrete conditions because the seasonal moisture cycling (monsoon wet to winter dry) drives heave-and-settlement similar to Fort Worth's Blackland Prairie, though at lower plasticity index levels. The 5,312-foot altitude affects concrete curing: the thinner atmosphere accelerates surface moisture loss, requiring more aggressive curing protocol than sea-level markets.

Albuquerque concrete mix: standard 3,000-4,000 PSI mix with air entrainment (5-7%) is mandatory for all exterior flatwork because the 200+ annual freeze-thaw cycles from extreme diurnal temperature swings (40-50 degrees daily) create aggressive freeze-thaw degradation. The altitude-enhanced UV accelerates surface degradation, and the desert-dry environment causes rapid moisture loss during curing. Curing protocol is critical: keep fresh concrete moist for 7 days minimum using curing compound, plastic sheeting, or wet burlap. The very hard water (200-300 ppm from ABCWUA) leaves mineral deposits on concrete surfaces cleaned with city water; use purified water for any cleaning that precedes sealer or stain application.

Reinforcement in Albuquerque: fiber-mesh reinforcement with #4 rebar on 24-inch centers for flatwork on expansive adobe-clay properties in the North Valley and South Valley. Fiber-mesh only for flatwork on stable alluvium and volcanic-tuff properties. Expansion joints every 8-10 feet to accommodate the extreme diurnal temperature cycling. Control joints sawcut within 12-24 hours of placement are critical because the rapid surface drying at altitude and low humidity creates shrinkage cracking faster than sea-level markets. Colored concrete (integral pigment) reinforcement should follow standard patterns because the pigment does not affect structural performance.

Local factors: concrete work in Albuquerque, NM

Home stock in Albuquerque. With homes averaging just 30 years old, Albuquerque has a relatively new building stock. Most concrete work work involves upgrades and efficiency improvements rather than end-of-life replacements. Code compliance is rarely an issue since these homes were built to recent standards.

HOA & compliance. HOA rules affect some but not all Albuquerque homeowners. If applicable, confirm your HOA's requirements for concrete work before getting quotes. This avoids the situation where your preferred material or color gets rejected after you have already signed a contract.

How the local trade market looks. With steady but not explosive growth, Albuquerque's contractor market offers reasonable availability. Most shops can schedule work within 1-3 weeks, and shopping for quotes is straightforward. Albuquerque's large market has concrete contractors ranging from 1-person decorative specialists to large commercial outfits that also do residential. For standard flatwork (driveways, patios), mid-size companies with dedicated residential crews typically offer the best combination of pricing and quality.

Best months to book. Albuquerque allows year-round concrete work, but the ideal window is spring and fall when temperatures support proper curing without the heat-related challenges of summer. Avoid scheduling large pours during the hottest months — early morning pours are the only reliable option.
