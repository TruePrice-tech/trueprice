What is the cheapest fence material?

Chain link is the cheapest at $10-$20 per linear foot installed. Pressure-treated pine is the cheapest wood option at $15-$25/ft. Vinyl and composite start at $20-$30/ft but never need painting.

What is the best fence for privacy?

A 6-foot solid board (dog-ear, board-on-board, or shadowbox) fence provides the most privacy. Board-on-board is best since it has no gaps even when boards shrink. Vinyl and composite also offer full privacy panels.

Albuquerque, NM Fence Guide

Albuquerque's high-desert environment at 5,000 feet creates unique fence challenges that have little in common with the rest of the country. The combination of intense UV radiation, extremely low humidity, temperature swings of 30-40 degrees daily, and alkaline soil makes material selection critical. Wood fences that would last 20 years in the Pacific Northwest may dry out, crack, and split within 10-12 years in Albuquerque without diligent maintenance. The desert sun is the primary enemy of every organic fence material here.

Coyote fencing (latilla fencing made from peeled juniper or cedar poles) is the iconic Albuquerque fence style, deeply tied to New Mexico's Pueblo and Spanish Colonial architectural heritage. Coyote fence runs $30-$50 per linear foot installed, with the latillas (poles) standing vertically between horizontal rails. It provides complete privacy, blends with adobe and stucco architecture, and ages to a silver-gray patina that most Albuquerque residents consider attractive. The downside is maintenance: the poles check and crack in the dry climate, and individual poles need replacement every 8-12 years as they deteriorate.

Standard wood privacy fencing (cedar or redwood boards on a post-and-rail frame) is also common in Albuquerque but requires more UV protection than in humid climates. The dry air wicks moisture from wood aggressively, causing checking, splitting, and warping within 2-3 years if the wood is left unfinished. A semitransparent oil-based stain with UV inhibitors, applied within 6 months of installation and refreshed every 2-3 years, is the minimum maintenance program. Water-based stains do not penetrate as deeply and peel in Albuquerque's dry conditions.

Ornamental metal fencing (wrought iron and tubular steel) is the most practical material for Albuquerque's climate. Metal is immune to UV degradation, termite damage, and the moisture cycling that destroys wood. Powder-coated steel fencing ($25-$45 per linear foot installed) requires minimal maintenance beyond occasional washing and rust touch-up at scratch points. The open design also complements the desert landscape aesthetic and complies with most HOA requirements in Northeast Heights, Rio Rancho, and East Mountains communities.

The City of Albuquerque requires a fence permit for fences over 3 feet in the front yard setback and over 6 feet elsewhere. The permitting process is straightforward, running $25-$50. Albuquerque's zoning code limits fence height to 6 feet in residential zones with exceptions for corner lots and properties adjacent to commercial zones. Certain neighborhoods in the Historic District and the Downtown neighborhoods have design overlay requirements that may restrict fence materials and styles. Check with the city's Planning Department if your property falls in a historic zone.

Trade labor costs in Albuquerque, NM

According to BLS data, construction laborers in the Albuquerque, NM metro earn a median of $19.62/hr (11% below the national median of $22.08/hr). Carpenters earn $25.71/hr (near the $25.27/hr national median).

Lower labor costs are the main reason fence installation quotes in Albuquerque come in below the national average. Labor typically represents 40-60% of a fence installation quote, so even a few percentage points above or below the national median shows up in your bottom line.

Local market conditions. Albuquerque has moderate construction activity. Contractors are busy but accessible with 1-3 weeks' notice. Seasonal timing. In Albuquerque, fence contractors are busiest March through June when homeowners want fencing before summer. Fall (September-November) offers better availability and occasionally lower prices as outdoor project demand fades.
