Frequently asked insulation questions

What is a vapor barrier and do I need one?

A vapor barrier prevents moisture from passing through walls and ceilings. In cold climates, install on the warm (interior) side. In hot-humid climates, install on the exterior side. Closed-cell spray foam acts as its own vapor barrier.

How long does insulation last?

Fiberglass batts last 80-100 years if they stay dry and undisturbed. Cellulose lasts 20-30 years before settling reduces effectiveness. Spray foam lasts the life of the structure. All insulation loses value if it gets wet.

How much does spray foam insulation cost?

Open-cell spray foam costs $1-$2 per board foot installed. Closed-cell costs $2-$4 per board foot. A 1,000 sqft attic with 6 inches of closed-cell runs $6,000-$12,000. It is the most expensive but most effective option.

What is the best type of insulation?

Spray foam has the highest R-value per inch (R-6 to R-7 for closed-cell) and creates an air seal. Blown-in cellulose is the best value for attics. Fiberglass batts are cheapest for open walls during new construction.

Does insulation reduce noise?

Yes. Dense insulation (cellulose, mineral wool, spray foam) reduces sound transmission 50-80%. Mineral wool batts are the best for sound deadening between interior rooms. Standard fiberglass batts provide moderate noise reduction.

Attic insulation strategy: a Albuquerque guide

The traditional Pueblo Revival flat roof with parapet walls creates a shallow cavity between the ceiling and the roof membrane that may only accommodate 4-6 inches of insulation. Albuquerque's wall cavity retrofit approach

Albuquerque wall insulation follows the same adobe-specific logic as El Paso: the thick adobe walls (12-18 inches) provide inherent thermal mass (R-8 to R-12 total) that should be PRESERVED rather than insulated over. Interior-side insulation on adobe walls reduces the thermal-mass benefit and can trap moisture inside the wall. The correct approach: maximize attic insulation (where the sun's heat enters) while leaving the adobe walls to perform their thermal-mass function. Northeast Heights block-and-frame homes benefit from standard dense-pack cellulose injection ($1.50-$2.50/sqft) into the wood-frame wall cavities. The volcanic-tuff mesa properties may have crawlspace-foundation walls that benefit from rigid-foam board insulation on the interior ($2-$4/sqft for R-10).

A Albuquerque guide: air sealing priorities

Albuquerque air sealing: the dry high-desert air means infiltration is a sensible-heat (temperature) pathway rather than a latent-heat (moisture) pathway. Standard leak targets apply: can-light fixtures, plumbing penetrations, duct boots, attic-hatch seals. Adobe homes have unique air-sealing targets: the window-to-wall interface in the deep reveals (12-18 inches) needs flexible sealant that accommodates the thermal movement of the thick wall. The 200+ freeze-thaw cycles from diurnal temperature swings stress rigid sealants; polyurethane or silicone sealants outperform rigid spray foam at dynamic joints. Air sealing at the attic level captures 60-70% of the total infiltration benefit regardless of wall type.

Albuquerque moisture control and vapor strategy

Albuquerque moisture management: the dry high-desert climate simplifies vapor management dramatically compared to humid markets like Baltimore. Vapor barriers are generally unnecessary because the ambient humidity is too low to create condensation risk inside wall cavities. The monsoon-season humidity window (July-August) briefly raises dew points but not enough to produce sustained condensation in properly ventilated attic assemblies. Adobe-wall moisture management is the Albuquerque-specific consideration: the thick walls naturally absorb and release moisture (they're essentially giant earth-block batteries for both heat and moisture), and any insulation or coating that prevents this moisture migration causes plaster spalling on the interior surface. Vapor-permeable insulation approaches (mineral wool, cellulose) are appropriate; closed-cell spray foam is generally not.
