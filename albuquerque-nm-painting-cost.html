Albuquerque painting: answers to common questions

Is spray painting better than brush and roller?

Spraying is faster and gives a smoother finish on large, flat surfaces. Brush and roller are better for detail work and touchups. Many pros spray first, then back-roll for adhesion.

How often should a house be painted?

Wood siding needs repainting every 5-7 years. Stucco and fiber cement last 8-12 years between coats. Vinyl and aluminum siding rarely need painting. Climate, sun exposure, and paint quality affect timing.

What should I look for in a painting quote?

A professional quote specifies: paint brand and sheen, number of coats, prep work included, surface repairs, primer usage, timeline, warranty, and what is excluded (trim, doors, ceilings).

Climate and paint durability within Albuquerque

Albuquerque's high-desert UV at 5,312 feet strips paint faster than any sea-level market in this group: south and west exposures on stucco fade and chalk within 2-3 years, while the same paint on north-facing surfaces may last 6-8 years. The extreme 40-50 degree diurnal temperature swing (85F afternoon to 35F predawn during spring and fall) creates daily expansion-contraction cycling that fatigues paint films at joints and substrate transitions faster than moderate-swing climates. The March-May wind season drives fine sand into freshly applied paint, embedding grit that creates rough surfaces prone to dirt accumulation. The July-August monsoon season delivers 40-50% of annual precipitation in intense afternoon downpours that test every paint-film failure point identified during the dry season.

Albuquerque's dry high-desert climate produces virtually zero mildew risk -- the polar opposite of Baltimore and Orlando. North-facing surfaces may develop minor biological growth during the monsoon season's brief humidity window, but standard power washing removes it without mildewcide treatment. The moisture concern in Albuquerque is internal rather than external: the thick adobe walls in North Valley and Old Town absorb and release moisture seasonally, and any paint film that prevents this moisture migration causes plaster spalling. Irrigation overspray on stucco produces efflorescence (white mineral deposits from the hard Rio Grande water) that must be cleaned before painting. The very hard water (200-300 ppm) also leaves mineral spots on painted surfaces if city water is used for power washing without a final rinse.

Albuquerque's siding materials and substrates

Albuquerque's exterior substrates are uniquely diverse: traditional adobe with lime plaster in the North Valley, South Valley, and Old Town (requiring breathable mineral silicate or lime-based coatings); Portland cement stucco over block construction in the Northeast Heights (accepting standard acrylic); wood trim, fascia, and vigas on Pueblo Revival and Territorial homes (requiring UV-resistant opaque stains or paint); and conventional stucco-on-frame in Far Northeast newer construction. The adobe-plaster substrate is Albuquerque-specific and requires coating expertise that most US markets never encounter: standard acrylic latex traps moisture inside the adobe wall, causing plaster delamination within 2-3 years. Only mineral silicate (Keim), lime-based (Romabio, Graphenstone), or specially formulated breathable acrylic (SEFA Group KLAR-Wall) should be applied to lime-plaster adobe substrates.

Albuquerque and common contractor upsells
