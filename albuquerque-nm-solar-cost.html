Frequently asked solar questions

What happens to solar panels during a power outage?

Grid-tied systems without batteries shut off during outages for safety (anti-islanding). A battery backup or hybrid inverter lets you use solar power during grid failures.

How long do solar panels last?

Modern panels are warrantied for 25-30 years and continue producing at reduced efficiency beyond that. Inverters last 10-15 years (string) or 25 years (microinverters) and are the main replacement cost.

Can I add a battery to my solar system?

Yes. Adding a home battery (10-13 kWh) costs $8,000-$15,000 installed. Batteries provide backup power during outages and can help avoid peak-rate charges in time-of-use markets.

Do solar panels work on cloudy days?

Yes. Panels produce 10-25% of their rated output on overcast days. Annual production matters more than any single day. Systems are sized to account for regional cloud cover patterns.

Is solar worth it in Albuquerque?

Solar is a strong investment for most Albuquerque homes, with typical payback in 6-10 years followed by 15-20 years of free electricity. The 30% federal tax credit cuts the net cost substantially. Your specific return depends on roof orientation, shading, energy consumption, and local utility rates. Three quotes with site-specific production estimates give you the data to decide.

What solar incentives are available in Albuquerque?

Every Albuquerque homeowner installing solar qualifies for the 30% federal Investment Tax Credit with no dollar limit. Beyond that, NM may offer state tax credits, utility-specific rebates, Solar Renewable Energy Credits, and property tax exemptions. The DSIRE database has the current list for your area. These stacked incentives typically reduce net cost by an additional 10-30%.

When is the best time to install solar in Albuquerque?

Albuquerque homeowners should aim for spring or fall to install solar. Comfortable working temperatures and strong solar production begins immediately. Allow 6-12 weeks from signing a contract through permitting, utility interconnection approval, and installation. Start gathering quotes 2-3 months before your target window. Remember that the 30% ITC applies to the tax year in which installation is completed.

A Albuquerque look at roof types and mounting implications

Albuquerque's flat-roof architectural tradition (50-60% of housing stock) provides unique solar advantages: flat roofs allow tilt-frame racking at the optimal 25-30° angle regardless of building orientation, eliminating the south-facing-roof requirement that limits production on misoriented pitched-roof homes. Tilt-frame systems on flat roofs produce 5-10% more annual energy than flush-mounted pitched-roof systems at non-optimal orientations. North Valley and Old Town adobe homes with traditional vigas need structural assessment for panel and racking loads because the earthen roof structure has different load-bearing characteristics than modern trusses. Northeast Heights and Far Northeast pitched-roof homes provide standard installation conditions.

HOA rules and solar access law in Albuquerque

New Mexico Solar Rights Act (NMSA 47-3-4.1) protects residential solar installation from unreasonable restrictions. The Landmarks and Urban Conservation Commission reviews visible panel installations in the Downtown and Old Town historic districts, which may restrict placement on contributing Pueblo Revival and Territorial-style structures to non-street-visible locations. Flat-roof systems that sit below parapet lines are generally not visible and avoid historic-review complications. HOA restrictions in Far Northeast master-planned communities are minimal under the state solar-rights protection.

Solar permitting process near Albuquerque

Building Safety Division issues solar permits with 3-5 business day turnaround for standard residential systems. NM CID licensing is verified during the permit application process. Structural engineering review may be required on flat-roof adobe homes where the viga-and-latilla structure needs load verification for tilt-frame racking. Bernalillo County handles unincorporated-area permits (South Valley, North Valley unincorporated) under separate jurisdiction. Landmarks Commission review for Downtown and Old Town properties adds 4-6 weeks before building permit issuance.

Battery storage options and rebates for Albuquerque homeowners

Battery storage in Albuquerque serves a moderate resilience function: monsoon-season lightning-driven outages lasting 1-4 hours are the primary grid-reliability concern, but PNM's grid is generally stable. Climate risks for solar installations within Albuquerque

Albuquerque's primary solar risks: windblown sand during March-May wind events abrades panel glass surfaces, producing micro-scratches that reduce transmittance by 3-7% over 10 years. Annual panel cleaning ($150-$250) mitigates this. Monsoon-season hail is occasional but typically small (pea to marble size), within standard panel IEC 61215 test specifications. The extreme UV at 5,312 feet accelerates panel encapsulant yellowing 10-15% faster than sea-level markets, but Tier 1 manufacturer warranties account for this degradation rate. The 200+ annual freeze-thaw cycles from extreme diurnal temperature swings stress racking expansion joints, making stainless-steel mounting hardware preferable to aluminum in this market.

PNM (Public Service Company of New Mexico) serves Albuquerque and offers net metering for residential solar systems up to 10 kW (with larger systems considered on a case-by-case basis). Excess production is credited at the full retail rate ($0.13-$0.15 per kWh), and credits roll forward month to month. PNM's solar interconnection process is well-established since New Mexico has one of the highest per-capita solar adoption rates in the country. The utility's interconnection approval typically takes 2-4 weeks after installation.

New Mexico offers a Solar Market Development Tax Credit of 10% of system cost (capped at $6,000), which stacks with the federal 30% ITC. Combined, these two credits alone reduce a $24,000 system cost by approximately $13,200 in tax savings. New Mexico also exempts solar equipment from state gross receipts tax (the state's equivalent of sales tax) and provides a property tax exemption for solar energy systems. The stacked incentives make Albuquerque one of the fastest-payback solar markets in the Sun Belt.

Albuquerque's intense UV and dust create specific maintenance considerations. Dust accumulation during dry periods (particularly during spring wind season) can reduce panel production by 5-15% until washed off by rain or manual cleaning. Since Albuquerque receives only 9 inches of annual rainfall, rain cleaning is insufficient. One or two manual cleanings per year (garden hose from the ground, $0 DIY or $150-$250 professional) keep production optimized. The intense UV does not damage panels (they are designed for full-sun exposure) but it does degrade exposed wiring insulation and mounting hardware faster than in less intense environments.

Solar installation costs in Albuquerque run $2.40-$3.30 per watt before incentives. A typical 7 kW system costs $16,800-$23,100 before incentives. Albuquerque's excellent solar resource produces 11,000-13,000 kWh per year from this system size, often exceeding annual household consumption.
