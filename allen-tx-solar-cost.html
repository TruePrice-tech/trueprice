Frequently asked solar questions

How long does solar take to pay for itself in Allen?

Payback in Allen depends on electricity rates, sun hours, and incentives. Most systems pay back in 6-10 years and then generate free electricity for another 15-20 years.

What is the difference between string inverters and microinverters?

String inverters are cheaper but the whole array drops output when one panel is shaded. Microinverters optimize each panel independently, cost 15-20% more, and last longer.

Should I buy or lease solar panels?

Buying maximizes long-term savings and increases home value. Leasing eliminates upfront cost but the leasing company keeps the tax credit, and panels may complicate home sales.

Is solar worth it in Allen?

In Allen, residential solar typically pays for itself in 6-10 years and then produces free power for 15-20 more. The 30% federal Investment Tax Credit reduces your net cost significantly. Your actual payback timeline depends on roof angle, shade exposure, electricity consumption, and your utility's rate plan. Request 3 quotes with production modeling specific to your roof for an accurate comparison.

What solar incentives are available in Allen?

All Allen homeowners qualify for the 30% federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC), which has no dollar cap. Beyond that, check your state's solar incentive database (DSIRE) for state tax credits, utility rebates, Solar Renewable Energy Credits (SRECs), property tax exemptions, and net metering policies. These vary by state and utility and can reduce your net cost by an additional 10-30% beyond the federal credit.

When is the best time to install solar in Allen?

For Allen, schedule solar installation during fall or winter. Cooler weather makes roof work safer and the system is ready for the high-production spring and summer months. Keep in mind the full process (quoting, permitting, utility approval, installation) takes 6-12 weeks. Start 2-3 months before your target date. If tax-year timing matters, plan around the installation completion date since that triggers the ITC.

Allen, TX Solar Guide

Allen receives excellent solar irradiance for a North Texas suburb. The city averages 5.0-5.5 peak sun hours per day, with abundant sunshine from March through October and moderate cloud cover during winter months. The flat terrain and typical suburban lot layouts in Allen's major subdivisions provide good south-facing roof exposure on most homes. Allen's latitude (33 degrees N) means panels at a 25-30 degree tilt angle capture close to the maximum possible annual energy.

Allen falls within the Oncor service territory in Texas's deregulated electricity market. As with the rest of the DFW metro, your retail electric provider (REP) determines your electricity rate and solar buyback terms. REPs vary widely in their solar policies: some offer 1:1 retail credit for excess production, while others pay wholesale rates as low as $0.03-$0.05 per kWh. Before committing to solar in Allen, compare REP solar buyback rates and select a provider that maximizes the return on your system's excess production.

Allen's HOA environment adds a layer to solar installations that most Texas cities do not have. While Texas law (SB 398, the solar access law) prohibits HOAs from outright banning solar panels, HOAs can impose reasonable restrictions on panel placement and aesthetics. Many Allen HOAs require that panels be installed on rear-facing or non-street-visible roof surfaces, which can reduce optimal production by 10-20% compared to an unconstrained south-facing installation. Some Allen HOAs also require that panels match the roof color (black-on-black panels on dark shingles).

Hail risk is a factor for solar panels in Allen, as it is across Collin County. North Texas hail events can produce stones exceeding 2 inches in diameter. While Tier 1 solar panels are tested to withstand 1-inch hail at 50 mph, larger hail can cause damage. Check your homeowner's insurance policy for solar panel coverage (most standard policies cover them as attached structures), and consider panels with thicker glass (3.2mm versus standard 2.8mm or 2.5mm) for better hail resistance. Some Allen installers offer supplemental hail coverage.

Solar installation costs in Allen run $2.50-$3.50 per watt before the federal ITC. A typical 8 kW system costs $20,000-$28,000 before the 30% ITC, netting $14,000-$19,600. Texas has no state solar tax credit or rebate, so the federal ITC is the primary incentive. Allen's solar resource produces 11,000-13,500 kWh per year from this system size. Depending on your REP's buyback rate and your electricity consumption, simple payback runs 9-14 years. The system then produces free electricity for the remaining 11-16 years of its 25-year warranty.
