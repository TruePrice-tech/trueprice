Frequently asked solar questions

How long does solar take to pay for itself in Allen?

Payback in Allen depends on electricity rates, sun hours, and incentives. Most systems pay back in 6-10 years and then generate free electricity for another 15-20 years.

What is the difference between string inverters and microinverters?

String inverters are cheaper but the whole array drops output when one panel is shaded. Microinverters optimize each panel independently, cost 15-20% more, and last longer.

Should I buy or lease solar panels?

Buying maximizes long-term savings and increases home value. Leasing eliminates upfront cost but the leasing company keeps the tax credit, and panels may complicate home sales.

Can my Allen HOA block me from installing solar panels?

No. Texas SB 398 (the solar access law) prohibits HOAs from outright banning solar panel installations. However, your HOA can impose 'reasonable restrictions' on panel placement, including requiring installation on non-street-visible roof surfaces and mandating color compatibility (black-on-black panels on dark roofs, for example). These restrictions may reduce optimal production by 10-20%. Submit your proposed panel layout to your HOA's architectural review committee before installation to avoid compliance issues. If your HOA attempts to block installation entirely, the state law provides legal recourse.

How does Texas's deregulated market affect solar in Allen?

Your retail electric provider (REP) determines both your electricity rate and the buyback rate for excess solar production. Some REPs offer 1:1 retail credit; others pay wholesale rates of $0.03-$0.05/kWh. The difference can be worth $300-$800 per year on a typical Allen system. Before going solar: check your current REP's solar buyback policy, compare alternatives (you can switch REPs in the deregulated market), and size your system to align with your buyback terms. With a favorable REP, Allen solar payback runs 9-11 years; with an unfavorable REP, 12-14 years.

Allen, TX Solar Guide

Allen receives excellent solar irradiance for a North Texas suburb. The flat terrain and typical suburban lot layouts in Allen's major subdivisions provide good south-facing roof exposure on most homes. Allen falls within the Oncor service territory in Texas's deregulated electricity market. REPs vary widely in their solar policies: some offer 1:1 retail credit for excess production, while others pay wholesale rates as low as $0.03-$0.05 per kWh. Before committing to solar in Allen, compare REP solar buyback rates and select a provider that maximizes the return on your system's excess production.

Allen's HOA environment adds a layer to solar installations that most Texas cities do not have. While Texas law (SB 398, the solar access law) prohibits HOAs from outright banning solar panels, HOAs can impose reasonable restrictions on panel placement and aesthetics. Many Allen HOAs require that panels be installed on rear-facing or non-street-visible roof surfaces, which can reduce optimal production by 10-20% compared to an unconstrained south-facing installation. Some Allen HOAs also require that panels match the roof color (black-on-black panels on dark shingles).

While Tier 1 solar panels are tested to withstand 1-inch hail at 50 mph, larger hail can cause damage. Some Allen installers offer supplemental hail coverage.
