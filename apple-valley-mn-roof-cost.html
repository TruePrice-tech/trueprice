Roofing questions for Apple Valley homeowners

How many layers of shingles can I have?

Most building codes allow a maximum of two layers. If you already have two layers, the old roof must be torn off before the new one is installed, adding $1,000-$3,000 to the cost.

Does a new roof increase home value?

A new roof recovers roughly 60-70% of its cost at resale and eliminates a major buyer objection. Homes with new roofs sell faster and with fewer inspection contingencies.

Do I need a permit for roof replacement in Apple Valley?

Yes. Most Apple Valley jurisdictions require a building permit for roof replacement. Your contractor should handle the permit and schedule inspection.

What is the difference between architectural and 3-tab shingles?

Architectural shingles are thicker, have a dimensional look, and last 25-30 years. 3-tab shingles are flat, thinner, and last 15-20 years but cost 30-40% less upfront.

How long does a roof last in Apple Valley?

Expect architectural asphalt shingles to last 20-25 years in Apple Valley, with 3-tab shingles at 15-20 years, metal roofing at 40-60, and tile at 50+. The biggest lifespan variable here is UV exposure since it accelerates granule loss and material breakdown. Adequate attic ventilation and lighter-colored materials both help counteract this.

Do I need a permit to replace my roof in Apple Valley?

Most Apple Valley jurisdictions require a permit for a complete roof replacement. This is the contractor's responsibility to obtain before work starts. Skipping permits creates real risks: insurance coverage gaps, code violation liability, and mandatory disclosure when selling.

What's the best season to replace a roof in Apple Valley?

The optimal window for roof replacement in Apple Valley is late summer or early fall, stable weather allows proper curing and contractors are less backlogged than spring. Scheduling during off-peak periods can save 10-15% on labor costs since contractors have more availability and may offer competitive pricing to fill their calendar. Avoid scheduling immediately after a major storm event when contractor demand (and prices) spike.
