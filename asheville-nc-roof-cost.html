What Asheville residents ask about roofing

How many layers of shingles can I have?

Most building codes allow a maximum of two layers. If you already have two layers, the old roof must be torn off before the new one is installed, adding $1,000-$3,000 to the cost.

Does a new roof increase home value?

A new roof recovers roughly 60-70% of its cost at resale and eliminates a major buyer objection. Homes with new roofs sell faster and with fewer inspection contingencies.

Do I need a permit for roof replacement in Asheville?

Yes. Most Asheville jurisdictions require a building permit for roof replacement. Your contractor should handle the permit and schedule inspection.

What is the difference between architectural and 3-tab shingles?

Architectural shingles are thicker, have a dimensional look, and last 25-30 years. 3-tab shingles are flat, thinner, and last 15-20 years but cost 30-40% less upfront.

How do I prevent ice dams on my Asheville roof?

Preventing ice dams in Asheville is primarily an insulation and ventilation issue, not a roofing one. Start with R-49 or better attic insulation and seal all air leaks from the living space into the attic. Ensure continuous soffit-to-ridge ventilation keeps the roof deck uniformly cold. Ice and water shield membrane on the first 3-6 feet from eaves provides backup protection. Fix the heat loss first or new roofing alone will not solve the problem.

Do I need a permit to replace my roof in Asheville?

Most Asheville jurisdictions require a permit for a complete roof replacement. This is the contractor's responsibility to obtain before work starts. Skipping permits creates real risks: insurance coverage gaps, code violation liability, and mandatory disclosure when selling.

What's the best season to replace a roof in Asheville?

The optimal window for roof replacement in Asheville is late spring or early fall, materials install best above 45F and before snow season pricing surcharges. Scheduling during off-peak periods can save 10-15% on labor costs since contractors have more availability and may offer competitive pricing to fill their calendar. Avoid scheduling immediately after a major storm event when contractor demand (and prices) spike.
