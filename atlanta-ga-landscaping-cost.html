Common landscaping questions in Atlanta

How much does a retaining wall cost?

Retaining walls cost $20-$50 per square face foot for concrete block, $25-$60 for natural stone, and $15-$30 for timber. A 50 linear foot wall at 3 feet tall runs $3,000-$9,000 installed.

What is xeriscaping?

Xeriscaping uses drought-tolerant plants, efficient irrigation, and permeable surfaces to minimize water use. It cuts outdoor water consumption 50-75% and is increasingly required in water-restricted regions.

Do I need a permit for landscaping in Atlanta?

Most landscaping in Atlanta does not require permits. Exceptions include retaining walls over 4 feet, grading that changes drainage, connecting to city water for irrigation, and work near property lines.

How often should I replace mulch?

Replenish organic mulch annually (top off to 3 inches). Full replacement every 2-3 years prevents matting and fungal buildup. Rock and rubber mulch last 5-10 years but do not improve soil.

What is the cheapest way to landscape a yard?

Seed instead of sod, use native plants from local nurseries, mulch with free municipal compost, and phase the project over 2-3 seasons. A basic DIY front yard makeover can be done for $500-$1,500.

More questions about landscaping in Atlanta

How much does mulch cost per yard?

Bulk mulch costs $25-$50 per cubic yard delivered. Plan 3 inches deep — one cubic yard covers about 108 sq ft. A 1,000 sq ft bed needs roughly 10 yards ($250-$500 in materials) plus $30-$50/yard for professional spreading and edging.

What does a sprinkler system cost in Atlanta?

A residential irrigation system in Atlanta costs $2,500-$5,000 for a quarter-acre lot with 4-6 zones. Smart wifi controllers add $100-$300 and cut water usage 20-40%. Drip-irrigation zones for planting beds cost $500-$1,000 extra and are worth it on shrubs and perennials.

How much does sod installation cost?

Sod costs $1-$2 per square foot installed including soil prep and rolling. Hydroseeding runs $0.05-$0.10/sqft and establishes in 4-6 weeks. Traditional broadcast seeding is the cheapest at $0.10-$0.25/sqft but needs 6-8 weeks and frequent watering. A 5,000 sq ft lawn lands between $5,000 and $10,000 in sod.

Do I need a permit for landscaping in Atlanta?

Most landscaping in Atlanta — planting, mulching, sod, basic irrigation — does not need a permit. Retaining walls over 4 feet, drainage tied to the storm system, tree removal in some neighborhoods, and any grading that changes lot drainage usually do. Confirm with your landscaper before they start digging.

Lawn and turf choices across Atlanta

Warm-season zoysia (Emerald, Meyer, Zeon, Empire) and Bermuda (Tifway 419, Celebration) dominate Atlanta residential lawns in sunny positions. Tall fescue (Kentucky 31, Rebel blends) dominates shaded Druid Hills and Ansley Park yards. St Augustine appears on some Decatur and Kirkwood lots. The Atlanta metro sits right on the transition zone between warm and cool-season turf, which complicates lawn decisions for homeowners.

Invasive pests and plants near Atlanta

Emerald ash borer is confirmed in Fulton, DeKalb, and Gwinnett counties as of 2024-2026. Asian ambrosia beetle attacks stressed ornamentals. Crape myrtle bark scale (Acanthococcus lagerstroemiae) affects the iconic Southern street tree. Joro spider (Trichonephila clavata), while harmless, is now the most visible invasive in Atlanta yards. Kudzu (Pueraria montana) and Chinese privet (Ligustrum sinense) dominate invasive-plant management. Spotted lanternfly has not yet reached Atlanta but Georgia Department of Agriculture monitors.

Tree services and ordinances: a Atlanta guide

Atlanta Tree Protection Ordinance (Article 26 of the Zoning Code) protects trees over 6 inches DBH on private lots; any removal requires an Arborist Division permit and replacement planting or payment into the Atlanta Tree Conservation Commission fund. Decatur, Roswell, and Smyrna have similar ordinances. Licensed arborists through ISA charge $800-$3,500 per mature tree removal. Snowjam 2014 and ice-storm cleanup historically spike pricing 40-60 percent. Pine-bark beetle and oak wilt drive preventive treatments.

Atlanta-area seasonal maintenance rhythm

Atlanta landscape maintenance runs March through November active, with December-February as the dormant-pruning season. Warm-season grasses green up late-March and peak June-September. Pre-emergent weed control applications happen mid-February and mid-September. Fall overseed with annual ryegrass extends green cover on Bermuda lawns November-April. Pollen season (mid-March through April) produces a yellow coating on every hardscape surface; post-pollen power-washing is a seasonal service line.

Stormwater rules for Atlanta homeowners

Atlanta Department of Watershed Management stormwater ordinance requires on-site detention for any residential project adding over 5,000 sqft of impervious surface. Metropolitan North Georgia Water Planning District coordinates regional stormwater standards. The Chattahoochee River basin rules add further restrictions for parcels within the Metropolitan River Protection Act buffer. Rain-garden and cistern installations qualify for Atlanta Watershed rebates. Permeable pavers qualify for stormwater-mitigation credit.

Local factors: landscaping in Atlanta, GA

Atlanta's housing profile. With average home age near 41 years, Atlanta homes are at the typical first-replacement milestone for major systems. Original furnaces, AC units, water heaters, and roofs installed when the homes were built are aging out. landscaping is hitting peak demand in this age band.

HOA and neighborhood rules. About half of Atlanta's newer subdivisions have HOA governance. Check whether your community requires architectural review for landscaping work. Even when HOAs exist here, approval timelines tend to be shorter than in heavily regulated markets.

Contractor market picture. Rapid growth in Atlanta has stretched the local contractor workforce thin. Lead times of 3-6 weeks are common during peak season, and the best-reviewed companies book out even further. Planning ahead is the single biggest way to control costs. Atlanta's large market supports full-service landscape design firms, installation-only crews, and maintenance companies. For projects over $8,000, a dedicated design firm with installation capability typically provides better results than splitting design and installation across separate companies.

Timing considerations. Atlanta offers a longer landscaping season than cold-climate markets. The busiest period is March through June when spring planting demand peaks. Fall (October-December) is an excellent time for plantings, hardscape projects, and irrigation system installation at lower competition and often better pricing.

Neighborhood patterns. In Atlanta, the highest volume of landscaping work tends to come out of Midtown, Virginia-Highland, Inman Park, and Decatur — larger lots, older homes, and higher home values all drive replacement demand. Contractors familiar with these neighborhoods often already know the typical building stock, permit nuances, and HOA quirks.

What drives cost. Labor costs in Atlanta track within a few points of the national average for landscaping project, so material selection and project scope are the bigger pricing levers for homeowners. At 41 years average home age, Atlanta properties are hitting their first major replacement cycle for systems and components. landscaping project demand is at peak levels in this age band, which keeps contractor schedules full but pricing competitive. High construction demand in Atlanta creates a seller's market for landscaping project contractors. Booking 3-4 weeks ahead is typical; emergency or rush jobs carry 15-25% premiums.
