Austin Native Plant Palette

Texas Hill Country native palette centers on live oak, Texas madrone, Texas mountain laurel, Ashe juniper (cedar), mountain laurel, yaupon, and Mexican plum. Wildflower Center (Lady Bird Johnson Wildflower Center) at UT is the definitive regional reference. Natural Gardener Austin and Barton Springs Nursery supply most of the Austin native market. Hill Country grass plantings use blackfoot daisy, engelmann daisy, and winecup. Bastrop lost pines (loblolly pine) represent a distinct sub-regional palette outside the main Hill Country.

Austin Soil Conditions and Amendments

Austin soils split between Austin chalk east of MoPac and thin clay over limestone ledges west of the Balcones fault. Depth to bedrock is highly variable and often constrains planting. Lady Bird Johnson Wildflower Center and Hill Country Natives soil advice sets the regional standard. Geo Growers and Organics Alive supply most Austin amendments. Expanded shale and pumice amendments address limestone-adjacent thin soils. Texas A&M AgriLife Extension soil testing ($10-$25) is the baseline. Gypsum applications are rare compared to Dallas.

Austin Irrigation Rules and Rebates

Austin Water restricts landscape watering to twice per week under Stage 1 conservation, once per week under Stage 2, and alternating by odd/even address. TCEQ Chapter 344 requires licensed irrigators (LI license) for any new system install. Backflow prevention (TCEQ Chapter 290) is mandatory. Austin Water WaterWise rebates pay $50-$200 for WaterSense smart controllers. Drip-conversion rebates are available up to $600 per property. Round Rock, Cedar Park, and Pflugerville run separate conservation programs.

Austin Water WaterWise Landscape Rebate pays up to $700 for turf-to-native conversions; the Grow Green rebate program adds additional incentives. The Edwards Aquifer Recharge Zone environmental review adds documentation layers on southwest Austin lots. Travis County Water Control and Improvement Districts run separate programs. Lower Colorado River Authority (LCRA) Water Smart program coordinates regional efforts. Smart-controller rebates through Austin Water pay $50-$200 per qualifying device.

Austin Lawn and Turf Choices

Warm-season Bermuda (Tifway 419, Celebration, Tiffany) dominates Austin residential lawns. Zoysia (Empire, JaMur, Zeon) is gaining on Westlake and Rollingwood tract homes for shade tolerance. Buffalograss (Cody, Legacy) is the signature native alternative for full-sun lots and qualifies for Austin Water rebates. St Augustine handles shaded older lots. Cool-season fescue is not viable. The Austin summer heat-and-drought combination pushes most new landscapes toward turf-replacement or native-grass meadow installs.

Austin Invasive Pests and Plants

Red imported fire ants dominate Austin yard pest management. Emerald ash borer was confirmed in Travis County in 2024; Texas A&M AgriLife Extension tracks spread. Crape myrtle bark scale is a significant ornamental pest across the metro. Oak wilt (Ceratocystis fagacearum) is particularly aggressive on Hill Country red oaks and live oaks; Texas Oak Wilt Qualified sanitation rules limit pruning to November-January window. Giant salvinia affects Lake Austin and Lady Bird Lake aquatic plantings.

Austin HOA and Design Review

Austin metro HOA restrictions are common in master-planned communities (Circle C, Avery Ranch, Steiner Ranch, Mueller). Texas Property Code 202.007 protects drought-tolerant landscaping against HOA turf-required covenants. Westlake and Rollingwood have strict tree-preservation and color-palette requirements enforced through local architectural review committees. The City of Austin Heritage Tree Ordinance adds municipal-level protection above HOA rules. Barton Hills Civic Association enforces informal character standards.

Austin Tree Services and Ordinances

Austin Heritage Tree Ordinance protects trees over 19 inches DBH on any private lot; any removal requires an Austin Environmental Division permit and substantial mitigation planting or payment to the tree fund. Protected-tree ordinance also covers trees 8-19 inches DBH under separate review. Licensed arborists through ISA charge $800-$3,500 per mature tree removal. Oak-wilt certified arborists follow Texas Oak Wilt Qualified Logger guidelines. Ice-storm cleanup (2023 February freeze) spikes pricing 40-60 percent.

Austin Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

Austin landscape maintenance season runs March through November active with summer-stress dormancy on cool-season plants. Warm-season grasses green up late-March and peak June-September. Pre-emergent weed control applications happen mid-February and mid-September. Pollen and cedar-fever season (December-February) creates dust management workload on hardscaping. Freeze protection during 2021 Uri-level events drove many homeowners to replace frost-tender species with Hill Country native alternatives.

Austin Stormwater Rules

Austin Watershed Protection Department stormwater ordinance requires on-site detention for residential projects adding impervious cover above thresholds specific to Edwards Aquifer Recharge Zone parcels. The Water Quality Protection Plan requirement adds 3-5 weeks to any southwest Austin permit. Travis County stormwater rules for unincorporated areas are less stringent than city. Grow Green rebates cover rain gardens, bioswales, and rainwater harvesting. Permeable pavers qualify for impervious-cover mitigation credit.

The dominant Austin landscaping scam is the post-ice-storm or post-flooding door-to-door crew offering tree cleanup and yard debris removal for $600-$2,500 cash, demanding upfront payment, then disappearing. Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation (TDLR) licenses irrigators (LI) but not general landscapers. Verify LI license number and $500K minimum general liability insurance before cash changes hands. Texas Attorney General Consumer Protection Division tracks repeat complaints. About landscaping in Austin, TX

Why pricing varies in Austin. Labor rates here track within a few points of the national average, so most of the price variation comes from material costs and local market dynamics rather than wage differentials. For landscaping specifically, our cost-multiplier model puts Austin at roughly 9% below the national midpoint.

Local conditions. Austin has local climate conditions that affect project scheduling and material choices. Native and drought-tolerant species (yaupon, muhly grass, lantana) cut irrigation costs and survive both flood and drought.

Permits, codes, and licensing. Most landscaping projects in Austin require a permit; confirm with the local building department before signing.

Contractor market. As a major metro of 885,400 residents, the local contractor market is deep — expect 10+ legitimate bidders on any standard residential job. Austin's rapid recent growth has tightened the contractor market — expect longer lead times and less negotiating room than smaller markets.
