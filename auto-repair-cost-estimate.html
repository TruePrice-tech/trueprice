What the Estimator Returns

For any repair you select, the estimator returns:

  • Typical part cost range. OEM (original equipment manufacturer) and aftermarket pricing for your specific car. OEM is typically 30-100% more than aftermarket for the same component.
  • Standard labor hours. Pulled from the industry-standard repair time manuals (Mitchell, AllData, MotorAge). This is the "book time" — what most shops bill, regardless of how long the actual job takes. Book time vs actual time is one of the biggest sources of repair-bill disputes.
  • Local hourly rate. Mechanic labor rates vary 2x across U.S. metros. The estimator uses BLS mechanic wage data plus a city-specific shop overhead multiplier to estimate the hourly rate in your zip code.
  • Total range. Part cost + (book hours × local hourly rate) gives you the typical fair price. Most shop quotes within 15% of this number are reasonable; outliers warrant questions.
  • Common upsells. For each repair, the estimator flags the upsells most commonly bundled — so you know what to question when the shop suggests "while we're in there..."

How Repair Estimates Compare to Actual Quotes

The estimate gives you the fair-market range. Actual quotes from individual shops vary based on:

  • Shop type. Dealerships are usually highest (premium for OEM and brand training). Independent shops with ASE-certified techs are mid-range. Chain shops vary widely.
  • Part choice. OEM costs more but is identical to factory. Aftermarket is cheaper but quality varies — some aftermarket brands match OEM (Bosch, Denso, ACDelco), others don't.
  • Labor billing method. Most shops bill book time (the labor manual's standard hours for the repair) regardless of actual time. A few bill actual time. Book time billing is industry standard but it's worth knowing.
  • Diagnostic fees. Most shops charge $100-$200 to diagnose the issue. Reputable shops credit the diagnostic fee toward repair if you authorize work. Less reputable stack diagnostic fees.
  • Shop fees. Environmental disposal fees, supplies, etc. — usually 3-7% of the bill. Some legitimate, some markup. A shop that adds 15%+ in "shop fees" is padding.

Common Auto Repair Upsells

The estimator flags these for repairs they're commonly bundled with:

  • Brake fluid flush. Recommended every 60,000 miles in service manuals; often suggested at every brake service.
  • Transmission flush. Recommended every 30,000-60,000 miles depending on vehicle; many shops push at every visit. Some sealed transmissions don't need fluid changes at all.
  • Fuel injection cleaning. Rarely needed unless symptoms present (rough idle, poor fuel economy). Often pushed as preventive.
  • Coolant flush. Recommended at 60,000-100,000 miles. Often pushed earlier.
  • Power steering flush. Most modern cars use electric power steering — no fluid to flush. Always check before agreeing.
  • "While we're in there" repairs. Suggestions for additional work that wasn't part of the original visit. Some legitimate (replacing a worn belt while the timing cover is open); some pure upsell.
