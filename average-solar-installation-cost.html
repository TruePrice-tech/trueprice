Average Solar Installation Cost

Average solar installation cost in 2026 is $25,000 to $35,000 before incentives for a 10 kW rooftop system on a typical home. After the 30% federal Investment Tax Credit, net cost is $17,500 to $24,500. Per-watt pricing averages $2.50-$3.50 for rooftop and $2.90-$4.20 for ground-mount. State and utility rebates can stack another $1,500-$5,000 in strong-incentive states.

Why "Per Watt" Is the Right Comparison Metric

Solar quotes vary widely in total dollars because system sizes vary widely. The honest comparison metric is dollars per watt of installed capacity. A $24,000 quote for an 8 kW system is $3.00/watt; a $33,000 quote for a 12 kW system is $2.75/watt — actually cheaper despite the higher headline number. Always reduce solar quotes to per-watt for apples-to-apples comparison.

What's Actually In Your Solar Quote

A complete solar quote should itemize:

  • Panels. Brand, model, wattage per panel, total quantity. Tier-1 panels (REC, Q Cells, LG, Panasonic, Silfab) carry stronger warranties; Tier-2 brands save $0.10-$0.20/watt but with shorter warranties.
  • Inverter. String inverter (Solis, Fronius, SMA — cheapest, single point of failure) vs microinverters (Enphase — $0.30-$0.50/watt extra, but per-panel monitoring and shading tolerance).
  • Racking and mounting. IronRidge, SnapNRack, Unirac are common. Adds $0.20-$0.30/watt.
  • Electrical interconnect. AC disconnect, production meter, main panel breaker (or sub-panel if main panel doesn't have spare capacity).
  • Permits and interconnection. Building permit ($150-$500), utility interconnection application ($0-$500), inspection fees.
  • Monitoring. Cellular gateway or wifi monitoring hardware.
  • Labor and installation. Typically broken out separately, $0.40-$0.80/watt.

If a quote shows a single number with no breakdown, ask for the itemization. Quotes that lump everything into "turnkey installed price" make it hard to compare against competitors and hide where premiums are being added.

Hidden Cost: The "Dealer Fee" Trap

One pricing pattern that significantly inflates solar quotes: the dealer fee, also called "loan origination fee" or "financed price uplift." If you finance through the contractor's preferred lender, the cash equivalent price is often 10-30% lower than the financed quote. The difference is the dealer fee — built into the financed total because the lender pays the contractor a kickback.

Always ask: "What's the cash price?" The cash price is the actual cost; the financed quote includes the dealer fee. If you intend to finance, you can often get a lower-rate loan elsewhere (HELOC, personal loan, credit union solar loan) and pay the cash price.

Rule of thumb: if the cash price and financed price differ by more than 5%, that gap is the dealer fee.

Beyond the Federal Credit: State Stack-Ons

The 30% federal Investment Tax Credit applies in all 50 states. Some states stack significant additional incentives:

  • California SGIP — battery storage rebates of $1,000-$5,000+ (income-qualified up to full system cost)
  • Massachusetts SMART — production-based incentive paid over 10 years; can total $5,000-$15,000
  • New York NY-Sun — $1,500-$3,500 per system depending on region
  • New Jersey SREC — Solar Renewable Energy Credits, marketable monthly; typical 10-year value $5,000-$10,000
  • Maryland — $1,000 grant + state SREC market
  • Property tax exemption — most states exempt the value of solar from property tax assessment
  • Sales tax exemption — many states exempt solar equipment from sales tax (saves 5-10%)

Check DSIRE (dsireusa.org) for your specific state's programs. Reputable solar contractors will know your local incentives and factor them into the final price; if a contractor doesn't mention state incentives, ask directly.

How to Tell If Your Solar Quote Is Fair

Three quotes per-watt that cluster within 10-15% of each other suggests fair market pricing for your area. A quote significantly above the others is usually loaded with dealer fees or premium hardware that may not be necessary. A quote significantly below usually has weaker panel warranties, lower-tier inverter, or installer who may not be around in 5 years for warranty service.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average solar installation cost in 2026?

For a 10 kW residential rooftop system (typical for a 2,000-2,500 sq ft home), expect $25,000 to $35,000 before incentives. After the 30% federal Investment Tax Credit, net cost runs $17,500 to $24,500. Per-watt pricing averages $2.50 to $3.50 nationally for rooftop and $2.90 to $4.20 for ground-mount.

How is solar priced per watt?

A 10 kW (10,000 watt) system at $3/watt costs $30,000. Pricing varies by panel tier, inverter type (string vs microinverters), and racking complexity. Per-watt has been declining 5-10% per year since 2010 but flattened in 2024-2025.

How much does the federal solar tax credit save?

The federal Investment Tax Credit covers 30% of total solar installation cost — equipment, labor, permits, and battery storage if installed at the same time. No cap. The credit is non-refundable but rolls forward indefinitely. Steps down to 26% in 2033 and 22% in 2034.

Are state and utility solar rebates worth pursuing?

Yes in strong incentive states (California SGIP, Massachusetts SMART, New York NY-Sun, New Jersey SREC, Maryland) where they add $1,500-$5,000 in savings. Most other states offer property tax exemption and net metering but no direct rebate. Check DSIRE for your state.

What about solar with battery storage?

Adding a 10-13.5 kWh battery costs $9,000-$15,000 installed. Combined with a 10 kW solar system, total is $34,000-$50,000 before incentives. The 30% federal credit applies to battery cost too if installed with the solar, bringing net to $24,000-$35,000.
