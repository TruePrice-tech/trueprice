Why Houston is Different

Houston's HVAC market is unlike anywhere else in the country. Here's why:

Your AC is a survival system, not a comfort feature. Houston averages 99 days above 90F per year. Your air conditioner runs 8+ months, often 14-18 hours per day from May through October. That means a system rated for 15-20 years nationally might last 10-12 in Houston. Plan accordingly -- if your system is 10+ years old, start budgeting now rather than waiting for an emergency replacement in August.

Humidity is the real enemy. Houston's average relative humidity hovers around 75%. Your AC does double duty: cooling the air AND removing moisture. An undersized system will cool the air but leave the house clammy, leading to mold issues. Proper load calculations (Manual J) matter more in Houston than almost anywhere else. If your contractor doesn't mention a load calculation, that's a red flag.

Attic ductwork is brutal. Most Houston homes have ductwork in the attic, where summer temperatures reach 140-160F. That means your cooled air is running through an oven before it reaches your rooms. Insulated ductwork (R-8 minimum) and sealed connections aren't optional here -- they're essential. Leaky attic ducts in Houston can add 25-40% to your energy bill.

The R-410A to R-454B refrigerant transition. As of January 2025, new equipment uses R-454B (Puron Advance) instead of R-410A. If you're replacing just the condenser and keeping your existing air handler, confirm refrigerant compatibility. Mixing systems can void warranties and reduce efficiency. In 2026, most Houston installs are full-system replacements for this reason.

Hurricane season adds risk. Outdoor condensers are vulnerable to wind-blown debris and flooding. If you live in a flood-prone area (much of Houston), consider an elevated condenser pad or platform. After Hurricane Beryl in 2024, replacement demand spiked and wait times stretched to 3+ weeks. Having a relationship with a contractor before storm season matters.

Heat Pumps in Houston: The Honest Take

Heat pumps are gaining ground in Houston, but the market is different from the Southeast or Pacific Northwest. Here's the reality:

Cooling performance is identical. A heat pump in cooling mode is just an air conditioner. No difference. The question is whether you want it for heating too.

Houston's heating demand is minimal. You'll run the heater maybe 30-50 days per year, and hard freezes below 30F are rare (5-10 days). A heat pump handles this easily. But gas furnaces are cheap in Houston (natural gas rates are among the lowest in the country), so the energy savings from a heat pump's heating mode are modest -- maybe $100-200/year.

The real case for heat pumps: Federal tax credits. The 25C credit gives you up to $2,000 for a qualifying heat pump, which can close the price gap with a traditional AC+furnace combo. If you're replacing anyway, the math often works in the heat pump's favor after the credit.

When to Replace in Houston

Best time: Late fall or winter (November through February). This is Houston's HVAC off-season. Contractors have open schedules, and some offer 5-15% discounts to keep crews busy. Your old system can limp through mild winter temps while you shop and schedule.

Worst time: June through September. This is emergency replacement season. Contractors are booked 1-3 weeks out, prices are firm (no negotiating leverage), and you're paying for a hotel or sweating it out while you wait. If your system is 10+ years old, do NOT gamble on it surviving another Houston summer.

Post-hurricane window: After a major storm, HVAC demand spikes from flood-damaged systems. Wait 4-6 weeks for the surge to pass if your situation isn't urgent. Emergency pricing can add 15-25% during peak demand.

Common Mistakes Houston Homeowners Make

After analyzing 26 quotes, we see the same patterns over and over:

1. Accepting the first quote. Houston has hundreds of HVAC companies. The spread between the highest and lowest quote in our data was $6,200 for equivalent work. Getting 3 quotes is the single biggest money-saving move you can make.

2. Ignoring ductwork. If your ductwork is 20+ years old and sitting in a Houston attic, it's probably leaking 20-30% of your conditioned air into the attic space. A new high-efficiency system connected to leaking ducts is like buying a sports car with flat tires. Budget $1,500-3,500 for duct sealing or $4,000-8,000 for duct replacement if needed.

3. Skipping the permit. Some Houston contractors skip the City of Houston mechanical permit to save $150-300 and speed up the job. This can void your manufacturer warranty and create problems when you sell the house. Always insist on a permit.

4. Financing at dealer rates. Many HVAC companies offer 0% financing for 12-18 months but charge a higher equipment price to cover the financing cost. Compare the financed price to the cash price. If the cash price is significantly lower, consider a personal loan or home equity line instead.

5. Oversizing "for extra cooling." Bigger is not better in Houston. An oversized AC short-cycles (turns on and off rapidly), dehumidifies poorly, and wears out faster. Trust the Manual J load calculation, not the contractor who says "you need a 5-ton for this house" without measuring anything.

Houston-Specific Maintenance Tips

Your HVAC system will last longer and cost less to operate in Houston if you follow these maintenance practices:

  • Change your filter monthly during summer. Houston's AC runs nearly nonstop May-October. A clogged filter restricts airflow, reduces efficiency, and can freeze the evaporator coil. Use a MERV 8-11 filter (MERV 13+ restricts airflow on many residential systems).
  • Clean the condenser coils twice per year. Houston's humidity and tree debris coat outdoor coils. Hose them down (no pressure washer) in spring before the cooling season and again in fall.
  • Flush the condensate drain line quarterly. Houston's humidity means your AC produces gallons of condensation daily. The drain line can clog with algae and slime, causing water damage. Pour a cup of white vinegar down the drain line access every 3 months.
  • Schedule a professional tune-up annually. March or April, before the cooling season. A good tech checks refrigerant charge, electrical connections, capacitors, and airflow. Cost: $89-175. Prevention is cheaper than a $300+ emergency call in July.
  • Keep 2 feet clear around the outdoor unit. Houston vegetation grows fast. Trim bushes, vines, and grass away from the condenser to ensure proper airflow.
  • Check the electrical disconnect annually. Houston humidity corrodes electrical connections. A corroded disconnect can cause intermittent failures that are maddening to diagnose. A visual inspection takes 30 seconds.
  • Consider an annual maintenance contract. Houston HVAC maintenance plans run $120-250/year for bi-annual tune-ups with priority scheduling. During July emergencies when wait times hit 5-7 days for non-contract customers, contract holders typically get 24-48 hour response. The priority scheduling alone is worth the cost.
