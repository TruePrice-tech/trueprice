What Does Painting Actually Cost in Denver, CO?

Updated April 2026 · Based on 24 real quotes analyzed by TruePrice

Denver is one of the hardest cities on exterior paint in the entire country. The combination of high-altitude UV radiation, extreme temperature swings (it can be 65F one day and 15F the next), bone-dry air, and hailstorms means your exterior paint job degrades faster here than in almost any other major metro. That's why getting the right paint, the right prep, and the right price matters more in Denver than in most places. Here's what 24 real painting quotes from Denver homeowners look like.

The Real Numbers

Across 24 painting quotes analyzed in the Denver metro area, the average total was $5,218. That covers a full exterior repaint on a typical 2-story home (1,800-2,400 sqft of paintable surface), including prep, primer on bare spots, two coats of quality exterior paint, and standard trim work.

Denver sits at a 1.00x cost multiplier vs the national average. Colorado's labor market is moderate -- higher than the Southeast but lower than the coasts. Denver's large population of painting contractors keeps the market competitive, but the short outdoor painting season (May through October) concentrates demand.

Why Denver is Different

UV radiation at 5,280 feet is brutal on paint. Denver receives about 25% more UV radiation than sea-level cities. UV is the primary cause of paint fading, chalking, and film breakdown. A paint job that lasts 10-12 years in Charlotte or Atlanta might last 6-8 years in Denver -- especially on south- and west-facing walls that catch full afternoon sun. This means two things: (1) budget for more frequent repainting, and (2) use paint with serious UV protection (more on that below).

Dry air means no mildew but more cracking. Denver's average humidity is 35-45%, compared to 60-75% in the Southeast. You'll almost never see mildew or mold on Denver exteriors (a common problem in Atlanta or Houston). But dry air causes wood to shrink and crack, pulling paint with it. Lap siding, window trim, and fascia boards are especially vulnerable. Proper caulking and flexible paint formulations are essential here -- rigid paint films crack faster in Denver's dry climate.

Altitude affects how paint dries. At 5,280 feet, atmospheric pressure is about 17% lower than sea level. Paint solvents evaporate faster in thinner air, which means the paint skins over before it fully flows and levels. For spray application, this means adjusting pressure settings and tip sizes. For brush/roll application, it means working smaller sections and maintaining a wet edge. An experienced Denver painter accounts for this instinctively -- an out-of-state crew may not.

Temperature swings are extreme. Denver regularly sees 40-50 degree temperature swings in a single day. A sunny January afternoon can hit 60F; by midnight it's 10F. Paint and the substrate expand and contract with these swings. Over hundreds of cycles per year, this thermal stress causes paint adhesion failure -- especially at joints, corners, and transitions between materials. High-quality acrylic latex with good elasticity handles this far better than cheaper formulations.

Hail is a recurring problem. Denver metro averages 2-3 significant hailstorms per year. Hail damages paint directly (pitting, chipping) and damages the substrate underneath. If your siding has unrepaired hail dings, painting over them is throwing money away -- the damaged spots will fail first. Budget for siding repair before the paint job.

Paint Brand Recommendations for Denver

Paint quality matters more in Denver than in most cities because of the UV and thermal stress. Here's what works:

Best Exterior Paints for Denver

  • Sherwin-Williams Duration -- The gold standard for Denver exteriors. Excellent UV resistance, flexible film that handles thermal cycling, and self-priming on most surfaces. $55-70/gallon. Most Denver painters know this product inside and out.
  • Benjamin Moore Aura Exterior -- Premium performance with outstanding color retention. Better fade resistance than Duration in some independent tests. $65-80/gallon. Slightly less common in Denver than Sherwin-Williams.
  • Sherwin-Williams Emerald Exterior -- The step up from Duration. Better UV blockers and a thicker film build. $70-85/gallon. Worth it for south/west-facing walls that take the most abuse.
  • PPG Timeless Exterior -- Strong UV performance at a slightly lower price than Sherwin-Williams premium lines. $50-65/gallon. Growing in the Denver market.

Avoid for Denver Exteriors

  • Big-box store bargain paints -- Behr Marquee and similar retail paints are fine for interiors but lack the UV stabilizers and film flexibility that Denver exteriors demand. A $30/gallon paint that lasts 4 years costs more than a $65/gallon paint that lasts 8 years.
  • Oil-based exterior paint -- Once standard, but acrylic latex outperforms oil in Denver's thermal cycling. Oil becomes brittle and cracks; acrylic stays flexible. Colorado also has VOC regulations that limit oil-based products.

For Decks and Fences

  • TWP (Total Wood Preservative) 1500 Series -- Penetrating oil stain. The Denver standard for cedar and redwood decks. Requires reapplication every 2-3 years but protects against UV beautifully.
  • Penofin -- Another penetrating stain popular in the Denver market. Brazilian rosewood oil base. $45-55/gallon.
  • Avoid film-forming deck stains -- They peel in Denver's freeze-thaw cycles. Penetrating stains wear gradually and look better over time.

Seasonal Timing for Denver Painting

Best time: Late May through early October. Denver's painting season is short. You need consistent daytime temperatures above 50F (many premium paints require 50F+) and dry conditions. The ideal window is June through September -- warm days, low humidity, long daylight hours.

Book early: Because the season is compressed into 5 months, the best Denver painters are booked by March for the summer season. If you want your preferred contractor, get quotes in January/February and schedule for May/June. Waiting until April to start calling means you're choosing from whoever has cancellations.

Worst time: November through March. Nighttime temperatures regularly drop below 35F, which prevents proper paint curing. Some contractors will paint on warm winter days, but if temperatures drop below 35F within 4 hours of application, the paint film may not form properly -- leading to premature failure. This is money wasted.

Afternoon storms: Denver gets afternoon thunderstorms almost daily from mid-June through August. Professional painters start at 7 AM and try to finish exterior work by 1-2 PM. If your quote assumes 8-hour days, the contractor may not be accounting for Denver weather patterns.

Common Mistakes Denver Homeowners Make

From 24 quotes analyzed, the patterns are clear:

1. Choosing paint based on price per gallon. In Denver, paint quality is the single biggest factor in how long your paint job lasts. A $30/gallon paint that needs repainting in 5 years costs more over 15 years than a $65/gallon paint that lasts 10 years -- and that's before you factor in the labor cost of repainting (which is 70-80% of the total cost anyway). Always choose a premium exterior paint rated for UV resistance.

2. Painting in the wrong conditions. We see quotes from contractors who schedule exterior work in November or March. If nighttime temperatures drop below the paint's minimum cure temperature (35-50F depending on product), the paint film won't form properly and will fail prematurely. Insist on a schedule that allows at least 4 hours of cure time above minimum temperature after application.

3. Skipping the scrape-and-sand prep. In Denver's dry climate, paint peels and cracks more than it mildews. Proper prep -- scraping loose paint, sanding edges smooth, and priming bare wood -- is 40-50% of the labor on a Denver exterior job. If a quote seems cheap, ask how many hours of prep they're including. Low prep = fast failure.

4. Ignoring wood rot before painting. Painting over rotten wood is throwing money away. The paint seals moisture into the rot, accelerating decay. Denver's freeze-thaw cycle makes this worse -- water trapped in rotted wood freezes, expands, and pops the fresh paint off within one season. Budget for wood repair ($8-15/linear foot for trim, $200-600 per damaged siding board) before the paint job.

5. Not asking about the south/west wall strategy. Denver's sun-facing walls get hammered by UV. A good Denver painter may recommend an extra coat or a darker tint of UV-blocking primer on south and west exposures. This adds $200-400 to the job but can add 2-3 years of life on the walls that fail fastest.

How to Vet a Denver Painter

Denver's short painting season means good painters book up fast. Here's how to find and vet quality contractors:

  1. Ask for a detailed written quote. A professional quote should specify: paint brand and product name, number of coats (body and trim separately), prep work included, linear feet of caulking, estimated duration, and warranty terms. "Paint exterior - $4,500" is not a professional quote.
  2. Verify EPA RRP certification. If your home is pre-1978, federal law requires RRP certification for any work disturbing more than 6 sqft of painted surface. Ask for the contractor's EPA certification number and verify at EPA's Lead-Safe search.
  3. Check references and recent work. Ask for 3 references from the past 12 months, specifically exterior work in the Denver area. Drive by one or two of them to see the quality firsthand.
  4. Confirm insurance. General liability ($1M minimum) protects you if a painter damages your property. Workers' comp protects you if someone falls off a ladder on your property. Ask for certificates and call the insurer to verify.
  5. Ask about their paint brand relationship. The best Denver painters have accounts with Sherwin-Williams or Benjamin Moore commercial stores and buy at contractor pricing. They know the product lines, application requirements, and warranty processes. If a painter is buying their supplies at Home Depot, that's a yellow flag.

Interior vs. Exterior: Where to Spend Your Money

If your budget is limited, prioritize exterior. Denver's climate degrades exterior paint 3-5x faster than interior. Interior paint in Denver lasts 8-15 years easily (dry air = no mildew, no peeling). Exterior paint on sun-facing walls may need attention every 6-8 years. Put your money where the damage happens.

Stucco and Composite: Special Considerations

Not all Denver homes have wood or fiber cement siding. Here's what to know about other common surfaces:

Stucco (common in newer Denver suburbs): Stucco in Denver's freeze-thaw climate develops hairline cracks that let moisture in. When that moisture freezes, cracks widen. Before painting stucco, all cracks must be patched with elastomeric caulk or stucco patch compound. The paint itself should be an elastomeric formula (Sherwin-Williams Loxon XP, Benjamin Moore Aura Grand Entrance) that bridges minor cracks. Standard latex on stucco in Denver will crack within 2-3 years.

Composite/engineered wood siding (LP SmartSide, HardiePlank): These products come factory-primed or pre-finished, but the factory finish typically lasts 8-12 years in Denver's UV environment. When it's time to repaint, the prep is minimal (light sand, clean) compared to wood. But you must use a coating compatible with the substrate -- check the siding manufacturer's recommendations. Using the wrong paint can void the siding warranty.

Brick: Denver has substantial brick housing stock, especially in older neighborhoods. Painting brick is controversial -- once painted, you're committed to repainting every 7-10 years because you can't easily strip paint from brick. If the brick is in good condition, consider limewash or mineral-based stain (Romabio, BioDomus) instead. These breathe better and age more gracefully on Denver brick than film-forming paints.

A note on color selection: Denver's intense UV causes dark colors to fade faster than light colors. If you choose a deep red, navy, or forest green for your exterior, expect it to show fading within 3-4 years on south-facing walls. Lighter colors and earth tones hold up significantly better. If you want a dark accent, limit it to protected areas (porch ceilings, north-facing trim) where UV exposure is reduced. Premium paints with ceramic microspheres (like Sherwin-Williams Emerald) hold dark colors better than standard formulations.

Denver's HOA color restrictions: Many Denver suburbs and even some urban neighborhoods have HOA or historic district guidelines that restrict exterior colors. If you live in a historic district (Curtis Park, Five Points, Baker), check with Denver Landmark Preservation before changing your exterior color. Unapproved changes can result in forced repainting at your expense. Your painter should ask about this during the estimate process -- if they don't, mention it yourself.
