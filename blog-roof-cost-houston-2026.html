What Does a New Roof Actually Cost in Houston, TX?

Updated April 2026 · Based on 17 real quotes analyzed by TruePrice

Houston roofing is not like roofing anywhere else. Between hurricane season, Gulf hailstorms, and an army of storm chasers that materialize after every major weather event, getting a fair quote requires knowing what's normal here. We analyzed 17 real roofing quotes from Houston homeowners -- not contractor advertisements, not lead-gen estimates. Here's the truth.

The Real Numbers

Across 17 roofing quotes analyzed in the Houston metro area, the average total was $11,880. That's for a standard architectural shingle replacement with full tear-off on a single-story home. Houston's average is higher than the national average partly because wind-rated materials and hurricane-compliant installation methods cost more, and partly because Houston homes tend to have larger footprints than the national average.

Houston sits at a 0.94x cost multiplier vs the national average for base labor and materials. But wind-rated upgrades, code-required hurricane straps, and sealed-deck requirements effectively bring the total cost back to or above national averages for comparable square footage.

Why Houston is Different

A few things make Houston's roofing market distinct from almost any other city in the country:

Hurricane wind ratings are not optional. The Texas Windstorm Insurance Association (TWIA) requires specific wind-rated installation in coastal counties. Harris County (Houston proper) isn't technically in the TWIA zone, but most insurers still require 110+ mph wind ratings. This means six-nail patterns instead of four, sealed underlayment (not just draped), and specific starter strip requirements. These methods add 8-15% to install cost vs. a standard install in, say, Charlotte or Denver.

Hail from Gulf storms is different than plains hail. Houston doesn't get the massive hailstones that Dallas or Colorado see, but Gulf thunderstorms produce frequent 1-1.5" hail that degrades shingles over time. The damage is often gradual rather than catastrophic -- your roof slowly loses granules over 3-5 storm seasons until it fails. Impact-resistant shingles (Class 4) are worth serious consideration here, and most Houston insurers offer 10-28% premium discounts for them.

Insurance claims drive the market. In our data, 9 of 17 Houston quotes were related to insurance claims (storm damage, wind damage, or hail). This means two things: (1) many Houston roofers are experienced with the insurance process and will help coordinate your claim, and (2) some contractors price specifically to match insurance payouts rather than competitive market rates. If you're paying out-of-pocket, you can often negotiate below what an insurance-claim contractor would quote.

Storm chasers are a real problem. After every major storm event, out-of-state roofing crews flood Houston. They knock on doors, offer "free inspections," and sometimes disappear after collecting payment. Red flags: no local address, no Texas contractor registration, they offer to cover your deductible (this is insurance fraud in Texas), or they pressure you to sign before your insurance adjuster visits.

Flat roofs are more common than you'd expect. Many Houston homes, especially in mid-century neighborhoods like Meyerland, have sections of flat or low-slope roof. These require completely different materials (modified bitumen, TPO) and different contractors. If your home has any flat sections, make sure your quote addresses them specifically -- don't let a contractor treat a low-slope section like a steep-slope section.

When to Reroof in Houston

Best time: Late fall through early spring (October-March). Houston's mild winters make year-round roofing possible, and the fall/winter window avoids both hurricane season and the extreme summer heat that makes attic work dangerous. Contractor availability is better and you may save 5-10%.

Worst time: Immediately after a named storm or major hail event. Demand surges, storm chasers arrive, prices jump, and quality drops because contractors rush to handle volume. If your roof isn't actively leaking, wait 6-8 weeks for the market to normalize.

Hurricane season planning: If your roof is borderline (12+ years old, missing granules, prior storm damage), replace it BEFORE hurricane season (June 1). Getting caught mid-season with a failing roof means emergency tarping, water damage, and emergency-pricing replacements.

Flat Roofs and Low-Slope Sections in Houston

Many mid-century Houston homes (especially in Meyerland, Braeswood, and Bellaire) have flat or low-slope roof sections. These require completely different materials and expertise than steep-slope shingles:

  • Modified bitumen (mod-bit): The most common flat-roof material in Houston. Torch-applied or self-adhered. $400-650/square installed. Typical lifespan: 15-20 years. Handles Houston heat well.
  • TPO (Thermoplastic Polyolefin): White, reflective membrane that reduces cooling costs. $450-700/square installed. Increasingly popular in Houston for its energy efficiency. 20-25 year lifespan.
  • EPDM (rubber): Budget option at $350-550/square installed. Less popular in Houston because dark colors absorb heat. White EPDM is available but TPO has largely replaced it.

If your Houston home has both steep-slope and flat sections, make sure your contractor has experience with both systems. Many shingle roofers subcontract flat-roof work -- ask who's actually doing the flat section and verify their qualifications separately.

Common Mistakes Houston Homeowners Make

After analyzing 17 Houston roofing quotes, we see these errors repeatedly:

1. Signing with a storm chaser before getting local quotes. Out-of-state crews offer convenience ("we'll handle everything with your insurance"), but if warranty issues arise in 3 years, good luck finding them. Use a contractor with a permanent Houston-area address and at least 5 years of local operations.

2. Not understanding their insurance deductible. Many Houston homeowners don't realize their wind/hail deductible is a percentage (1-2% of insured value), not a flat dollar amount. On a $400,000 home, that's $4,000-$8,000 out of pocket. Know this number before you file a claim so there are no surprises.

3. Accepting an overlay instead of tear-off. Some budget contractors propose layering new shingles over old ones (an overlay). This hides existing damage, adds weight to the roof structure, voids most manufacturer warranties, and prevents proper underlayment and flashing work. Houston's building code allows overlays in limited circumstances, but it's almost never worth it. Insist on a full tear-off.

4. Ignoring ventilation. Houston's heat and humidity make attic ventilation critical. A properly ventilated attic reduces cooling costs and extends shingle life. Your quote should include ridge vent, soffit venting, and possibly powered attic ventilation if your attic gets extremely hot. If the quote doesn't mention ventilation at all, ask about it.

5. Choosing strictly on price. The cheapest Houston roofing quote in our dataset was $4,200 less than the most expensive one for comparable scope. The cheapest contractor specified no underlayment brand, no ice and water shield, and "cleanup included" with no specifics. The most expensive one itemized everything and included a 10-year workmanship warranty. Price matters, but the details behind the price matter more.

Choosing a Houston Roofer: What to Verify

Houston's roofing market has more fly-by-night operators than almost any other city because of the storm-driven demand cycle. Before signing with anyone, verify these five things:

  1. Permanent local address. Not a PO box, not a "virtual office." Drive by if you have to. A real company has a real location with trucks, materials, and employees you can see.
  2. Texas contractor registration. While Texas doesn't license roofers at the state level, the City of Houston requires contractors to register. Ask for their registration number and verify it with the city.
  3. Insurance certificates. Request a Certificate of Insurance showing general liability ($1M minimum) and workers' compensation. Call the insurance company to verify it's active -- some contractors let coverage lapse after getting the certificate. If a worker falls off your roof and the contractor has no workers' comp, you could be liable.
  4. References from the past 12 months. Not references from 5 years ago. Recent work shows current quality and reliability. Call 2-3 references and ask: "Would you hire them again?"
  5. Manufacturer certification (optional but valuable). GAF Master Elite, OC Platinum Preferred, or CertainTeed SELECT ShingleMaster contractors have been vetted by the manufacturer and can offer extended warranties (25-50 year non-prorated). Only 2-3% of contractors hold these certifications.
  6. BBB rating and online reviews. Check the Better Business Bureau, Google Reviews, and Yelp. Look for patterns -- one bad review can be a fluke, but five complaints about the same issue (poor cleanup, warranty avoidance, no-show for callbacks) tells you something real.
  7. Written warranty terms. Get the workmanship warranty in writing before signing. A verbal "we stand behind our work" is worthless. Good Houston roofers offer 5-10 year written workmanship warranties. Anything less than 2 years is a red flag.

Taking an extra week to vet your contractor properly can save you thousands in warranty disputes, shoddy work, and the headache of dealing with a company that disappears after cashing your check.
