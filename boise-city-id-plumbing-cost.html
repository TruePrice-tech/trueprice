Boise City plumbing: answers to common questions

What does a plumbing inspection include?

A thorough inspection covers supply lines, drain lines, water heater condition, fixture connections, shutoff valves, water pressure testing, and a visual check for leaks and corrosion.

How much does a plumber charge per hour in Boise City?

Plumbers in Boise City typically charge $75-$150 per hour. Emergency and after-hours calls add a $100-$200 trip fee on top of the hourly rate.

How often should I have my drains cleaned in Boise City?

Annual drain maintenance is recommended for most Boise City homes. Older homes with mature trees nearby may need semi-annual service to prevent root intrusion.

Can I do plumbing repairs myself?

Simple tasks like replacing a faucet cartridge or fixing a running toilet are DIY-friendly. Anything involving supply lines, gas connections, sewer work, or permits should be left to a licensed plumber.

Should I repair or replace old pipes?

If your home has galvanized steel or polybutylene pipes, full replacement is usually worthwhile. Copper or PEX in good condition can be repaired locally. A camera inspection reveals the true condition.

Boise City, ID Plumbing Guide

Boise's plumbing market has been transformed by the city's population boom. What was a midsized Western city with affordable, available trade services a decade ago now has wait times and pricing that rival much larger metros. Licensed plumbers in the Treasure Valley are fully booked during the spring-through-fall construction season, and emergency call premiums can double the cost of scheduled work. Planning plumbing projects 2-4 weeks ahead is essential in today's Boise market.

Boise water, supplied by United Water Idaho (now Veolia) and the city's own system, comes from the Boise River and deep wells. Water quality is good with moderate hardness (6-10 grains per gallon). The bigger plumbing concern is Boise's cold winters: temperatures regularly drop below 10F from December through February, and the region's relatively dry cold air penetrates insulation gaps aggressively. Pipe freeze damage is a leading plumbing emergency call in Boise, particularly in homes with crawl space plumbing or supply lines in exterior walls.

The Boise Bench, North End, and downtown neighborhoods contain Boise's oldest housing stock from the early 1900s through the 1950s. These homes commonly have galvanized steel supply lines and clay tile sewer lines that have exceeded their useful life. The North End's large mature trees, particularly American elms and silver maples, are root intrusion machines that crack and clog clay sewer lines within 15-20 years of installation. Many North End homeowners budget for annual sewer jetting as routine maintenance.

Idaho's contractor licensing operates at the state level through the Division of Building Safety. Boise requires permits for all plumbing work that modifies water supply, drain-waste-vent systems, or gas piping. The city has adopted the International Plumbing Code with Idaho amendments. Inspection turnaround is currently running 3-5 days due to the construction volume, which means plumbing projects take longer than the work itself would suggest.

One Boise-specific consideration: the city sits in a geothermal zone, and some older properties have geothermal wells or remnant geothermal heating connections. If you encounter unusual piping or well heads during renovation, contact the Idaho Department of Water Resources before proceeding. Improperly abandoned geothermal wells can contaminate groundwater and carry significant liability.
