Buffalo fencing FAQ

Who owns the fence between two properties?

Typically the homeowner who installed it. If the fence sits exactly on the property line, both neighbors share responsibility. Always get a survey before building to confirm the boundary.

What is the difference between dog-ear and flat-top fence?

Dog-ear pickets have angled cuts at the top for water runoff and a traditional look. Flat-top pickets have a clean, modern appearance but hold slightly more water, which can accelerate rot.

Are permits required to build a fence in Buffalo?

Most Buffalo jurisdictions require a permit for fences over 6 feet tall. Many require permits for any fence. You also need to check setback requirements, HOA rules, and call 811 for utility locates before digging.

Does a fence increase property value?

A well-built fence adds 1-5% to property value depending on material and neighborhood norms. Privacy fencing adds more value in neighborhoods where it is expected. Chain link adds minimal value.

How deep should fence posts be in Buffalo?

Buffalo's freeze-thaw cycling will push shallow fence posts out of the ground within a couple of winters. Set posts 36-48 inches deep, at least 6 inches past the frost line. Bottom the hole with 8-10 inches of compacted gravel for drainage, then fill with concrete to grade. There is no shortcut on depth in this climate; it is the single most important factor in long-term fence stability.

Are permits required to build a fence in Buffalo?

In Buffalo, fence permits are standard for installations over 6 feet and sometimes required for any fence. Three things to handle before installation: call 811 for utility marking, confirm property boundaries (budget $300-$600 for a survey if needed), and review any HOA rules. The fence contractor should manage the permitting as part of the project scope.

How much does fence installation cost per foot in Buffalo?

Fence installation in Buffalo typically costs $15-$30 per linear foot for wood privacy, $20-$40 for vinyl, $10-$20 for chain link, and $25-$55 for aluminum or wrought iron. A 150 linear foot backyard fence runs $2,250-$6,000 depending on material. Getting 3 quotes with matching scope ensures fair pricing.

Home stock in Buffalo. Homes in Buffalo average about 58 years in age. At this stage, most have already had at least one major system replacement. fence installation work here often involves matching or upgrading around previous renovations rather than dealing with purely original construction.

HOA & compliance. HOA involvement is uncommon in Buffalo, so most fence installation projects avoid architectural review delays. City permitting alone governs the process.

How the local trade market looks. In a mature market like Buffalo, contractors rely on repeat business and referrals rather than new-construction volume. Buffalo's large market supports both dedicated fence companies and general contractors who do fencing. Dedicated fence installers typically quote 15-25% less because they buy materials in bulk and run crews that do nothing but fence work.

Best months to book. Fence installation in Buffalo requires workable ground — frozen soil makes post-hole digging impossible without equipment. The window runs April through November, with September-October offering the best combination of availability and weather.

What drives cost. Labor costs in Buffalo track within a few points of the national average for fence installation, so material selection and project scope are the bigger pricing levers for homeowners. The 58-year average home age in Buffalo means most fence installation projects encounter at least one behind-the-wall surprise. Experienced local contractors price this risk in; lowball bids from out-of-area contractors often don't.

Red flags to watch for. Any Buffalo contractor who asks for more than 30% upfront before materials are ordered is a red flag. Standard practice is 10-15% deposit, materials-on-delivery payment, and final payment on completion. Watch for fence installation quotes in Buffalo that lack line-item detail. A professional estimate breaks out labor, materials, permits, and cleanup separately. Lump-sum bids hide margin and make change orders impossible to evaluate. In Buffalo, fence installation on homes over 46 years old should include a contingency line item (10-15% of total).
