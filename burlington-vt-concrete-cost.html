Questions Burlington homeowners ask about concrete

Should I seal my concrete?

Yes. Sealing prevents water penetration, staining, freeze-thaw damage, and salt damage. Apply a penetrating sealer every 2-3 years. It costs $0.50-$2.00 per square foot for professional application.

How much does concrete work cost in Burlington?

Concrete costs in Burlington range from $6-$12 per square foot for basic flatwork (driveways, patios) to $15-$30/sqft for stamped or decorative finishes, including labor and materials.

What is the difference between concrete and cement?

Cement is an ingredient in concrete. Concrete is a mix of cement (10-15%), water (15-20%), and aggregates (sand and gravel, 60-75%). Concrete is the finished building material; cement is the binding powder.

How much does a concrete foundation cost?

A full basement foundation costs $15,000-$30,000. A slab foundation for a house costs $5,000-$15,000. A garage slab costs $2,500-$6,000. Price depends on size, depth, and soil conditions.

How long before I can drive on new concrete?

Wait 24-48 hours for foot traffic, 7 days for vehicle traffic, and 30 days for full cure. Avoid deicing salt for the first winter. New concrete reaches 90% strength in 7 days and full strength in 28 days.

What shapes concrete work pricing in Burlington, VT

Why pricing varies in Burlington. Labor rates here track within a few points of the national average, so most of the price variation comes from material costs and local market dynamics rather than wage differentials. For concrete work specifically, our cost-multiplier model puts Burlington at roughly 6% above the national midpoint.

Local conditions. Burlington has local climate conditions that affect project scheduling and material choices. Burlington's extreme freeze-thaw cycles demand premium concrete work. Air-entrained mix, curing blankets, and frost-protected sub-bases are all mandatory. Plan concrete pours for May through September when overnight temperatures stay above 40°F. Cold-weather pours require heated enclosures that add 20-30% to labor costs. Older properties in Burlington (averaging 50 years) often have settling concrete that affects drainage. Re-pouring may require sub-grade correction (compaction, drainage tile) that new construction doesn't need. Budget 10-20% extra for site preparation on established properties.

Permits, codes, and licensing. Most concrete work projects in Burlington require a permit; confirm with the local building department before signing.

Contractor market. At 42,284 residents, the local pool is moderate; expect 4-6 active contractors for most standard residential work. Burlington has seen steady growth, which means a healthy supply of established contractors with multi-year track records.
