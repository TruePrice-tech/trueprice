Fence Installation Cost in California (2026)

Installing 150 linear feet of fencing in California typically runs $6,800–$17,500, with wood the dominant residential choice. Concrete-set posts must clear the 12-inch design frost line. California Building Code Chapter 7A WUI overlay mandates ignition-resistant fencing within 5 feet of any structure in CAL FIRE-mapped State Responsibility Area Fire Hazard Severity Zones — eliminating cedar lap, redwood, and untreated wood fence within the defensible-space radius, and forcing metal, masonry, or fire-resistant composite for the first 5 feet abutting any habitable structure across Sonoma, Napa, El Dorado, Riverside, and San Bernardino county WUI overlays.

California climate & post-design drivers

  • Frost-line trench depth: 12-inch design minimum for post footings
  • Dominant residential fence material: wood
  • Hurricane wind tier: Low — sub-110 mph design wind, standard 8-foot post spacing
  • Termite pressure (TPCT zone): High — TPCT zone 2, PT post requirement
  • HOA fencing-rule prevalence: High — large planned-community HOA penetration restricts material/color/height

California licensing, HOA & boundary law

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: California Contractors State License Board — C-13 (Fencing) (official site)
  • Permit: city or county permit typical for residential fence over 6 feet; CAL FIRE WUI screening required in mapped fire-hazard zones

How fence installation costs vary in California

California Building Code Chapter 7A WUI overlay mandates ignition-resistant fencing within 5 feet of any structure in CAL FIRE-mapped State Responsibility Area Fire Hazard Severity Zones — eliminating cedar lap, redwood, and untreated wood fence within the defensible-space radius, and forcing metal, masonry, or fire-resistant composite for the first 5 feet abutting any habitable structure across Sonoma, Napa, El Dorado, Riverside, and San Bernardino county WUI overlays.

State-specific code or insurance rule: California Civil Code §841 (the 'Good Neighbor Fence Act' as amended 2014) requires adjoining landowners to share equally in the cost of constructing and maintaining a boundary fence — with mandatory 30-day written notice including project description, total cost estimate, proposed materials, and proposed cost-sharing arrangement, and a rebuttable presumption that fence work yields equal benefit to both landowners that places the burden on a non-paying neighbor to prove unequal benefit before a court.
