Roofing questions for Castle Rock homeowners

How many layers of shingles can I have?

Most building codes allow a maximum of two layers. If you already have two layers, the old roof must be torn off before the new one is installed, adding $1,000-$3,000 to the cost.

Does a new roof increase home value?

A new roof recovers roughly 60-70% of its cost at resale and eliminates a major buyer objection. Homes with new roofs sell faster and with fewer inspection contingencies.

Do I need a permit for roof replacement in Castle Rock?

Yes. Most Castle Rock jurisdictions require a building permit for roof replacement. Your contractor should handle the permit and schedule inspection.

What is the difference between architectural and 3-tab shingles?

Architectural shingles are thicker, have a dimensional look, and last 25-30 years. 3-tab shingles are flat, thinner, and last 15-20 years but cost 30-40% less upfront.

Do I need impact-resistant shingles in Castle Rock?

Given Castle Rock's position in an active hail corridor, Class 4 impact-resistant shingles are a smart investment. Standard shingles here often fail early from repeated hail strikes. IR-rated materials handle the punishment significantly better, and most CO insurers offer 15-25% premium discounts that offset the 10-20% higher material cost within a few years.

Are permits required to replace my roof in Castle Rock?

Most Castle Rock jurisdictions require a permit for a complete roof replacement. This is the contractor's responsibility to obtain before work starts. Skipping permits creates real risks: insurance coverage gaps, code violation liability, and mandatory disclosure when selling.

Best time of year to replace a roof in Castle Rock?

The optimal window for roof replacement in Castle Rock is late spring or early fall, materials install best above 45F and before snow season pricing surcharges. Scheduling during off-peak periods can save 10-15% on labor costs since contractors have more availability and may offer competitive pricing to fill their calendar. Avoid scheduling immediately after a major storm event when contractor demand (and prices) spike.
