What Charlotte residents ask about plumbing

How do I know if I have a slab leak?

Signs include unexplained spikes in your water bill, warm spots on the floor, the sound of running water when fixtures are off, and cracks in the foundation or damp baseboards.

What is a backflow preventer and do I need one?

A backflow preventer stops contaminated water from flowing back into the clean supply. Most municipalities require them on irrigation systems and commercial properties. Some require annual testing.

Do I need a plumbing permit in Charlotte?

Most Charlotte jurisdictions require permits for new lines, water heater installs, and sewer work. Minor repairs like faucet swaps and toilet replacements typically do not need permits.

How long do plumbing fixtures typically last?

Faucets last 15-20 years, toilets 25-50 years, water heaters 8-12 years (tank) or 15-20 years (tankless), and supply lines should be replaced every 20-25 years.

How do I protect my plumbing during a hurricane in Charlotte?

Plumbing protection during Charlotte hurricanes requires advance preparation. Test your main water shutoff valve annually so it works when needed. Close it before evacuating. After the storm, follow all boil-water advisories and inspect for sewer backflow if your area flooded. Installing a backflow preventer ($300-$800) is increasingly required by local code and protects against contaminated floodwater entering your home's pipes.

What should a plumbing quote include in Charlotte?

A professional plumbing quote in Charlotte should itemize: labor hours, material costs and brands, permit fees if applicable, cleanup, and warranty terms. The quote should include the plumber's license number and proof of insurance. Get three written quotes for any job over $500 to ensure fair pricing.

When is the best time to schedule plumbing work in Charlotte?

Charlotte homeowners should target early fall for planned plumbing work. Between summer water-heater demand and winter freeze calls, which translates to better availability and pricing. Emergency service runs 50-100% more than scheduled jobs, so proactive replacement of aging equipment is the financially smarter path.
