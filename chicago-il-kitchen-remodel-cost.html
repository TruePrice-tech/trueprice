Kitchen Remodel questions for Chicago homeowners

Is it worth remodeling a kitchen before selling?

A mid-range kitchen remodel recovers 70-80% of its cost at resale. Minor updates (paint, hardware, backsplash) recover even more. Full gut renovations rarely pay back dollar for dollar.

How much value does a kitchen island add?

A kitchen island adds $3,000-$10,000 to project cost. It increases counter space, storage, and resale appeal. You need at least 42 inches of clearance on all sides for traffic flow.

How much does it cost to move kitchen plumbing?

Moving a sink or dishwasher 3-5 feet costs $500-$1,500. Moving plumbing to a different wall or adding a kitchen island with plumbing costs $2,000-$5,000 depending on access and distance.

What kitchen layout is most efficient?

The work triangle (sink, stove, fridge within 4-9 feet of each other) remains the gold standard. L-shaped and U-shaped layouts are most efficient. Galley kitchens work well for single-cook households.

Chicago Kitchen Stock and Typical Layouts

The 1920s Chicago bungalow belt stretches from Portage Park through Beverly and features the iconic pass-through kitchen of 100-140 sqft with original pantry and back-stair access. Lincoln Park and Lakeview two-flats have narrow galleys that were once dumbwaiter-served. Wicker Park and Logan Square three-flats show front-to-back vintage kitchens with built-in bins. North Shore suburban ranches in Evanston and Skokie run 160-220 sqft with post-war layouts that absorb modern island-plus-peninsula plans reasonably well.

Chicago bungalow structural ridge walls run front-to-back and are load-bearing on nearly every 25-foot lot, so opening a kitchen to the dining room almost always requires a flush-beam LVL or PSL with a licensed structural engineer letter. Two-flat kitchens stacked above each other share joist bays that limit hood venting to exterior sidewalls. North Shore split-levels in Glenview and Wilmette have low-headroom soffits over kitchens that complicate tall-cabinet installs.

Chicago Permits and Electrical Scope

Chicago Department of Buildings issues the kitchen permit via E-Plan with a separate electrical permit under the Chicago Electrical Code (which is not the NEC — the city has its own conduit-required rule). Every new circuit must run in EMT or flexible metal conduit, which adds $12-$25 per foot over Romex. ComEd 200A service upgrades run $4,200-$8,500 because most bungalows still have 60A-100A services. Gas work requires a separate plumbing permit through the Department of Business Affairs.

Chicago E-Plan review runs 3-6 weeks on a straightforward permit, longer in peak season (April-June). Inspection scheduling through the 311 portal adds days. DuPage, Cook, and Lake counties have their own portals for suburban work with completely different turnaround times; Evanston runs faster than Chicago proper while Wilmette historic districts run slower. Cold-weather concrete pour restrictions delay any required footing work November through March.

Chicago Plumbing, Water, and Rough-In

Chicago water is notoriously hard and sodium-heavy because of Lake Michigan source water, and the lead service-line replacement program requires disclosure before any sink relocation. Chicago Department of Water Management requires a water meter for new fixture work in formerly unmetered two-flats. Licensed plumbers (required by Illinois Plumbing License Law, 225 ILCS 320) charge $130-$180/hour in-city versus $95-$130 in DuPage and Lake County.

Chicago kitchen remodels often encounter a 40-gallon Peoples Gas tank tucked into a basement utility room; the swap to a heat-pump water heater is tricky because unheated Chicago basements drop below the efficient range. Rheem hybrid units with gas backup are common. Cook County offers a $500 rebate through ComEd on heat-pump water heater swaps tied to kitchen permits. Venting changes require a separate gas-permit re-inspection through the 311 portal.
