Chicago Native Plant Palette

Chicago-region native palette centers on tallgrass prairie species: little bluestem, switchgrass, prairie dropseed, purple coneflower, black-eyed Susan, and rattlesnake master. Woodland natives include wild geranium, Solomon's seal, and Dutchman's breeches for shade beds. The Chicago Botanic Garden's McDonald Woods and the Morton Arboretum supply regional natives. Possibility Place Nursery in Monee is the commercial-scale native supplier. Milkweed for monarch habitat (common, swamp, butterfly weed) is a specific regional specialty.

Chicago Soil Conditions and Amendments

Chicago topsoil is glacial till with a dense clay fraction that drains slowly and compacts severely under foot traffic. South Side neighborhoods sit on hydric wetland-derived soils that hold water. Urban yards commonly contain coal-ash fill from 19th-century heating systems and lead-contaminated soil near pre-1978 housing. University of Illinois Extension soil testing ($15-$35) is the standard. Midwest Mulch in Lockport and Organic Recycling Systems supply amendments. Pine-fines compost and expanded shale are common clay-breaking strategies.

Chicago Irrigation Rules and Rebates

Chicago Department of Water Management requires a water meter on any new irrigation system. MWRD Metropolitan Water Reclamation District does not restrict residential watering days but requires backflow-prevention devices (ASSE 1013 RPZ) on all systems. Oak Park, Evanston, and most collar counties follow Cook County standards. ComEd offers no irrigation-specific rebates but the Nicor Gas SmartSaver program rebates certain gas-powered irrigation pumps. Naperville and Schaumburg restrict daytime watering during declared drought.

Chicago does not offer turf-replacement rebates the way western cities do. MWRD runs the Space to Grow schoolyard green-infrastructure program but not residential. The Chicago Sustainable Backyards rebate pays up to $550 for native plants, rain barrels, trees, and compost bins on residential properties. ComEd and Nicor Gas rebates do not cover landscape scope. Cook County Forest Preserve District workshops train homeowners on prairie restoration and native substitution.

Chicago Lawn and Turf Choices

Cool-season Kentucky bluegrass and turf-type tall fescue dominate Chicago-area residential lawns. Shade-tolerant fescue blends from Pearl's Premium and Premier Seed are common on North Shore bungalow-belt lots. Zoysia (Meyer, Zenith) is pushed aggressively by some local sod farms but the winter-dormancy brown period from November through April makes it unpopular. Cool-season bluegrass reseed happens late August through early October when the soil is still warm but night temperatures have dropped.

Chicago Invasive Pests and Plants

Emerald ash borer (Agrilus planipennis) has killed nearly every mature ash across Chicago and surrounding counties; ongoing removals dominate arborist work. Japanese beetle (Popillia japonica) is a persistent turf and ornamental pest treated with Milky Spore or imidacloprid. Spotted lanternfly has been confirmed in DuPage County as of 2024; Illinois Department of Agriculture mandates reporting. Gypsy moth (Lymantria dispar dispar) defoliates oaks on the North Shore. Box tree moth affects boxwood plantings.

Chicago HOA and Design Review

Chicago HOA restrictions are common in North Shore master-planned communities (Lake Forest, Highland Park, Winnetka) and in western suburbs (Naperville, Hinsdale, Oak Brook). Illinois Common Interest Community Association Act 765 ILCS 160 provides modest protection for water-wise landscaping against HOA turf-required covenants. Many early-20th-century Winnetka and Glencoe subdivisions have deed restrictions on tree removal and driveway materials enforced through civic associations.

Chicago Tree Services and Ordinances

Chicago Department of Streets and Sanitation Bureau of Forestry manages parkway trees; homeowners cannot prune or remove them without a City Council ward-level permit. Private tree removal over 6 inches DBH often requires an Urban Canopy permit depending on ward and zoning overlay. Licensed arborists through ISA or TCIA charge $800-$3,500 per mature tree removal. Post-derecho and ice-storm cleanup spikes pricing 40-60 percent. Emerald ash borer removals dominate current workload.

Chicago Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

Chicago landscape season runs April 15 through mid-November. Spring cleanup includes winter-salt-damaged perennial replacement, lawn dethatching, and early-season fertilizer application. Summer irrigation runs June through August. Fall cleanup (October 15-December 1) includes leaf collection, bed winterizing, and sprinkler blow-out. Winter plow service is a combined-service model in most Chicago suburbs, adding $600-$2,500 season-long. Spring ice-dam damage creates early-April workload.

Chicago Stormwater Rules

MWRD Chicago Stormwater Ordinance applies to any project creating over 15,000 sqft of impervious surface. The Combined Sewer System across most of Chicago proper mandates on-site detention to reduce CSO discharge. Deep Tunnel (TARP) infrastructure reduces but does not eliminate residential detention requirements. Chicago Sustainable Backyards and RainReady programs fund rain gardens, cisterns, and permeable paving. Cook County Bioswale program cost-shares with municipal partners.

Common Chicago Landscaping Scams

The dominant Chicago landscaping scam is the post-storm tree crew that goes door-to-door offering limb removal for $600-$1,800 cash, demands payment up front, and then leaves work unfinished. Illinois does not license landscape contractors generally, but tree-care work often requires an arborist credential through ISA certification. Cook County Department of Building and Zoning tracks complaints against repeat bad actors. Always verify insurance (general liability minimum $1M) and ISA certification before cash changes hands.
