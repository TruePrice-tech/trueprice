Fence Installation Cost in Colorado (2026)

Installing 150 linear feet of fencing in Colorado typically runs $5,200–$13,800, with 6-foot western red cedar privacy the dominant residential choice. Concrete-set posts must clear the 36-inch design frost line. Colorado's 36-inch frost line drives mandatory deep-set concrete-anchored posts on every Front Range residential fence installation — Front Range diurnal swings of 50°F+ between January nights and noon also drive cedar checking and cupping within 3-5 years, with stained cedar privacy retaining dominant suburban share over vinyl due to HOA preference and Western aesthetics.

Colorado climate & post-design drivers

  • Frost-line trench depth: 36-inch design minimum for post footings
  • Dominant residential fence material: Western red cedar privacy — dominant in moderate-climate suburban subdivisions
  • Hurricane wind tier: Moderate — 110–140 mph design wind drives 8-foot post spacing
  • Termite pressure (TPCT zone): Low — minimal termite pressure
  • HOA fencing-rule prevalence: High — large planned-community HOA penetration restricts material/color/height

Colorado licensing, HOA & boundary law

  • License status: No statewide license — municipal credentials only
  • License board: No statewide fencing license — Denver, Colorado Springs, Aurora issue municipal credentials (official site)
  • Permit: city or county permit typical for residential fence over 6 feet; HOA approval required in most master-planned communities

How fence installation costs vary in Colorado

Colorado's 36-inch frost line drives mandatory deep-set concrete-anchored posts on every Front Range residential fence installation — Front Range diurnal swings of 50°F+ between January nights and noon also drive cedar checking and cupping within 3-5 years, with stained cedar privacy retaining dominant suburban share over vinyl due to HOA preference and Western aesthetics.

State-specific code or insurance rule: Colorado Revised Statutes §35-46-101 (the Colorado Fence Law) is one of the strictest 'fence out' livestock statutes in the country — making cattle and sheep producers explicitly NOT liable for damage caused by their livestock on neighboring property unless that property is enclosed by a 'lawful fence' as specifically defined (4-strand barbed wire, 48 inches high, posts not more than 20 feet apart) — placing the burden on residential and crop-farming landowners to fence livestock off their own land in 'open range' counties.
