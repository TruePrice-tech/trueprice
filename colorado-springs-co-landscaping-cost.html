Questions Colorado Springs homeowners ask about landscaping

Should I get multiple quotes?

Always get at least 3 written quotes. Compare line-item details, not just total price. The lowest quote often omits scope items that appear as change orders later.

How do I find a good landscaping contractor in Colorado Springs?

Check licensing through your state's contractor board, verify insurance, read reviews from Colorado Springs homeowners, and get at least 3 written quotes with line-item detail.

How much should I pay upfront?

Never pay more than 10-15% as a deposit before work begins. Standard payment structure: deposit, progress payment when materials arrive, final payment on completion.

Colorado Springs Soil Conditions and Amendments

Colorado Springs soil splits by geology: Pikes Peak decomposed granite in the Westside and Broadmoor is sandy, well-drained, and nutrient-poor, requiring organic amendment for most non-native plantings. Niobrara clay in the eastern neighborhoods (Briargate, Powers, Stetson Hills) is expansive and poorly drained, requiring raised beds and amended backfill for drainage-sensitive plants. The alkaline pH (7.5-8.0) across most of the metro limits plant selection to species tolerant of alkaline conditions. The moderate water hardness (100-150 ppm from CSU's Pikes Peak watershed) is gentle on irrigation systems. Soil testing ($25-$40 through Colorado State University Extension) is essential because the granite-to-clay transition creates dramatically different conditions within short distances.

Colorado Springs Irrigation Rules and Rebates

CSU enforces watering-day restrictions during drought: up to 3 days per week during non-drought, reduced during drought declarations. Smart controllers are required on new installations. The semi-arid climate (17 inches annual precipitation) makes supplemental irrigation necessary for all non-native plantings from May through September. Drip irrigation is standard for beds. The extreme diurnal temperature swing (60+ degrees during shoulder seasons) means early-morning irrigation is essential to prevent ice formation on plant surfaces during cold predawn hours when the irrigation cycle runs. CSU's four-service structure means water-service coordination for irrigation taps routes through the same utility that handles electric, gas, and wastewater.

CSU offers modest turf-conversion incentives through the Water Conservation Program. Colorado Springs' WaterWise program provides free landscape-design consultations for water-efficient conversions. The economic argument: a 1,000 sqft bluegrass lawn costs $250-$400/year in CSU water charges, while a native-grass or xeriscape conversion reduces this to $50-$100. The cultural shift toward water-conscious landscaping is accelerating in Colorado Springs as water-supply concerns grow along the Front Range. The military population's PCS turnover creates opportunities to convert existing bluegrass to native-grass during the transition period.

Colorado Springs Lawn and Turf Choices

Colorado Springs lawns: Kentucky bluegrass is the traditional cool-season choice but requires significant irrigation (1-1.5 inches/week during summer). Buffalo grass (native short-grass prairie species) provides a drought-tolerant alternative at 50-70% less water than bluegrass, but its brown winter dormancy is a dealbreaker for some homeowners. Tall fescue serves as a compromise between water efficiency and year-round appearance. The military-housing market often features standard bluegrass sod installed at builder grade. Blue grama and buffalo grass are the native-lawn options for water-conscious homeowners. Artificial turf ($8-$14/sqft) has growing share in the Briargate and Stetson Hills HOA markets.

Colorado Springs Invasive Pests and Plants

Colorado Springs invasive species: cheatgrass increases wildfire risk in the WUI interface, Dalmatian toadflax displaces native grasslands, and Russian olive trees aggressively colonize drainage corridors. Mountain pine beetle has devastated Ponderosa pine forests on the Westside and in Black Forest, reducing the native-pine canopy by 30-40% since 2008. Ips beetle attacks drought-stressed ornamental pines. Voles damage lawn and groundcover through tunneling. Deer browse is a significant issue for unfenced gardens in the Westside, Broadmoor, and Black Forest interface. Bear activity in the Westside and Broadmoor foothills requires bear-resistant landscape management (no fruit trees near structures, no compost accessible to bears).

Colorado Springs Tree Services and Ordinances

Colorado Springs tree service: mountain pine beetle has been the defining arboricultural event: the beetle infestation that peaked in 2008-2012 killed 30-40% of the Ponderosa pine forest in the Westside, Broadmoor, and Black Forest neighborhoods. Colorado Springs Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

Colorado Springs seasonal calendar: January-February minimal maintenance, snow-management on hardscape. March-April spring cleanup after snowmelt, pre-emergent, dormant pruning. May spring planting after last frost (typically May 15 at 6,035 feet -- significantly later than lower-elevation markets). June-August growing season: weekly mowing, consistent irrigation, monitor for pine beetle activity. September fall planting window (the preferred window for trees because fall-planted roots establish before winter dormancy). October-November fall cleanup, winterize irrigation (drain all exposed components before first hard freeze). December snow season begins. Professional maintenance: $200-$400/month during May-October growing season.

Colorado Springs Stormwater Rules

Colorado Springs stormwater: the Waldo Canyon Fire (2012) and Black Forest Fire (2013) burn scars increased downstream stormwater volumes by 200-400% in affected drainages because the fire removed vegetation that previously intercepted rainfall. Properties downstream of the burn scars (Ute Pass corridor, northern suburban interface) face elevated flood risk. Fountain Creek and Monument Creek corridors experience flash flooding during intense thunderstorm events. Landscape grading must route runoff away from foundations. Rain gardens and bioswales manage lot-level drainage. The semi-arid climate means stormwater management is event-driven (concentrated during thunderstorms and snowmelt) rather than continuous.
