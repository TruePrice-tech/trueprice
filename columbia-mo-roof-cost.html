What Columbia residents ask about roofing

How do I know if my roof deck needs replacing?

Signs include sagging between rafters, soft or spongy spots when walked on, visible water stains from the attic, and rotted or delaminating plywood. Your roofer checks this during tear-off.

What roofing material is best for Columbia?

Material choice depends on Columbia's climate, your budget, and desired lifespan. Architectural shingles offer the best balance of cost and durability for most homeowners.

What is a roofing square?

A roofing square equals 100 square feet. A typical 2,000 sqft roof is about 20 squares. Contractors price materials and labor per square.

Should I repair or replace my roof?

If damage covers less than 30% of the roof and the roof is under 15 years old, repair is usually sufficient. Beyond that, replacement provides better long-term value.

Should I choose the lowest roofing quote?

Not always. Lower bids sometimes exclude flashing, ventilation upgrades, or disposal costs that appear later as change orders. Compare scope, not just price.

How long does a roof last in Columbia?

Roof lifespan in Columbia depends on material and maintenance: architectural shingles run 20-25 years, 3-tab shingles 15-20, metal 40-60, and tile 50 or more. UV radiation breaks down materials faster than most homeowners expect, so lighter colors and proper attic ventilation both add years. These numbers assume no major storm damage and regular maintenance.

Is a Columbia permit needed to replace my roof?

Yes, Columbia generally requires a building permit for full roof replacements. Your roofer should pull this as part of the job. Without a permit, your insurance coverage may be compromised and you face disclosure obligations if you sell the home.

When should I replace a roof in Columbia?

Columbia homeowners should target early fall for roof replacement work. After summer storms subside and before winter weather complicates scheduling, giving you the best combination of weather conditions and contractor pricing. Off-season scheduling typically saves 10-15% compared to peak demand periods, especially the weeks immediately following severe weather events.

Bid-to-bid variation comes down to material grade, labor assumptions, flashing work, ventilation upgrades, and warranty tier. Lower bids often exclude items that appear as change orders later.
