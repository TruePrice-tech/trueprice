# HVAC Regulations, Rebates, and Tax Credits (2026)

## 1. Federal tax credits

### IRA Section 25C — Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit (heat pumps)
- **Status as of 2026: EXPIRED for installs completed Jan 1 2026 or later.** Original IRA timeline went through 2032; OBBB / FY26 reconciliation accelerated the sunset for 25C heat pump category at end of 2025. Customers who installed and took commissioning of a qualifying heat pump on or before Dec 31 2025 can still claim the credit on their 2025 return filed in 2026.
- **Credit amount (for 2025-and-earlier installs being claimed on 2025 returns):** 30% of project cost up to $2,000 cap for heat pumps and heat pump water heaters; the broader 25C envelope is capped at $3,200/yr total.
- **Qualifying equipment:** Must meet the highest CEE efficiency tier in effect at the time of install (varies by north/south climate zone). North zone air-source heat pumps must be ENERGY STAR Cold Climate certified.
- **Woogoro should display:** "Federal 25C heat pump credit expired Dec 31 2025. If you installed before that date you can still claim up to $2,000 on your 2025 return."

### IRA Section 25D — Residential Clean Energy Credit (geothermal)
- **Status: Active.** Geothermal heat pumps still qualify for the 30% uncapped credit. Sunsets to 26% in 2033, 22% in 2034, ends 2035.
- **No income cap, no project cap.** A $35,000 ground-source install yields a $10,500 credit.
- **Also covers:** Solar PV, solar thermal, small wind, fuel cells, battery storage ≥3 kWh.

### IRA HEEHRA / Home Electrification and Appliance Rebates (HEAR)
- **Status: State-administered, rolling out through 2026.** Each state DOE/energy office runs its own program; some launched 2025, others rolling in 2026, a few not finalized.
- **Heat pump rebate cap: up to $8,000** for qualifying air-source heat pump installs.
- **Income tiers:**
  - Households at <80% Area Median Income (AMI): up to **100% of project cost** rebated, max $8,000 heat pump.
  - Households at 80-150% AMI: up to **50% of project cost**, max $4,000 heat pump.
  - Households >150% AMI: not eligible for HEEHRA, but can use 25C (where still active) and utility rebates.
- **Other HEEHRA caps in same envelope:** $1,750 heat pump water heater, $840 electric stove/cooktop, $840 heat pump dryer, $4,000 electric panel upgrade, $1,600 insulation/air sealing/ventilation, $2,500 wiring. Total household cap $14,000.
- **Stacking:** HEEHRA can be combined with state and utility rebates AND (for pre-2026 installs) the 25C credit, but combined incentives cannot exceed total project cost.

### Federal HOMES (Home Owner Managing Energy Savings) rebate
- Performance-based, paid on modeled or measured energy savings. Up to $4,000 (or $8,000 for low-income) per home for installs achieving ≥35% modeled savings. Also state-administered, also rolling.

## 2. ENERGY STAR Most Efficient HVAC tiers (2026)
- **Air-source heat pump:** SEER2 ≥16.0, EER2 ≥10.0, HSPF2 ≥9.0 baseline ENERGY STAR. "Most Efficient 2026" tier requires SEER2 ≥17.0, EER2 ≥11.5, HSPF2 ≥9.5, and variable-speed compressor.
- **ENERGY STAR Cold Climate:** Adds COP ≥1.75 at 5°F and capacity ratio ≥70% at 5°F vs 47°F. Required to qualify for 25C in the "north" zone (and for many state HEEHRA heat pump rebates).
- **Central AC:** SEER2 ≥15.2 (south) / ≥16.0 (north) for ENERGY STAR.
- **Gas furnace:** AFUE ≥97% for ENERGY STAR Most Efficient.
- **AHRI Directory match required** for any rebate or tax credit — equipment must be listed as a tested matched system.

## 4. Contractor licensing thresholds (state level)

HVAC licensing varies wildly. Categories:

- **State-level mandatory license (separate HVAC/mechanical license):** AL, AK, AR, CA (C-20), CT, DE, FL (CMC), GA, HI, ID, KY, LA, MD, MI, MS, NV, NJ, NM, NC, OK, OR, RI, SC, TN, TX, UT, VA, WA, WV. Typically requires 2-4 years experience + exam + bond/insurance.
- **State registration only (no exam):** AZ (KB-1/KB-2 contractor), CO (state contractor + local mech), MT, ND, OH, SD, WI.
- **Local/municipal licensing only (no state HVAC license):** IL, IN, IA, KS, ME, MA, MN, MO, NE, NH, NY, PA, VT, WY. Major cities (Chicago, NYC, Boston, Philly) all have their own license.
- **Bond/insurance threshold range:** $5,000-$50,000 surety bond plus $300K-$1M general liability.
- **Project value threshold:** Most states require licensing for any HVAC work involving refrigerant or gas; some exempt minor repair under $500-$2,500.

## 5. Federal certifications and design standards

### EPA Section 608 — Refrigerant handling (federal, mandatory)
- Required by Clean Air Act for *anyone* who opens a refrigerant circuit. Four levels: Type I (small appliances), Type II (high-pressure: residential AC, heat pumps), Type III (low-pressure chillers), **Universal** (all three).
- Certification does not expire and never needs renewal.
- Penalties for working without 608: fines up to $44,539 per day per violation.

### NATE (North American Technician Excellence)
- Voluntary industry certification, the de facto technical credential. Specialty exams: Air Conditioning, Air Distribution, Heat Pumps (Air-to-Air), Gas Furnaces, Oil Furnaces, Hydronics Gas, Light Commercial Refrigeration, HVAC Efficiency Analyst.
- 2-year renewal cycle with continuing education.
- Required by some manufacturer Authorized Dealer programs (Trane Comfort Specialist, Carrier Factory Authorized Dealer).

### ACCA design manuals (industry standard)
- **Manual J** — Residential load calculation. Required by 2021 IECC and most state energy codes for permitted residential HVAC work.
- **Manual S** — Equipment selection based on Manual J output. Caps oversizing.
- **Manual D** — Duct design. Required when ductwork is new or modified.
- **Manual T** — Air distribution / register sizing.
- **Manual N / Manual Q** — Light commercial.
- Most states with mechanical licensing require contractors to demonstrate ACCA Manual J and D competency on the licensing exam.

### Permits and inspections
- Required in essentially every jurisdiction for system replacement, even like-for-like. Permit cost typically $100-$500. Permit triggers a final inspection by city/county building department. No-permit installs frequently surface as a problem at home sale or insurance claim.

## 6. Refrigerant phase-out timeline

### R-22 (HCFC, GWP 1,810)
- Production and import banned Jan 1 2020. Existing equipment can be serviced only with reclaimed R-22 — virgin stock illegal. Should not appear on any new install in 2026.

### R-410A (HFC, GWP 2,088)
- **Manufacturing and import of new HVAC equipment using refrigerants with GWP >700 banned Jan 1 2025** under EPA AIM Act Technology Transitions rule.
- Sell-through of pre-2025 equipment inventory was permitted through Dec 31 2025. As of 2026 only A2L low-GWP refrigerants are legal in new installs.
- Existing R-410A systems can be serviced indefinitely with virgin or reclaimed R-410A; the refrigerant itself is not banned.

### R-454B (HFO blend, GWP 466) — Trane, Carrier, Lennox, Daikin US, Rheem, York
- Mildly flammable A2L classification. Requires updated installation training, leak detection, and code-compliant mechanical room/charge limits. Drop-in replacement performance similar to R-410A.
- **Supply note: 2025 saw R-454B cylinder shortages with prices up 300%+**, causing some quote inflation in 2026.

### R-32 (HFC, GWP 675) — Daikin global, Mitsubishi, Fujitsu, some LG
- Also A2L. Already widely used internationally. Slightly higher GWP than R-454B but more mature global supply.

### What this means for homeowners in 2026
- Any new install proposal in 2026 must specify R-454B or R-32 equipment, not R-410A.
- Adding charge to an R-22 system is essentially never appropriate — it's a stop-gap until replacement.
- Existing R-410A homeowners do not need to replace early; servicing remains legal.

## 7. Quick-reference rebate stacking decision tree (Woogoro logic)

1. Is the install date 2026 or later?
   - Yes → 25C federal credit unavailable. Show HEEHRA + utility + state only.
   - No → Add $2,000 25C heat pump credit.
2. Is it a geothermal/ground-source heat pump?
   - Yes → 25D 30% uncapped credit, no AMI test.
3. Is household income <80% AMI for the county?
   - Yes → HEEHRA up to $8,000 + 100% project rebate eligible.
4. Is household income 80-150% AMI?
   - Yes → HEEHRA partial rebate up to $4,000 / 50% of cost.
5. ZIP utility lookup → top 3 utility rebates with dollar amounts.
6. State energy office program lookup (NY Clean Heat, Mass Save, TECH CA, NJ Clean Energy, ETO, etc.).
7. Sum all of the above, cap at total project cost, display "Net cost after incentives."
