# Siding Regulations and Rebates

Sources: IRS guidance on Section 25C (Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit), Inflation Reduction Act statutory text, EPA Lead RRP rule (40 CFR 745 Subpart E), OSHA silica standard (29 CFR 1926.1153), state contractor licensing boards (CSLB, MHIC, NCLBGC, FL DBPR, TX TRCC successor agencies), IECC 2021/2024 envelope provisions, ICC and ASHRAE references, Hardie HZ documentation, and major utility EE program directories (DSIRE).

## Federal tax credits

### IRA Section 25C - Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit

Effective 2023-2032. Replaced and expanded the older 10C credit. Annual cap of **$1,200 for the building envelope category** (insulation, air sealing, windows, doors), separate $2,000 cap for heat pumps / heat pump water heaters / biomass.

**Insulation portion of insulated siding - the gray area:**

The statute (IRC 25C(c)(1)(A)) covers "insulation material or system... specifically and primarily designed to reduce heat loss or gain... when installed in or on a dwelling unit." IRS Notice 2013-70 (still the operative guidance pending updated regs) allows the credit on the **insulation component** of insulated siding, not the siding itself, provided:

1. The product meets the prescriptive criteria in the most recent IECC (R-value or assembly U-factor for the relevant climate zone), AND
2. The taxpayer can document the cost attributable to the insulation portion separately from the siding cost.

In practice this means **only insulated vinyl siding (with the foam backer)** has any meaningful chance of qualifying, and only if:
- The manufacturer publishes a Manufacturer's Certification Statement assigning a specific dollar value or percentage to the insulation portion (Progressive Foam, CertainTeed, Mastic, and Alside have historically issued these).
- The contractor itemizes the insulation upgrade separately on the invoice.
- The R-value meets the climate zone IECC threshold (continuous insulation R-2 to R-5 depending on zone - insulated vinyl typically delivers R-2 to R-4, which is below threshold in zones 4+ if used as the only continuous insulation).

**Bottom line:** the credit is theoretically available but rarely claimed correctly. Homeowners installing insulated vinyl should:
1. Ask the contractor for the Manufacturer's Certification Statement.
2. Get the insulation portion cost broken out separately on the contract.
3. Claim 30% of that broken-out cost on Form 5695, capped at the $1,200 envelope total.
4. Combine with continuous exterior rigid foam (under siding) which more cleanly qualifies.

Standard fiber cement, LP SmartSide, wood, brick, stone, and uninsulated vinyl: **do not qualify** for 25C.

### Section 25D residential clean energy credit

Does **not** apply to siding. (25D is solar/wind/geothermal/battery only.)

## Lead paint - EPA RRP rule

**40 CFR 745 Subpart E - Renovation, Repair, and Painting Program.**

Applies to any renovation that disturbs more than 6 sqft of painted surface (interior) or 20 sqft of painted surface (exterior) in a pre-1978 housing unit or child-occupied facility. **A full re-side easily exceeds 20 sqft.**

Requirements:
- Contractor firm must be **EPA RRP Certified Firm** (or state-authorized in MA, RI, WI, IA, AL, KS, MS, NC, OK, OR, UT, GA, DE, NJ - states with delegated programs).
- At least one **Certified Renovator** (8-hour course) on-site.
- Provide homeowner with EPA's "Renovate Right" pamphlet before work starts and get signed acknowledgment.
- Lead-safe work practices: containment plastic, HEPA vacuum cleanup, prohibited practices (no torching, no high-speed sanding without HEPA shroud, no power planing).
- Recordkeeping for 3 years.

Penalties up to **$37,000+ per violation per day** (adjusted annually).

Contractor exemption: lead test kit (EPA-recognized) confirming the painted surface is lead-free.

## Asbestos - federal and state

Cement-asbestos siding (the wavy or wood-grained "shingle" panels common 1940s-1980s) is **regulated as a Category I non-friable ACM** under EPA NESHAP (40 CFR 61 Subpart M). It becomes regulated friable asbestos when broken, sanded, or sawed.

Federal:
- NESHAP applies to demolitions; renovation thresholds vary by quantity.
- Worker exposure regulated by OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1101 (construction asbestos standard).

State rules (more aggressive than federal in many cases):
- **CA, OR, WA, MA, NY, NJ, MN, IL, CT, RI, VT, NH, ME**: licensed asbestos abatement contractor required for any disturbance over a small de minimis amount (often as low as 100 sqft or 10 linear feet).
- Notification typically required 10 business days before disturbance.
- Disposal at licensed asbestos landfill only, with manifest tracking.

A reputable siding contractor will require a **PLM bulk sample test** ($25-75 from any environmental lab) before quoting any pre-1990 home with cement shingle siding.

## OSHA silica - fiber cement

**29 CFR 1926.1153** (the 2017 silica rule) covers cutting fiber cement (Hardie, Allura, Nichiha, etc.). Required engineering controls when cutting:
- Vacuum-shrouded saw with HEPA dust collection, OR
- Wet cutting, OR
- Outdoor cutting with worker upwind plus respirator.

Hardie's official install instructions specifically reference Table 1 of the silica rule. A crew dry-cutting Hardie with a circular saw on a bare deck is in violation. Not a homeowner liability but a quality/competence flag.

## State contractor licensing for siding

There is no federal contractor license. State requirements vary widely:

**Strong licensing (siding contractor must hold a specific license):**
- **California (CSLB)**: C-61/D-12 Synthetic Products, B General Building, or C-35 Lathing & Plastering for stucco. License required for any job over $500.
- **Florida (DBPR)**: Certified or Registered Building Contractor, or specialty Aluminum Specialty Contractor for vinyl/aluminum.
- **North Carolina (NCLBGC)**: General contractor license required over $40,000.
- **Virginia (DPOR)**: Class A/B/C contractor license depending on contract size.
- **Maryland (MHIC)**: Home Improvement Contractor license, with bond.
- **Nevada, Arizona, Oregon, Washington, Tennessee, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, Georgia, Hawaii, Utah, New Mexico, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, Connecticut, New Jersey**: state license required.

**Weak / no statewide siding license (often local only):**
- **Texas, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Indiana, Kansas, Missouri, Illinois, Iowa, Wisconsin, Michigan, Kentucky, Wyoming, Colorado (local), Maine, Vermont, New Hampshire**: no statewide license for siding specifically. Many cities/counties require local registration. PA requires HICPA registration ($50/year, no skill test).

**Insurance**: every state effectively requires general liability + workers comp on jobsite, even where licensing is weak. Always verify a current COI before signing.

## HOA approval

Approximately **74 million Americans (~30% of US households)** live in HOA-governed communities (CAI 2024 data). Almost all HOAs with architectural review require:
- Material approval (sometimes restricted to original material)
- Color approval (palette + sample submission)
- Sometimes brand/profile approval
- Application 30-90 days in advance
- Sometimes neighbor notification

**Failure to get HOA approval before re-siding can result in forced removal** at the homeowner's expense. Real cases on record. The TruePrice estimator should always prompt for HOA status and surface a warning to get written approval before signing the contract.

## Historic district approval

Local historic preservation commissions (LPC in NYC, Landmarks in many cities) regulate exterior changes on contributing structures in historic districts. Typical rules:
- Original material must be retained or replaced in-kind
- Vinyl and aluminum almost always prohibited
- Fiber cement sometimes approved as a wood substitute, sometimes not
- Mortar / stucco must match historic profile and color

**National Register listing alone does not impose review** - it's the local district overlay that does. The Section 106 federal process applies only to federally-funded or federally-permitted projects.

Add 60-180 days to project timeline in most historic districts.

## Local utility rebates for insulated siding

Genuinely rare. The DSIRE database (NC State / DOE) lists only a handful of utility programs that explicitly include insulated siding:
- Some Midwest co-ops (rural electric) offer envelope rebates that can include insulated siding when paired with an energy audit.
- A few Northeast utilities (NYSERDA programs, MassSave) include continuous exterior insulation under siding as part of "deep energy retrofit" rebates.
- Most utility envelope programs cover **attic/wall cavity insulation** only, not siding.

For homeowners genuinely chasing rebates, the better play is **continuous rigid foam under any siding type**, claimed as wall insulation, plus a Home Energy Score / blower door test to qualify for bigger envelope rebates. Insulated siding alone almost never moves the needle.

## Permit thresholds

Wide variation. Common patterns:
- **Permit always required**: full re-side, sheathing replacement, structural changes, anything in HVHZ (Florida coastal), historic districts.
- **Permit usually required**: any project over a dollar threshold ($500-$5,000 depending on jurisdiction), or any job involving water-resistive barrier installation.
- **Permit sometimes waived**: small repairs (under 100 sqft replacement), like-for-like patching.
- **Permit theoretically not required (but actually needed at sale)**: most jurisdictions technically require any siding work over X sqft to be permitted, even if local enforcement is lax. It catches up at home inspection during sale.

A good contractor pulls the permit and includes the fee in the quote. The fee is typically $100-500. If the contractor says "we don't need a permit for siding" without checking the local code, they are wrong by default.

## Water management code requirements

**IRC R703** (and IBC equivalent) requires a "water-resistive barrier" behind exterior wall covering, with flashing at all penetrations and at the base. 2018 and later IRC requires:
- Continuous WRB (house wrap, building paper, or coated sheathing)
- Flashing at all openings (windows, doors, penetrations)
- Kickout flashing at roof-wall intersections (the missing kickout is one of the most common rot causes)
- Drainage gap behind stucco (often a second layer of paper or a drainage mat)
- Through-wall flashing at the base for masonry veneer

**IRC R703.7** specifically requires a drainage plane behind adhered (manufactured stone) veneer, after the 2010s class-action problems with missing drainage causing wall rot. Critical to confirm on any stone veneer quote.

## Energy code - continuous insulation requirements

**IECC 2021** (and 2024) prescriptive wood-frame wall requirements:

| Climate Zone | Cavity Insulation | OR Cavity + Continuous |
|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | R-13 | R-13 |
| 3 | R-20 OR R-13+5ci | R-13 + R-5 continuous |
| 4 (except marine) | R-20 OR R-13+5ci | R-13 + R-5 continuous |
| 4 marine, 5 | R-20+5ci OR R-13+10ci | R-13 + R-10 continuous |
| 6 | R-20+5ci OR R-13+10ci | R-13 + R-10 continuous |
| 7-8 | R-20+5ci OR R-13+10ci | R-13 + R-10 continuous |

"+5ci" or "+10ci" means continuous exterior insulation in addition to cavity. In climate zones 4 marine through 8, **continuous exterior insulation is functionally required** unless the wall is built with R-20+ deep cavity.

For a re-side project, climate zones 5+ should strongly consider adding rigid foam under the new siding both for code compliance (if the project triggers energy code, which varies by jurisdiction interpretation) and for thermal performance. This adds $1.50-3.50/sqft to the project but is the single biggest energy benefit available from a re-side.

Some jurisdictions (CA Title 24, WA, OR, NY) interpret a full re-side as triggering the "alterations" energy code section, which can require continuous insulation even on retrofits. CA Title 24 §150.2 is the relevant section in California.

## Hurricane / wind zone codes

- **Florida HVHZ (Miami-Dade, Broward)**: NOA-approved siding products only. Vinyl must be wind-rated to 110-180 mph depending on exposure. Special fastening schedules. Adds 5-15% to material cost.
- **Florida non-HVHZ**: Florida Product Approval required for all exterior wall coverings.
- **Coastal NC, SC, GA, TX**: similar wind-rated fastening, often local amendments to IRC.
- **Tornado-prone Midwest**: no special siding code, but homeowners are increasingly choosing steel/Hardie/LP for hail resistance.
