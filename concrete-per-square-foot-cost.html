Concrete Per Square Foot Cost

Plain poured concrete costs $6 to $12 per square foot installed in 2026 for driveways, patios, and sidewalks. Stamped or decorative concrete runs $12 to $20 per square foot. Slab-on-grade work for foundations runs $4 to $8 per square foot. The range varies by slab thickness, reinforcement (rebar vs mesh), finish quality, and how much site prep is needed.

What Drives Per-Square-Foot Variation

Thickness. 4 inches is standard for residential flatwork; 6 inches is recommended for driveways with heavy vehicles. Going from 4 inches to 6 inches adds 50% more concrete material (the slab is 50% deeper) — roughly $1.50-$3 per square foot.

Reinforcement. Wire mesh (welded wire fabric) adds $0.30-$0.60/sq ft. Rebar (#3 or #4 bars on 18-inch grid) adds $0.80-$1.50/sq ft. Driveways without rebar will crack in 2-5 years; this is not a place to save money.

Site prep. Pricing assumes a level site ready for forms. If excavation, removal of existing concrete, soil amendment for clay, or extensive grading is needed, expect $1.50-$5/sq ft added. Removing existing concrete alone runs $2-$4/sq ft.

Finish. Broom finish (basic, slip-resistant) is included in standard pricing. Trowel finish (smoother, indoor use) adds $0.50-$1/sq ft. Stamped finish doubles the per-foot cost because it requires specialty stamps, sealers, and skilled labor.

Concrete strength (PSI). 3,000 PSI is residential standard. 4,000 PSI is recommended for driveways and harsh climates. 5,000+ PSI for heavy commercial. Each 1,000 PSI step up adds $0.30-$0.50/sq ft. For driveways, the upgrade from 3,000 to 4,000 PSI is one of the highest-ROI choices.

Color and decorative work. Integral pigment (color mixed into the concrete) adds $1-$3/sq ft. Surface stains and dyes add $1-$5/sq ft. Both require ongoing sealing maintenance.

What Should Be in a Concrete Quote

  • Square footage measured (not estimated) — confirm with a tape measure if you're uncertain
  • Concrete strength (PSI) specified — 3,000 minimum, 4,000 for driveways
  • Slab thickness in inches — 4 inches standard, 6 inches for vehicle weight
  • Reinforcement type — mesh vs rebar, with grid spacing
  • Subbase — gravel base depth (4-6 inches typical) and compaction
  • Forms — included in standard pricing; verify they're being installed properly (straight, level)
  • Expansion joints — required every 8-12 feet to allow for thermal movement
  • Control joints — sawcut or tooled, 1/4 of slab thickness deep
  • Finish type — broom, trowel, stamped, exposed aggregate
  • Curing — wet cure or curing compound for 7+ days
  • Cleanup and disposal — including removal of any existing concrete

Quotes that don't specify thickness, PSI, or reinforcement are underspecified. Ask for them in writing — these are the line items where contractors substitute cheaper materials when the work is hidden under finished concrete.

How to Tell If Your Concrete Quote Is Fair

Three quotes per square foot for the same scope (same thickness, reinforcement, finish, and prep) should cluster within 15-25%. Concrete pricing varies more than other trades because labor productivity differs significantly between crews — fast crews can pour 800 sq ft per day; slower crews half that. Both can produce quality work; price reflects the labor productivity.

A quote significantly below the others usually means: thinner slab not specified, no rebar (mesh only), substandard PSI, or sub-base shortcut. A quote significantly above usually means premium finish, higher PSI, or thicker slab.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does concrete cost per square foot in 2026?

Plain poured concrete (driveway, patio, sidewalk) runs $6 to $12 per square foot installed. Stamped or decorative concrete runs $12 to $20 per square foot. Slab-on-grade for foundations or large flatwork runs $4 to $8 per square foot.

What's included in concrete per-square-foot pricing?

Standard pricing includes site grading and prep, forms, reinforcement (wire mesh or rebar), concrete material (4-inch slab standard), basic broom or trowel finish, expansion joints, and curing/cleanup. NOT typically included: deep excavation, soil compaction, drainage, sealing/staining, or removal of existing concrete.

How thick should concrete be?

4 inches is standard for sidewalks, patios, and residential driveways. 6 inches is recommended for driveways with heavy vehicles or in cold climates with frost. 8+ inches for commercial driveways or shop floors. Going from 4 inches to 6 inches adds roughly $1.50-$3.00 per square foot.

Does concrete need rebar?

Yes, almost always. Wire mesh is the basic option, sufficient for sidewalks and patios. Rebar is the better choice for driveways, particularly with vehicles over 5,000 lbs. A driveway without any reinforcement will crack within 2-5 years.

Is stamped concrete worth the extra cost?

Stamped concrete costs roughly 2x plain ($12-$20/sq ft vs $6-$12/sq ft) but adds significant curb appeal and resale value. The downside: stamped concrete shows wear and color fading more obviously than plain, and re-sealing every 2-3 years is required.
