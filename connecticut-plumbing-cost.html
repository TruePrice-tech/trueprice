Plumbing & Repipe Cost in Connecticut (2026)

Plumbing repipe in Connecticut typically runs $7,800–$18,500 for a whole-house 2-bath PEX repipe, with Type-L copper the dominant retrofit choice and the International Plumbing Code (IPC) as the adopted enforcement basis. Buried service-line trench depth must clear the 42-inch design frost line. Connecticut water-quality & freeze drivers

  • IECC climate zone: 5A — drives freeze-protection scope on supply lines
  • Frost-line trench depth: 42-inch design minimum for buried water service
  • Water hardness profile: Soft (under 3.5 grains/gallon) — minimal scaling, no softener required
  • Dominant retrofit supply material: Type-L copper — dominant on retrofits in high-soft-water and pre-1990 housing stock
  • Adopted plumbing code basis: International Plumbing Code (IPC) — ICC model adopted statewide
  • Lead service line risk tier: High — pre-1986 lead service line corridor under active LCRR replacement

Connecticut licensing & permits

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: Connecticut Department of Consumer Protection — P-1 (Unlimited) / P-7 (Limited Sub) / P-8 (Limited Apprentice) plumbing tiers (official site)
  • Permit: town plumbing permit required for repipe, water-heater swap, or service-line replacement

How plumbing and repipe costs vary in Connecticut

Aging post-WWII Hartford, Bridgeport, and New Haven housing stock sits on cast-iron drain stacks reaching end-of-life with hub-and-spigot joint failures dropping water into wood framing — and brackish Long Island Sound coastal floodplain corrodes galvanized service lines on Greenwich-to-New London shoreline properties faster than inland New England copper systems.

State-specific code or insurance rule: Connecticut Public Act 22-58 directs the Department of Public Health to maintain a Lead Service Line Replacement (LSLR) Inventory accessible to homeowners by address, with disclosure obligations on water suppliers and a state-funded grant program for low-income LSLR — a more aggressive stance than the federal LCRR baseline.
