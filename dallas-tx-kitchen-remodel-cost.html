Dallas Kitchen Stock and Typical Layouts

DFW kitchen stock runs from 1920s M Streets and Kessler Park bungalows (100-140 sqft with original pantry) through 1950s Lake Highlands ranches (140-190 sqft with single-wall layouts) to 2000s Plano, Frisco, and Allen tract two-stories (250-400 sqft open to great room). Highland Park and University Park Tudor and Georgian-revival homes have 250-400 sqft kitchens with butler's pantries and scullery rooms. East Dallas 1940s Forest Hills kitchens are small but have original built-ins worth preserving.

Expansive Houston Black clay under much of East Dallas, Lake Highlands, and Lakewood causes foundation movement that cracks tile counters and unsealed grout over 5-10 years. Post-tensioned slabs in Plano, Frisco, and Allen tracts require PT-cable X-ray before any penetration. Highland Park and University Park have strict FAR (floor area ratio) rules that limit kitchen additions even on large lots. HOA architectural committees in master-planned communities control hood vent placement and color choices.

Dallas Permits and Electrical Scope

City of Dallas Building Inspection issues permits through Dallas Permits Online with 1-2 week turnaround on residential kitchen permits. Oncor panel upgrades to 200A run $2,600-$4,800. Texas 2020-NEC adoption requires AFCI on kitchen circuits. Plano, Frisco, Garland, Richardson, Irving, and the Park Cities each run separate portals with distinct schedules. Park Cities specifically requires architect-sealed plans on any permitted kitchen work, which adds $3,500-$8,000 in plan-prep cost.

Dallas Permits Online runs 1-2 weeks on straightforward kitchen remodels. Park Cities (Highland Park and University Park) requires architect-stamped plans and runs 3-5 weeks. Plano, Frisco, and Allen run 2-3 weeks but have stricter tree-protection requirements. Garland and Mesquite run faster with less review depth. HOA architectural committees add 2-6 weeks on top of any municipal permit, and in master-planned communities these reviews happen on monthly meeting cycles.

Dallas Countertop Trends and Local Stone Yards

Quartz (Cambria, Silestone, Caesarstone) dominates DFW new-construction tract homes; granite remains popular in older-neighborhood remodels particularly on East Dallas and Lake Highlands mid-century restorations. Stone yards including Star Granite in Plano and Aria Stone Gallery in Dallas Design District supply most fabricators. Dallas Plumbing, Water, and Rough-In

Dallas Water Utilities delivers hard water from Lake Ray Hubbard with TDS 180-320 ppm. Slab-on-grade construction dominates post-1980 DFW housing, which forces slab saw-cutting for plumbing relocation. Texas master plumbers (TSBPE-licensed) charge $110-$160/hour. The City of Dallas Plumbing Code amends the IPC; rough-in inspections are required before slab pour on any repour work. Pier-and-beam East Dallas and M Streets houses allow easier plumbing relocation through the crawlspace.

DFW kitchen remodels typically encounter a 40-50 gallon gas tank in a garage or attic. Atmos Energy rates keep gas tanks attractive, but Oncor rebates now pay $300-$1,000 on heat-pump water heater swaps tied to kitchen permits. Attic-mounted tanks are a specific DFW problem because they leak into ceilings; proactive swap to a tankless (Rinnai RUR199 or Rheem RTGH-95) runs $2,800-$4,800 installed. Plano and Frisco building codes require expansion tanks on any replacement.
