Does Homeowners Insurance Cover Plumbing?

By Geoff Lane, founder of Woogoro · Updated April 2026 · Methodology

Standard homeowners insurance covers sudden plumbing damage (burst pipe, water heater rupture) but excludes gradual leaks, neglect, and the cost of repairing the source pipe itself. Typical coverage is $5,000 to $50,000 for water damage, with $1,000 to $2,500 deductibles. Sewer backup, service line, and equipment breakdown are sold as separate endorsements ($40 to $200 per year each). Below is what is covered, what is not, and how to win a denied claim.

Covered vs Excluded: The Sudden vs Gradual Rule

Almost every plumbing-related insurance question reduces to one distinction: was the damage sudden and accidental, or was it gradual and foreseeable? Standard HO-3 and HO-5 policies (the two most common) cover the first and exclude the second.

HO-3 is the standard "broad form" homeowners policy. HO-5 is the premium "open peril" version with broader coverage. Both follow the sudden-vs-gradual rule.

What's Covered When a Sudden Leak Happens

For a covered event (frozen pipe burst, sudden tank rupture), the policy pays for the resulting damage up to your dwelling and personal property limits. Typical inclusions:

  • Water extraction and drying. IICRC-certified mitigation company (typically $1,000 to $5,000 for a single-room event, $5,000 to $20,000 for a flooded basement). Most insurers pre-authorize this within hours.
  • Drywall removal and replacement. Affected portions, usually 2 to 4 feet up the wall in a flood event. Materials and labor.
  • Flooring repair or replacement. Carpet, hardwood, laminate, tile. Hardwood often requires full-room replacement to match.
  • Cabinetry and trim. Kitchen and bath cabinets that wicked water are usually replaced; trim and baseboards almost always.
  • Personal property contents. Furniture, electronics, clothing, paper goods. Subject to your contents limit (usually 50-70 percent of dwelling coverage) and any sublimits on jewelry, art, or electronics.
  • Mold remediation if caught within 14 days. Most policies cover up to a $5,000 to $10,000 sublimit for mold caused by a covered event.
  • Additional Living Expenses (ALE). Hotel, meals above your normal grocery spend, laundry, pet boarding. Only kicks in if the home is uninhabitable. Usually 20 to 30 percent of dwelling limit.

What's NOT Covered (Even on a Sudden Event)

  • The source pipe itself. If a copper line bursts, the policy pays for water damage but not for the new copper pipe and labor to install it. Plan to pay $400 to $1,500 out of pocket for the actual plumbing repair.
  • The water heater itself. If the tank ruptures, water damage is covered but tank replacement ($1,000 to $3,500) is your problem unless the policy has equipment breakdown coverage.
  • Sewer backup without rider. Even if the backup was caused by a sudden municipal main blockage, no rider equals no coverage.
  • Sump pump failure without rider. A $400 sump pump that fails during a storm and floods the basement is excluded under standard HO-3.
  • Mold past 14 days. If the mitigation was slow or you waited too long to file, mold gets denied as a "consequential" gradual loss.
  • Code upgrades. If your repair requires updating to current plumbing code (new expansion tank, sediment trap, sealed combustion vent), only an "ordinance or law" endorsement (often 10 percent of dwelling coverage) pays for upgrades.
  • Earth movement. Slab leaks caused by foundation settling or soil expansion may be excluded under earth movement clauses.
  • Vacancy. If the home is unoccupied 30 to 60 days at the time of the leak, many policies have a vacancy exclusion that voids water damage claims.

Endorsements Worth Adding

Three add-on endorsements close most of the gaps in a standard homeowners policy. Each costs less than $200 per year.

  • Sewer and drain backup endorsement ($50 to $200 per year, $5,000 to $25,000 coverage). Covers damage from city sewer main backups, household drain backups, and sump pump failures. Mandatory if you have a finished basement. Available from State Farm, Allstate, USAA, Farmers, Liberty Mutual, and most major carriers.
  • Service line coverage ($40 to $100 per year, $10,000 to $25,000 coverage). Covers underground utility lines from house to street: water main, sewer lateral, gas, electric, cable. Especially valuable for homes over 30 years old. The water main repair alone can be $2,500 to $8,000 without coverage.
  • Equipment breakdown endorsement ($25 to $75 per year, $50,000 to $100,000 coverage). Covers mechanical and electrical failure of major equipment (water heaters, HVAC, well pumps, sump pumps). Fills the gap where the equipment itself is excluded under standard policies.
  • Ordinance or law coverage ($25 to $100 per year). Pays the additional cost of bringing repairs up to current code. Often added automatically at 10 percent of dwelling coverage; can be increased to 25 to 50 percent.

Replacement Cost vs Actual Cash Value

One detail that surprises homeowners after a major plumbing claim: how the policy values your damaged property. Two valuation methods exist.

  • Replacement Cost Value (RCV). Pays what it costs to rebuild or replace today, no depreciation. The standard for the dwelling itself on most modern policies. RCV usually pays in two stages: actual cash value first, then the depreciation holdback after you complete repairs and submit receipts.
  • Actual Cash Value (ACV). Pays replacement cost minus depreciation. A 10-year-old $1,500 carpet might pay $400 ACV. Common on personal property and on cheaper policies. Always upgrade contents to RCV if available.

Check your declarations page. If you see "ACV" anywhere, you are leaving money on the table.

Hidden Costs and Out-of-Pocket Reality

  • Deductible. $1,000 to $2,500 typical. Some carriers (Travelers, Liberty Mutual) sell separate higher water damage deductibles of 1 to 5 percent of dwelling coverage, which can be $5,000 to $20,000 on a $400,000 home.
  • The pipe repair itself. Always your cost. Budget $400 to $2,500 for the actual plumbing fix.
  • Code upgrades without endorsement. Up to 50 percent of repair cost on older homes.
  • Mold past 14 days. Usually denied. Out-of-pocket remediation is $2,000 to $30,000.
  • ALE only kicks in if uninhabitable. If a single bathroom is unusable but the rest of the house works, no hotel reimbursement.
  • Personal property sublimits. Cash, jewelry, art, firearms, and electronics often have $1,000 to $5,000 sublimits unless scheduled separately. Read your policy.
  • Premium increases after a claim. One water damage claim raises rates 10 to 25 percent for 3 to 5 years. Two claims in 5 years can result in non-renewal.

Red Flags During a Claim

  • Adjuster lowballs the water damage estimate. Common on initial visits. Get 2 to 3 independent contractor estimates and submit them. The adjuster's number is the opening offer, not the final.
  • "Your pipe was old" exclusion abuse. Adjusters sometimes deny otherwise-covered burst pipes by claiming pre-existing wear. Push back with a plumber's written statement that the failure was sudden.
  • Insurer's preferred contractor. Some carriers steer you to a "preferred" mitigation or repair contractor. You are not required to use them. Independent contractors usually deliver better repairs but sometimes longer timelines.
  • Pressure to sign a Sworn Proof of Loss too early. This locks in the claim amount. Do not sign until all repair estimates are in.
  • Mold denial because of "delay." If you reported the leak promptly and mitigated within 14 days, mold should be covered up to the policy's mold sublimit. Document timing carefully.
  • Refusal to cover ALE. If your home has no working bathroom, kitchen, or HVAC, it qualifies as uninhabitable. Push back if denied.
  • Slow payment. Most states require insurers to acknowledge claims within 15 days and pay or deny within 30 to 60 days. File a complaint with your state insurance department for unreasonable delays.

For claims over $25,000, consider hiring a public adjuster. They charge 10 to 15 percent of the settlement but often recover 2 to 3 times what the homeowner gets DIY.

How to File a Plumbing Insurance Claim

  1. Stop the water. Shut off the main valve at the meter or street. Every homeowner should know where this is before an emergency.
  2. Document everything. Photo and video the source, the affected rooms, and damaged personal property. Time-stamped originals are the strongest evidence.
  3. Call the carrier within 24 hours. Get the claim number, adjuster name and phone, and confirm whether emergency mitigation is pre-authorized.
  4. Hire IICRC-certified water mitigation. Extraction, drying, dehumidification within 24 to 48 hours prevents mold and is usually covered.
  5. Get 2 to 3 independent repair estimates. Licensed plumbers for the source repair, restoration contractors for drywall and flooring.
  6. Track ALE expenses. Hotel, restaurant meals (above normal grocery spend), laundry, pet boarding. Save receipts.
  7. Submit detailed inventory of damaged personal property. Photos, model numbers, purchase dates and prices. The contents settlement scales with documentation.
  8. Negotiate the adjuster's number. The first offer is often 60 to 80 percent of fair value. Counter with your independent estimates.
  9. For claims over $25K, consider a public adjuster. Licensed in 45 states. They work for you, not the carrier, on a 10 to 15 percent contingency.

Plumbing Insurance FAQ

Does homeowners insurance cover a burst pipe?

Yes, almost always. Sudden and accidental water discharge from a burst pipe is the textbook covered event under standard HO-3 and HO-5 homeowners policies. The damage to drywall, flooring, cabinets, and personal property is covered up to your dwelling and contents limits. The repair to the pipe itself is generally not covered, only the resulting water damage.

Does homeowners insurance cover a slow leak?

Generally no. The 14-day rule is the industry standard: damage from leaks present for more than 14 days is treated as a maintenance issue and excluded as 'gradual damage' or 'neglect.' Mold remediation, rotted floors, and warped cabinets from a slow leak are usually denied. Some policies have stricter exclusions for leaks longer than 7 days.

Does homeowners insurance cover sewer line backups?

Not under standard policies. Sewer and drain backup is a separate endorsement that costs $50 to $200 per year and provides $5,000 to $25,000 in coverage. Without this rider, sewage backup damage to basements is excluded. About 95 percent of insurers offer this endorsement and it is highly recommended for any home with a finished basement.

What is service line coverage and do I need it?

Service line coverage is a separate endorsement covering the buried pipes and wires running from your house to the street, including water main, sewer line, gas line, and electrical service. Cost is $40 to $100 per year for $10,000 to $25,000 in coverage. Worth it if your home is more than 30 years old or you have mature trees with roots near the line. Standard policies exclude these underground utility runs.

Will my insurance pay if my water heater leaks?

Yes for the resulting water damage if it was a sudden rupture. The water heater itself is usually not covered, only the damage it caused to floors, walls, and personal property. If the leak was gradual (rust, corrosion, slow drip over weeks), the claim may be denied as a maintenance issue. Replacement of the water heater is your responsibility unless the policy includes equipment breakdown coverage.

How much will I pay out of pocket for a plumbing-related claim?

Your deductible plus anything above policy limits. Typical deductibles run $1,000 to $2,500. Some policies have a separate higher water damage deductible (often 1 to 5 percent of dwelling coverage). For a $20,000 burst pipe claim with a $2,000 deductible, you receive $18,000. Mold remediation often has its own sublimit of $5,000 to $10,000.
