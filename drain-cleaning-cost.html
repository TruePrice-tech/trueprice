Snake vs Hydro-Jet: Which One Do You Actually Need?

The single biggest decision on a drain visit is method. Choosing wrong wastes money or fails to fix the problem.

  • Snake / cable cleaning ($150-$500): A motorized cable with a cutting head spins through the drain to break up soft clogs. Effective for hair, paper, food waste, and partial grease. Limitations: a snake punches a hole through a clog rather than scouring the pipe wall, so it leaves residue behind. Snake on a root-infested line typically buys you 2 to 6 weeks before the clog returns. 30 to 90 day warranty is industry standard.
  • Hydro-jetting ($400-$900): 3,500 to 4,000 PSI water through a directional nozzle scours the pipe wall clean. Cuts roots, grease, and scale. Required when a snake re-clogs within weeks, when there is grease buildup (kitchen lines and restaurants), or when the pipe is mature cast iron with scale. Camera inspection should always be paired ($300 to $700 alone, often free with the jetting). 6 to 12 month warranty typical.
  • Chemical drain cleaners: Skip them. Lye and sulfuric drain cleaners (Liquid Plumr, Drano Max) damage cast iron, corrode old galvanized pipe, and rarely fix the actual blockage. They make plumbers' work harder and more expensive when they get called. The exception is enzyme-based monthly maintenance ($10 to $20) which is fine for prevention.
  • Manual auger (DIY): A $20 hand auger handles 80% of toilet clogs. A $35 to $80 25 foot drum cable handles many sink clogs. If you have time and a stomach for the mess, DIY is the cheapest option for first-time soft clogs.

Decision rule: first-time clog, snake. Recurring within months, hydro-jet plus camera. Whole-house backup with no flood, snake first; if water keeps coming back, jet. If you smell sewer gas, the issue is upstream of the clog and a camera should be your first call.

Drain Cleaning Cost by Drain Location

The drain's location affects both access and difficulty, which drives price even on the same method.

  • Kitchen sink ($200-$450): Grease and food waste accumulate at the P-trap and the 1.5 inch branch line. Most are snake jobs. Persistent kitchen clogs usually mean grease coating in the branch line; that needs hydro-jet at $400 to $700.
  • Bathroom sink and tub ($150-$300): Hair and soap scum at the P-trap or pop-up assembly. Often a 30 to 60 minute fix, sometimes solvable by removing and cleaning the pop-up only.
  • Toilet ($150-$350): A closet auger is the right tool. Hand auger DIY is $20 and works on 80% of toilet clogs. A plumber visit is $150 to $350 for a snaking; if the wax ring or flange is damaged, $300 to $600.
  • Shower drain ($175-$400): Hair and soap. Removing the strainer and using a zip tool clears most. Plumber call $175 to $400.
  • Laundry standpipe ($175-$400): Lint and detergent residue. Snake job, similar to a kitchen line.
  • Main line / sewer lateral ($250-$900): The pipe from the house to the city main. Roots, grease, scale, foreign objects. Always start with a camera. Snake $250 to $500. Hydro-jet $600 to $900. The big one for severity and price.
  • Floor drain (basement) ($200-$500): Often connects to the main. If the floor drain backs up, you have a main-line issue, not a floor-drain issue.

Recurring Clogs: When the Pipe Itself Is the Problem

If a drain has clogged twice in a year, the pipe is telling you something. The four common structural causes are below.

  • Tree root infiltration: The dominant cause in homes 30+ years old with clay (vitrified clay tile) or cast iron sewer lines. Roots enter through joints in clay or cracks in cast iron. Hydro-jet cuts them back; foaming root treatment maintains. Spot repair $1,500 to $5,000 for the affected joint. Full lateral replacement $3,000 to $25,000 (see our sewer line repair cost page).
  • Belly (sag) in the line: A section of pipe has dropped below grade, creating a low spot where waste pools and dries. Camera will show standing water in a section of otherwise sound pipe. Cannot be cleaned permanently. Spot repair to re-grade or pipe replacement is the only real fix.
  • Offset, fractured, or collapsed section: The pipe has shifted (often from soil movement or earthquake) or cracked. Camera shows the misalignment. Spot repair if isolated, full replacement if widespread.
  • Grease buildup that re-forms: Common in restaurants but also in homes that pour cooking grease. Hydro-jet clears it but the household behavior must change or the buildup returns in 2 to 6 months. Add an under-sink grease trap if it is chronic.

Diagnosis order for any drain that has clogged 2+ times in a year: hydro-jet plus camera. Total cost $700 to $1,400. Without the camera, you are paying for a plumber to guess and sell the highest-margin guess.

Camera Inspection: When to Demand the Video File

A sewer camera is a 1/2 inch self-leveling camera on a stiff push rod, fed through a cleanout or removed toilet. The plumber sees and records the inside of your pipe in real time. Most cameras have a transmitter that allows the plumber to mark the location and depth of any issue from above ground.

  • What a camera should reveal: pipe material (clay, cast iron, PVC, ABS, Orangeburg), pipe condition (smooth and round = healthy, scaled and pitted = aging), root intrusions and their location in linear feet from the entry point, bellies (visible standing water), offsets, fractures, foreign objects, and where the line ties into the city main.
  • What you should receive: a video file (MP4 or AirDrop), a written report listing footage, depth, and pipe material, and a clear recommendation tied to specific footage. "Replace from cleanout to street" without a video is unverifiable.
  • When camera alone is overkill: first-time clogs, single-fixture branch line issues. Use camera on main line work and any time replacement is recommended.
  • Red flag: any plumber recommending pipe replacement without showing video footage.

The $99 Drain Special: Industry Bait-and-Switch

The advertised $99 drain cleaning is one of the most reliable bait-and-switch patterns in residential plumbing. Here is how it actually works in 2026.

  • The fine print: $99 typically requires an accessible cleanout, one drain only, clearance within 30 minutes, no diagnostic, and "standard equipment." Most homes do not meet at least one of those conditions.
  • The on-site upcharge: Tech arrives, "cannot get clearance" with the standard cable, recommends an upgraded jet ($600 to $900) or a cleanout install ($300 to $1,500). The original $99 disappears.
  • The diagnostic add-on: Some chains charge a separate diagnostic fee on top of the $99 service call. Net cost without any actual cleaning: $200 to $400.
  • The camera shopping cart: After clearing, the tech offers a "free" camera inspection that is used to recommend $5,000 to $25,000 in pipe replacement. The video is rarely emailed unless you ask.
  • What to do instead: Get a flat written quote before the truck rolls. A reputable plumber will commit to a price band ($200 to $500 for a typical residential snake job) on the phone given basic info. If the visit reveals additional scope, the tech calls for written approval before billing more.

If a drain-cleaning chain refuses to give a written ceiling on the phone, find another plumber. The $99 ad is almost always a teaser.

Hidden Drain Cleaning Costs Most Homeowners Miss

  • Cleanout access ($300-$1,500): If your home has no accessible cleanout (common in homes built before 1980), the plumber must access via a pulled toilet or vent stack, or install a cleanout. Pulled toilet is $75 to $200 add-on. Cleanout install is $300 to $1,500.
  • Camera inspection separate fee ($300-$700): Many shops charge for the camera even when bundled with the cleaning. Read the line items.
  • Root removal vs spot repair vs full replacement: Sequential ladder of cost. Hydro-jet roots $600-$900. Spot repair to remove the affected joint $1,500-$5,000. Full lateral replacement $3,000-$25,000.
  • Drywall and tile patching after access cuts: If the plumber has to open a wall to access a clogged stack or vent, the patch is rarely included. Budget $200 to $1,200 for drywall, paint, or tile repair afterward.
  • After-hours premium (50-100%): Nights, weekends, and holidays. A $300 weekday job becomes $450 to $600 on Sunday.
  • Service call fee ($75-$200): Some plumbers charge this on top of the cleaning, others credit it toward the work. Ask.
  • Trip charge for second visit: If parts need to be ordered (rare for snaking, common for cleanout installs), the second visit may carry a separate trip fee.
  • Outdoor/landscaping disturbance: Cleanout installs in landscaped beds may damage plants, sprinklers, or sod. Restoration is rarely included.

Drain Cleaning Quote Red Flags (2026)

  • $99 drain special advertising: Almost always upcharges to $400 to $900 once on site. Get a written ceiling before the visit.
  • Recommending pipe replacement without camera footage: A camera shows the actual problem. Without one, the plumber is selling a recommendation, not a diagnosis.
  • No warranty on the cleaning: Industry standard is 30 to 90 days for snake, 6 to 12 months for hydro-jet. No warranty means the plumber knows the work will not hold.
  • Cash-only or unusual payment: Bypasses normal accounting and consumer protection. Reputable plumbers accept check, ACH, and card.
  • Per-foot pricing on cable cleaning: Lets the bill scale on jobs the tech finishes in 30 minutes. Demand flat-rate.
  • Refusing to email the camera video file: You paid for the inspection. The video is your record. Refusal is a sign the diagnosis was inflated.
  • Aggressive on-site upselling within the first 10 minutes: Tech arrives, "discovers" $5,000 in problems before doing the job you called for. Take a beat, get a second opinion.
  • Same-day pressure to sign on excavation work: Legitimate plumbers hold pricing 30 days. Same-day urgency on five-figure repairs is a sales tactic.
  • License number missing from the quote: Plumbing license is required for sewer work in nearly every state. Verify with the state board.
  • Vague scope on big jobs: "We have to dig the whole yard" with no footage and no permit plan is the most expensive sentence in plumbing.

How to Get the Best Drain Cleaning Quote

  1. Describe the symptom precisely. Single fixture or whole house? First time or recurring? Slow drain or full backup? Any sewer gas smell? Vague descriptions invite vague (and inflated) quotes.
  2. Get a written flat-rate quote before the truck rolls. Reputable plumbers commit to a price band on the phone given basic info. A refusal to commit is a red flag.
  3. Ask about cleanout access up front. If you do not have an accessible cleanout, the plumber must work around it. Knowing this in advance avoids the $300 to $1,500 surprise.
  4. Demand a camera before any spot repair. If the plumber recommends excavation or pipe replacement, the camera video must come first. Get the file emailed to you.
  5. Verify the warranty in writing. Snake: 30 to 90 days. Hydro-jet: 6 to 12 months. Cleanouts: typically not warrantied. Get exclusions in writing.
  6. For recurring clogs, get a second opinion before excavation. Diagnosis-shopping is the single biggest savings lever on chronic drain issues. A fourth opinion on a $15,000 sewer recommendation is cheap.
  7. Check license and insurance. Active state plumbing license number on the quote. GL and workers comp certificates available on request.

Drain Cleaning FAQ

How much does drain cleaning cost in 2026?

Drain cleaning costs $150 to $500 for a standard snake or cable service in 2026. Hydro-jetting (high-pressure water) runs $400 to $900. Camera inspection runs $300 to $700 as a standalone service or is sometimes bundled free with hydro-jetting. Most single-fixture clogs (kitchen sink, bathroom drain) are snake jobs in the $200 to $350 range. Main-line clogs and recurring clogs typically need hydro-jet plus camera, $700 to $1,400 combined.

What is the difference between snaking and hydro-jetting a drain?

A snake (motorized cable) breaks through soft clogs like hair, grease, and paper. A hydro-jet uses 3,500 to 4,000 PSI water through a directional nozzle to scour the entire pipe wall and cut through roots and scale. Snake is the right call for first-time clogs and known soft blockages. Hydro-jet is required for recurring clogs, root infiltration, and grease buildup in main lines. Snaking a root-infested line clears a hole in the roots that re-clogs in 2 to 6 weeks; hydro-jet cuts the roots back to the pipe wall and lasts 6 to 18 months.

Do I really need a camera inspection?

For first-time single-fixture clogs, no. For any drain that has clogged twice in the same year, or any main-line clog, yes. A camera ($300 to $700) shows whether you have roots, a belly (low spot holding water), a fractured pipe, a foreign object, or scale buildup. Without that diagnosis, the next plumber can sell you whatever repair has the highest margin. Demand the video file (most plumbers can email or AirDrop it) and a written report of the linear footage where the issue is.

What does a $99 drain cleaning special actually cost?

The advertised $99 drain cleaning is almost always a tease. The published terms typically require an accessible cleanout (which most homes do not have), one drain only, and clearance within 30 minutes. The actual on-site bill commonly lands at $400 to $900 once the tech upgrades to hydro-jet, charges a cleanout install ($300 to $1,500), or recommends a camera ($300 to $700). Reputable plumbers quote a flat written price before starting, with a per-hour cap if scope changes. Always demand the written total before authorizing work.

Why does my drain keep clogging?

Recurring clogs almost always mean the pipe itself is failing or invaded. The four common causes are: tree roots through clay or cast iron pipe at joints (most common in homes 30+ years old), a belly (a section sagging below grade so debris settles), a fractured or offset pipe section, and grease buildup that re-forms (common in restaurants and homes that pour cooking grease). The diagnosis requires a camera inspection. Repeat snaking is a maintenance pattern that costs more long-term than a one-time hydro-jet plus camera plus targeted spot repair.

How long should a drain cleaning last?

A reputable snake job carries a 30 to 90 day warranty for the same drain. Hydro-jet typically carries a 6 month warranty for grease and a 12 month warranty for roots. Camera-confirmed root cleanouts in healthy pipe often last 12 to 24 months between treatments. If a drain re-clogs inside the warranty window, the plumber should return free. If it re-clogs the day after expiration, the issue is structural, not maintenance, and you need a camera and a repair plan.
