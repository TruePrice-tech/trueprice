How much does a concrete patio cost?

A 300 sqft concrete patio costs $1,800-$3,600 for basic broom finish. Stamped patterns add $3-$8/sqft. Colored concrete adds $1-$3/sqft. An exposed aggregate finish adds $2-$4/sqft.

About concrete work in Duluth, MN

Where Duluth's pricing comes from. At 35% above national average labor rates, Duluth's contractor pricing starts from a higher baseline than most US markets. The gap reflects both local wages and regional demand for skilled trades. On concrete work, Duluth runs about 10% above the US average based on our pricing data across metro areas.

Local conditions in Duluth. Duluth has local climate conditions that affect project scheduling and material choices. Duluth's extreme freeze-thaw cycles demand premium concrete work. Air-entrained mix, curing blankets, and frost-protected sub-bases are all mandatory. Plan concrete pours for May through September when overnight temperatures stay above 40°F. Cold-weather pours require heated enclosures that add 20-30% to labor costs. Standard concrete in Duluth runs $6-10 per square foot for basic flatwork (driveways, walkways). Decorative options (stamped, colored, exposed aggregate) add $4-8 per square foot. The biggest hidden cost is demolition and removal of existing concrete — budget $2-4 per square foot for tearout of old slabs.

Duluth permits and code requirements. Permits are usually required for concrete work in Duluth; verify the specifics with the local building department before committing.

The Duluth contractor pool. At 86,128 residents, the local pool is moderate; expect 4-6 active contractors for most standard residential work. The Duluth area's flat growth keeps contractor availability high year-round. Seasonal discounts of 10-15% are realistic for flexible project timelines.

Local factors: concrete work in Duluth, MN

Home stock in Duluth. Duluth's homes, averaging 48 years, are entering the window where original-equipment replacements become unavoidable. This is the sweet spot for concrete work work since systems are aging but the homes are modern enough to avoid major code-upgrade surprises.

HOA & compliance. HOA rules affect some but not all Duluth homeowners. If applicable, confirm your HOA's requirements for concrete work before getting quotes. This avoids the situation where your preferred material or color gets rejected after you have already signed a contract.

How the local trade market looks. Duluth's flat growth pattern keeps contractor pricing competitive. You will likely find more willingness to negotiate and shorter lead times than in booming cities. However, fewer contractors means fewer quotes to compare, so check references carefully. In Duluth (86,128 residents), concrete work is often done by small local companies or general contractors. For decorative work (stamped, colored), verify the contractor has a portfolio of completed projects in your specific pattern and color — decorative concrete expertise varies widely.

Best months to book. The concrete season in Duluth runs May through October when overnight temps stay above 40°F consistently. Spring is peak demand as homeowners address winter frost damage. Fall pours (September-October) offer better contractor availability and fewer weather delays.

What drives cost. Labor is the dominant cost driver for concrete work in Duluth — local wages run 35% above the national average, which adds 18% or more to a typical driveway or patio pour. At 48 years average home age, Duluth properties are hitting their first major replacement cycle for systems and components. concrete work demand is at peak levels in this age band, which keeps contractor schedules full but pricing competitive.

Red flags to watch for. Watch for concrete work quotes in Duluth that lack line-item detail. A professional estimate breaks out labor, materials, permits, and cleanup separately. Lump-sum bids hide margin and make change orders impossible to evaluate. Check that any Duluth contractor doing concrete work carries both general liability insurance ($1M minimum) and workers' compensation. Request certificates directly from the insurer, not just copies the contractor provides.
