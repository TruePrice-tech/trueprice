El Paso landscaping FAQ

How much does outdoor lighting cost?

Professional landscape lighting costs $2,000-$5,000 for path lights, uplights, and accent lighting. LED fixtures cost more upfront but last 50,000+ hours. Solar options cost less but provide dimmer, less reliable light.

How much does a retaining wall cost?

Retaining walls cost $20-$50 per square face foot for concrete block, $25-$60 for natural stone, and $15-$30 for timber. A 50 linear foot wall at 3 feet tall runs $3,000-$9,000 installed.

What is xeriscaping?

Xeriscaping uses drought-tolerant plants, efficient irrigation, and permeable surfaces to minimize water use. It cuts outdoor water consumption 50-75% and is increasingly required in water-restricted regions.

Do I need a permit for landscaping in El Paso?

Most landscaping in El Paso does not require permits. Exceptions include retaining walls over 4 feet, grading that changes drainage, connecting to city water for irrigation, and work near property lines.

El Paso Native Plant Palette

El Paso's Chihuahuan Desert flora provides the most distinctly arid-adapted palette among these metros: native desert species including creosote bush, ocotillo, agave, penstemon, desert willow, black dalea, and little bluestem thrive without supplemental irrigation once established. The Franklin Mountains' biodiversity (over 1,000 plant species in the park) provides a rich source palette for native landscape design. Xeriscaping is not a trend in El Paso -- it's the historical default that predates the term. Many older neighborhoods (Sunset Heights, Kern Place, Upper Valley) have been landscaped with desert-adapted plants for decades because the 8-inch annual precipitation eliminates high-water alternatives without supplemental irrigation. The Rio Grande bosque corridor's cottonwoods and willows provide a riparian palette for valley-floor properties.

El Paso Soil Conditions and Amendments

El Paso's desert soils: caliche hardpan at 12-36 inches requires breaking through for tree planting holes and raised-bed construction. The native caliche-influenced soil is alkaline (pH 7.5-8.5) and nutrient-poor, requiring organic amendment for non-native plants. Desert-native plants thrive in unamended caliche soil because they evolved in it. The very hard water (250-350 ppm from El Paso Water Utilities) deposits minerals in soil and on plant leaves, creating a white-salt crust on the soil surface during summer evaporation. Compost amendment improves water-holding capacity in the sandy desert soil for plants that need more moisture than the desert baseline. Tree planting holes through caliche must include drainage gravel below the rootball because the impermeable caliche creates a "bathtub" that drowns roots without drainage.

El Paso Irrigation Rules and Rebates

El Paso Water Utilities enforces year-round water conservation: watering restricted to before 9am or after 7pm, maximum 3 days per week even during non-drought conditions. Smart controllers with rain sensors and soil-moisture sensors are required on new installations. Drip irrigation is the standard for all shrub and tree beds because the desert environment evaporates 70-80% of overhead spray before it reaches the soil surface. EPWU offers rebates for irrigation-efficiency upgrades. The very hard water clogs drip emitters within 6-12 months; acid-flushing ($50-$100/system annually) maintains flow. Rainwater harvesting ($800-$3,000 for residential systems) supplements municipal water with the monsoon-season precipitation.

EPWU's water-conservation programs offer turf-removal rebates ($1-$2/sqft) and xeriscape-design assistance. The City's WaterSmart program provides free landscape consultations for water-efficiency conversions. The economic argument is straightforward: a 1,000 sqft bermuda lawn costs $400-$600/year in water at EPWU rates, while a xeriscape conversion eliminates this cost after the initial investment ($3-$5/sqft installed). The combined rebate and water-savings payback makes turf conversion one of the highest-ROI landscape investments in El Paso, typically paying back in 2-3 years.

El Paso Lawn and Turf Choices

El Paso lawns: bermuda (Tifway 419, Santa Ana) is the only viable warm-season lawn grass in the Chihuahuan Desert, requiring 1-1.5 inches of water per week from May through September. Lawn area is increasingly limited to functional spaces (play areas, pet runs) rather than decorative display because EPWU water costs and restrictions make turf maintenance expensive relative to xeriscape alternatives. Artificial turf ($8-$14/sqft) has growing share for front-yard aesthetic where HOAs require "green" appearance. Buffalo grass provides a minimal-water native alternative ($1-$2/sqft seeded) but goes dormant and brown during winter, which some homeowners find unacceptable. Turf-free front yards are increasingly the norm in El Paso.

El Paso Invasive Pests and Plants

El Paso invasive species: salt cedar (tamarisk) along the Rio Grande and arroyo corridors is the most ecologically destructive invasive. Buffelgrass threatens native desert vegetation in the Franklin Mountains. Fire ants have established in the Lower Valley and irrigated landscapes. Bark beetles attack stressed desert willow and mesquite during drought years. The desert-urban interface brings javelina, coyotes, and rattlesnakes into landscaped areas -- landscape design should avoid creating hiding cover (dense groundcover, rock piles) adjacent to entry doors and play areas. The very hard water's mineral deposits on plant leaves can be mistaken for pest damage.

El Paso HOA and Design Review

El Paso HOA prevalence: low in established neighborhoods, moderate in Far Westside master-planned communities. Where HOAs exist, they increasingly accept and encourage xeriscape over turf because EPWU water restrictions make turf maintenance problematic. Sunset Heights Historic District may review visible landscape changes on contributing properties. The broader El Paso culture strongly supports desert-adapted landscaping: unlike markets where xeriscape is a "statement," in El Paso it's the norm, and lush-green-lawn-only HOA requirements are increasingly seen as environmentally inappropriate for the Chihuahuan Desert.

El Paso Tree Services and Ordinances

El Paso tree service: desert trees (mesquite, palo verde, desert willow) require different pruning approaches than humid-climate trees. "Topping" (an aggressive pruning practice) kills desert trees that cannot regenerate canopy from topped branches. ISA-certified arborists with desert-tree experience ($150-$300 consultation) provide appropriate pruning guidance. Mesquite ($300-$800 for pruning, $500-$2,000 for removal) is the dominant landscape tree in El Paso. Rio Grande cottonwoods along the bosque corridor are protected by the TX Parks & Wildlife riparian-conservation program; removal requires agency coordination.

El Paso Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

El Paso seasonal calendar: January-February minimal maintenance (most plants dormant). March spring planting window as temperatures moderate. April-May pre-irrigation check: flush drip system of mineral deposits, verify controller programming, check for freeze-damaged components from winter. June-September monsoon and maximum heat: irrigate early morning, manage weed growth from monsoon moisture, clean mineral deposits from leaves. October-November fall planting window (preferred for desert trees because fall-planted roots establish during mild winter). December winterize irrigation (drain exposed pipes and PVB backflow preventer). Professional maintenance: $100-$250/month for xeriscape; $200-$400/month for properties with turf.

El Paso Stormwater Rules

El Paso stormwater: the arroyo system channels Franklin Mountains runoff through developed neighborhoods during monsoon thunderstorms. Landscape grading must route monsoon-season runoff away from foundations and prevent the pooling that saturates bearing soils. Rain gardens and detention basins capture monsoon water for passive irrigation rather than allowing it to flow to the arroyo system. The 8-inch annual precipitation (concentrated in July-August) means stormwater management is a monsoon-specific challenge rather than a year-round concern. Permeable hardscape and water-harvesting landscape design reduce runoff and capture the precious monsoon moisture for plant use.
