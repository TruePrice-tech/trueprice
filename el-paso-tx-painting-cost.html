Frequently asked painting questions

What paint sheen should I use?

Flat/matte hides imperfections (ceilings, low-traffic walls). Eggshell and satin work for most walls. Semi-gloss is ideal for trim, doors, and kitchens/baths. High-gloss is for accent trim and furniture.

How much does paint prep work cost?

Prep is 50-70% of the total job cost. Scraping, sanding, caulking, priming, and repairs can cost $1,500-$4,000 on an exterior. Skipping prep leads to peeling paint within 1-2 years.

Do I need to prime before painting?

Prime over bare wood, drywall patches, stains, dark colors being covered with light ones, and previously unpainted surfaces. Paint-and-primer combos work for simple color changes on already-painted walls.

Climate and paint durability within El Paso

El Paso's Chihuahuan Desert delivers the most intense UV exposure of any major metro in this group: 300+ clear days at 3,800-foot elevation produce solar radiation that strips paint color and degrades film integrity 35-40% faster than sea-level humid markets. South and west exposures on stucco homes in the Upper Valley and Westside fade visibly within 18-24 months -- a repaint cycle that coastal markets would not encounter for 5-7 years. The 90-degree diurnal temperature swing (100F afternoon to 40F predawn during shoulder seasons) produces daily expansion-contraction cycling that eventually fatigues the paint film at joints, corners, and changes in substrate material. Windblown sand from the Franklin Mountains and Hueco Bolson abrades painted surfaces year-round, accelerating the UV-driven degradation.

El Paso's desert climate produces virtually zero mildew risk on exterior paint surfaces -- the polar opposite of humid markets like Baltimore and Orlando. The single-digit humidity during May-June makes mildewcide additives unnecessary. The moisture concern in El Paso is at the adobe-to-stucco interface on Sunset Heights and Kern Place homes: the thick adobe walls absorb and release moisture through the seasons (monsoon wet to winter dry), and any paint film that traps this moisture against the adobe produces spalling and delamination. This is why mineral silicate and lime-based coatings (vapor-permeable) are mandatory on adobe substrates -- standard acrylic latex is a moisture trap on these walls.

El Paso's paint product specifications

For El Paso's extreme UV desert environment: elastomeric acrylic (Sherwin-Williams Loxon XP, PPG PermaCrete) on stucco provides the best combination of UV resistance, crack-bridging, and moisture management. Standard acrylic latex fades and chalks within 2-3 years on south and west exposures. Adobe-plaster substrates in Sunset Heights and Kern Place require mineral silicate or lime-based paint (Keim Mineral Paints, Romabio) rather than standard acrylic because the lime plaster needs a vapor-permeable coating to release the moisture that migrates through the thick adobe walls.

El Paso's paint aesthetic draws from the Chihuahuan Desert palette: earth tones (ochre, terracotta, warm sand, cinnamon, sage) dominate the traditional market and complement both the desert landscape and the Territorial/Pueblo Revival architecture. The "Desert Modernism" contemporary aesthetic in the Upper Valley and new Westside developments uses cooler tones (concrete gray, warm white, charcoal accent) that reflect the influence of Mexican contemporary architecture from across the border in Chihuahua. The painted-stucco tradition means El Paso homeowners are more comfortable with full-body stucco repaints (the standard maintenance item) than markets where painting is primarily a trim-and-accent activity on brick or vinyl-sided homes.

Prep culture and methods throughout El Paso

El Paso's prep requirements: Sunset Heights and Kern Place pre-1940 adobe homes require careful assessment of the lime plaster before any painting. Loose or delaminating plaster must be repaired with lime-based patching (not Portland cement) before painting. Pre-1978 lead testing adds $200-$500. 1960s-80s stucco needs power washing at 2,000-2,500 PSI and crack repair with elastomeric caulk or patch before topcoat. Windblown sand accumulation in textures and joints must be cleaned out to ensure paint adhesion -- a step that contractors from non-desert markets often skip. The very hard water (250-350 ppm) leaves mineral deposits on surfaces washed with city water; using purified water for the final rinse prevents white spotting under the paint film.
