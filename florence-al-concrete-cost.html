Frequently asked concrete questions

What is the best time to pour concrete?

Pour when temperatures stay between 50-80F for at least 3 days. Spring and fall are ideal. Avoid pouring in rain, direct hot sun, or below freezing. Cold and heat both compromise cure quality.

How thick should a concrete driveway be?

Residential driveways should be 4 inches thick for cars and 5-6 inches for heavy vehicles like RVs. A 6-inch compacted gravel base underneath is essential. Thickened edges (6 inches) prevent cracking at the perimeter.

Is concrete or asphalt better for a driveway?

Concrete lasts 30+ years vs. 15-20 for asphalt. Concrete costs more upfront ($6-$12/sqft vs. $3-$6/sqft) but needs less maintenance. Asphalt needs resealing every 3-5 years and is softer in extreme heat.

Do I need a permit for concrete work in Florence?

In Florence, permits are typically required for driveways, sidewalks, and any concrete work near property lines or public right-of-way. Backyard patios may be exempt depending on size. Check local requirements.

concrete work pricing explained: Florence, AL

Why pricing varies in Florence. Labor rates here run roughly 11% below the national average per BLS metro data, which is the main reason this city's pricing sits below the national midpoint. For concrete work specifically, our cost-multiplier model puts Florence at roughly 19% below the national midpoint.

Local conditions. Florence has local climate conditions that affect project scheduling and material choices. Concrete in Florence cures differently than in dry climates — high humidity slows evaporation, which actually improves curing strength but extends the timeline. The bigger concern is expansive clay soils common in this region, which cause heave and cracking. Proper sub-base preparation (4-6 inches of compacted gravel) is non-negotiable. Standard concrete in Florence runs $6-10 per square foot for basic flatwork (driveways, walkways). Decorative options (stamped, colored, exposed aggregate) add $4-8 per square foot. The biggest hidden cost is demolition and removal of existing concrete — budget $2-4 per square foot for tearout of old slabs.

Permits, codes, and licensing. Most concrete work projects in Florence require a permit; confirm with the local building department before signing.

Contractor market. At 40,059 residents, the local pool is moderate; expect 4-6 active contractors for most standard residential work. Florence's stable population means contractor capacity rarely tightens, so off-season pricing (typically Nov-Feb) can save 10-15% on non-emergency work.

Florence, AL-specific concrete work considerations

Home stock in Florence. Florence's housing stock averages 34 years old — relatively recent construction that was largely built to modern code. concrete work projects here tend to be simpler: fewer surprise compliance issues, quicker permit turnaround, and contractors who can quote from memory.

HOA & compliance. HOA involvement is uncommon in Florence, so most concrete work projects avoid architectural review delays. City permitting alone governs the process.

Local market conditions. Florence has a stable, lower-growth construction market, which usually means more competitive contractor pricing than fast-growing metros. The tradeoff: smaller contractor pool, so reputation and reviews matter more — a few bad actors can dominate the local landscape. In Florence (40,059 residents), concrete work is often done by small local companies or general contractors. For decorative work (stamped, colored), verify the contractor has a portfolio of completed projects in your specific pattern and color — decorative concrete expertise varies widely.

Seasonal timing. Florence allows year-round concrete work, but the ideal window is spring and fall when temperatures support proper curing without the heat-related challenges of summer. Avoid scheduling large pours during the hottest months — early morning pours are the only reliable option.

What drives cost. Lower labor costs are Florence's advantage for concrete work — local wages run 11% below the national average. This puts Florence in the bottom third nationally for concrete work labor costs. Florence's relatively young housing stock (34 years average) simplifies most concrete work projects. Modern code compliance, standard dimensions, and accessible construction reduce both time and cost versus older homes.

Red flags to watch for. Check that any Florence contractor doing concrete work carries both general liability insurance ($1M minimum) and workers' compensation. Request certificates directly from the insurer, not just copies the contractor provides. In Florence, verify your concrete work contractor pulls the permit themselves — never pull it in your own name. If they ask you to pull the permit, they may not be properly licensed to do the work.
