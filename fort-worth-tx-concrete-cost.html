What Fort Worth residents ask about concrete

Do I need a permit for concrete work in Fort Worth?

In Fort Worth, permits are typically required for driveways, sidewalks, and any concrete work near property lines or public right-of-way. Backyard patios may be exempt depending on size. Check local requirements.

What is stamped concrete?

Stamped concrete uses patterned mats pressed into wet concrete to mimic brick, stone, slate, or wood. It costs $12-$25/sqft installed. It requires resealing every 2-3 years to maintain appearance and prevent wear.

What causes concrete to crack?

Common causes: shrinkage during curing, frost heave from inadequate base prep, tree roots, heavy vehicle loads beyond design, poor control joint placement, and water pooling due to improper grading.

Can cracked concrete be repaired?

Hairline cracks can be sealed with flexible caulk or epoxy for $1-$5 per linear foot. Wider cracks may need patching compound. If the slab has heaved or settled significantly, mudjacking ($5-$10/sqft) or replacement is needed.

Understanding subgrade and mix design in Fort Worth

Fort Worth's Blackland Prairie expansive montmorillonite clay is the most challenging concrete sub-base environment among these 10 metros. The clay's plasticity index (PI 35-55) means the soil swells dramatically when wet and shrinks when dry, creating heave-and-settlement cycles that crack concrete flatwork within 3-5 years if the sub-base is not properly engineered. The standard Fort Worth protocol: remove 6-8 inches of native clay, replace with compacted gravel or select fill, install geotextile fabric between the clay and the gravel to prevent clay migration, and pour on the engineered pad. Skipping this sub-base preparation on Blackland Prairie clay is the single most common reason for premature concrete failure in the DFW market. Ridglea, Wedgwood, and Benbrook sit on the deepest clay with the most aggressive movement potential.

Fort Worth concrete mix: 3,500-4,500 PSI minimum for driveways and exterior flatwork exposed to the triple-threat environment (hail impact, freeze-thaw from occasional deep freezes like Uri, and heat-driven expansion). Air-entrained mix (5-6% air content) is recommended for all exterior flatwork because Fort Worth's rare but proven freeze events (February 2021 at -2F for 4 days) produce freeze-thaw spalling on non-air-entrained surfaces. Hot-weather mix with retarder is standard from June through September when ambient temperatures exceed 95F. Fiber-mesh reinforcement is the baseline; #4 rebar on 18-inch centers for driveways on deep-clay properties.

Reinforcement in Fort Worth: the Blackland Prairie clay demands more aggressive reinforcement than stable-soil markets. #4 rebar on 18-inch centers with fiber mesh is the local standard for driveways on deep-clay properties in Ridglea, Wedgwood, and Benbrook. Fiber-mesh-only reinforcement is adequate on engineered-fill properties in Alliance and Walsh where the clay has been removed. Expansion joints every 8-10 feet (tighter than the national 10-12 foot standard) accommodate the temperature extremes: 100F+ summer expansion to rare sub-zero winter contraction represents a 100+ degree temperature range that standard joint spacing cannot handle without supplemental sawcuts.

Climate Impact on Fort Worth Concrete

Fort Worth's climate creates the most diverse concrete-degradation pattern among these metros: UV-driven surface chalking on south and west exposures during 100F+ summers, freeze-thaw spalling during the rare but proven deep freezes (Uri at -2F demonstrated that "rare" means "has happened"), hail-impact surface cratering from the Palmer Divide-adjacent storm corridor, and road-salt corrosion on driveway surfaces where de-icing chemicals are applied during freeze events. This quadruple-threat means Fort Worth concrete must be specified for the worst case on every axis: air-entrained mix for freeze resistance, adequate reinforcement for clay heave, sealer for salt protection, and quality finishing for UV resistance.

Fort Worth's concrete-damage events: the February 2021 Winter Storm Uri (4 days at -2F) produced widespread concrete damage from frost heave on shallow sub-bases and freeze-thaw spalling on non-air-entrained surfaces. Road-salt application during Uri's extended freeze period created chemical corrosion on driveway surfaces that persisted through the spring thaw. The annual hail events produce cosmetic surface damage (pitting, cratering) that is primarily aesthetic rather than structural. The drought-to-rain moisture cycling on Blackland Prairie clay produces the most persistent concrete damage: soil movement beneath flatwork creates cracks and settlement that accumulate over multiple wet-dry cycles.
