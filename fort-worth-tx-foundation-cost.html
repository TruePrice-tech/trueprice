Fort Worth foundation repair FAQ

What is mudjacking?

Mudjacking pumps a cement slurry under a settled slab to raise it back to level. It costs $500-$1,500 per section and works for driveways, sidewalks, and garage floors. Polyurethane foam injection (polyjacking) is a lighter, longer-lasting alternative.

How long does foundation repair last?

Steel push piers and helical piers are permanent solutions that last the life of the structure. Carbon fiber reinforcement lasts 25+ years. Epoxy crack injection lasts 5-10 years if the underlying cause is addressed.

Does homeowners insurance cover foundation repair?

Standard policies do not cover foundation repair from settling, soil movement, or normal wear. Coverage may apply if damage results from a covered event like a plumbing leak or sudden ground collapse. Review your policy carefully.

What are the signs of foundation problems?

Warning signs include diagonal cracks above door frames, sticking doors and windows, uneven or sloping floors, gaps between walls and ceiling, and cracks wider than 1/4 inch in the foundation wall.

How Fort Worth Foundations Fail

Fort Worth's dominant foundation failure mode is differential settlement driven by uneven moisture distribution in the expansive Blackland Prairie clay. The classic pattern: one side of the house stays moist (from irrigation, poor drainage, or shade) while the opposite side dries out during Texas's summer heat, causing 1-3 inches of differential movement that produces diagonal drywall cracks from window and door corners, sticking doors and windows, visible floor slope, and in severe cases, structural framing distortion. The movement is seasonal and often reversible in its early stages: fall rains re-saturate the dried side and partially restore level, which is why some homeowners delay repair until the movement exceeds the seasonal recovery range. Post-tension slab foundations (standard on post-2000 construction) resist clay movement better than conventional slabs but can fail catastrophically if a tendon corrodes through.

Fort Worth foundation issues by neighborhood: Ridglea and Westover Hills (deepest Blackland clay, 1940s-60s construction without post-tension slabs) generate the highest repair volume and the most severe cases. Wedgwood and Benbrook (1970s-80s construction on moderate clay) face standard North Texas clay-movement patterns. Arlington Heights and Fairmount-Southside (1890s-1920s pier-and-beam) experience different failure modes: wood-post deterioration and foundation-beam settlement rather than slab-clay interaction. Alliance and Walsh (post-2000 post-tension slabs on engineered fill) have lower incidence but are not immune to perimeter clay effects where native soil interfaces with the engineered building pad.

Drainage and Moisture Management in Fort Worth

Drainage management is the #1 preventive measure for foundation health in Fort Worth. The expansive Blackland Prairie clay responds directly to moisture: wet clay swells, dry clay shrinks, and the differential between wet and dry sides of the house drives the settlement that damages foundations. Proper drainage: 6 inches of fall in the first 10 feet from the foundation, functional gutters with 6-foot downspout extensions, and foundation watering with a soaker hose on a timer during drought periods (June-October). The soaker-hose maintenance protocol is a Fort Worth-specific recommendation that most non-Texas contractors would never suggest: keeping the soil consistently moist during drought prevents the shrinkage that causes the most aggressive settlement.

Fort Worth foundation moisture management is the most critical homeowner-accessible preventive measure: maintain consistent soil moisture around the foundation perimeter year-round. During drought (June-October), run a soaker hose on a timer 2-3 times per week to prevent the clay shrinkage that drives settlement. During wet periods, ensure drainage routes water away from the foundation to prevent over-saturation. The ideal is a consistently moist (not saturated) soil condition within 3 feet of the foundation perimeter. Mature trees within 15 feet of the foundation desiccate the soil through root uptake; root barriers ($1,000-$3,000) interrupt this pathway without removing the tree. The post-Uri lesson: even homes with successful moisture management can experience foundation distress from unprecedented events outside the normal moisture-cycling range.

When to Schedule Fort Worth Foundation Work

Foundation repair in Fort Worth: dry-season work (June-October) provides stable soil conditions for piering because the clay has contracted and pier resistance measurements are conservative. Wet-season work (November-May) risks piering into swollen clay that overestimates bearing capacity. The February 2021 Winter Storm Uri produced unusual foundation effects: the 4-day freeze followed by rapid thaw caused frost heave on shallow pier installations and saturated soils that produced delayed settlement 3-6 months after the event. Emergency stabilization can happen in any season but piering accuracy is best during the dry period.

The Blackland Prairie clay's responsiveness to moisture extremes means that both extreme drought and extreme saturation produce foundation damage -- there is no "safe" extreme.
