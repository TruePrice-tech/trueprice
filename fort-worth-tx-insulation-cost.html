What Fort Worth residents ask about insulation

Do I need a permit for insulation in Fort Worth?

Most Fort Worth jurisdictions do not require permits for adding insulation to existing spaces. Permits may be needed if the work involves removing drywall or altering vapor barriers. Check local codes.

What is a vapor barrier and do I need one?

A vapor barrier prevents moisture from passing through walls and ceilings. In cold climates, install on the warm (interior) side. In hot-humid climates, install on the exterior side. Closed-cell spray foam acts as its own vapor barrier.

How long does insulation last?

Fiberglass batts last 80-100 years if they stay dry and undisturbed. Cellulose lasts 20-30 years before settling reduces effectiveness. Spray foam lasts the life of the structure. All insulation loses value if it gets wet.

How much does spray foam insulation cost?

Open-cell spray foam costs $1-$2 per board foot installed. Closed-cell costs $2-$4 per board foot. A 1,000 sqft attic with 6 inches of closed-cell runs $6,000-$12,000. It is the most expensive but most effective option.

Fort Worth-area attic insulation strategy

Fort Worth attic insulation: the 1940s-60s brick ranch stock across Ridglea, Arlington Heights, and Westover Hills was built with R-11 to R-19 fiberglass batts. The extreme summer heat (20+ days above 100F) pushes attic temperatures to 140-160F, making attic insulation the highest-ROI energy improvement after HVAC replacement. Blown-in fiberglass or cellulose over existing batts to R-38+ runs $1-$2/sqft. Radiant barriers ($0.50-$1.25/sqft) reflect the radiant heat from the dark roof deck before it enters the attic airspace, reducing attic peak temperature by 20-30F. The February 2021 Winter Storm Uri demonstrated that attic insulation also prevents the catastrophic heat loss that made some Fort Worth homes uninhabitable during the 4-day grid blackout: homes with R-38+ attics maintained habitable temperatures 6-10 hours longer than R-11 homes after power loss.

Understanding wall cavity retrofit approach in Fort Worth

Fort Worth wall insulation: the 1940s-60s brick ranch stock has a distinctive wall assembly -- brick veneer over wood frame with an air gap between the brick and the sheathing. This air gap complicates traditional dense-pack injection because the insulation can fill the air gap rather than the stud cavity if not properly controlled. Experienced Fort Worth insulation contractors use tubed injection from the interior rather than drilling through the brick, which preserves the brick veneer integrity and ensures the insulation reaches the stud cavity. Standard dense-pack cellulose to R-13 runs $1.50-$2.50/sqft. Post-Uri, wall insulation demand increased 200% as homeowners sought thermal-resilience improvements beyond HVAC upgrades.

Air Sealing Priorities specific to Fort Worth

Fort Worth air sealing: the Blackland Prairie clay's seasonal foundation movement creates unique air-sealing challenges because the building shifts slightly with the clay, opening and closing gaps at the sill plate, wall-to-foundation interface, and around penetrations. Standard air-sealing materials (caulk, spray foam) may crack or separate during clay-movement cycles. Flexible sealants (polyurethane, silicone) that accommodate movement outperform rigid spray foam at these dynamic interfaces. Can-light fixtures, plumbing penetrations, and attic-hatch seals are standard leak points. The post-Uri awareness of building-envelope performance has made air sealing a mainstream Fort Worth home-improvement category rather than a building-science specialty.

Common Fort Worth Insulation Upsells and What They Actually Buy

Fort Worth insulation upsells worth accepting: air sealing before blown-in ($300-$600, essential), radiant barrier in the attic ($0.50-$1.25/sqft, significant ROI in the 140-160F attic environment), duct sealing ($400-$800 for mastic application at all joints, prevents the 20-30% duct-leakage energy loss common on 1940s-60s metal trunk lines), and spray-foam on rim joists ($400-$800, seals the largest single air-leak pathway at the basement/crawlspace level). Upsells to decline: spray-foaming an entire attic (overkill in the DFW climate where blown-in cellulose + radiant barrier achieves equivalent performance at 40% of the cost), and "premium" blown-in products at 2-3x the price of standard cellulose without documented R-value improvement.

Historic homes and preservation rules within Fort Worth

Fort Worth historic homes: Fairmount-Southside's 1890s-1920s Queen Anne and Victorian homes present similar challenges to Baltimore's rowhouse stock -- original plaster-and-lath walls with no cavity insulation, balloon-frame construction with continuous wall cavities from basement to attic, and limited options for adding insulation without affecting the interior finish. Balloon-frame firestopping (blocking the continuous cavity with mineral wool at each floor level) is both a code-compliance and energy-efficiency improvement. Dense-pack cellulose injection ($2-$3.50/sqft) is the preferred method. The Fairmount-Southside Landmarks Commission may review exterior modifications, so any approach that changes the exterior profile (continuous insulation, cladding) requires Commission coordination.

Moisture control and vapor strategy within Fort Worth

Fort Worth moisture management: the humid-summer climate (dew points in the low 70s) creates condensation risk in air-conditioned buildings where cool, dry interior air meets warm, humid attic or wall-cavity air. Proper vapor-barrier placement: in Fort Worth's cooling-dominant climate, the vapor barrier belongs on the exterior (warm-humid) side of the wall assembly, not the interior. This is opposite from cold-climate practice, and contractors from cold-climate backgrounds sometimes place it incorrectly. Spray-foam crawlspace insulation must include vapor barriers and dehumidification to prevent the moisture damage that the Blackland Prairie's high water table promotes.
