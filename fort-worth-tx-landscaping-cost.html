Questions Fort Worth homeowners ask about landscaping

How much does a retaining wall cost?

Retaining walls cost $20-$50 per square face foot for concrete block, $25-$60 for natural stone, and $15-$30 for timber. A 50 linear foot wall at 3 feet tall runs $3,000-$9,000 installed.

What is xeriscaping?

Xeriscaping uses drought-tolerant plants, efficient irrigation, and permeable surfaces to minimize water use. It cuts outdoor water consumption 50-75% and is increasingly required in water-restricted regions.

Do I need a permit for landscaping in Fort Worth?

Most landscaping in Fort Worth does not require permits. Exceptions include retaining walls over 4 feet, grading that changes drainage, connecting to city water for irrigation, and work near property lines.

How often should I replace mulch?

Replenish organic mulch annually (top off to 3 inches). Full replacement every 2-3 years prevents matting and fungal buildup. Rock and rubber mulch last 5-10 years but do not improve soil.

What is the cheapest way to landscape a yard?

Seed instead of sod, use native plants from local nurseries, mulch with free municipal compost, and phase the project over 2-3 seasons. A basic DIY front yard makeover can be done for $500-$1,500.

Fort Worth: native plant palette

Fort Worth's blackland-prairie-to-cross-timbers ecological transition supports a diverse native palette: Texas sage (cenizo), flame acanthus, salvia greggii, turk's cap, inland sea oats, buffalo grass, and prairie blazing star thrive with minimal supplemental irrigation once established. The post-oak and blackjack-oak cross timbers ecosystem in western Fort Worth and Benbrook provides shade canopy that constrains planting plans. The February 2021 Winter Storm Uri killed or severely damaged thousands of non-cold-hardy landscape plants across the metro, permanently shifting the Fort Worth market toward proven cold-hardy natives that survive -2F temperatures without protection. Ridglea and Arlington Heights' mid-century builder landscapes are the primary renovation market.

Soil conditions and amendments throughout Fort Worth

Fort Worth's Blackland Prairie clay is the most challenging landscape soil among these 10 metros: the expansive montmorillonite clay swells when wet and shrinks when dry, creating the same heave-and-settlement cycles that damage foundations. Landscape beds require 6-8 inches of raised planting mix above the native clay to provide adequate drainage for root zones. Tree planting requires "planting proud" (rootball 2-3 inches above grade) because the clay swells around the trunk and creates a drainage basin that suffocates roots. Gypsum amendment ($0.50-$1.00/sqft) improves clay structure but does not eliminate the expansion characteristic. The moderate water hardness (150-200 ppm from Fort Worth Water) leaves mineral deposits in drip-irrigation emitters.

A Fort Worth look at irrigation rules and rebates

Fort Worth Water enforces stage-based irrigation restrictions during drought: Stage 1 limits watering to 2 days per week; Stage 2 to 1 day. Smart controllers with rain sensors are required on new installations. Drip irrigation for beds and spray for turf is the standard dual-system design. The post-Uri awareness of freeze-protection has added winter-drain-down capability to irrigation design requirements: exposed PVB (pressure vacuum breaker) backflow preventers froze and shattered on thousands of Fort Worth properties during Uri's 4-day deep freeze, adding $200-$500 per irrigation system in repairs. Freeze-resistant RPZ (reduced pressure zone) backflow preventers are increasingly specified despite the higher cost ($300-$500 versus $100-$200 for PVB).

Fort Worth Water offers modest turf-conversion incentives during drought stages. The City's WaterWise program provides landscape-design assistance for drought-tolerant conversions. Texas does not have a state-level turf-conversion rebate program comparable to California's. The economic argument for water-efficient landscaping is driven by tiered water rates that penalize high-volume use rather than by direct rebates. The post-Uri market has driven organic demand for lower-maintenance, cold-hardy landscapes that the pre-Uri market rarely considered.

Fort Worth-area lawn and turf choices

Fort Worth lawns: bermuda (Tifway 419, Celebration) dominates the full-sun market. Zoysia (Emerald, Palisades) serves the shade-tolerant and premium markets. St. Augustine (Raleigh, Palmetto) provides lush growth in partial shade but the February 2021 Uri freeze killed 60-70% of St. Augustine across the metro because the tropical species cannot survive sustained sub-20F temperatures. Post-Uri, zoysia has gained significant share as the cold-hardy alternative that provides similar appearance to St. Augustine. Buffalo grass serves the drought-tolerant native-lawn market at lower water requirements. Sod installation runs $1-$2/sqft; seed at $0.10-$0.20/sqft.

Invasive pests and plants within Fort Worth

Fort Worth invasive species: Chinese privet, Chinaberry, and johnsongrass are the most aggressive landscape invaders. Fire ants are the dominant landscape pest across Tarrant County; fire-ant management ($50-$100/treatment for broadcast application) is a recurring expense on all turf properties. Webworms, bagworms, and emerald ash borer threaten the tree canopy. Post-Uri freeze damage created entry points for bark beetles and borers on stress-weakened trees. Oak wilt fungus (Ceratocystis fagacearum) threatens the cross-timbers live-oak population; avoid pruning oaks from February through June when the fungal vectors are active.

Fort Worth HOA and Design Review

Fort Worth HOA landscape restrictions: high in Alliance, Walsh, Southlake, Keller, and Colleyville master-planned communities. Common requirements: maintained bermuda or zoysia front lawn (no bare soil, no weeds above 6 inches), foundation shrubs at minimum 3-foot height, seasonal color bed at front entry, tree canopy at minimum one shade tree per 3,000 sqft of lot. Fairmount-Southside Historic District may review landscape changes on contributing properties. HOA enforcement intensity varies: Southlake and Colleyville are among the strictest HOA-landscape-enforcement markets in the DFW Metroplex.

Tree services and ordinances around Fort Worth

Fort Worth tree service: the post-Uri freeze killed or severely damaged an estimated 50,000-100,000 residential trees across Tarrant County, creating a sustained tree-removal and replacement demand that lasted through 2024. Tree pruning runs $250-$1,200 per tree. Tree removal runs $500-$3,000 per tree. ISA-certified arborists ($200-$400 consultation) should assess post-Uri stress symptoms before removal is committed. The cross-timbers oak canopy in western Fort Worth is the neighborhood's primary shade asset; preserving post-oaks and live oaks should be the default landscape approach.

Understanding seasonal maintenance rhythm in Fort Worth

Fort Worth seasonal calendar: January-February dormant-season pruning and pre-emergent herbicide. March-April spring planting and fertilization after last frost (typically March 15). May warm-season-grass green-up and first mowing cycle. June-September weekly mowing, bi-weekly irrigation (if not in drought stage), fire-ant treatment. October-November fall planting (the secondary window), overseeding perennial ryegrass for winter color on bermuda lawns. December leaf cleanup from pecan, oak, and sweetgum canopy. Professional maintenance: $150-$350/month for full-service including mowing, bed maintenance, and irrigation adjustment.

Stormwater rules across Fort Worth

Fort Worth stormwater: the Blackland Prairie clay's impermeability concentrates runoff rather than allowing infiltration, which makes landscape grading and drainage critical. The Trinity River floodplain intersects developed neighborhoods where heavy-rain events produce yard flooding. French drains ($15-$25/lf) route water away from foundations and prevent the pooling that drives clay expansion. Rain gardens and bioswales are gaining adoption as stormwater-management tools in new landscape designs, though Fort Worth does not offer the stormwater-fee credits that some California and Pacific Northwest cities provide.
