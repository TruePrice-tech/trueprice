What Fresno residents ask about insulation

Is blown-in insulation better than batts?

Blown-in fills gaps and voids that batts leave around wiring, pipes, and framing. It performs 15-25% better than batts in real-world conditions. Batts are easier to install in open walls during new construction.

Do I need a permit for insulation in Fresno?

Most Fresno jurisdictions do not require permits for adding insulation to existing spaces. Permits may be needed if the work involves removing drywall or altering vapor barriers. Check local codes.

What is a vapor barrier and do I need one?

A vapor barrier prevents moisture from passing through walls and ceilings. In cold climates, install on the warm (interior) side. In hot-humid climates, install on the exterior side. Closed-cell spray foam acts as its own vapor barrier.

The payback at PG&E's $0.26/kWh with TOU peak pricing is among the fastest in any California market: 2-3 years for the combined blown-in + radiant-barrier package.

Wall cavity retrofit approach for Fresno homeowners

South-facing walls have the highest temperature differential during summer. Air Sealing Priorities for Fresno homeowners

Fresno air sealing: the slab-on-grade construction eliminates the basement/crawlspace air-leakage pathway that complicates older homes in other markets. The primary leak points are at the ceiling level: can-light fixtures (replace with IC-rated LED), plumbing penetrations through the attic floor, HVAC duct boot connections, and attic-hatch seals. Air sealing at the ceiling level ($300-$600 for a typical 1,500 sqft home) captures 60-70% of the total infiltration benefit because the extreme attic-temperature differential (170F attic vs 75F conditioned space) drives the strongest stack-effect air movement of any location in the building envelope.

Common Fresno Insulation Upsells and What They Actually Buy

Fresno insulation upsells worth accepting: radiant barrier in the attic ($0.50-$1.25/sqft, the single highest-ROI improvement in the extreme Valley heat), air sealing before blown-in ($300-$600), duct insulation upgrade to R-8 in the 160F+ attic ($800-$1,500, required by Title 24 during qualifying renovations), and attic-ventilation assessment (proper ridge-and-soffit balance maximizes insulation performance). Fresno energy code snapshot: overview (Zone 3B)

Fresno energy code: California Title 24 Part 6 requires R-38 attic insulation, R-13 wall insulation, and R-8 duct insulation on qualifying renovations. HERS rater verification at final inspection confirms compliance. Title 24 is the most stringent residential energy code in the country and applies during any renovation that triggers the code's scope thresholds. The practical implication: any Fresno renovation that opens walls or accesses the attic triggers the current R-value requirements, making insulation upgrade a mandatory-minimum-spec project rather than a discretionary improvement during qualifying renovation work.

DOE recommended insulation levels for Zone 3B are attic R-38 to R-49 and walls R-13. Code minimums in Fresno are attic R-38 and walls R-13. Exceeding code by one R-tier typically returns within 4 to 7 years of utility savings in this market.

Moisture Control and Vapor Strategy in Fresno

Fresno moisture management: the dry Central Valley summer eliminates most moisture concerns from April through October. The Tule fog season (November-February) is the critical moisture period: persistent damp 40-degree conditions create condensation risk on cold surfaces, including the underside of attic sheathing where temperature differences across the insulation layer can produce dew-point conditions. Proper attic ventilation (balanced ridge and soffit) must be maintained after adding blown-in insulation -- never block soffit vents. The Tule fog condensation risk is a Fresno-specific concern that no other California market faces because the Central Valley's radiation fog pattern is unique to the San Joaquin Valley.
