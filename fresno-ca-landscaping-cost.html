Questions Fresno homeowners ask about landscaping

Does landscaping increase home value?

Well-designed landscaping adds 5-15% to home value. Mature trees alone add $1,000-$10,000 each. Curb appeal landscaping recovers 100-200% of investment at resale, one of the highest ROI improvements.

How much does tree removal cost?

Small trees (under 30 feet) cost $200-$500 to remove. Medium trees (30-60 feet) cost $500-$1,000. Large trees (60-100 feet) cost $1,000-$2,000+. Stump grinding adds $100-$400 per stump.

How much does outdoor lighting cost?

Professional landscape lighting costs $2,000-$5,000 for path lights, uplights, and accent lighting. LED fixtures cost more upfront but last 50,000+ hours. Solar options cost less but provide dimmer, less reliable light.

How much does a retaining wall cost?

Retaining walls cost $20-$50 per square face foot for concrete block, $25-$60 for natural stone, and $15-$30 for timber. A 50 linear foot wall at 3 feet tall runs $3,000-$9,000 installed.

What is xeriscaping?

Xeriscaping uses drought-tolerant plants, efficient irrigation, and permeable surfaces to minimize water use. It cuts outdoor water consumption 50-75% and is increasingly required in water-restricted regions.

Fresno Native Plant Palette

Fresno's San Joaquin Valley location supports a California-Central-Valley native palette: valley oak, blue oak, California buckeye, manzanita, deer grass, California fuchsia, and native bunch grasses thrive when properly established. The extreme heat (30-45 days above 100F) eliminates many native species that succeed in coastal or mountain California, making Fresno's native-plant selection distinct from Bay Area or Southern California palettes. The agricultural heritage means many Fresno residents are comfortable with functional landscapes (fruit trees, vegetable gardens, efficient irrigation) over purely ornamental designs. UC Master Gardener Fresno County provides free consultation on Valley-adapted plant selection.

Fresno Soil Conditions and Amendments

Fresno's Valley-floor alluvium is generally well-drained sandy loam requiring moderate organic amendment for most plantings. The agricultural history means some urban lots were previously orchards or farmland with residual pesticide and fertilizer profiles that can affect garden plantings. Soil testing ($25-$40 through UC Cooperative Extension) is recommended before edible-garden installation. The moderate water hardness (100-150 ppm) is gentle on irrigation systems. The flat terrain and high water table in some central Fresno areas means raised beds ($3-$8/sqft) improve drainage for plants sensitive to waterlogging.

Fresno Irrigation Rules and Rebates

City of Fresno water-conservation ordinances enforce watering-day restrictions during drought declarations. Smart controllers are required on new installations. Drip irrigation is standard for beds. PG&E TOU rate structure means irrigation-pump electricity costs vary by time of day -- programming irrigation to run during off-peak hours saves energy cost. The Valley's extreme heat means irrigation timing matters: watering at midday wastes 30-40% to evaporation, while pre-dawn watering minimizes loss. SJVAPCD (San Joaquin Valley Air Pollution Control District) dust-suppression regulations may affect large-scale landscape installation during Spare the Air days.

City of Fresno offers modest turf-conversion incentives. The economic argument is driven by PG&E water-heating and irrigation-pump electricity costs combined with tiered water rates. CalWEP (California Water Efficiency Partnership) provides statewide resources. The agricultural-economy pricing environment means Fresno landscape conversions cost 15-20% less than Bay Area equivalent scope, making the ROI calculation more favorable at lower cost thresholds.

Fresno Lawn and Turf Choices

Fresno lawns: bermuda (Tifway 419, Celebration) is the standard warm-season lawn. Tall fescue provides year-round green but requires 30-40% more water than bermuda. Buffalo grass offers the lowest-water turf option. The extreme heat means any lawn requires consistent irrigation from May through October -- the 100F+ temperatures kill unwatered turf within 7-10 days. Artificial turf ($8-$14/sqft) has growing share as water costs increase and drought-consciousness deepens. The agricultural-community background means many Fresno homeowners approach their yards productively: fruit trees, vegetable gardens, and food-producing landscapes are culturally common rather than niche interests.

Fresno Invasive Pests and Plants

Fresno invasive species: tree of heaven (Ailanthus altissima) and yellow star-thistle are the most aggressive landscape invaders. Whiteflies, citrus leafminer, and Asian citrus psyllid threaten the Valley's important citrus-tree population (both commercial and residential). Navel orangeworm and codling moth affect fruit and nut trees. Pocket gophers are the #1 landscape pest Valley-wide. The agricultural pest-management culture means Fresno homeowners are generally more knowledgeable about IPM approaches than non-agricultural metros.

Fresno HOA and Design Review

Fresno HOA prevalence: moderate in Woodward Park and Clovis master-planned communities. Common requirements: maintained front lawn (bermuda or fescue), foundation plantings, seasonal color bed. California AB 2104 prohibits HOA fines for brown lawns during drought. The Tower District has no HOA restrictions, allowing maximum design freedom. The agricultural heritage means productive landscapes (fruit trees in front yards, raised vegetable beds) are culturally acceptable in Fresno even in HOA communities, though some HOAs may restrict visible garden infrastructure.

Fresno Tree Services and Ordinances

Fresno tree service: the Valley oak and blue oak canopy in Fig Garden and Van Ness neighborhoods provides essential shade that makes outdoor living tolerable during 100F+ summers. Tree pruning runs $250-$1,000. Tree removal runs $500-$2,500. ISA-certified arborists ($150-$300 consultation) should assess before removal. The extreme heat creates water-stress-related tree health issues during drought years; deep-watering (soaker hose at the drip line, 4-6 hours weekly during summer) prevents the drought-stress decline that leaves trees vulnerable to bark beetles and borers. Fresno's urban-forestry program provides free street-tree planting.

Fresno Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

Fresno seasonal calendar: January-February dormant pruning during Tule fog season. March spring planting after last frost (typically February 28 in the Valley). April-May transition to summer irrigation, pre-emergent application, mulch refresh. June-September extreme heat: early-morning mowing, consistent irrigation, monitor for heat-stress on trees and shrubs. October-November fall planting window (the preferred planting season because fall-planted roots establish during mild winter). December Tule fog onset: reduce activity, address mold on north-facing surfaces. Professional maintenance: $150-$350/month including mowing, bed maintenance, and irrigation.

Fresno Stormwater Rules

Fresno stormwater: the flat Valley terrain requires engineered drainage because there is no natural slope to carry water away. The City's recharge-basin system percolates stormwater into the groundwater aquifer, and landscape design should support this through permeable surfaces and on-site infiltration. The Tule fog season's persistent ground moisture can waterlog poorly drained landscape beds. French drains ($12-$20/lf) manage water routing on flat lots where surface grading alone cannot achieve adequate drainage. Central Valley Regional Water Quality Control Board regulations may affect large-area landscape projects that change impervious-surface coverage.

A complete landscaping project in Fresno typically breaks down as: plant material (30-40%), hardscape/materials (20-30%), labor (25-35%), and design (5-10%).
