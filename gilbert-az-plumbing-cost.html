Frequently asked plumbing questions

How do I know if I have a slab leak?

Signs include unexplained spikes in your water bill, warm spots on the floor, the sound of running water when fixtures are off, and cracks in the foundation or damp baseboards.

What is a backflow preventer and do I need one?

A backflow preventer stops contaminated water from flowing back into the clean supply. Most municipalities require them on irrigation systems and commercial properties. Some require annual testing.

Do I need a plumbing permit in Gilbert?

Most Gilbert jurisdictions require permits for new lines, water heater installs, and sewer work. Minor repairs like faucet swaps and toilet replacements typically do not need permits.

How long do plumbing fixtures typically last?

Faucets last 15-20 years, toilets 25-50 years, water heaters 8-12 years (tank) or 15-20 years (tankless), and supply lines should be replaced every 20-25 years.

Does Gilbert's hard water damage plumbing?

Yes. Hard water in the Gilbert area deposits mineral scale inside pipes, reduces water heater efficiency by 20-30%, and shortens fixture lifespan. A water softener ($1,500-$3,000 installed) or whole-house water conditioner addresses the problem at the source. Descaling your water heater annually adds 3-5 years to its lifespan. If you notice white buildup on faucets or reduced shower pressure, scale is the likely cause.

What should a plumbing quote include in Gilbert?

Every Gilbert plumbing quote should break down: labor, materials with brand names, permit fees where required, disposal, and warranty coverage. Verify the plumber's license number and insurance are documented. For any job over $500, three written quotes with identical scope give you a reliable pricing baseline.

When is the best time to schedule plumbing work in Gilbert?

Plan your Gilbert plumbing projects for fall or spring. Moderate temperatures make excavation work easier and contractor schedules are less congested, and you avoid the emergency premium that adds 50-100% to the bill. Water heaters, supply lines, and fixtures nearing end-of-life are worth replacing on your schedule rather than theirs.
