Common roofing questions in Greenwood

How many layers of shingles can I have?

Most building codes allow a maximum of two layers. If you already have two layers, the old roof must be torn off before the new one is installed, adding $1,000-$3,000 to the cost.

Does a new roof increase home value?

A new roof recovers roughly 60-70% of its cost at resale and eliminates a major buyer objection. Homes with new roofs sell faster and with fewer inspection contingencies.

Do I need a permit for roof replacement in Greenwood?

Yes. Most Greenwood jurisdictions require a building permit for roof replacement. Your contractor should handle the permit and schedule inspection.

What is the difference between architectural and 3-tab shingles?

Architectural shingles are thicker, have a dimensional look, and last 25-30 years. 3-tab shingles are flat, thinner, and last 15-20 years but cost 30-40% less upfront.

How do I prevent ice dams on my Greenwood roof?

The root cause of ice dams is heat leaking into the attic, not the roof material itself. In Greenwood, you need R-49+ attic insulation and continuous ventilation from soffit to ridge to keep the roof deck cold. Any roofing quote here should include ice and water shield on at least the first 3-6 feet from every eave. Replacing the roof without fixing the thermal envelope underneath just delays the same problem.

Are permits required to replace my roof in Greenwood?

Yes, Greenwood generally requires a building permit for full roof replacements. Your roofer should pull this as part of the job. Without a permit, your insurance coverage may be compromised and you face disclosure obligations if you sell the home.

Best time of year to replace a roof in Greenwood?

Greenwood homeowners should target late spring or early fall for roof replacement work. Materials install best above 45F and before snow season pricing surcharges, giving you the best combination of weather conditions and contractor pricing. Off-season scheduling typically saves 10-15% compared to peak demand periods, especially the weeks immediately following severe weather events.
