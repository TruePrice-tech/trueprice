Gutter Installation Cost Guide

By Geoff Lane, founder of Woogoro · Updated April 2026

Gutters protect your home's foundation, siding, and landscaping by directing rainwater away from the structure. Replacing or installing new gutters is a relatively affordable project compared to most exterior work, but costs vary based on material, style, and home size. This 2026 guide covers pricing for every common gutter type, explains where your money goes, and helps you decide which option delivers the best value.

Seamless aluminum gutters are the most popular choice in 2026, offering the best balance of cost, durability, and appearance. They are fabricated on-site from a continuous coil, which eliminates the seams that are the most common leak points in sectional gutters.

Labor is a larger share for gutter projects because the work involves precise slope alignment, custom fabrication for seamless systems, and often working at height on ladders or lifts.

Factors That Affect Your Gutter Cost

  • Home size and stories. A single-story ranch with 150 linear feet of gutters costs far less than a two-story colonial with 250+ linear feet. Multi-story homes require scaffolding or lifts, adding 20–40% to labor costs.
  • Gutter size. Standard 5-inch K-style gutters handle most residential applications. Homes with large roof areas or steep pitches may need 6-inch gutters, which cost 15–20% more but handle significantly more water volume.
  • Seamless vs. sectional. Seamless gutters cost more upfront but reduce leak risk and maintenance over time. The premium is typically $2–$5 per linear foot over sectional gutters of the same material.
  • Fascia condition. If your fascia boards are rotted or damaged, they must be repaired or replaced before new gutters go up. Fascia replacement adds $6–$15 per linear foot depending on the material.
  • Gutter guards. Adding gutter guards at the time of installation is more cost-effective than retrofitting later. Expect to add $7–$22 per linear foot depending on the guard type.
  • Region. Gutter installation costs in the Northeast and Pacific West run 15–25% above the national average. Southern and Midwestern states tend to be at or below baseline pricing.

How to Save Money on Gutter Installation

Gutters are a straightforward project, but there are still ways to get better value without cutting corners.

  • Choose aluminum seamless. Aluminum seamless gutters offer the best long-term value for most homes. They cost moderately more than vinyl or sectional aluminum, but their 20–30 year lifespan and low maintenance make them the most cost-effective over time.
  • Bundle with roofing work. If you are getting a roof replacement, ask your roofer to include new gutters. Many roofers install gutters and can offer a bundled discount, saving 10–15% compared to a separate gutter contractor.
  • Get at least three quotes. Gutter pricing varies significantly between contractors. Make sure each quote specifies material, gauge, gutter size, number of downspouts, and whether old gutter removal is included.
  • Install gutter guards upfront. Adding guards during gutter installation saves the cost of a separate service call and ladder setup later. Over 20 years, gutter guards can also save thousands in cleaning costs.
  • Schedule in the off-season. Late fall and winter are typically slower for gutter installers. You may get better pricing and faster scheduling by avoiding the spring and summer months when demand peaks.
  • Skip copper unless it is essential. Copper gutters look stunning but cost 3–5 times more than aluminum. Unless you have a historic home where copper is architecturally appropriate, aluminum delivers similar performance at a fraction of the price.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much do new gutters cost in 2026?

For a typical home with 200 linear feet, seamless aluminum gutters cost $1,600–$3,000 installed. Vinyl runs $800–$1,600, steel runs $1,800–$3,200, and copper runs $5,000–$9,000. These prices include old gutter removal, new installation, and downspouts.

Are seamless gutters worth the extra cost?

Yes, for most homes. Seamless gutters have fewer joints, which means fewer potential leak points. They also look cleaner and require less maintenance. The premium over sectional gutters is typically $2–$5 per linear foot, which pays for itself through reduced repair and cleaning costs.

How long do gutters last?

Vinyl gutters last 10–15 years. Aluminum and steel gutters last 20–30 years with proper maintenance. Copper gutters can last 50–80 years. The most common failure point for all types is clogging and sagging from debris buildup, which is preventable with gutter guards and regular cleaning.

Do I need gutter guards?

Gutter guards are highly recommended if you have trees near your roofline. They reduce cleaning frequency from 2–4 times per year to once per year or less. Micro-mesh guards ($7–$15 per linear foot) offer the best protection and are generally worth the investment for most homeowners.

Can I install gutters myself?

Sectional vinyl or aluminum gutters are a feasible DIY project for single-story homes. However, seamless gutters require a forming machine that only contractors have. For multi-story homes, the height and ladder work make professional installation the safer and more practical choice.

How many downspouts do I need?

A general rule is one downspout for every 30–40 linear feet of gutter. A typical home with 200 linear feet of gutters needs 5–7 downspouts. Homes in heavy rainfall areas or with large roof planes may need additional downspouts to prevent overflow.
