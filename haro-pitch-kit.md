# HARO / Connectively Pitch Kit

Backlinks are the biggest remaining SEO lever for Woogoro per the 2026-04-20 audit. HARO-style pitches are the cheapest scalable way to earn them: reporters post queries, you reply with a quotable answer, you get cited and linked when the piece runs.

**Target cadence:** 3 pitches per week. ~15 min each. Realistic pickup rate is 15–25%, so ~2 published citations per month is the baseline goal.

---

## Where to monitor

- **Connectively** (the HARO rebrand after Cision bought it, connectively.us). Free tier delivers 3 emails/day covering ~40 queries per digest.
- **Qwoted** (qwoted.com). Free tier is limited; the paid tier ($149/mo) is not worth it yet.
- **Featured.com**. Browsable queries, no digest spam. Replies go through a form. Good for evergreen expert content.
- **Terkel.io**. Similar to Featured; faster-moving.
- **SourceBottle**. Smaller but frequently surfaces niche home-improvement queries.

Pick one to start (Connectively or Featured) and run it for 4 weeks before adding another. Inbox fatigue kills this habit.

## Queries worth pitching

Pitch only when a query matches Woogoro's actual edge:

- Contractor quote reading / cost benchmarking (roofing, HVAC, plumbing, electrical, solar, painting, etc.)
- Home improvement costs by region
- How to evaluate a home-service bid
- Medical bill errors, No Surprises Act, insurance EOBs
- Auto repair markups, dealership vs independent shops
- Moving company scams, binding vs non-binding estimates
- Legal fee structures (flat, contingency, hourly)
- Hidden fees in service-industry pricing
- First-time homebuyer cost planning

**Skip:**
- "Top 10 Tools for X" listicles (product plugs, not expert quotes — your link gets bundled with competitors, weak signal)
- Generic "best advice for new homeowners" — too crowded, pickup rate <5%
- Anything crypto, AI investing, gambling, pharma, adult
- Queries from outlets under DR 30 (check with Ahrefs or Moz free tier — a link from a DR 10 blog is worth less than your time)
- "Sponsored" or "pay-to-feature" requests (not journalism, links often `nofollow`)

## Speed discipline

Reporters pick the first 5–10 good responses. If a digest hits your inbox at 9:00 AM and you reply at 6:00 PM, you lost.

- Target: reply within **60 minutes** of the digest arrival
- Process: open digest → skim subject lines → open the 2–3 that match → draft using the template below → send
- If you can't reply within 2 hours, skip. It's done.

## Master pitch template

Subject: `Re: [query subject line, verbatim]`

```
Hi [reporter first name if given, else "there"],

Responding to your query about [topic in one phrase].

[2–4 sentence answer. State the number/fact first, then the "why." Reporters paste one paragraph into a piece. Make yours paste-ready.]

[One data point or specific number that makes this quotable. Cite the source inline so they can verify in one click.]

[Optional: one-sentence actionable takeaway the reader can use today.]

Full quote you can use verbatim (attributed):
"[40–60 word quote that stands alone. First person. Direct. No hedging.]"
— Geoff Lane, founder, Woogoro (https://woogoro.com)

Happy to expand on any of this, send additional data, or hop on a 15-minute call if helpful. Deadline noted — will respond same day to any follow-ups.

Best,
Geoff Lane
```

Followed by the **credentials block** below.

## Three starter pitches

Use these as reference shapes when the corresponding query type shows up. Rewrite the specifics to match the actual query — verbatim copy-paste reads as spam.

### Pitch A — "What should a fair roofing quote include?"

```
Subject: Re: What should a fair roofing quote include?

Hi there,

Seven line items separate a thorough roofing quote from one that will
generate change orders: tear-off scope (single vs two layers),
underlayment type (synthetic vs felt), ice-and-water shield at eaves
and valleys, flashing (step, counter, drip edge), ventilation (ridge,
soffit, intake), permit/disposal/dump fees broken out, and warranty
terms (manufacturer AND workmanship, with year counts).

From the quotes we analyze, 60%+ omit at least three of these. The
biggest silent cost is deck replacement — contractors price a quote
assuming the plywood is good, then supplement for rotten decking at
$60–90 per sheet once they tear off. For a 2,500 sq ft roof with 10%
deck damage, that's a $1,500–2,200 surprise on day one of the job.

Full quote you can use verbatim:
"A complete roofing quote should break out seven things: tear-off
scope, underlayment, ice-and-water shield, flashing, ventilation,
permit and disposal fees, and warranty terms — with year counts on
both workmanship and manufacturer coverage. Quotes that roll these
into a single line are the ones that generate the largest change
orders."
— Geoff Lane, founder, Woogoro

[credentials block]
```

### Pitch B — "How can homeowners catch medical billing errors?"

```
Subject: Re: How can homeowners catch medical billing errors?

Hi [name],

Three checks catch most overcharges on a hospital bill:

1. Request a fully itemized bill with CPT/HCPCS codes. If the hospital
   sends a summary without codes, you can't verify anything. This is
   free and you're entitled to it under the Hospital Price Transparency
   Rule (45 CFR § 180).

2. Spot-check the top 3 line items against Medicare's rate for that
   CPT code (search "CPT [code] Medicare fee schedule"). Commercial
   rates typically run 2.0–2.5x Medicare. A charge at 4x+ is worth a
   formal dispute.

3. Check for unbundling. If you see CPT 80053 (comprehensive metabolic
   panel) billed alongside CPT 80048 (basic metabolic panel) or 82947
   (glucose), those are covered in the comprehensive panel and
   shouldn't be billed separately under NCCI rules.

Full quote:
"Hospitals aren't allowed to refuse an itemized bill with CPT codes —
and once you have the codes, a 30-minute audit against Medicare rates
catches most of the errors. The common ones are unbundling (billing a
bundled panel's components separately), upcoding (a level-5 ER visit
for routine care), and duplicate charges on the same date of service."
— Geoff Lane, founder, Woogoro

[credentials block]
```

### Pitch C — "What's the most commonly mispriced line item on an HVAC quote?"

```
Subject: Re: What's the most commonly mispriced line item on an HVAC quote?

Hi there,

Electrical work. It's routinely misquoted in two directions and
homeowners almost never know to check.

The under-quote: installers assume your existing disconnect, whip,
and breaker will work with the new unit. Often they don't — a higher-
SEER2 system typically draws less amps than the old unit, but code
(NEC 440) still requires the disconnect to be sized correctly and
within sight of the unit. If the installer "forgot" this, add $400–900.

The over-quote: panel upgrades. A $3,500 panel upgrade line item
shows up on maybe 25% of HVAC quotes and is real only about a third
of the time. The other two-thirds are "the panel is old" (age alone
doesn't require replacement) or "we need a new 2-pole breaker"
(that's $40 of parts, not a panel upgrade).

Full quote:
"Electrical work is the most reliably-mispriced line item on HVAC
quotes. The disconnect and whip get under-quoted because installers
forget to check them; panel upgrades get over-quoted because it's
the most profitable supplemental line item in the industry. A second
opinion from a licensed electrician — not the HVAC company — settles
both in 15 minutes."
— Geoff Lane, founder, Woogoro

[credentials block]
```

## Pickup tracking

Every time a piece runs:

1. Add a row to `data/haro-pickups.json` (create if missing): date, outlet, DR/DA, URL, query subject, your key quote
2. Check the link is `dofollow` (View Source → find your URL → confirm no `rel="nofollow"` or `rel="sponsored"`). If nofollow, it still helps brand mentions but doesn't pass link equity.
3. Once a quarter, review the list for patterns: which query types pay off, which outlets keep coming back, what headlines performed.

## What NOT to do

- **Don't mass-pitch.** Reporters compare replies side by side. A generic template that hits 10 unrelated queries in one day gets you blacklisted.
- **Don't pitch without reading the query carefully.** Off-topic replies waste the reporter's time and train them to ignore your address.
- **Don't oversell Woogoro.** Your product plug is the signature block. The body is for helping the reporter. If they want to feature the tool, they will.
- **Don't follow up more than once.** One polite nudge 48 hours after the deadline is fine. Past that, move on.
- **Don't use AI-obvious phrasing.** Reporters skim dozens of replies; "Certainly! Here are seven comprehensive insights..." is the kiss of death. Write like a human.

## Memory refresh

If you stop seeing results, the queries have probably drifted. Rebuild the "queries worth pitching" list from the last 2 weeks of digests and drop categories that stopped showing up.
