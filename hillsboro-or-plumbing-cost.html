Frequently asked plumbing questions

What causes low water pressure?

Common causes include corroded galvanized pipes, a partially closed main valve, mineral buildup in fixtures, a failing pressure regulator, or a hidden leak in the supply line.

Why does my water heater make popping noises?

Popping or rumbling sounds come from sediment buildup at the bottom of the tank. Flushing the tank annually prevents this and extends the heater's lifespan by 2-3 years.

How do I know if I have a slab leak?

Signs include unexplained spikes in your water bill, warm spots on the floor, the sound of running water when fixtures are off, and cracks in the foundation or damp baseboards.

What is a backflow preventer and do I need one?

A backflow preventer stops contaminated water from flowing back into the clean supply. Most municipalities require them on irrigation systems and commercial properties. Some require annual testing.

Do I need a plumbing permit in Hillsboro?

Most Hillsboro jurisdictions require permits for new lines, water heater installs, and sewer work. Minor repairs like faucet swaps and toilet replacements typically do not need permits.

How often should I have my plumbing inspected in Hillsboro?

A yearly plumbing check in Hillsboro prevents the kind of surprises that cost thousands. The priority items: water heater (8-12 year lifespan, check anode rod and sediment), braided steel supply lines under sinks (replace after 10 years regardless of appearance), and main sewer line condition. Camera sewer inspections ($150-$400) every 3-5 years are standard practice for older homes.

What should a plumbing quote include in Hillsboro?

A professional plumbing quote in Hillsboro should itemize: labor hours, material costs and brands, permit fees if applicable, cleanup, and warranty terms. The quote should include the plumber's license number and proof of insurance. Get three written quotes for any job over $500 to ensure fair pricing.

When is the best time to schedule plumbing work in Hillsboro?

Hillsboro homeowners should target late spring or early fall for planned plumbing work. Dry weather simplifies any excavation and contractor availability is better, which translates to better availability and pricing. Emergency service runs 50-100% more than scheduled jobs, so proactive replacement of aging equipment is the financially smarter path.
