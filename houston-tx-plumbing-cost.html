Houston plumbing: answers to common questions

What does a plumbing inspection include?

A thorough inspection covers supply lines, drain lines, water heater condition, fixture connections, shutoff valves, water pressure testing, and a visual check for leaks and corrosion.

How much does a plumber charge per hour in Houston?

Plumbers in Houston typically charge $75-$150 per hour. Emergency and after-hours calls add a $100-$200 trip fee on top of the hourly rate.

How often should I have my drains cleaned in Houston?

Annual drain maintenance is recommended for most Houston homes. Older homes with mature trees nearby may need semi-annual service to prevent root intrusion.

Can I do plumbing repairs myself?

Simple tasks like replacing a faucet cartridge or fixing a running toilet are DIY-friendly. Anything involving supply lines, gas connections, sewer work, or permits should be left to a licensed plumber.

Should I repair or replace old pipes?

If your home has galvanized steel or polybutylene pipes, full replacement is usually worthwhile. Copper or PEX in good condition can be repaired locally. A camera inspection reveals the true condition.

How do I protect my plumbing during a hurricane in Houston?

Start by locating and testing your main water shutoff valve before hurricane season. If you evacuate, shut off the main to prevent contamination from line breaks. Post-storm, wait for the all-clear or boil-water advisory before using tap water. Sewer backflow is a real risk if flooding occurred; a backflow preventer valve ($300-$800 installed) provides protection and is increasingly mandated by Houston code.

What should a plumbing quote include in Houston?

In Houston, a solid plumbing quote includes labor hours, specified materials and brands, any permit costs, cleanup and disposal fees, and warranty terms. The contractor's license and insurance should be listed. Get three quotes scoped identically to compare pricing fairly on jobs over $500.

When is the best time to schedule plumbing work in Houston?

The sweet spot for scheduling plumbing in Houston is fall or winter. Demand drops after summer's peak water-heater season and before spring's outdoor plumbing projects, meaning better contractor availability and fairer pricing. Since emergency plumbing runs 50-100% above scheduled rates, proactive maintenance during off-peak periods is a straightforward way to save.

Houston-area plumbing licensing and permits

Texas State Board of Plumbing Examiners (TSBPE) issues plumbing licenses in three tiers: Tradesman, Journeyman, and Master. Only a Master Plumber or Journeyman working under a Master can pull permits. Verify at tsbpe.texas.gov. Houston permits route through City of Houston Public Works via the ProjectDox portal with 1-to-3-business-day turnaround. Harris County handles unincorporated-area permits. No separate Houston local license is required, but TSBPE state licensure is mandatory for any work over $500.

Houston's water heater culture

Houston residential water heaters are predominantly gas tank units from AO Smith, Bradford White, and Rheem at 40 or 50 gallons, often located in garage attics because of the slab-on-grade construction. Condensing tankless Rinnai RUR199iN or Navien NPE-A240A2 units require direct-vent plastic PVC flues. Recirculation loop retrofits became common after Winter Storm Uri in 2021.

A Houston guide: freeze risk and winterization

February 2021 Winter Storm Uri exposed Houston's vulnerability to extreme cold. Hundreds of thousands of homes experienced burst pipes and the repair backlog lasted months. Houston homes are not built for freeze protection; insulation is minimal, pipes often run through unconditioned attic spaces, and exterior hose bibs rarely have frost-free valves. Post-Uri pipe insulation and recirculation loop retrofits became standard, and the 2022 ice events reinforced the pattern. Foam pipe insulation on attic lines plus Woodford Model 17 frost-free hose bibs are now baseline upgrades.
