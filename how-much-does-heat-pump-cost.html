How Much Does a Heat Pump Cost?

By Geoff Lane, founder of Woogoro · Updated April 2026

Heat pump installation costs $5,200 to $11,000 in 2026 for a typical ducted air-source system on a 2,000 sq ft home. Ductless mini-splits run $3,800 to $9,500. Geothermal systems cost $25,000 to $45,000 because of ground-loop drilling. After the federal IRA tax credit (up to $2,000) and state/utility rebates, net cost can drop $4,000-$10,000.

The Three Heat Pump Categories That Determine Your Quote

"How much does a heat pump cost" is really three different questions, because three very different system types share the name. Knowing which category your project falls into is the first thing that should happen on any contractor visit — if a quote arrives without that distinction made clearly, the price is unanchored from the actual scope.

What Actually Drives Price Variation Within Each Category

Inside each category, three variables explain most of the spread you'll see across quotes for the same home:

Tonnage. A heat pump is sized in tons of cooling/heating capacity. A 2-ton unit (suitable for ~1,000 sq ft) costs roughly $5,200 installed; a 5-ton unit (~2,500 sq ft) costs roughly $10,500. Contractors should run a Manual J load calculation to size correctly — if a contractor sizes by square footage alone, that's a flag worth pushing back on.

SEER2 efficiency rating. The federal minimum is 14.3 SEER2 (effective 2023). Common tiers: 14.3-15.2 SEER2 (basic, lowest cost), 16-17 SEER2 (mid-range, ~15-25% more than basic), 18-22 SEER2 (premium, 30-50% more). Higher SEER2 means lower operating cost but longer payback — typically 8-12 years to recoup the upfront premium through energy savings.

Refrigerant transition. The industry is mid-transition from R-410A to R-454B (lower global-warming-potential refrigerant). 2026 systems are predominantly R-454B. If a contractor quotes an R-410A system at a discount, it's likely older inventory — fine for installation but worth noting that some R-410A parts will become harder to source after 2030.

Tax Credits and Rebates That Reduce Net Cost

The federal Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) created two stacking incentive programs that significantly reduce out-of-pocket cost on qualifying heat pumps:

  • Energy Efficient Home Improvement Tax Credit (25C). Up to $2,000 per year for qualifying heat pumps. Available to all households regardless of income. Credit is claimed on your federal tax return.
  • High-Efficiency Electric Home Rebate Program (HEEHRA). Income-qualified rebate of up to $8,000 for heat pumps, distributed at point of sale. Eligibility caps at 80-150% of area median income depending on benefit tier. Administered by states; rollout has been uneven, so check your state energy office for current status.
  • Utility-specific rebates. Many electric utilities offer additional rebates ranging from $200 to $4,000 for heat pump installations. These often stack with the federal incentives. Check your utility's rebate page or DSIRE (the federal incentive database).

For a typical $9,000 ducted heat pump install, federal + state + utility rebates can realistically bring net cost to $4,500-$6,000 in many markets. The "true" cost is what you pay after these stack — not the contractor's headline number.

Common Quote Red Flags Specific to Heat Pumps

  • No Manual J load calculation referenced. Sizing by square footage alone usually leads to oversizing, which causes short-cycling, comfort problems, and shorter equipment life.
  • Vague refrigerant specification. Quotes should specify R-454B (current standard) or R-410A (legacy stock) — not just "refrigerant included."
  • Backup heat strip not addressed. Cold-climate systems should have backup heat (electric strips or dual-fuel gas furnace) sized appropriately. Missing or undersized backup leads to comfort complaints in cold snaps.
  • Warranty mismatch. Equipment usually has 10-year parts warranty from manufacturer; labor/install warranty from contractor should be at least 2 years. A one-year labor warranty is a downgrade signal.
  • Permit fees "TBD" or excluded. Most jurisdictions require a permit and inspection. Excluded permits often signal the contractor doesn't pull permits — which can void manufacturer warranty and create insurance issues.

Ducted vs Ductless: Which Saves More

For homes that already have ductwork in good condition, a ducted air-source heat pump is almost always the cheaper path — both upfront and over the system's lifespan. Ductwork is the expensive part of any forced-air system; reusing it means the $5,200-$11,000 cost only includes the heat pump unit and air handler.

For homes without ductwork (older homes, additions, mid-century slabs), retrofitting ducts adds $3,000-$8,000 and weeks of disruption. In those cases, a multi-zone ductless mini-split system at $5,500-$9,500 often wins on total installed cost and avoids the disruption entirely. Ductless also allows zone-by-zone temperature control, which can lower operating costs by another 10-20% versus a single-zone ducted system.

Geothermal makes financial sense in two scenarios: (1) you have land for ground loops and plan to stay 15+ years to recoup the higher upfront cost, or (2) your local utility offers exceptional geothermal-specific rebates that close the cost gap to air-source. In most other cases, air-source heat pumps deliver 80-90% of the comfort and efficiency at half the upfront cost.

How to Tell If Your Specific Quote Is Fair

Heat pump quotes legitimately vary by 30-50% across contractors for the same home. Three quotes that cluster within 15% of each other suggests a fair market price; one quote significantly higher or lower than the others usually means one contractor priced an apples-to-oranges scope.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a heat pump cost in 2026?

For a ducted air-source heat pump on an average 2,000 sq ft home, expect $5,200 to $11,000 installed. Ductless mini-splits run $3,800 to $9,500 depending on the number of zones. Geothermal heat pumps cost $25,000 to $45,000 because of ground-loop drilling. After the federal IRA tax credit of up to $2,000 (plus state/utility rebates that can stack to $5,000-$8,000), net cost drops substantially.

Why is the price range for heat pumps so wide?

Three things drive most of the spread: tonnage (a 2-ton system for a small home costs roughly half what a 5-ton system for a large home costs), efficiency tier (a 16 SEER2 unit costs 30-40% less than a 22 SEER2 unit), and whether your home has existing usable ductwork. Adding ductwork can add $3,000-$8,000 to the project.

Is a heat pump cheaper than a furnace?

Upfront, no — a heat pump system typically costs $1,500 to $3,500 more than a comparable gas furnace + AC combo. Operationally, heat pumps cost less to run in moderate climates (Atlanta, Dallas, Phoenix, Pacific Northwest) where heating loads are modest. In cold climates (Minneapolis, Chicago, Boston), gas furnaces still tend to win on annual operating cost unless electricity is unusually cheap. See our central AC vs heat pump comparison for the full breakdown.

Do heat pumps qualify for tax credits in 2026?

Yes. The federal Inflation Reduction Act provides up to $2,000 in tax credits for qualifying air-source heat pumps installed in 2026. Income-qualified households can also access HEEHRA point-of-sale rebates up to $8,000. Many state utilities add their own rebates on top — check your state energy office or local utility for current programs.

How long do heat pumps last?

Air-source heat pumps last 12 to 15 years on average, slightly less than central AC (15-20 years) because heat pumps run year-round for both heating and cooling. Geothermal heat pump indoor components last 20-25 years; the underground loop typically lasts 50+ years. Maintenance — annual coil cleaning, filter changes, refrigerant checks — extends both.
