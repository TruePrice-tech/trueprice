New Roof Cost 2026: $9,000 to $30,000 Installed

By Geoff Lane, founder of Woogoro · Updated April 2026 · Methodology

A new roof costs $9,000 to $16,000 in 2026 for a typical 2,000 sq ft home with architectural asphalt shingles, or $15,000 to $30,000 with standing-seam metal. Per "square" (100 sq ft of roof), pricing runs $350-$600 for shingles and $600-$1,200 for metal. The biggest cost variable is not size, it is complexity: valleys, dormers, multiple levels, and whether the existing deck needs replacing.

Why "Per Square" Matters More Than Total Cost

Roofers price by the "square", 100 square feet of roof surface, and that is the right unit to compare quotes. Two contractors quoting a $14,000 total for the same house may have very different per-square pricing if one is including roof deck replacement and the other is not. Always ask for the per-square breakdown.

Roof surface is meaningfully larger than your home's floor area. A 2,000 sq ft single-story home with a moderate 6:12 pitch has roughly 25 squares of roof (including overhangs). A two-story 2,000 sq ft home has only about 14 squares because the second floor sits on top of the first. This is why a larger floor plan with two stories often costs less to roof than a smaller single-story sprawl.

What Drives Variation Within Each Material

  • Roof complexity. Cut-up rooflines with valleys, dormers, hips, and intersecting planes take 30-50% more labor than a simple gable. Complexity drives more flashing, more cuts, more underlayment, more waste, and more places for water to find a way in if installation is not tight.
  • Roof pitch. Steep roofs (above 9:12) require harness work, slower pacing, and sometimes special equipment. Expect 15-25% premium over a 6:12 walkable pitch. Very low pitches (under 4:12) require special underlayment for water resistance, adding 5-15%.
  • Tear-off vs overlay. Tear-off (removing existing shingles to expose deck) costs $1-$2 per sq ft on top of the new roof. Overlay (laying new shingles over old) is cheaper but only allowed if the deck and existing shingles are in good shape, and most jurisdictions limit you to one overlay before requiring tear-off. Overlay also adds weight and shortens new shingle life.
  • Deck replacement. If the roof deck (plywood or OSB sheathing) is rotted from leaks, it has to be replaced before new roofing goes on. Partial deck replacement runs $70-$120 per sheet of plywood. Whole-roof deck replacement on a 25-square home can add $3,000-$6,000.
  • Stories and access. A two-story or three-story tear-off requires staging, fall protection, and longer dump-trailer runs. Add 10-20% over a single-story home of equal square count.
  • Region. California, the Northeast, and Pacific Northwest are 15-30% above the national median. Memphis, Indianapolis, and the inland South are 5-12% below. See the city table further down.

What Should Be in a Roofing Quote

A complete roofing quote itemizes every line below. Round-number quotes ("$14,000 total") hide the same scope omissions over and over.

  • Tear-off and disposal (or overlay if applicable), broken out as a line item with dump-fee allowance
  • Roof deck inspection with a per-sheet rate for any rotted plywood replacement
  • Underlayment, synthetic preferred over felt, specified by manufacturer name
  • Ice and water shield on eaves, valleys, around penetrations (required by code in cold climates, smart in any climate)
  • Drip edge on eaves and rakes (required by most building codes since 2012)
  • Flashing at chimneys, walls, vents, replaced new not reused (reused flashing is the #1 source of post-install leaks)
  • Ridge vents and intake vents sized to NFA (net free area) requirements, balanced 50/50 between intake and exhaust
  • Shingles with manufacturer and product line specified, not just "30-year shingles"
  • Starter strip and hip-and-ridge (matched to shingle line, not generic)
  • Pipe boots and step flashing, replaced not reused
  • Cleanup and disposal, including magnetic sweep for nails
  • Workmanship warranty, 5-10 years is standard, 25-year is premium
  • Manufacturer warranty registration, with full system credentials (e.g., GAF Master Elite, Owens Corning Platinum)

If any of these are missing or lumped under generic "labor," ask for them itemized. Missing ice and water shield, undersized ventilation, and reused flashing are the three most common scope omissions that bite homeowners later.

Hidden Roof Costs Most Homeowners Miss

Most roof-quote surprises come from a small set of recurring omissions. Watch for these before you sign.

  • Deck replacement ($70-$120 per 4x8 sheet of plywood). Almost always priced as a per-sheet allowance because the contractor cannot see what is under the existing roof until tear-off. Get the per-sheet rate in writing.
  • Permit fees ($150-$500). Sometimes left off the quote and added later. Should be in the contractor's name.
  • Skylight reseal or replacement ($300-$1,500 per skylight). Existing skylights almost always need new flashing kits when the roof comes off.
  • Chimney cap and counterflashing ($200-$1,200). If the chimney has not been touched in 20 years, original flashing is shot.
  • Satellite dish and solar panel removal/reinstall ($150-$500). Some contractors charge separately; others include in the base bid.
  • Gutter detachment and reattachment ($200-$600). Required when fascia or drip edge needs replacing.
  • Fascia and soffit repair ($8-$25 per linear foot). Common on homes with poor attic ventilation that has rotted the eaves.
  • Code upgrades. Modern code may require synthetic underlayment, drip edge, and minimum ridge ventilation that older roofs did not have. Permit office signoff forces them.
  • HOA-required color or texture. Architectural shingles and stone-coated steel come in specific palettes; HOA-mandated upgrades can add 10-25%.

Roofing Quote Red Flags

  • Storm-chaser door knocks. After a major hail or wind event, out-of-state contractors flood the area with door-to-door pitches. Many are unlicensed in your state and disappear before warranty issues surface. Use only contractors with a verifiable local presence and at least 3 years in business.
  • "We will eat the deductible". Insurance fraud in most states. Voids your claim, puts you on the hook, and is a strong signal of dishonesty in other parts of the bid.
  • Round-number quotes with no per-square or per-line breakdown. Always demand line items. Compare per-square pricing across bidders, not totals.
  • Same-day pressure to sign. Legitimate contractors hold their price 30 days. "Today only" is sales pressure, not a real discount.
  • Cash-only or large deposits. 25-50% deposit is normal. Full payment up front is a fraud red flag.
  • Refusal to pull the permit. If they ask you to pull the permit yourself, they may not be properly licensed in your jurisdiction.
  • No proof of liability insurance and workers comp. Ask for the certificate, then call the insurer to verify it is current. Roofing has the highest worker injury rate in residential construction; uninsured crews mean your homeowners insurance is on the hook for any accident.
  • Below-market quotes (30%+ under others). Usually missing scope: skipping ice and water shield, reusing flashing, no deck inspection, generic shingle, no warranty registration.
  • Reused flashing. The #1 source of leaks after a new roof. New flashing is cheap; the only reason to reuse it is to cut corners.
  • Overlay instead of tear-off when not appropriate. See the deep-dive in our replacement vs repair guide.

Insurance Claim vs Out-of-Pocket: When to Use Which

Roofing is the one home-improvement vertical where insurance is often involved. Understand which path applies before shopping.

If your roof is failing from age (worn shingles, granule loss, age beyond warranty), insurance will not cover it. Replacement is out-of-pocket and you are shopping competitively across contractors.

If your roof has sudden damage (hail, wind, fallen tree) and your policy includes wind/hail coverage at replacement cost, an insurance claim usually makes more sense than out-of-pocket. Your insurance typically covers full replacement value minus your deductible. Get the claim approved BEFORE picking a contractor: the adjuster's scope-of-loss document defines what is covered.

Some policies are actual-cash-value (ACV) rather than replacement-cost, especially after roofs reach 15-20 years of age. ACV pays out the depreciated value (often 30-50% of replacement), leaving a large gap to cover yourself. Read your declarations page before the next storm.

How to Get the Best Roof Quote

  1. Measure first. Pull the home's footprint and pitch from your county tax records or rough-measure with a tape from the ground. Pitch matters: 6:12 is walkable, 9:12+ is harness work and a 15-25% premium.
  2. Pick your top 2 materials. Get every contractor to quote both, typically architectural asphalt vs standing-seam metal. Comparing only one option tells you nothing about your alternatives.
  3. Get 3 written quotes. Itemized, on letterhead, with a quote-valid-through date. Single-quote homeowners pay roughly 20% above market on average.
  4. Verify line items match. Same shingle product line, same underlayment, same flashing scope (replaced not reused), same per-sheet deck-repair rate, same ventilation spec.
  5. Check certifications. Manufacturer-certified installers (GAF Master Elite, Owens Corning Platinum, CertainTeed Master Shingle Applicator) qualify for extended manufacturer warranties that uncertified installers cannot transfer.
  6. Confirm permit and licensing. Contractor pulls the permit in their name. Verify state license number and current liability insurance certificate.
  7. Pay schedule sanity-check. 25-50% deposit is normal. Final payment after the inspection and final cleanup, with all warranty paperwork delivered.

For more detail on negotiating after you have quotes in hand, see how to compare roofing quotes and how to negotiate a roofing quote.

Roofing Permits, Code, and Inspections

Most U.S. cities require a roofing permit for full replacement. Permit fees usually run $150-$500, processed in 1-3 weeks. The contractor should pull the permit; if they ask you to pull it, that is a licensing red flag.

Modern building codes generally require:

  • Drip edge on eaves and rakes (mandated by most jurisdictions since 2012)
  • Ice and water shield on eaves, valleys, and around penetrations in cold climates
  • Synthetic underlayment instead of 15-lb felt in many jurisdictions
  • Balanced ventilation sized to net free area (NFA) requirements, with 50/50 split between intake and exhaust
  • Maximum two layers of shingles before tear-off is mandatory
  • Structural inspection if switching to a heavier material (asphalt to tile or slate)

Final inspection is usually scheduled by the contractor with the permit office. Do not pay the final balance until the city inspector has signed off and the permit is closed.

How Much Can You Save on a Roof?

  • Get 3 quotes (saves 10-25%). Single-quote homeowners pay 15-25% above market on average. Highest-ROI move on the entire project.
  • Off-season install (saves 5-15%). Late fall through winter in temperate climates. Crew schedules open up and per-square rates drop.
  • Architectural asphalt instead of premium impact-rated (saves 25-40%). Skip the upgrade unless you live in a hail or wildfire zone where the premium pays back in insurance discounts.
  • Avoid storm-chaser pricing. Door-knocking out-of-state crews after a hail event are typically 20-40% above local market. Local established roofers know the storm hit too.
  • Insurance claim instead of out-of-pocket (when applicable). If your policy is replacement-cost and the damage is sudden, insurance saves you the entire roof cost minus the deductible.
  • Manufacturer-certified installer (saves on lifetime cost). Pays slightly more upfront but extends warranty 5-25 years; lifetime savings on premature replacement can be $5,000+.
  • Skip overlay in favor of tear-off. Counterintuitive, but overlays void warranties and shorten roof life by 20-30%, raising lifetime cost.
  • Bundle gutter and fascia work. If your gutters or fascia need attention anyway, doing it during the roof job avoids a second mobilization fee.

Roofing FAQ

How much does a new roof cost in 2026?

For a typical 2,000 sq ft single-story home with a moderate-pitch roof, expect $9,000 to $16,000 for architectural asphalt shingles and $15,000 to $30,000 for standing-seam metal. Tile or slate runs $20,000 to $60,000 or more. The single biggest variable is roof complexity: multi-level homes with valleys, dormers, and chimneys can run 30 to 50 percent more than a simple gable roof of the same square footage.

What is a "square" in roofing?

A roofing square equals 100 square feet of roof surface. A typical 2,000 sq ft home has roughly 22 to 30 squares of roof, more than the floor area because roof surface is angled and includes overhangs. Most contractors price by the square. Asphalt shingles in 2026 run $350 to $600 per square installed; standing-seam metal runs $600 to $1,200 per square.

Should I file an insurance claim for a new roof?

Only if you have storm damage that meets your policy's threshold. Insurance covers sudden damage like hail, wind, and fallen trees, not gradual wear or age. If you have hail damage and your policy is replacement-cost rather than actual-cash-value, insurance typically covers full replacement minus your deductible. Be cautious of contractors who promise to eat the deductible: that is insurance fraud in most states. See our roof insurance claim guide for the full process.

How much does it cost to replace a roof on a 1,500 sq ft house?

For a 1,500 sq ft single-story home (typically 18 to 22 squares of roof), asphalt shingle replacement runs $7,000 to $13,000. Metal roof replacement runs $13,000 to $24,000. Smaller homes do not scale linearly: minimum job costs (mobilization, dump fees, permits) mean you do not save 25 percent by being 25 percent smaller. Per-square-foot cost actually rises slightly on smaller jobs.

How long should a new roof last?

3-tab asphalt shingles last 15 to 20 years. Architectural (dimensional) asphalt shingles last 20 to 30 years. Premium impact-rated asphalt shingles last 30 to 50 years. Standing-seam metal lasts 40 to 70 years. Concrete tile lasts 50 or more years. Real-world life is heavily affected by attic ventilation: poorly vented attics shorten any roof's life by 25 to 40 percent from heat and moisture damage.

Is a metal roof worth the extra cost?

Standing-seam metal costs roughly 1.7 to 2.0 times asphalt upfront ($15,000 to $30,000 vs $9,000 to $16,000) but lasts 2 to 3 times longer (40 to 70 years vs 20 to 30). Lifetime cost favors metal if you stay in the home 20 or more years, or live in a wildfire, hail, or hurricane zone where impact resistance matters. For a 7 to 10 year stay, asphalt almost always wins on resale-adjusted value.

Do I need a permit for a new roof?

Most U.S. cities require a roofing permit for full replacement. Permit fees usually run $150 to $500. The contractor should pull the permit; if they ask you to pull it yourself, that is a licensing red flag. Some jurisdictions also require a structural inspection if you are switching from asphalt to a heavier material like tile or slate, since those add significant load to the roof deck and trusses.

Tear-off or overlay: which is right for my roof?

Tear-off (removing the old shingles down to the deck) is always the safer long-term choice and is required by code in most jurisdictions if there are already two layers. Overlay (laying new shingles over old) saves $1 to $2 per square foot but hides deck rot, traps heat, voids most manufacturer warranties, and shortens the new roof's life by 20 to 30 percent. If a contractor pushes overlay to be "competitive on price," get another quote.
