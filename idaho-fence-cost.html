Fence Installation Cost in Idaho (2026)

Installing 150 linear feet of fencing in Idaho typically runs $4,800–$12,500, with 6-foot western red cedar privacy the dominant residential choice. Concrete-set posts must clear the 30-inch design frost line. Idaho's 30-inch frost line drives mandatory deep-set concrete-anchored posts on every residential fence installation — and Snake River Plain volcanic-pumice soils provide poor concrete-anchorage cohesion, requiring belled-out post-footing diameters and gravel-bedded post bases on most Boise, Twin Falls, and Idaho Falls installations to prevent post heave and rotation in expansive volcanic-ash subgrades.

Idaho climate & post-design drivers

  • Frost-line trench depth: 30-inch design minimum for post footings
  • Dominant residential fence material: Western red cedar privacy — dominant in moderate-climate suburban subdivisions
  • Hurricane wind tier: Low — sub-110 mph design wind, standard 8-foot post spacing
  • Termite pressure (TPCT zone): Low — minimal termite pressure
  • HOA fencing-rule prevalence: Moderate — suburban HOA prevalence on newer subdivisions

Idaho licensing, HOA & boundary law

  • License status: Statewide registration required (no exam)
  • License board: Idaho Contractors Board (registration only, no exam) (official site)
  • Permit: state or city permit typical for residential fence over 6 feet

How fence installation costs vary in Idaho

Idaho's 30-inch frost line drives mandatory deep-set concrete-anchored posts on every residential fence installation — and Snake River Plain volcanic-pumice soils provide poor concrete-anchorage cohesion, requiring belled-out post-footing diameters and gravel-bedded post bases on most Boise, Twin Falls, and Idaho Falls installations to prevent post heave and rotation in expansive volcanic-ash subgrades.

State-specific code or insurance rule: Idaho Code §35-101 (the Idaho Open Range Statute) designates the entire state as 'open range' under default law — making cattle and sheep producers explicitly NOT liable for damage caused by their livestock on neighboring property unless that property is enclosed by a 'legal fence' as specifically defined (3-strand barbed wire, 36 inches high, posts not more than 20 feet apart, gates) — placing the burden on residential and crop-farming landowners to fence livestock off their own land statewide.
