Siding Replacement Cost in Indiana (2026)

Re-siding a 1,500 sq ft home in Indiana typically runs $8,800–$24,500, with vinyl panel the dominant residential cladding choice. Indiana sits at the eastern edge of the central U.S. hail and tornado corridor with annual EF1-EF3 exposure across the entire state — Indianapolis and Fort Wayne retrofit specs increasingly default to Class 4 impact-rated vinyl or fiber-cement, and the Wabash River valley flood-elevation overlay drives below-grade rim-joist re-flashing on every full envelope re-side in FEMA-mapped Tippecanoe and Vanderburgh county properties.

Indiana climate & cladding drivers

  • IECC climate zone: 4A-5A
  • Dominant residential cladding: Vinyl panel — dominant low-budget retrofit choice in non-WUI, non-coastal corridors
  • Hurricane wind tier: Moderate wind exposure — 110–140 mph design wind on coastal counties
  • Wildland-urban interface (WUI) cladding rule: No statewide WUI cladding mandate
  • Salt-air corrosion zone: No salt-air corrosion exposure (interior continental)

Indiana licensing & permits

  • License status: No statewide license — municipal credentials only
  • License board: No statewide siding license — Indianapolis, Fort Wayne, Evansville issue local credentials (official site)
  • Permit: city building permit required for full re-side in incorporated municipalities

How siding replacement costs vary in Indiana

Indiana sits at the eastern edge of the central U.S. hail and tornado corridor with annual EF1-EF3 exposure across the entire state — Indianapolis and Fort Wayne retrofit specs increasingly default to Class 4 impact-rated vinyl or fiber-cement, and the Wabash River valley flood-elevation overlay drives below-grade rim-joist re-flashing on every full envelope re-side in FEMA-mapped Tippecanoe and Vanderburgh county properties.

State-specific code or insurance rule: Indiana operates under 675 IAC 14 (Indiana Residential Code) based on a substantially modified 2003 IRC — making Indiana one of the most lagging code-cycle states in the country, with WRB and rainscreen-gap requirements that became standard elsewhere in 2015 still not enforced statewide, and only municipalities like Indianapolis, Carmel, and Bloomington that have independently adopted current codes apply modern envelope standards.
