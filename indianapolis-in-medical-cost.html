Healthcare in Indianapolis, IN: what locals should know

Hospital landscape

Indianapolis is served by multiple hospital systems competing for patients. Understanding which hospitals are in your insurance network before an emergency can save you thousands in out-of-network charges.

Insurance coverage

Check your plan's network carefully. In Indianapolis, the difference between in-network and out-of-network charges for the same procedure can be 3-5x.

Negotiation leverage

Every Indianapolis hospital has a financial assistance program. Ask for the self-pay rate before accepting any bill at face value. Most Indianapolis hospitals offer 20-40% prompt-pay discounts.

Neighborhood access

Broad Ripple, Meridian-Kessler, Irvington residents have access to community health centers with sliding-fee scales for primary care, often at a fraction of ER costs.

Indianapolis and hospital systems and safety-net providers

Indiana University Health, Community Health Network, Ascension St. Vincent, and Franciscan Health serve the metro. IU Health Methodist Hospital is the Level I trauma center. Eskenazi Health is the county-owned safety-net system for Marion County, operating Eskenazi Hospital and a network of community health centers. IU Health is the largest health system in the state by revenue.

Indiana's uninsured rate is approximately 7.8%, slightly above the national average despite Medicaid expansion through the HIP 2.0 waiver. Marion County's rate is slightly higher at around 9%. HIP 2.0's POWER Account structure and healthy-behavior requirements create administrative barriers that reduce effective enrollment compared to standard Medicaid expansion.

Average Medical Procedure Costs in Indianapolis

An ER visit at IU Health Methodist averages $2,200-$3,800, while Eskenazi Hospital's ER charges $1,000-$2,000 for comparable acuity. MRI at Community Hospital East runs $900-$2,000; freestanding imaging centers in Carmel and Fishers offer the same scan for $280-$550. Hip replacement at Franciscan Health Indianapolis costs $25,000-$43,000 before insurance negotiated rates.

Indiana's Hospital Association publishes hospital financial data. Eskenazi's published rates are the most accessible safety-net benchmark in the metro.

Balance billing protections and patient rights: Indianapolis edition

Indiana does not have comprehensive state-level balance billing legislation. Federal No Surprises Act protections are the primary safeguard for Indianapolis residents. The Indiana Department of Insurance handles consumer complaints about insurance billing practices. Some Indiana insurers have voluntary surprise billing policies in plan contracts.

Indiana expanded Medicaid through the HIP 2.0 (Healthy Indiana Plan) waiver in 2015, covering adults up to 138% FPL. HIP 2.0 requires enrollees to make monthly POWER Account contributions and complete healthy-behavior requirements. Approximately 800,000 Hoosiers are enrolled. The POWER Account requirement creates administrative churn that periodically drops enrollees for missed payments.
