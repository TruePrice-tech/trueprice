Common fencing questions in Irving

How long does a wood fence last?

Pressure-treated pine lasts 15-20 years. Cedar lasts 15-25 years. Redwood lasts 20-30 years. All wood fences last longer with stain or sealant applied every 2-3 years.

How tall can I build my fence?

Most municipalities allow 6 feet in backyards and 3-4 feet in front yards without a variance. Some HOAs restrict fence height further. Always check local zoning before building.

What is the cheapest fence material?

Chain link is the cheapest at $10-$20 per linear foot installed. Pressure-treated pine is the cheapest wood option at $15-$25/ft. Vinyl and composite start at $20-$30/ft but never need painting.

What is the best fence for privacy?

A 6-foot solid board (dog-ear, board-on-board, or shadowbox) fence provides the most privacy. Board-on-board is best since it has no gaps even when boards shrink. Vinyl and composite also offer full privacy panels.

Understanding fence installation costs in Irving, TX

Why pricing varies in Irving. Local conditions. Irving has local climate conditions that affect project scheduling and material choices. Wood fences in Irving face a double threat: UV degradation and moisture rot. Pressure-treated pine rated for ground contact (UC4A) is the minimum standard — cedar, which performs well in dry climates, rots in 5-7 years here without aggressive sealing. Budget for annual sealing or staining to reach a 15-year lifespan. HOA-governed communities in Irving frequently dictate fence height, style, and material. Get written pre-approval before purchasing materials — replacing a $5,000 fence because it's 6 inches too tall or the wrong shade is an expensive mistake.

Permits, codes, and licensing. Most fence installation projects in Irving require a permit; confirm with the local building department before signing.

Contractor market. With a population of 228,653, the local market has enough contractors to drive real competition on price, but you should still get three quotes. Irving's rapid recent growth has tightened the contractor market — expect longer lead times and less negotiating room than smaller markets.
