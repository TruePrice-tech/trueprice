Jacksonville solar FAQ

Will solar panels damage my roof?

Properly installed panels do not damage roofs. Reputable installers use flashing and sealant at every penetration point. If your roof needs replacement within 5-10 years, do it before installing solar.

How much roof space do I need for solar?

Each 400W panel needs about 18 square feet. A 8kW system (20 panels) requires roughly 360 square feet of unshaded, south-facing roof space. East and west orientations work but produce 10-15% less.

What is net metering?

Net metering credits you for excess electricity your panels send to the grid. Your meter runs backward during the day and forward at night. Policies and credit rates vary by state and utility.

What happens to solar panels during a power outage?

Grid-tied systems without batteries shut off during outages for safety (anti-islanding). A battery backup or hybrid inverter lets you use solar power during grid failures.

Will solar panels survive a hurricane in Jacksonville?

With the right installation, solar panels withstand 130+ mph winds and frequently survive hurricanes better than surrounding roofing materials. The keys are wind-rated racking certified for Jacksonville's exposure zone, properly sealed roof penetrations, and regular maintenance to keep hardware tight. After any major storm, have your solar installer inspect racking attachments and electrical connections.

What solar incentives are available in Jacksonville?

Start with the 30% federal ITC, which applies to all Jacksonville solar installations with no cap. Then check DSIRE for FL-level incentives: state tax credits, utility rebates, SREC markets, property tax exemptions, and net metering terms. Each utility has different programs and rates. The combined federal-plus-state incentive stack typically reduces net cost by 40-60% in markets with strong state programs.

What's the best season to install solar in Jacksonville?

The best installation window in Jacksonville is fall or winter, cooler weather makes roof work safer and the system is ready for the high-production spring and summer months. Note that the process from quote to installation takes 6-12 weeks including permitting, utility approval, and scheduling. Start the process 2-3 months before your target installation date. The federal tax credit applies to the year of installation, so timing relative to tax year can matter.

Local Solar Guide for Jacksonville, FL

Jacksonville's solar production sits below desert benchmarks but above national averages. The combination of long summers, high cooling loads, and rising utility rates keeps payback timelines competitive, and manageable cleaning cadence (1-2 times per year) offsets the humidity-driven soiling issue.

Jacksonville's hurricane exposure adds a design consideration: panel racking systems must be rated for high-wind loads, and some insurers require specific mounting certifications. Wind-rated racking adds $500-$1,500 to installation cost but is required by building code. Verify that your installer uses racking certified for the wind speed zone applicable to your location and that the installation is permitted and inspected.

With an average home age of 35 years in Jacksonville, roof condition assessment is critical before any solar installation. Panels last 25-30 years, so if your roof has less than 15 years of remaining life, replace it before installing solar. Removing panels for a roof replacement later costs $1,500-$3,000 in de-install and reinstall labor. Most reputable solar companies include a roof assessment in their site survey.

While many states have solar access laws that prevent HOAs from outright banning panels, Jacksonville HOAs can still regulate placement, angle, and visibility from the street. Submit your solar plan to the architectural review board early in the process. Delays in HOA approval are the most common non-technical reason solar installations stall.

Jacksonville's rapid growth often strains the local utility grid, which can strengthen the case for solar-plus-battery systems. Time-of-use rate structures are increasingly common in growing markets, and solar production aligns well with peak-rate afternoon hours, maximizing the bill offset.
