Frequently asked landscaping questions

How do I find a good landscaping contractor in Las Vegas?

Check licensing through your state's contractor board, verify insurance, read reviews from Las Vegas homeowners, and get at least 3 written quotes with line-item detail.

How much should I pay upfront?

Never pay more than 10-15% as a deposit before work begins. Standard payment structure: deposit, progress payment when materials arrive, final payment on completion.

What should I look for in a contractor quote?

A professional quote breaks out labor, materials, permits, and cleanup separately. It includes a timeline, payment schedule, warranty terms, and the contractor's license number.

Do I need a permit for landscaping in Las Vegas?

Most landscaping projects in Las Vegas require a permit. Your contractor should pull the permit and schedule the inspection. Never pull permits in your own name.

What are common red flags in contractor quotes?

Red flags include: no line-item detail, same-day signing pressure, request for more than 30% upfront, no license or insurance verification, and verbal-only warranties.

Las Vegas Soil Conditions and Amendments

Vegas Valley soils are alkaline Mojave-caliche and playa deposits with pH 7.8-8.5 that cause iron chlorosis on most non-native plantings. Hydrocompactible silts in northwest Valley collapse under wetting and require controlled-density backfill. Viragrow and Soil Perfector amendments supply the Las Vegas market. UNLV Cooperative Extension soil testing ($20-$50) is the baseline. Sulfur applications acidify soil for citrus and iron-hungry ornamentals. Gypsum addresses sodic soil concerns common on Henderson lots.

Las Vegas Irrigation Rules and Rebates

Las Vegas Valley Water District (SNWA) implements the strictest residential watering schedule in the country: mandatory once per week in winter, daily-restricted in summer, and no daytime watering year-round. Violations carry $80-$500 fines. SNWA licenses contractors through the Water Smart Contractor program. Nevada State Plumbing Code requires backflow prevention (ASSE 1013 RPZ) on all systems. SNWA rebates pay $80-$300 for qualifying smart-controller installs. Drip-conversion is mandatory for any permitted landscape install.

SNWA Water Smart Landscapes rebate pays $3/sqft for grass-to-xeriscape conversion up to 10,000 sqft, the most aggressive turf-replacement rebate in the country. Nevada AB 356 (2021) banned 'non-functional turf' in HOA common areas by 2027. SNWA coin-operated smart meters affect any fixture-count change on permit. The Henderson Water Conservation program and Boulder City Water Conservation run parallel rebate structures. Smart-controller rebates pay $80-$300 per qualifying device.

Las Vegas Lawn and Turf Choices

Cool-season fescue is largely prohibited under SNWA Water Smart Landscapes rules for new installs. Warm-season Bermuda (Tifway 419, Celebration) is permitted but declining in favor of artificial turf and xeriscape. The SNWA Water Smart Landscapes rebate specifically incentivizes turf removal. Buffalograss is permitted but uncommon. Artificial turf is heavily marketed in Summerlin, Anthem, and Henderson tract homes despite heat-island concerns. Native grasses (desert needlegrass, purple three-awn) are accent plantings rather than lawn surface.

Las Vegas Invasive Pests and Plants

Red imported fire ants have appeared sporadically in Clark County but are not yet established. Palo verde root borer affects blue palo verde specimens planted on tract-home lots. Rugose spiraling whitefly affects Indian laurel fig plantings. Salt cedar (tamarix) along Las Vegas Wash drainages is a regulated invasive plant. Desert broom (Baccharis sarothroides) is a native but behaves aggressively in disturbed soils. Conquistadora scale affects desert willow plantings. Citrus quarantine compliance applies for any citrus nursery stock.

Las Vegas HOA and Design Review

Vegas HOA restrictions are pervasive in Summerlin, Anthem, MacDonald Highlands, The Ridges, and Henderson master-planned communities. Nevada AB 356 (2021) limits HOA turf-required covenants and mandates xeriscape conversion in common areas by 2027. Summerlin West and Summerlin Village 18 require plant-palette compliance from approved lists. MacDonald Highlands and Ascaya specifically require Architectural Review Committee approval on any exterior visible change. Color palettes are tightly limited, often to desert-tone-only finishes.

Las Vegas Tree Services and Ordinances

Las Vegas has limited formal tree protection outside Clark County unincorporated-area parks. Private-lot trees on residential parcels can generally be removed without city permit. HOA architectural committees in Summerlin and MacDonald Highlands are the primary tree-protection mechanism. Licensed arborists through ISA charge $500-$2,500 per mature tree removal. Summer monsoon and microburst cleanup (August-September) spikes pricing 30-50 percent. Palm trimming (dozens of specimens per lot) drives typical seasonal workload.

Las Vegas Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

Vegas landscape maintenance has two distinct seasons: October-April active-use when Bermuda lawns can be overseeded with rye and xeriscape plants show active growth, and May-September summer-stress dormancy when maintenance focuses on irrigation efficiency and heat-stressed-plant recovery. Palm pruning happens April-May before summer. Frost protection on tender tropicals during December-February freeze events. Pre-emergent applications happen mid-February and mid-September for any remaining turf.

Las Vegas Stormwater Rules

Clark County Regional Flood Control District rules apply to projects creating impervious-surface impact on drainage. The Las Vegas Wash coordinates regional stormwater efforts. Rainwater-harvesting is legal but impractical given only 4-5 inches annual rainfall. Summer monsoon flash-flooding drives sizing of retention basins on master-planned-community lots. Permeable-pavement credits through CCRFCD help reduce detention requirements. HOAs often coordinate drainage across multiple lots.
