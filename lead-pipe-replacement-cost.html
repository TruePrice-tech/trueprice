How to Know If You Have a Lead Service Line

The 2024 Lead and Copper Rule Revisions required every public water utility to publish a service line inventory by October 16, 2024. This is your first stop. Five-step verification:

  1. Check your utility's service line map. Most utilities publish online maps now. Search "[your city] lead service line inventory." If your address shows "lead," "galvanized requiring replacement," or "unknown," you have a covered home.
  2. Scratch test at the meter. Find where the water service enters your home (basement, crawlspace, or garage). Scrape the pipe with a key or coin. Lead is soft and shows shiny silver under the dull gray surface.
  3. Magnet test. Lead is non-magnetic. Galvanized steel is magnetic. Copper is non-magnetic but turns the scratched area copper-colored. If a magnet does not stick and a scratch shows shiny silver, it is lead.
  4. Look at the connector at the meter. Lead pipe is gray, dull, and often has a bulbous "wiped joint" at the connection. Modern copper has clean soldered joints. Galvanized has threaded fittings.
  5. Request a certified water test. Most utilities now offer free lead testing under LCRR. Anything over 10 parts per billion (ppb) at first draw is actionable. The EPA action level is 15 ppb, but health agencies recommend treating any detection above 1 ppb seriously.

Homes most likely to have lead service lines:

  • Built before 1986 (when the Safe Drinking Water Act banned new lead pipes)
  • Highest concentration in cities built up before 1950: Northeast and Midwest
  • EPA estimates 9 to 12 million U.S. homes still have lead service lines
  • Highest concentrations: Chicago (400,000+), Cleveland, Detroit, Milwaukee, Pittsburgh, Newark, Trenton, parts of NYC

The 2024 Lead and Copper Rule Revisions (LCRR)

The EPA finalized LCRR in October 2024 under the Safe Drinking Water Act. Key requirements affecting homeowners:

  • Inventory deadline (October 2024, already past): Every public water system must publish a service line inventory identifying every connection as lead, galvanized requiring replacement, non-lead, or unknown.
  • 10-year replacement deadline (October 2037 for most systems): Utilities must replace 100 percent of identified lead and lead-equivalent lines within 10 years of the rule's effective date.
  • Annual replacement rate: Most utilities must replace 7 to 10 percent of identified lead lines per year. Some cities (Chicago, Cleveland) have extensions due to inventory size.
  • Action level lowered: The lead action level dropped from 15 ppb to 10 ppb. Triggering this requires utility-funded replacement.
  • Tap sampling required: Utilities must offer free testing to all customers and prioritize sampling at homes with children, schools, daycare facilities, and pregnant residents.
  • Public notice within 24 hours for any sample exceeding the action level.
  • Partial replacement strongly discouraged and often prohibited under newer state rules.

Funding Sources in 2026

Federal money for lead service line replacement is the largest it has ever been. Most homeowners now pay little or nothing out of pocket if they engage with the right programs.

  • EPA Drinking Water State Revolving Fund (DWSRF). $15 billion appropriated under the 2021 Bipartisan Infrastructure Law specifically for lead service line replacement. Distributed through state utility programs. Most homeowners access it via their utility, not directly.
  • Bipartisan Infrastructure Law lead grant. An additional $11.7 billion in general DWSRF capitalization that often funds lead replacement.
  • WIIN Act lead grants. $20 million per year for disadvantaged communities, no match required.
  • Newark, NJ: Replaced 23,000+ lead service lines 2019-2022 at no cost to homeowners. Program completed.
  • Pittsburgh Water and Sewer Authority: Free full replacement for any lead service line. Ongoing.
  • Lansing, MI: Completed full citywide replacement in 2017 at zero homeowner cost (one of the first).
  • Detroit Water and Sewerage Department: Free replacement program through DWSRF funding, ongoing.
  • Milwaukee Water Works: Free replacement for income-qualifying households, low-cost financing for others.
  • Chicago Department of Water Management: Free replacement for income-qualified residents under the Equity Lead Service Line Replacement Program. Inventory of 400,000+ lines.
  • Trenton Water Works: 100 percent free replacement.
  • DC Water: Lead Pipe Replacement Assistance Program, up to 100 percent funding for income-qualified.
  • State programs: NJ, MI, IL, MA, NY, CA, and others run additional state-level funding. Search "[your state] lead service line program."

If your utility does not have a published program, call and ask. Pressure from LCRR is real, and customer requests document demand for state funding allocations.

Why Partial Replacement Is Discouraged

Partial replacement happens when the utility replaces its side of the line (street to property line) but the homeowner declines or cannot afford the property-side portion. Until 2024, this was common. Research now shows it makes the problem worse short-term.

  • Disturbance releases lead particles. Cutting and reconnecting the lead line dislodges scale that was holding lead in place. Lead in tap water can spike 5 to 10 times normal levels for weeks.
  • Dissimilar-metal junction accelerates corrosion. A copper-to-lead junction creates a galvanic cell that pulls lead into the water faster than the original lead-to-lead junction did.
  • EPA and CDC guidance: Both now recommend full replacement only. Most state DWSRF programs will not fund partial.
  • City rules: Newark, Pittsburgh, Lansing, and others require full replacement when work is done. The utility-side replacement is held until the homeowner-side is funded.
  • If partial happened in your home: use NSF/ANSI 53 certified filter on cold drinking water taps for at least 6 months, run cold water 5 minutes after each shutoff, and request 90-day post-work testing.

Interior Galvanized Plumbing (Often Overlooked)

Lead in the water does not only come from the service line. Galvanized steel pipes that were ever downstream of lead have absorbed lead into their internal scale and continue to leach for decades. The 2024 LCRR added these to the replacement requirement.

  • If your service line is or was lead and your interior plumbing is galvanized: budget $1,500 to $6,000 for galvanized replacement (PEX is the cheap modern choice; see our copper vs PEX guide).
  • Interior galvanized that has never been downstream of lead is lower priority but still leaches zinc and iron over time.
  • Many DWSRF programs now include interior galvanized replacement in the funding package.
  • Symptoms of contaminated galvanized: rust-colored or yellow water, low pressure, taste complaints, visible green or white scale at fixtures.

DIY Testing vs Certified Lab Tests

  • DIY home test kits ($25-$50). Decent for screening, not for legal proof. Examples: Watersafe Lead, First Alert Lead. Detection threshold around 5 ppb.
  • Certified lab tests ($25-$200). EPA-certified labs (search "[your state] EPA-certified water testing lab"). First draw and 5-minute flush samples are standard. Detection to 1 ppb. Required for legal documentation.
  • Free utility tests. Most utilities now offer free certified tests under LCRR. Always start here.
  • NSF/ANSI 53 certified filters as interim ($30-$300). Pitcher filters (Brita Elite, ZeroWater), faucet filters (PUR), and reverse osmosis systems all have NSF/ANSI 53 lead-removal certified models. Verify the certification for "lead reduction" specifically. Replace cartridges per schedule, otherwise effectiveness drops sharply.

Hidden Costs Most Homeowners Miss

  • Yard, sidewalk, driveway restoration ($500-$5,000). Often separated from the pipe-replacement quote. Concrete cuts run $5-$15 per square foot to remove plus same to repour. Mature landscaping is rarely fully restored.
  • Interior galvanized replacement ($1,500-$6,000). If downstream of lead, this is now part of LCRR. Some funding covers it, some does not.
  • Water softener bypass during work. If you have a softener, the technician needs to bypass it for the pressure tests. Easy if accessible, $100-$300 service call if not.
  • Pre and post certified lab testing ($50-$400 round trip). Often free from the utility but worth a private second test if results are borderline.
  • Filter replacement during 6-month flush period. Even with full replacement, run NSF/ANSI 53 filter for 6 months. Replacement cartridges $20-$80.
  • Mainline shutoff valve replacement ($150-$400). Old shutoffs often fail when the line is disturbed. Replace while access is open.
  • Post-work pressure adjustment. New lines may run at higher pressure than old lead. Pressure-reducing valve adjustment or replacement may be needed.

Red Flags in a Lead Pipe Replacement Quote

  • Contractor not coordinating with the utility. The utility owns up to the property line and must do that portion. A quote that ignores utility involvement is wrong, period.
  • No permit included. Lead service line replacement requires a permit and an inspection in every U.S. city.
  • Partial replacement still being marketed. Some contractors quote homeowner-side only without explaining the partial-replacement problem. Push back.
  • Scary-numbers marketing from non-licensed companies. "Your lead pipe is poisoning your family for $25,000" is a sales pitch, not an estimate. Always verify the contractor's state plumbing license.
  • No mention of post-replacement testing. Required under LCRR best practice. Should be included or arranged through the utility.
  • Quote much higher than $160 per linear foot. Above $200/ft is a red flag unless there are very unusual access challenges (rock, deep utilities, dense urban concrete).
  • Contractor refusing to coordinate with funding programs. Many DWSRF and city programs require participating contractors. If yours will not engage, find one who will.
  • Door-to-door sales after a public lead news story. Storm-chaser pattern. Verify license, get 2-3 quotes, never sign on first contact.

Step-by-Step: Replacing Your Lead Service Line

  1. Confirm lead. Utility map, scratch test, magnet test, certified water test.
  2. Call your utility. Ask about LCRR replacement program, DWSRF funding, income-qualifying assistance, full vs partial policies.
  3. Get NSF/ANSI 53 filter today. $30-$200 at any hardware store. Use on cold drinking and cooking taps until 6 months post-replacement.
  4. Request 2-3 quotes. If your utility has a participating contractor program, start there. Otherwise verify state plumbing license and DWSRF eligibility.
  5. Coordinate full replacement. Utility-side and homeowner-side scheduled together. Insist on full, not partial.
  6. Permit and inspection. Plumber pulls the permit. City inspector verifies the work meets code.
  7. Replace interior galvanized if downstream. Often funded under same LCRR program.
  8. Flush and test. Run cold water 5 minutes per fixture after restoration. Get certified post-replacement test (often free from utility).
  9. Continue filter use 6 months. Allows residual lead in interior plumbing to flush.

Lead Pipe Replacement FAQ

How much does it cost to replace a lead service line?

Lead service line replacement costs $2,500 to $8,000 per home in 2026 for the homeowner-side portion (from the property line to the house). Many homeowners pay $0 to $1,500 out of pocket because federal Lead and Copper Rule Revisions (LCRR) and EPA Drinking Water State Revolving Fund grants subsidize most or all of the cost in qualifying jurisdictions. Cities like Newark, Pittsburgh, Detroit, Milwaukee, and Chicago run programs that cover the full replacement.

How do I know if I have a lead service line?

Five tests, in order. First, check your water utility's service line inventory map (required by EPA LCRR as of October 2024). Second, scratch test: scrape the pipe near the meter with a key. Lead is soft and shows shiny silver. Galvanized stays dull gray. Third, magnet test: lead is non-magnetic, galvanized is magnetic. Fourth, inspect the connector at the meter. Fifth, request a free certified water test from your utility (most offer this since 2024). Homes built before 1986 are highest risk.

Does the EPA require lead pipe replacement?

Yes. The Lead and Copper Rule Revisions (LCRR), finalized October 2024, require all U.S. water utilities to identify lead service lines by October 2024 (already past) and replace 100 percent of identified lead lines within 10 years (by October 2037 for most systems). Utilities must replace at least 7-10 percent per year. Homeowners are not legally required to pay for replacement, but many programs require participation when offered.

Why is partial lead pipe replacement bad?

Partial replacement (utility replaces street side, homeowner declines property side) actually increases lead in tap water for weeks to months. Cutting and disturbing the pipe releases lead particles, and the dissimilar-metal junction between new copper and old lead accelerates corrosion. The EPA and CDC now strongly discourage partial replacement. Most state programs will only fund full replacement, and some cities (Newark, Pittsburgh) require the full job.

Will I have to pay for the part of the line on my property?

Often no, but it depends on your city. The water utility owns the pipe up to the property line (the curb stop or meter). The homeowner historically owns from the property line to the house. Newer programs increasingly fund the full line. Cities with full-funding programs as of 2026 include Newark NJ, Pittsburgh PA, Lansing MI, Trenton NJ, Madison WI, parts of Chicago IL, parts of Milwaukee WI, and Washington DC. Check with your utility.

How long does lead service line replacement take?

Replacement itself takes 1 to 2 days. Permitting and utility coordination usually adds 2 to 6 weeks. Trenchless pipe pulling minimizes yard disruption (most yards are restored within 1 week). Traditional excavation requires backfill, regrading, and restoration of grass, sidewalks, or driveways, adding 2 to 4 weeks for full restoration. During the work, water is shut off for 4 to 8 hours.
