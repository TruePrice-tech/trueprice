Los Angeles Native Plant Palette

LA native palette centers on California lilac (Ceanothus griseus horizontalis), toyon, coast live oak, Matilija poppy, and fremontodendron for sunny slopes. Hillside chaparral restorations use black sage, white sage, and California buckwheat. Theodore Payne Foundation in Sun Valley is the definitive native plant nursery serving the basin. Tree of Life Nursery in San Juan Capistrano supplies larger specimen natives. Hollywood Hills and Los Feliz xeriscape projects lean on agave parryi, artichoke agave, and desert spoon.

Los Angeles Soil Conditions and Amendments

LA basin soil varies from sandy-loam in Venice and Mar Vista to decomposed-granite-over-Puente-shale in the Hollywood Hills and Santa Monica Mountain foothills. Hancock Park and Hollywood sit on adobe-like clay that becomes brick-hard in summer. UC ANR cooperative extension soil testing ($45-$120) is the standard starting point. Kellogg Garden Products and Agromin in Oxnard supply most soil amendments. Composted steer manure and G&B Organics biodynamic blend are popular among LA landscape designers.

Los Angeles Lawn and Turf Choices

Cool-season fescue blends (dwarf tall fescue, Marathon II, Marathon III from Southland Sod Farms) historically dominated LA lawns. The 2014-2017 drought and ongoing water restrictions have pushed most new LA landscapes to warm-season Bermuda or native sedge (Carex pansa, Carex praegracilis). Artificial turf is gaining in San Fernando Valley tract homes despite the heat-island concerns. Warm-season buffalograss is a newer specification on sunny Westside lots.

Los Angeles Invasive Pests and Plants

Shot-hole borer (Kuroshio and polyphagous) is the dominant LA tree pest as of 2024-2026, killing oaks, sycamores, and avocados across the basin. California oak moth and gold-spotted oak borer affect hillside coast-live-oak specimens. Asian citrus psyllid (vector of Huanglongbing) requires CDFA-compliance on any citrus nursery stock. Emerald ash borer has not yet confirmed in LA but quarantines border the region. South American palm weevil has devastated Canary Island date palm populations.

Los Angeles HOA and Design Review

LA HOA restrictions are common in master-planned areas of the San Fernando Valley (Porter Ranch, Granada Hills), Palos Verdes, Pacific Palisades, and Rolling Hills. California Civil Code 4735 protects homeowners' right to drought-tolerant landscaping against HOA turf-required covenants. Palos Verdes Homes Association is particularly strict on tree-height limits and view-preservation rules. Hillside Grading Area designations in LADBS add further architectural review.

Los Angeles Tree Services and Ordinances

LA Urban Forestry Division manages street trees under the LADWP/Bureau of Street Services program; homeowners cannot prune or remove them without permission. Private tree removal on oaks over 25 inches DBH requires a Protected Tree permit under LAMC 17.02. Licensed arborists through ISA charge $1,000-$4,500 per mature tree removal. Santa Ana wind-season (October-November) spikes emergency pricing by 40-60 percent. Hillside cleanup after brush fires creates seasonal demand spikes.

Los Angeles Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

LA landscape maintenance runs year-round because winter rains create the growing season and summer is maintenance-dormancy. Fall reseed of cool-season fescue happens mid-September through mid-November. Winter pruning of deciduous fruit trees and roses happens January through February. The fire-season brush-clearance requirement under LAFD Brush Clearance Unit regulations mandates 200 feet of defensible space on any hillside lot, enforced by May 1 annually.

Los Angeles Stormwater Rules

LA stormwater rules under LADBS Green Streets and Green Alleys Program apply to projects over 500 sqft. The Low Impact Development ordinance mandates capture of the 85th-percentile storm (0.75 inches) on site. Rain-barrel and cistern installations qualify for LADWP rebates. The Metropolitan Water District's rain garden and bioswale cost-share program funds up to $2,000 per residential installation. Hillside detention basins are required on many lots in the Hollywood Hills and Pacific Palisades.

Common Los Angeles Landscaping Scams

The dominant LA landscaping scam is the mobile crew that knocks after Santa Ana winds offering to remove 'hazardous' limbs at a markup, takes a cash deposit, performs cosmetic work, and vanishes. California CSLB licensing (C-27 landscape classification) is the baseline verification. The CSLB license-lookup confirms the contractor's bond status and current insurance. Unlicensed work over $500 is a misdemeanor and the bond claim is unenforceable against uninsured operators.
