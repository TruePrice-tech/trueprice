Repair vs. replace: three rules LA roofers use

  • The 75% lifespan rule. If the roof is past 75% of its expected lifespan (15–18 years for asphalt, 40+ for tile and metal, 60+ for slate), replacement is usually the better economic call — a repair just buys 2–4 years before the next failure.
  • The 30% repair rule. If repairs in the past 24 months total more than 30% of replacement cost for your home, replace. You're chasing failures that will keep accelerating.
  • The decking rule. If there's structural decking damage, wet insulation visible from the attic, or three or more active leaks, repair patches will keep failing. The decking is the real problem and only a tear-off reveals the full extent.

Repair numbers above use the LA roofing labor multiplier and are cross-checked against current 2026 industry pricing from the National Roofing Contractors Association (NRCA) and Roofing Contractor Magazine's annual cost survey. Tile and slate repair prices reflect the higher breakage rate, lift cost, and specialty crew requirements vs. asphalt.

How to verify a Los Angeles roofing contractor

California has the most rigorous contractor-license system of any state, and LA-specific code requirements (Title 24, fire zones, hillside ordinance) make verification more important here than in most metros. Three checks, ~3 minutes total — and the single highest-value defense against the most common LA roofing complaints: unlicensed contractors, large deposits taken with no work, and Title-24-non-compliant roofs that fail final inspection.

1. CSLB License — C-39 Roofing or B General Building

California requires a Contractors State License Board (CSLB) license for any contracted work over $500. Roofing has two valid classifications:

  • C-39 Roofing Contractor — specialty classification, can only legally do roofing and waterproofing work.
  • B General Building Contractor — can legally do roofing when it's part of a project involving two or more unrelated trades.

Verify at the CSLB lookup. Every California contractor's license number is required by law to appear on their quote, ad, business card, and contract:

cslb.ca.gov → Check a License

The license detail page shows: Status (must be Active), Classifications (C-39 or B), Bond ($25,000 contractor bond on file since 2026), Workers' Compensation (current carrier and policy), and Complaint Disclosure (judgments, citations, pending actions). Save a screenshot before signing — license status can change.

2. Cross-check Yelp, Google, and BBB

In LA, "yelp roofers los angeles" is the #1 contractor-trust search by a wide margin — homeowners check Yelp first. But Yelp alone misses contractors with manipulated reviews. Triangulate across three sources:

  • Yelp — look for review velocity (sustained over years, not a burst), photos of completed work, and complaint patterns (deposits, no-show, warranty walked back).
  • Google Business Profile — same patterns; also confirms business address and hours match the CSLB record.
  • BBB of Los Angeles & Silicon Valley — accreditation status, time in business, and resolved complaint history. bbb.org → BBB Los Angeles & Silicon Valley

A roofer with 5 stars on Yelp but no Google profile and no BBB record is suspicious. A roofer with consistent A-/A/A+ across all three over 5+ years is the reliable signal.

3. Certificate of insurance — direct from the insurer

Ask the roofer for their insurance carrier name and policy number, then request the certificate of insurance directly from the insurance company. Confirm:

  • General liability: $1M minimum is the LA industry standard; $2M is common for tile and high-elevation jobs
  • Workers' compensation: mandatory in California for any crew with employees; CSLB lookup also shows this
  • Coverage dates that span your full project window including any warranty period

A photocopy the roofer hands you isn't enough. The CSLB regularly cites contractors for forged or expired COIs — direct verification with the insurer takes 5 minutes by phone.

Six red flags worth walking away over

  • No CSLB license number on the quote. California law requires it. Missing license number is the single highest-signal red flag — walk away immediately.
  • Cash-only or large upfront deposit. CSLB caps roofing deposits at $1,000 or 10% of contract price, whichever is less. Any roofer asking for more is in violation of state law.
  • No Title 24 cool-roof product specified. If the quote doesn't name a CRRC-rated product, the roof can't pass final LADBS inspection. The roofer is either inexperienced or planning to non-comply.
  • No permit mentioned. Some LA roofers offer "no permit needed" to undercut others — it's required. Un-permitted work surfaces during home sales, refinances, and insurance claims.
  • Refusal to provide workers' comp proof. Without workers' comp, if a roofer's employee is injured on your property, you can be personally liable for medical and disability.
  • Storm-chaser pattern. Door-to-door solicitation, out-of-state phone numbers, urgency tactics, or appearances immediately after wildfire or Santa Ana wind events. LA has a heavy storm-chaser problem after major weather; legitimate local roofers don't door-knock.

California Title 24 cool-roof requirements in Los Angeles

This is the single biggest LA-specific roofing requirement that homeowners get blindsided by. California's Title 24, Part 6 Building Energy Efficiency Standards require minimum solar reflectance for residential roofs in most California climate zones — and almost all of Los Angeles falls in zones that require cool-roof compliance on any reroof exceeding 50% of total roof area.

Which LA areas trigger Title 24 cool roof

Los Angeles spans CEC Climate Zones 8 (most of coastal and central LA) and 9 (San Fernando Valley and inland LA). Both require cool-roof products for residential reroofs over 50% replacement:

  • Steep-slope roofs (most homes, >2:12 pitch): minimum CRRC-rated aged solar reflectance of 0.20, aged thermal emittance ≥ 0.75, or a Solar Reflectance Index (SRI) of 16. Many modern architectural shingles meet this from the box.
  • Low-slope / flat roofs (<2:12 pitch): minimum aged solar reflectance of 0.55, aged emittance ≥ 0.75. White TPO or coated cap-sheet typically required.
  • Exempted: roofs replacing <50% of total area (most patch and minor section repairs), and some historic-district roofs with documented exemption.

LADWP cool-roof rebate

The LA Department of Water and Power offers a residential Cool Roof Rebate of $0.20–$0.30 per square foot for LADWP customers installing CRRC-rated cool-roof products that exceed Title 24 minimums. For a typical 2,000 sq ft LA home, that's $400–$600 back.

ladwp.com → search "cool roof rebate" — paperwork must be filed within 365 days of installation, and the product must appear on the Cool Roof Rating Council directory.

The federal Inflation Reduction Act also offers a 30% Residential Clean Energy Credit through 2032 on the portion of a roof replacement that supports solar — relevant if you're combining the projects. See our LA solar cost page for current panel pricing.

Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone (VHFHSZ)

LA homes in CAL FIRE-designated Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zones have additional requirements under Chapter 7A of the California Building Code. The roof assembly must be Class A fire-rated (asphalt shingles, metal, tile, slate all qualify with proper underlayment), eaves must be boxed or have specific fire-resistant treatment, and attic ventilation must include ember-resistant vents. Many hillside neighborhoods, Mulholland-corridor properties, Santa Monica Mountains foothills, and parts of the San Fernando Valley fall in VHFHSZ.

Look up your address at the California State Fire Marshal Fire Hazard Severity Zone map.

Hillside ordinance

LA's hillside-zoned properties (15%+ slope) may need additional structural review and access documentation for the permit. Hillside re-roof permits regularly take 2–4 weeks longer than flat-lot permits and have additional inspection requirements. Your roofer should know whether your property is hillside-zoned; if they don't, that's a problem.

LA roofing materials: tile, asphalt, metal, and solar

Clay and concrete tile — the LA standard

Spanish, Mission, and Mediterranean tile is the iconic LA roofing material — it matches the architecture of pre-1940 LA neighborhoods (especially Hancock Park, Los Feliz, Silver Lake, and parts of the Valley), performs exceptionally well in LA's hot-dry climate, and is naturally Title-24-compliant on thermal performance. Clay tile lasts 50–100 years; concrete tile 30–50 years; both outlast their underlayment.

The biggest LA tile-roof question isn't installation — it's "my tile roof is leaking, do I need to replace it?" Usually no. Tile outlasts the felt underlayment beneath it (25–35 years), so a leaking tile roof typically means it's time to lift the tile, replace the underlayment, and reset. Most reusable tile can be salvaged; broken tile during lift is the wild card. Budget $4,000–$12,000 for a typical 2,000 sq ft underlayment-replacement job.

Tile weighs 900–1,200 lbs per square (100 sq ft). LA homes built for asphalt shingles need a structural calculation before switching to tile — sometimes the engineering is straightforward, sometimes it requires structural reinforcement that adds $5,000–$15,000.

Architectural asphalt shingles

The mid-market default in LA — most ranch, modern, and mid-century homes use architectural asphalt. 18–25 year lifespan in LA's UV exposure. GAF Timberline HDZ, Owens Corning Duration, CertainTeed Landmark, and Malarkey Highlander are the most commonly specified products. All major manufacturers now have Title-24-compliant cool-roof SKUs in standard colors. South- and west-facing slopes in the San Fernando Valley typically need replacement 3–5 years before north- and east-facing slopes due to UV degradation.

Standing-seam metal

Rising fast in LA for flat-and-modern designs, Mid-Century renovations, and Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone properties. 40–60 year lifespan, Class A fire rating with proper underlayment, high reflectance options for Title 24, and lower long-term maintenance than tile. Cost premium is real ($32,000 vs $14,600 for architectural at 2,000 sq ft) but lifespan amortization makes it competitive over 30+ years.

Solar roof integration

Most LA homeowners with 10+ years of roof life left should add solar at the same time as roof replacement. Adding solar to a 15-year-old roof later means you'll likely have to remove and reinstall panels ($1,500–$3,500) when the roof eventually fails. Combining the projects also unlocks the federal 30% Residential Clean Energy Credit through 2032 on the roof portion that supports solar (specifically, the roof underlayment and the section of decking under the array).

See LA solar cost → for current 2026 panel pricing in Los Angeles.

Slate and Tesla Solar Roof

Slate is rare in LA but appears on pre-1930 estates (Hancock Park, Pasadena historic districts). Lifespan 75–125 years. Quoted on a per-project basis — typical 2,000 sq ft slate roof runs $35,000–$70,000. Tesla Solar Roof is a niche but growing LA product; combined roof+solar pricing runs $50,000–$100,000+ for a 2,000 sq ft home depending on solar capacity. Best for new construction or major renovation where you're already replacing the entire roof structure.

What an LA roof replacement quote should include

Quotes that lump everything into "labor and materials" are designed to hide what's NOT included. A real itemized LA roofing quote should list each of these — and the contractor should be able to explain how each affects price:

  • Tear-off (number of layers — LA Building Code allows max 2 layers on most homes, but many LA roofs already have 2 from previous work)
  • Decking inspection and repair allowance (typically priced per sheet of OSB/plywood replaced)
  • Underlayment (synthetic preferred; for tile, the underlayment IS the waterproofing layer and matters more than the tile itself)
  • Drip edge at all eaves (metal flashing along the lower edge)
  • Flashing at all penetrations (chimney, vents, skylights, walls, valleys)
  • Ventilation (ridge vents or box vents — undersized ventilation voids most shingle warranties)
  • Title 24 cool-roof product (specific CRRC-rated product name and color)
  • Starter strip and ridge cap (matched to the field shingle)
  • Debris removal and disposal (typically dumpster on site; AQMD asbestos notification if pre-1980 layers)
  • LADBS or LA County permit (filed by the contractor, with your name on file)
  • Final inspection coordination (contractor schedules; you don't need to be there)
  • Written workmanship warranty (minimum 5 years; 10+ years is reputable-LA standard)

Note: ice and water shield appears on national roofing checklists but isn't needed in LA's climate (no freeze-thaw cycle). Some out-of-state-trained roofers still spec it, which adds cost without value in LA.

LA homes built before 1980: asbestos shingles, lead, and decking

Los Angeles has a median home build year of 1965 per Census ACS 2024 — older than most major US metros. That changes how a roof replacement should be quoted and performed on older LA homes.

Asbestos shingles

Asphalt shingles installed before 1980 commonly contain chrysotile asbestos as a reinforcing fiber. Disturbing asbestos shingles during tear-off requires:

  • South Coast AQMD notification (10 working days advance, fee applies)
  • Licensed abatement contractor (separate from the roofer)
  • Containment, wet-removal, and OSHA-compliant disposal
  • Cost adder: $2–$5 per sq ft on top of standard tear-off

A reputable LA roofer working on a pre-1980 home will recommend a $300–$600 asbestos sample analysis before tear-off rather than assume — sample-and-test is much cheaper than discovering asbestos mid-job and having to stop work for emergency abatement.

Decking on older LA homes

Pre-1960 LA homes often have 1x6 or 1x8 plank decking rather than modern 1/2" or 5/8" plywood or OSB. Plank decking can be re-roofed in place but has gaps that affect insulation, wind resistance, and code compliance for some shingle warranties. Budget an additional $1.50–$3.50 per sq ft if your roofer recommends overlaying plywood on top of plank decking, or full decking replacement.

Lead in older flashings

Older LA homes (pre-1986) may have lead flashings at chimneys and roof penetrations. Lead is durable and code-compliant but needs to be handled carefully during tear-off. Disposal is regulated under California Title 22 hazardous waste rules.

Roofing in Los Angeles: what locals should know

Heat and UV degradation

LA's hot-dry climate hits roofs with high UV intensity year-round — significantly more than inland or northern markets. South- and west-facing slopes typically need replacement 3–5 years before north- and east-facing slopes on the same home. Premium architectural shingles with high SRI ratings dramatically extend the south/west lifespan; standard 3-tab does not.

Wildfire season and contractor availability

Fire season in LA runs roughly May–October. After major wildfire events (Bobcat, Woolsey, Saddleridge, Palisades, Eaton), roofing demand spikes for 6–18 months from displaced and impacted homeowners. Quote spreads widen during these periods and storm-chaser contractors arrive from out of state. The best LA roofers are typically booked 4–8 weeks ahead during peak post-fire-season demand. Book before fire season when possible.

Santa Ana wind events

Santa Ana winds (60+ mph gusts October–January, occasionally into spring) are the highest roof-stress event in LA. Properly-installed architectural shingles handle 110+ mph wind ratings; tile is more vulnerable to displacement at the ridge and edges. After a Santa Ana event, walk your property and look at the ridge line, hips, and any obvious displaced tile or shingles before calling a roofer — minor displacement caught quickly can be fixed for $200–$500.

Wildfire ash and post-fire cleaning

Wildfire smoke and ash accumulate on LA roofs during fire season. Ash is mildly acidic and, left on the roof through the rainy season, can stain tile, accelerate granule loss on asphalt, and clog gutters. Roof-soft-washing (low-pressure detergent) post-fire-season is $400–$800 for a typical 2,000 sq ft home and extends roof lifespan meaningfully. Pressure-washing should NOT be used on shingle roofs — it strips granules.

HOA restrictions and historic districts

LA has high HOA prevalence (single-family planned communities, condo associations, and historic districts). Many HOAs restrict roofing materials, colors, and even visible solar arrays. Historic districts (Hancock Park, Angelino Heights, Whitley Heights, others) have additional design review requirements via the Cultural Heritage Commission. Always check HOA and historic-district rules before signing a roofing contract.

Climate and what it means for roofs

LA's hot-dry climate puts three major stressors on roofs: high UV exposure (12+ hours of strong sun in summer), low humidity with rapid thermal cycling (60°F day-to-night swings inland), and seasonal Santa Ana wind events. Cool-roof products are not just a code requirement but a real lifespan extender on south- and west-facing slopes. Wildfire smoke and ash add a cleaning cycle most other US metros don't have.

Permits and code

Roof replacement requires a permit in every part of LA County. City of Los Angeles uses LADBS (ladbs.org, ePlanLA portal); unincorporated and contract cities use LA County Building & Safety; major incorporated cities (Beverly Hills, Santa Monica, Pasadena, Burbank, Long Beach, Glendale) run their own. Title 24 cool-roof compliance is verified at final inspection. Class A fire assemblies are required in Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zones.

Contractor market

LA is the largest single-metro roofing market in the country. The CSLB licenses thousands of C-39 and B contractors in the LA area; the BBB of Los Angeles & Silicon Valley lists hundreds of accredited roofing contractors. Yelp dominates the LA contractor-search habit (more than any other US metro). Quote spreads are wide — commonly 30–50% on the same scope — because of the range of crew quality, material brands, and warranty terms. The storm-chaser problem after wildfires is significant; verify CSLB license before any conversation.

Frequently asked LA roofing questions

How much does roof replacement cost in Los Angeles in 2026?

A typical 2,000 sq ft LA roof replacement runs $11,800 for architectural asphalt shingles, $14,600 for premium architectural, $32,000 for standing-seam metal, and $33,400 for clay or concrete tile. How much does a roof repair cost in Los Angeles?

Should I repair my LA roof or replace it?

Three rules of thumb LA roofers use: (1) if the roof is past 75% of its expected lifespan (15–18 years for asphalt, 40+ for tile, 40+ for metal), replacement is usually the better economic call; (2) if repairs in the past 24 months total more than 30% of replacement cost, replace; (3) if there's structural decking damage, wet insulation, or multiple leaks, repair patches will just chase the next failure. For LA homes built before 1980, also check whether shingles contain asbestos before any repair work.

How do I verify a Los Angeles roofer is licensed?

California requires a Contractors State License Board (CSLB) license for any contracted work over $500. Roofing has its own specialty classification: C-39 Roofing Contractor. A B General Building Contractor can also legally perform roofing as part of a multi-trade project. At cslb.ca.gov, search the company name or license number to verify license status (Active), classification (C-39 or B), $25,000 contractor bond on file, workers' comp policy current, and complaint disclosure. Every California contractor's license number must appear on quotes, ads, business cards, and contracts.

What is California Title 24 / Cool Roof and does my LA roof need it?

Title 24, Part 6 is California's Building Energy Efficiency Standards. For residential reroofs replacing more than 50% of the roof in CEC Climate Zones 8 (most of coastal LA) and 9 (San Fernando Valley and inland LA), the roof must meet minimum aged solar reflectance. Do I need a permit to replace my roof in Los Angeles?

How long does a roof last in Los Angeles?

Smog, UV degradation, and Santa Ana wind events are the biggest LA-specific degradation factors.

How long does a roof replacement take in Los Angeles?

Tile roofs run 3–5 days because of the weight, breakage rate, and lift logistics. Metal standing seam takes 3–4 days. Add 1–3 weeks for the LADBS or LA County permit to issue, depending on jurisdiction and current backlog. Hillside or Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone homes can require additional structural and code review that adds 2–4 weeks to the permit timeline.

What's the best roofing material for Los Angeles?

How much does a Spanish or Mission tile roof repair cost in LA?

Tile-specific repairs in LA: cracked or slipped tile replacement (10–20 tiles) $400–$1,200, valley or ridge flashing replacement on a tile roof $800–$2,500, full underlayment replacement (most common tile-roof repair — tile outlasts the felt beneath it) $4,000–$12,000 for a 2,000 sq ft home. The underlayment job is the big one: clay tile can last 75 years but the underlayment fails at 25–35 years, so "my tile roof is leaking" usually means "time to lift the tile, replace the underlayment, and reset." Reusable tile keeps cost down; broken tile during lift is the wild card.

Should I add solar at the same time as a new roof?

For most LA homeowners with 10+ years of roof life left, yes. Adding solar to a 15-year-old roof means you'll likely have to remove and reinstall panels ($1,500–$3,500) when the roof eventually needs replacement. Combining the projects also unlocks federal residential clean energy credit (30% through 2032) on the roof portion that supports solar, and several CA utilities offer joint-installation incentives. Can I file a homeowners insurance claim for an LA roof?

In LA, insurance covers sudden, accidental damage (Santa Ana wind, falling tree, wildfire) but not gradual wear-and-tear or roof age. Hail is rare in LA, so the typical Plains/Midwest claim pattern doesn't apply. The biggest LA-specific claim category is wildfire-related: ember intrusion, smoke and ash, structural damage. Insurers may also pay for partial repair if a storm event causes a leak. Get an independent inspection (not the roofer who's bidding the job) before filing — insurers regularly deny claims that bundle storm damage with pre-existing wear.

How many roofing quotes should I get in Los Angeles?

Get at least three written CSLB-licensed quotes for any LA roof replacement. The LA market is large and competitive enough that quotes commonly vary 30–50% for the same scope. The spread is usually material quality, underlayment system, ventilation upgrades, and warranty terms — not labor rate. For repair work under $2,000, two quotes is often enough unless the scope is unclear; ask each roofer to itemize what's included so you can compare apples to apples.
