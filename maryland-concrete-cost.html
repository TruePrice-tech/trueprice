Concrete Driveway Cost in Maryland (2026)

Concrete driveway pours in Maryland typically run $3,400–$8,800 for a 600 sq ft 4-inch concrete driveway pour at 3500 psi compressive strength. Buried footings must clear the 30-inch design frost line, and the state sits in moderate freeze-thaw exposure (ACI F1). Maryland's combination of Chesapeake Bay Critical Area Act stormwater requirements within 1,000 ft of tidal waters, Maryland road de-icing salt exposure from Maryland Department of Transportation snow operations, and 30-inch frost depth drives 6%+ air-entrainment plus 0.40 maximum water-cement ratio on every Maryland residential pour — and Maryland's pre-1978 housing-stock concentration in Baltimore rowhouse districts requires specialty narrow-cavity concrete-driveway-replacement contractor specialization to manage existing rowhouse foundation tie-ins.

Maryland climate, soil & seismic drivers

  • Frost-line trench depth: 30-inch design minimum for buried footings
  • Freeze-thaw exposure (ACI 201.2R): F1 — moderate freeze-thaw, 4.5-6% air-entrainment recommended
  • Typical residential mix: 3500 psi compressive strength
  • Expansive soil risk: Low — sandy or stable subsoil, standard slab-on-grade design
  • Sulfate exposure (ACI 318): S0 — no sulfate exposure, Type I/II cement adequate
  • Seismic design category (ASCE 7-22): SDC B — minor seismic risk, standard tie-down requirements
  • Winter de-icer salt exposure: High — winter de-icing salt drives air-entrainment + corrosion-protected rebar

Maryland licensing & permits

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: Maryland Home Improvement Commission (MHIC) — Home Improvement Contractor (official site)
  • Permit: city or county building permit required for residential driveway pour > 200 sq ft; MHIC license required above $500 contract

How concrete driveway costs vary in Maryland

State-specific code or insurance rule: Maryland Business Regulation §8-301 requires every Maryland concrete contractor exceeding the $500 residential threshold to hold a Maryland Home Improvement Commission (MHIC) Home Improvement Contractor license — and Maryland's Chesapeake Bay Critical Area Act (Annotated Code §8-1801) imposes the most restrictive coastal stormwater management framework in the country requiring continuous concrete impervious-surface review on every residential pour within 1,000 ft of tidal waters, doubling typical Maryland coastal residential pour permit timelines and adding mandatory bioretention infiltration design on every covered project.
