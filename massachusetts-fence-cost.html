Fence Installation Cost in Massachusetts (2026)

Installing 150 linear feet of fencing in Massachusetts typically runs $5,800–$14,500, with 6-foot western red cedar privacy the dominant residential choice. Concrete-set posts must clear the 48-inch design frost line. Massachusetts's 48-inch frost line drives mandatory 4-foot+ post-hole depths and concrete-anchored installations on every residential fence — and Cape Cod, North Shore, and South Shore Atlantic salt-haze deposition corrodes standard galvanized chain-link mesh within 5-7 years, mandating PVC-coated or stainless mesh on shoreline retrofits within 1 mile of tidewater.

Massachusetts climate & post-design drivers

  • Frost-line trench depth: 48-inch design minimum for post footings
  • Dominant residential fence material: Western red cedar privacy — dominant in moderate-climate suburban subdivisions
  • Hurricane wind tier: Moderate — 110–140 mph design wind drives 8-foot post spacing
  • Termite pressure (TPCT zone): Moderate — periodic termite pressure
  • HOA fencing-rule prevalence: Moderate — suburban HOA prevalence on newer subdivisions

Massachusetts licensing, HOA & boundary law

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: Massachusetts Office of Consumer Affairs — Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration (official site)
  • Permit: town zoning permit typical for residential fence over 6 feet

How fence installation costs vary in Massachusetts

Massachusetts's 48-inch frost line drives mandatory 4-foot+ post-hole depths and concrete-anchored installations on every residential fence — and Cape Cod, North Shore, and South Shore Atlantic salt-haze deposition corrodes standard galvanized chain-link mesh within 5-7 years, mandating PVC-coated or stainless mesh on shoreline retrofits within 1 mile of tidewater.

State-specific code or insurance rule: Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 49 §21 (the Massachusetts Spite Fence Statute) is one of the most cited 'spite fence' laws in the country — making any fence over 6 feet tall built primarily to annoy or injure a neighboring property owner an actionable nuisance under M.G.L. c. 49 §21, with Rideout v. Knox (1888) Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court ruling establishing the doctrine that absolute property rights end where they injure neighbors maliciously rather than for reasonable enjoyment.
