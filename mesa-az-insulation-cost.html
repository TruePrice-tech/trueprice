Mesa insulation: answers to common questions

What is the difference between open-cell and closed-cell spray foam?

Open-cell (R-3.7/inch) is cheaper, flexible, and better for interior walls. Closed-cell (R-6.5/inch) is rigid, waterproof, adds structural strength, and is required in flood zones and below-grade applications.

How much energy does insulation save?

Insulating an under-insulated attic to current code saves 15-25% on heating and cooling costs. In a home spending $2,000/year on energy, that is $300-$500 annual savings, paying back a $1,500-$2,500 project in 3-5 years.

Should I insulate my walls or attic first?

Attic insulation provides the best return since heat rises. Most homes lose 25-30% of heat through the attic. Wall insulation is second priority and harder to retrofit. Floor insulation over crawl spaces is third.

Wall cavity retrofit approach around Mesa

The extreme outdoor-to-indoor temperature differential (115F+ outside, 75F inside) makes wall insulation more impactful in Mesa than in mild-climate markets because the thermal driving force is 40+ degrees versus 20-30 degrees in temperate cities.

Mesa air sealing priorities

Moisture Control and Vapor Strategy near Mesa

Mesa moisture management: the driest climate in this group eliminates virtually all moisture concerns for insulation design. Vapor barriers are unnecessary because the ambient humidity is too low to create condensation risk. Spray-foam insulation can be applied without the moisture-trapping risks that complicate its use in humid markets. The only moisture consideration: during the July-August monsoon season's brief humidity spike, newly installed spray-foam insulation should have cured for at least 72 hours before the monsoon moisture arrives. Post-monsoon is the ideal installation timing. The very hard water (300-400 ppm from SRP) should not be used for spray-foam mixing; use clean water per the manufacturer's specifications.
