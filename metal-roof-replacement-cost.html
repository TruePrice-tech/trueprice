Metal Roof Replacement Cost

Metal roofing has moved well beyond the barn roofs and industrial buildings most people picture. Today, metal is one of the fastest-growing segments of the residential roofing market, and for good reason: it outlasts asphalt by decades, shrugs off hail and high winds, and can cut cooling costs during summer months. The trade-off is a higher upfront price tag, which makes it critical to understand exactly what you are paying for before you sign a contract.

How Much Does a Metal Roof Cost?

For a typical single-family home with a roof area between 1,800 and 2,500 square feet, a full metal roof replacement generally falls in the $15,000 to $35,000 range. Larger or more complex roofs can push past $45,000, especially when standing seam panels and extensive custom flashing are involved.

Several factors determine where your project falls within that range: the type of metal panel, your roof's pitch and number of penetrations, local labor rates, and the scope of tear-off and deck prep work needed before installation begins.

Metal Roof Types and Pricing

Not all metal roofs are created equal. The four most common residential systems each carry different price points, aesthetics, and performance characteristics.

Metal Roof Type Cost Per Sq Ft (Installed) Best For
Standing seam $10 - $16 Premium durability, clean modern look
Corrugated metal panels $7 - $12 Budget-friendly metal option, rural or farmhouse style
Metal shingles $8 - $14 Traditional shingle appearance with metal longevity
Stone-coated steel $9 - $15 Mimics tile or shake, excellent hail resistance

Standing seam remains the gold standard for residential metal roofing because its concealed fastener design eliminates the exposed screws that can become leak points over time. Corrugated panels offer the lowest entry price but use exposed fasteners that may need maintenance after 15 to 20 years.

Metal vs Asphalt: Cost Comparison

On a pure upfront-cost basis, metal loses to asphalt every time. A metal roof typically costs two to three times more than an architectural shingle roof. But roofing is a long-term investment, and the math changes when you look at the full ownership picture.

Factor Asphalt Shingles Metal Roof
Installed cost (2,000 sq ft) $9,000 - $16,000 $20,000 - $36,000
Expected lifespan 20 - 30 years 40 - 70 years
Annual energy savings Baseline Up to 25% on cooling costs
Insurance discount Rarely Often 5% - 35% depending on state
Maintenance frequency Every 5 - 10 years Minimal over entire lifespan

Over a 50-year period, a homeowner with asphalt shingles will likely need two full replacements, while a metal roof owner will still be on their original installation. When you add insurance discounts and energy savings, the lifetime cost of metal is often comparable to, or lower than, repeated shingle replacements.

What Is Included in a Metal Roof Quote

Metal roof quotes include line items you will never see on a shingle estimate. Understanding these items helps you compare bids accurately and avoid surprises.

  • Metal panels or shingles -- the primary roofing material, priced per square foot or per roofing square (100 sq ft).
  • Concealed clips and fasteners -- standing seam systems use clips that attach panels to the deck while allowing thermal expansion. These are a separate material cost.
  • Custom-formed flashing -- valleys, rake edges, eave drip, wall transitions, and pipe boots are often fabricated on site with a portable brake. The complexity of your roof directly drives this cost.
  • Ridge cap and hip trim -- formed metal pieces that cap the peaks and hips of the roof.
  • High-temp underlayment -- metal roofs require underlayment rated for higher temperatures than standard felt paper, often a synthetic or peel-and-stick product.
  • Tear-off and disposal -- removing the old roof, hauling debris, and dumpster fees.
  • Deck repairs -- any rotten or damaged plywood discovered during tear-off, typically charged per sheet replaced.

When comparing quotes, make sure each bid includes the same scope. A quote that leaves out custom flashing or uses a lower-grade underlayment may look cheaper but will cost more over time in repairs and reduced performance.

Is a Metal Roof Worth the Investment?

The return on investment for a metal roof depends on how long you plan to stay in your home and your local climate conditions. Here is how the numbers typically play out:

  • Longevity -- a metal roof lasts 2 to 3 times longer than asphalt shingles, meaning you avoid at least one full replacement cycle over the life of your home.
  • Energy efficiency -- reflective metal coatings can reduce cooling costs by up to 25%, a significant advantage in southern and western states.
  • Resale value -- studies consistently show that metal roofs recoup 60% to 85% of their cost at resale, and in storm-prone markets, buyers actively seek metal-roofed homes.
  • Insurance savings -- many insurers offer premium discounts for impact-resistant metal roofing, particularly in hail-prone states like Texas, Colorado, and Oklahoma.
  • Low maintenance -- outside of the occasional inspection, metal roofs require almost no maintenance over their lifespan. No granule loss, no moss growth, no cracked or curling shingles.

For homeowners who plan to stay in their home for 10 or more years, or who live in areas with extreme weather, a metal roof is often the most cost-effective long-term choice despite the higher upfront price.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a metal roof replacement cost?

Most homeowners spend between $15,000 and $35,000 for a complete metal roof replacement on a typical-sized home. Larger roofs or premium standing seam systems can exceed $45,000.

How long does a metal roof last?

Metal roofs typically last 40 to 70 years depending on the material and installation quality. Standing seam roofs with concealed fasteners tend to last the longest because there are no exposed screws to degrade over time.

Can you install a metal roof over existing shingles?

In some cases, yes. Certain metal roofing systems can be installed over one layer of existing shingles, which saves on tear-off costs. However, this approach prevents inspection of the roof deck and may void some warranties. Most contractors recommend a full tear-off for the best long-term result.

Are metal roofs noisy during rain?

Modern metal roofs installed over solid sheathing with proper underlayment are no louder than asphalt shingle roofs during rain. The solid decking and underlayment layers absorb sound effectively. Noise is mainly a concern with metal roofs installed over open purlins without a solid deck beneath them.
