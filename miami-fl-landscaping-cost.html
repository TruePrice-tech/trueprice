What Miami residents ask about landscaping

What are the best low-maintenance landscaping plants?

Native perennials, ornamental grasses, and drought-tolerant shrubs need the least care. Avoid high-maintenance options like hybrid tea roses, non-native annuals, and fast-growing trees that require frequent pruning.

Does landscaping increase home value?

Well-designed landscaping adds 5-15% to home value. Mature trees alone add $1,000-$10,000 each. Curb appeal landscaping recovers 100-200% of investment at resale, one of the highest ROI improvements.

How much does tree removal cost?

Small trees (under 30 feet) cost $200-$500 to remove. Medium trees (30-60 feet) cost $500-$1,000. Large trees (60-100 feet) cost $1,000-$2,000+. Stump grinding adds $100-$400 per stump.

How much does outdoor lighting cost?

Professional landscape lighting costs $2,000-$5,000 for path lights, uplights, and accent lighting. LED fixtures cost more upfront but last 50,000+ hours. Solar options cost less but provide dimmer, less reliable light.

Native plant palette: a Miami guide

South Florida native palette centers on live oak, gumbo limbo, sea grape, coontie (cycad), firebush, and native coontie cycads. Fairchild Tropical Botanic Garden and the Kampong are the definitive regional references. Plant Creations Nursery in Homestead and Pinecrest Gardens demonstration beds supply most of the Miami native market. Coastal plantings use sea oats, beach sunflower, railroad vine, and saw palmetto. Inland Everglades-adjacent plantings use pond cypress, pond apple, and alligator flag.

Understanding soil conditions and amendments in Miami

Miami-Dade soils are oolitic limestone and Miami limestone with thin sandy-marl topsoil. Coastal mangrove peat requires over-excavation near Biscayne Bay and Coconut Grove. The high pH (7.8-8.4) and rocky underlay makes many non-native plantings struggle with iron chlorosis. Homestead Compost and Kendall Organics supply most of the South Florida amendment market. University of Florida IFAS Extension soil testing ($25-$70) provides the baseline. Sulfur and iron chelate applications address pH-driven nutrient lockout.

Miami: irrigation rules and rebates

South Florida Water Management District implements year-round residential watering restrictions: two days per week odd/even-address based, no daytime watering. Miami-Dade County enforces stricter rules than some other SFWMD jurisdictions. Florida Department of Environmental Protection irrigation codes require licensed contractors for permitted installs. Florida Green Industries Best Management Practices certification is the baseline. Backflow prevention (ASSE 1013 RPZ) is mandatory. Miami-Dade Water and Sewer smart-controller rebates pay $50-$150.

Miami-Dade County Water and Sewer Department does not offer direct turf-replacement rebates the way western cities do, but the Florida Water Star new-construction certification and Miami-Dade Backyard Water Conservation workshops subsidize native-plant conversions. SFWMD conservation programs cost-share rain gardens and bioretention. Smart-controller rebates through Miami-Dade Water pay $50-$150 per device. Florida Friendly Landscapes program provides education but minimal direct rebate.

Miami: lawn and turf choices

Warm-season St Augustine (Floratam dominates, Seville, Palmetto, Captiva varieties) is the Miami residential default. Zoysia (Empire, Zeon, JaMur) is gaining on newer tracts in Kendall and Doral. Bahia grass appears on some Homestead rural-feel lots. Bermuda is used for sunny sports-use lawns. Cool-season grasses are not viable. Chinch bug (Blissus insularis) and gray leaf spot (Pyricularia grisea) drive seasonal treatment on St Augustine. Sod-webworm outbreaks spike late-summer treatments.

Invasive pests and plants throughout Miami

Burmese python populations affect Everglades-adjacent residential landscapes. Rugose spiraling whitefly (Aleurodicus rugioperculatus) is the dominant South Florida ornamental pest, defoliating gumbo limbo, mango, and black olive. Ficus whitefly and fig whitefly affect Cuban laurel and benjamina plantings. Asian citrus psyllid (Huanglongbing vector) requires quarantine compliance. Red imported fire ants are established. Laurel wilt disease (Raffaelea lauricola) kills avocado and red bay plantings.

A Miami look at HOA and design review

Miami HOA restrictions are pervasive in Doral, Kendall, Aventura, and Miami Beach master-planned communities. Florida Statute 373.185 protects Florida-friendly landscaping against HOA turf-required covenants. Coral Gables city-wide architectural review (under the Mediterranean Revival guidelines) functions as a de-facto design overlay. Miami Beach Art Deco district architectural review adds layers. Key Biscayne Village has strict setback and color-palette requirements. Gated Fisher Island, Star Island, and Indian Creek have their own private review.

A Miami look at tree services and ordinances

Miami-Dade Tree and Forest Resources Section protects specimen trees (native species over 15 feet or specific rare species). City of Miami Beach and Coral Gables have stricter private-tree ordinances. Licensed arborists through ISA charge $800-$3,500 per mature tree removal. Hurricane cleanup (Irma 2017, Ian 2022) spikes pricing 50-80 percent. Certified Florida Arborist credential (through FCHP) is the regional baseline. Palm trimming dominates typical seasonal workload; royal palm, Canary Island date, and Washingtonia are the main species.

Seasonal maintenance rhythm for Miami homeowners

Miami landscape maintenance runs year-round with peak growth March-October. Warm-season grasses green up year-round and recover quickly from chinch-bug damage with supplemental irrigation. Pre-emergent applications happen January and July. Hurricane-season staging (June-November) creates episodic workload spikes. Cold-front protection for tropical plantings during rare December-February freeze events drove many homeowners to replace frost-tender species with hardier alternatives after the 2010 and 2022 freezes.

Stormwater rules throughout Miami

South Florida Water Management District stormwater rules apply to any residential project creating impervious-surface impact; Miami-Dade County supplements with local ordinances. The Biscayne Bay Aquatic Preserve and Marine Protection Area rules add further restrictions on bayfront parcels. Permeable pavers and rain gardens qualify for Backyard Water Conservation program credits. Coastal Construction Control Line jurisdiction adds environmental-review layers on Miami Beach, Key Biscayne, and Sunny Isles parcels.

Miami, FL landscaping: neighborhood-level context

Home stock in Miami. Miami's housing stock averages 28 years old, relatively recent construction that was largely built to modern code. landscaping projects here tend to be simpler: fewer surprise compliance issues, quicker permit turnaround, and contractors who can quote from memory.

HOA & compliance. HOA oversight is the norm across Miami's residential communities. landscaping projects require advance approval of materials, colors, and sometimes contractors. Build 2-4 weeks of review time into your project schedule.

Local market conditions. The Miami contractor market is balanced: enough work to keep shops busy, but enough competition that homeowners can still get timely service. Booking 1-3 weeks ahead typically secures your preferred contractor. Miami's large market supports full-service landscape design firms, installation-only crews, and maintenance companies. For projects over $8,000, a dedicated design firm with installation capability typically provides better results than splitting design and installation across separate companies.

Seasonal timing. Miami offers a longer landscaping season than cold-climate markets. The busiest period is March through June when spring planting demand peaks. Fall (October-December) is an excellent time for plantings, hardscape projects, and irrigation system installation at lower competition and often better pricing.

Neighborhood patterns. In Miami, the highest volume of landscaping work tends to come out of Coconut Grove, Coral Gables, Brickell, and Wynwood — larger lots, older homes, and higher home values all drive replacement demand. Contractors familiar with these neighborhoods often already know the typical building stock, permit nuances, and HOA quirks.

Geography note. South Beach, Vizcaya, Wynwood Walls These geographic realities subtly shape contractor pricing and availability in Miami.

What drives cost. With Miami labor rates near the national median, the cost difference between a budget and premium full yard makeover comes down to materials and scope rather than labor premiums. Newer construction in Miami (averaging 28 years) means landscaping project projects rarely encounter the hidden-scope surprises common in older markets. What you see in the quote is usually what you pay.

Red flags to watch for. Be cautious of landscaping project contractors in Miami who pressure you to sign same-day. Legitimate contractors expect you to get competing bids and will hold their price for 30 days. High-pressure sales tactics correlate with inflated pricing. Any Miami contractor who asks for more than 30% upfront before materials are ordered is a red flag. Standard practice is 10-15% deposit, materials-on-delivery payment, and final payment on completion.
