Miami Water Utility and Supply

Miami-Dade Water and Sewer Department (WASD) draws from the Biscayne Aquifer via the Hialeah, Preston, and Alexander Orr Water Treatment Plants. Hardness tests at 180 to 250 ppm (hard). WASD serves 2.5 million customers across Miami-Dade. The Biscayne Aquifer is a shallow unconfined aquifer that is vulnerable to saltwater intrusion from the rising sea level, and the county's ongoing Water Use Plan addresses long-term supply through additional deep-well development. The South Florida Water Management District manages regional supply.

Miami hardness of 180 to 250 ppm produces aggressive scale buildup in water heaters and fixtures. Annual AO Smith or Rheem tank flushing extends lifespan by 2 to 3 years. Whole-house softeners through Kinetico or Culligan are standard additions in coastal areas. WASD Consumer Confidence Reports document source hardness, and Biscayne Aquifer source water chemistry varies slightly by wellfield depending on saltwater-intrusion monitoring. The limestone aquifer substrate drives the consistent hardness profile.

Miami Sewer Infrastructure

WASD operates the Central District Wastewater Treatment Plant on Virginia Key, the North District Plant in North Miami, and the South District Plant on the Old Cutler Ridge. The 2013 EPA Consent Decree drives $1.6 billion in infrastructure upgrades over 15 years. Sanitary and stormwater are separate, but king-tide flooding affects lateral performance in coastal zones. Coconut Grove, Brickell, and Miami Beach low-lying areas see occasional sanitary-system surcharge during extreme tides combined with heavy rain events.

Miami's stormwater discharges through the C-series canal system managed by Miami-Dade County Department of Transportation and Public Works. The 2 to 3-foot water table makes stormwater infiltration limited, and pump-assisted drainage is common in Miami Beach and southeast Miami-Dade. King-tide flooding in September and October tests the system annually. Sea-level-rise adaptation through the Miami-Dade Sea Level Rise Strategy affects new-construction plumbing design including elevated finished-floor requirements.

Miami Water Heater Culture

Miami residential water heaters are a mix of gas and electric tank units at 40 or 50 gallons. Electric tanks dominate coastal areas due to limited natural gas infrastructure. TECO Peoples Gas serves inland Miami-Dade with natural gas. Hybrid heat pump water heaters from AO Smith Voltex or Rheem ProTerra qualify for FPL on Call Savings plus federal IRA credits and perform especially well in Miami's high ambient temperatures. Solar thermal water heating qualifies for Florida Department of Agriculture Solar Thermal Rebate Program.

Freeze Risk and Winterization in Miami

Miami does not freeze. Essentially 0 freeze-thaw cycles annually. Freeze protection is not a residential plumbing concern except during rare January or February cold snaps that occasionally bring 40F overnight lows to inland areas. Most Miami homes have zero freeze protection and the 2010 cold snap produced a rare burst-pipe wave that demonstrated the vulnerability. Outdoor hose bibs and irrigation backflow preventers are the primary exposure points, and insulation wraps on exposed fittings are worthwhile for inland parcels.

Emergency vs Scheduled Plumbing in Miami

Emergency plumbing in Miami runs 2 to 2.5 times scheduled rates with peaks during hurricane-season storm events. Miami-Dade Notice of Acceptance (NOA) documentation on hurricane-zone fixtures protects homeowners.

Wells and Septic Around Miami

Some outlying Miami-Dade homes in the Redland, Homestead, and Florida City areas use septic systems regulated by Florida Department of Health Miami-Dade County Environmental Health under Chapter 64E-6 Florida Administrative Code. Private wells tested through FDOH apply in unincorporated areas. The Miami-Dade County Aquifer Recharge protection zone adds overlay rules. Septic-to-sewer conversion in the Septic Phase Out program under the 2016 ordinance covers properties in designated flood-vulnerable zones, with costs of $12,000 to $35,000 per connection.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a plumber cost in Miami?

Plumber rates in Miami typically range from $85-$150/hour. Total project costs depend on the scope — a simple drain cleaning may cost $200-$450 while a water heater replacement runs $1,500-$4,500.

Should I repair or replace my water heater?

If your water heater is over 10 years old or the repair cost exceeds 50% of replacement, replacing is usually the better investment. Tankless water heaters last 20+ years and can reduce energy costs.

Is PEX or copper better for repiping?

PEX is 30-40% cheaper than copper and faster to install. Copper lasts longer (50+ years vs 25-30 for PEX) and has higher resale value. Both are code-compliant in most areas.
