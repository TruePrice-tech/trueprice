Concrete Driveway Cost in Michigan (2026)

Concrete driveway pours in Michigan typically run $3,400–$8,800 for a 600 sq ft 4-inch concrete driveway pour at 4000 psi compressive strength. Buried footings must clear the 42-inch design frost line, and the state sits in severe freeze-thaw exposure (ACI F2). Michigan's combination of 42-inch frost depth across the Lower Peninsula, F2 (severe) freeze-thaw exposure across the entire state, and Michigan road de-icing salt exposure on every grade-level driveway edge from Michigan Department of Transportation snow operations forces 6%+ air-entrainment plus 0.40 maximum water-cement ratio on every residential pour — and Michigan's lake-effect snow loads on Detroit, Grand Rapids, Traverse City, and Marquette landscapes drive minimum 4-inch slab thickness specifications plus continuous wire mesh reinforcement on every residential driveway pour.

Michigan climate, soil & seismic drivers

  • Frost-line trench depth: 42-inch design minimum for buried footings
  • Freeze-thaw exposure (ACI 201.2R): F2 — severe freeze-thaw + de-icer exposure, 5-7% air-entrainment required
  • Typical residential mix: 4000 psi compressive strength
  • Expansive soil risk: Moderate — periodic clay shrink-swell, standard footing reinforcement adequate
  • Sulfate exposure (ACI 318): S0 — no sulfate exposure, Type I/II cement adequate
  • Seismic design category (ASCE 7-22): SDC A — no seismic design considerations
  • Winter de-icer salt exposure: Very high — heavy de-icer use drives 0.40 max w/c + epoxy-coated rebar

Michigan licensing & permits

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs — Residential Builder or Maintenance & Alteration Contractor (Concrete specialty) (official site)
  • Permit: city or county building permit required for residential driveway pour > 200 sq ft; Michigan M&A Contractor (Concrete) license required above $600 contract

How concrete driveway costs vary in Michigan

State-specific code or insurance rule: Michigan PA 299 of 1980 created the Maintenance & Alteration Contractor (Concrete) specialty license — Michigan is one of only a handful of states with concrete as its own specialty trade license rather than rolled up under general residential building, plus Michigan Public Service Commission's Detroit Lake aggregate-source restrictions under Michigan Aggregate Industries Permit Program limit certain Detroit-area aggregate sources from residential pours due to historical alkali-aggregate reaction (AAR) issues affecting pre-1990 Michigan residential foundations.
