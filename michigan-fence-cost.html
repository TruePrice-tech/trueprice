Fence Installation Cost in Michigan (2026)

Installing 150 linear feet of fencing in Michigan typically runs $4,400–$11,500, with 4-to-6-foot galvanized chain-link the dominant residential choice. Concrete-set posts must clear the 42-inch design frost line. Michigan's 42-inch state-average frost line reaches 60+ inches in the Upper Peninsula's Houghton and Marquette counties — driving mandatory 4-to-6-foot post-hole depths and concrete-anchored installations on northern residential installs — and Lake Michigan and Lake Superior shoreline winter freeze-thaw cycling on chain-link mesh drives PVC-coated and vinyl-clad alternatives over standard galvanized.

Michigan climate & post-design drivers

  • Frost-line trench depth: 42-inch design minimum for post footings
  • Dominant residential fence material: Galvanized chain-link — dominant in rural and utility-yard applications
  • Hurricane wind tier: Moderate — 110–140 mph design wind drives 8-foot post spacing
  • Termite pressure (TPCT zone): Low — minimal termite pressure
  • HOA fencing-rule prevalence: Moderate — suburban HOA prevalence on newer subdivisions

Michigan licensing, HOA & boundary law

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs — Residential Builder License (official site)
  • Permit: state or municipal permit typical for residential fence over 6 feet

How fence installation costs vary in Michigan

State-specific code or insurance rule: Michigan Public Act 299 of 1980 (Occupational Code Article 24) requires every Residential Builder and Residential Maintenance & Alteration Contractor to hold a Michigan LARA-issued license for any residential project exceeding $600 — making Michigan one of the lowest-threshold mandatory-licensing states for fencing work — and the Michigan Compiled Laws §43.51 (Partition Fence Act) requires adjoining landowners to share equally in the cost of constructing and maintaining a partition fence in unincorporated townships under 'fence-in' livestock-containment rules.
