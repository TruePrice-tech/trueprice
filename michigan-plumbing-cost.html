Plumbing & Repipe Cost in Michigan (2026)

Plumbing repipe in Michigan typically runs $5,600–$14,000 for a whole-house 2-bath PEX repipe, with Type-L copper the dominant retrofit choice and the International Plumbing Code (IPC) as the adopted enforcement basis. Buried service-line trench depth must clear the 42-inch design frost line. Flint, Detroit, Benton Harbor, and Hamtramck operate under enforceable LSL replacement timelines stemming from the 2014-2019 Flint water crisis; LARA-licensed plumbers are required to use LSL-compliant fittings and conduct post-replacement flushing protocols, and Upper Peninsula properties on glacial-aquifer wells face naturally occurring iron and manganese loads exceeding 1 ppm secondary MCLs throughout the Keweenaw and Marquette ranges.

Michigan water-quality & freeze drivers

  • IECC climate zone: 5A-6A-7 — drives freeze-protection scope on supply lines
  • Frost-line trench depth: 42-inch design minimum for buried water service
  • Water hardness profile: Very hard (10.5+ grains/gallon) — whole-house softener standard on most repipes
  • Dominant retrofit supply material: Type-L copper — dominant on retrofits in high-soft-water and pre-1990 housing stock
  • Adopted plumbing code basis: International Plumbing Code (IPC) — ICC model adopted statewide
  • Lead service line risk tier: High — pre-1986 lead service line corridor under active LCRR replacement

Michigan licensing & permits

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) — Plumbing Board (Master / Journey) (official site)
  • Permit: city or LARA plumbing permit required; LSL replacement programs in Flint, Detroit, Benton Harbor

How plumbing and repipe costs vary in Michigan

State-specific code or insurance rule: Michigan's Lead and Copper Rule revisions (Public Act 273 of 2018) impose the most aggressive LSL replacement timeline in the nation — every community water supply must replace all lead service lines within 20 years (by 2041) regardless of cost or homeowner participation, and partial LSL replacements (utility side only) are explicitly prohibited as a public-health matter following the Flint experience.
