Plumbing & Repipe Cost in Missouri (2026)

Plumbing repipe in Missouri typically runs $4,900–$12,400 for a whole-house 2-bath PEX repipe, with PEX-A or PEX-B cross-linked polyethylene the dominant retrofit choice and the International Plumbing Code (IPC) as the adopted enforcement basis. Buried service-line trench depth must clear the 30-inch design frost line. The Ozark Plateau across southern Missouri from Springfield through Branson and into the Mark Twain National Forest sits atop karst limestone with sinkhole and losing-stream behavior — private wells require deeper casing depths to clear surface-derived bacteria, and historical Old Lead Belt mining in St. Francois County leaves residual soil-lead contamination affecting service-line replacement excavation protocols.

Missouri water-quality & freeze drivers

  • IECC climate zone: 4A-5A — drives freeze-protection scope on supply lines
  • Frost-line trench depth: 30-inch design minimum for buried water service
  • Water hardness profile: Hard (7–10.5 grains/gallon) — softener cost-effective on tankless and water-heater retrofits
  • Dominant retrofit supply material: PEX-A or PEX-B cross-linked polyethylene — dominant on whole-house repipes since 2005
  • Adopted plumbing code basis: International Plumbing Code (IPC) — ICC model adopted statewide
  • Lead service line risk tier: Moderate — partial pre-1986 service line inventory under utility review

Missouri licensing & permits

  • License status: No statewide license — municipal credentials only
  • License board: No statewide plumbing license — Kansas City, St. Louis, Springfield, Columbia each license Master/Journey separately (official site)
  • Permit: municipal plumbing permit required; on-site wastewater through Mo Dept of Health & Senior Services in unincorporated areas

How plumbing and repipe costs vary in Missouri

The Ozark Plateau across southern Missouri from Springfield through Branson and into the Mark Twain National Forest sits atop karst limestone with sinkhole and losing-stream behavior — private wells require deeper casing depths to clear surface-derived bacteria, and historical Old Lead Belt mining in St. Francois County leaves residual soil-lead contamination affecting service-line replacement excavation protocols.

State-specific code or insurance rule: Missouri has no statewide plumbing licensing statute — RSMo 327 covers architects and engineers but explicitly omits plumbers, leaving Kansas City, St. Louis, Springfield, Columbia, Independence, and Jefferson City to operate independent Master Plumber boards with no formal reciprocity, and the Missouri Department of Health & Senior Services regulates only the on-site wastewater portion of residential plumbing rather than supply-side work.
