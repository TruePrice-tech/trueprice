Frequently asked fencing questions

What is the cheapest fence material?

Chain link is the cheapest at $10-$20 per linear foot installed. Pressure-treated pine is the cheapest wood option at $15-$25/ft. Vinyl and composite start at $20-$30/ft but never need painting.

What is the best fence for privacy?

A 6-foot solid board (dog-ear, board-on-board, or shadowbox) fence provides the most privacy. Board-on-board is best since it has no gaps even when boards shrink. Vinyl and composite also offer full privacy panels.

How much does a new fence cost in Moline?

Fence costs in Moline range from $15-$35 per linear foot for wood, $20-$40 for vinyl, $15-$30 for chain link, and $25-$55 for aluminum or wrought iron, including materials and installation.

Should fence posts be set in concrete?

Yes, for most fences. Concrete-set posts are more stable and resist leaning. Some installers use compacted gravel for better drainage in wet climates, which can extend post life by reducing ground-level rot.

How deep should fence posts be in Moline?

Post depth is the most critical installation detail in Moline. The frost line here requires a minimum of 36-48 inches deep, extending at least 6 inches below frost depth. Proper technique: 8-10 inches of gravel base for drainage, concrete fill to grade. Posts set shallower than the frost line will heave and lean regardless of material quality or how well the fence itself is built.

Do I need a permit to build a fence in Moline?

Most Moline jurisdictions require permits for fences, especially those over 6 feet. Check with your local building department before starting. You must call 811 for utility locates before digging, verify property lines (consider a survey if boundaries are unclear), and check any HOA rules. Your contractor should handle permitting as part of the project.

Local Fencing Guide for Moline, IL

Moline's winters put long-term pressure on anything set in the ground. Fence posts are no exception: shallow footings heave, undersized concrete collars crack, and wood in direct ground contact rots from the bottom up. Proper depth and drainage are where lifespan is won or lost here.

Some Moline neighborhoods have HOA fence restrictions. If your property is in a managed community, check the CC&Rs for height limits, material restrictions, and color requirements before committing to a design. Even without an HOA, most Moline zoning codes restrict front-yard fence height to 3-4 feet.

Many Moline properties (average home age 44 years) have aging or deteriorated fences that predate current building codes. Replacement often requires updated post depth, property line verification, and sometimes a survey to confirm boundaries before new fence construction begins. Budget $300-$600 for a property survey if boundary markers are not clearly visible.
