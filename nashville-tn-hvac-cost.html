What SEER and AFUE to Aim For in Nashville

Cooling efficiency target

16+ SEER

16 SEER is the sensible Nashville minimum because heating, not cooling, dominates your Nashville Electric Service (NES) bill. The 14-to-16 SEER jump delivers modest savings here; the 16-to-20 jump delivers almost none. Put the efficiency budget toward the furnace or a cold-climate heat pump instead.

Heating efficiency target

92%+ AFUE

Nashville's heating season is meaningful but not extreme, so 90-92% AFUE is the sensible sweet spot. If you're replacing an 80% AFUE unit in an older neighborhood home, the jump to 92% saves $200-$400 per year at Nashville Electric Service (NES)'s current gas rate; the marginal step to 96% takes meaningfully longer to pay back.

Nashville's 2026 minimum efficiency rules. Federal floor for TN is 15 SEER2 (roughly 15.2 legacy SEER) under the DOE southern-region rule — any Nashville bid under that is illegal for new residential install. Any "14 SEER" number on a Nashville 2026 bid is either left-over old inventory or a labeling mix-up between SEER2 and legacy SEER; the AHRI listing at ahridirectory.org is the authoritative source.

Nashville rebate math and TN incentives. Nashville Electric Service offers rebates of $300-$1,500 for qualifying high-efficiency heat pumps and furnaces. Federal 25C tax credits (up to $2,000 for heat pumps) stack on top of utility rebates.. The 25C heat-pump credit sunset December 31, 2025 and does not apply to 2026 Nashville projects. Two IRA programs still apply in TN: HEAR (up to $8,000 for income-qualified households) and HOMES ($4,000-$8,000 performance-based). TN's energy office runs the programs; the installer captures the incentive on the invoice rather than you on your return.
