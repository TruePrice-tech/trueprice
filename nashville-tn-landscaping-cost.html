Nashville landscaping FAQ

Should I get multiple quotes?

Always get at least 3 written quotes. Compare line-item details, not just total price. The lowest quote often omits scope items that appear as change orders later.

How do I find a good landscaping contractor in Nashville?

Check licensing through your state's contractor board, verify insurance, read reviews from Nashville homeowners, and get at least 3 written quotes with line-item detail.

How much should I pay upfront?

Never pay more than 10-15% as a deposit before work begins. Standard payment structure: deposit, progress payment when materials arrive, final payment on completion.

What should I look for in a contractor quote?

A professional quote breaks out labor, materials, permits, and cleanup separately. It includes a timeline, payment schedule, warranty terms, and the contractor's license number.

Do I need a permit for landscaping in Nashville?

Most landscaping projects in Nashville require a permit. Your contractor should pull the permit and schedule the inspection. Never pull permits in your own name.

Nashville Soil Conditions and Amendments

Nashville Basin soil is Ordovician limestone-derived phosphatic clay with pH 7.0-7.8 and naturally high phosphorus content that benefits many ornamentals without additional fertilizer. The limestone substrate sits 6-24 inches below the surface across much of East Nashville and 12South, which constrains root depth and makes excavation expensive. Green Hills and Belle Meade lots have deeper topsoil over the limestone. Franklin (Williamson County) sits on the Harpeth River alluvium with better drainage. UT Extension soil testing ($12-$30) is the standard. The Nashville Basin limestone makes any landscape grading or tree-planting excavation 2-3x more expensive than clay-soil markets because specialized equipment is needed to break through the rock.

Nashville Lawn and Turf Choices

Nashville sits in the transition zone between warm and cool-season turf, making lawn-grass selection the most complex of any metro in this group. Bermuda (Tifway 419, Celebration) dominates sunny lots; tall fescue (Rebel, Millennium) handles shaded East Nashville and Green Hills yards. Zoysia (Empire, JaMur, Zeon) is gaining as a compromise species that tolerates Nashville's 40 freeze-thaw cycles better than pure Bermuda while requiring less water than fescue. The March 2020 tornado destroyed turf across East Nashville; most rebuilds switched from fescue to Bermuda or Zoysia to reduce long-term maintenance.
