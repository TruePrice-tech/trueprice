New Brunswick solar: answers to common questions

Should I buy or lease solar panels?

Buying maximizes long-term savings and increases home value. Leasing eliminates upfront cost but the leasing company keeps the tax credit, and panels may complicate home sales.

Does New Brunswick have any state or local solar incentives?

Incentives vary. Check the DSIRE database for New Brunswick-specific rebates, SRECs, property tax exemptions, and performance-based incentives that stack on top of the federal 30% ITC.

How does the federal solar tax credit work?

The Investment Tax Credit (ITC) lets you deduct 30% of your total solar installation cost from your federal taxes. It applies to purchased systems, not leased. There is no cap on the credit amount.

How many panels do I need?

Panel count depends on your electricity usage, panel wattage, and roof sun exposure. A typical home uses 20-30 panels (400W each) to offset 80-100% of electricity consumption.

How much does solar cost in New Brunswick?

Most homeowners in New Brunswick pay $15,000-$30,000 before incentives for a 6-10kW system. After the 30% federal tax credit, net cost drops to $10,500-$21,000.

Do I need a permit for solar installation in New Brunswick?

Most solar installation projects in New Brunswick require a permit. Your contractor should pull the permit and schedule the inspection. Never pull permits in your own name.

Red flags include: no line-item detail, same-day signing pressure, request for more than 30% upfront, no license or insurance verification, and verbal-only warranties.

When is the best time to schedule this work in New Brunswick?

Off-peak seasons offer better pricing and availability. In New Brunswick, scheduling during the shoulder season saves 10-20% versus peak-demand months.

How long does the project typically take?

Timeline depends on scope and complexity. Simple projects take 1-3 days; larger jobs can run 1-4 weeks. Weather, permits, and material delivery affect scheduling.

What New Brunswick, NJ homeowners should know about solar installation

Home stock in New Brunswick. The typical New Brunswick home is around 57 years old. Most systems are past their first-replacement cycle and approaching their second. Expect solar panel installation quotes to include mentions of upgraded electrical service, ductwork adjustments, or insulation retrofits that newer homes wouldn't need.

HOA & compliance. HOA prevalence in New Brunswick is moderate. If you're in a managed community, check covenants before signing a solar panel installation contract — material color, visible fixture type, or warranty requirements may affect your selection.

Local market conditions. New Brunswick has moderate construction activity. Contractors are busy but accessible with 1-3 weeks' notice. Off-season quotes (contractor-dependent) can run 8-15% below peak rates. New Brunswick's stable market means solar installer competition is moderate. Established local companies often provide better post-install service than national chains that cycle through markets based on incentive availability.

Seasonal timing. Permitting and utility interconnection in New Brunswick typically add 4-8 weeks after installation before your system goes live. Factor this lag into your timeline — a system installed in March may not start producing credits until May.

What drives cost. Labor is the dominant cost driver for solar installation in New Brunswick — local wages run 32% above the national average, which adds 16% or more to a typical residential solar system. The 57-year average home age in New Brunswick means most solar installation projects encounter at least one behind-the-wall surprise. Experienced local contractors price this risk in; lowball bids from out-of-area contractors often don't.

Red flags to watch for. Be cautious of solar installation contractors in New Brunswick who pressure you to sign same-day. Legitimate contractors expect you to get competing bids and will hold their price for 30 days. High-pressure sales tactics correlate with inflated pricing. Any New Brunswick contractor who asks for more than 30% upfront before materials are ordered is a red flag. Standard practice is 10-15% deposit, materials-on-delivery payment, and final payment on completion. For older New Brunswick homes (average 57 years), beware of solar installation quotes that don't mention code compliance. Modern codes have changed significantly since these homes were built — any work that triggers inspection should be priced with code upgrades included.
