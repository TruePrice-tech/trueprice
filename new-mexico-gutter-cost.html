Gutter Installation Cost in New Mexico (2026)

Whole-house gutter installation in New Mexico typically runs $1,000–$3,800, with 5-inch K-style seamless aluminum the dominant residential profile and roughly 14 inches of annual precipitation driving sizing. New Mexico's 14-inch annual rainfall is concentrated in the July-September North American Monsoon with intense afternoon thunderstorm cells that overwhelm unguttered residential eaves — Albuquerque, Santa Fe, and Las Cruces retrofit demand is driven by monsoon-driven foundation undermining and stucco staining on south- and west-facing eaves that bear the highest monsoon flow.

New Mexico rainfall & drainage drivers

  • Annual rainfall: 14 inches/yr — drives K-style sizing and downspout count
  • Dominant residential gutter profile: 5-inch K-style seamless aluminum — dominant low-rainfall retrofit profile
  • Ground snow load: 15 psf — drives gutter bracket spacing and ice-shield requirements
  • Ice-dam risk tier: Moderate — periodic ice-dam events on north-facing eaves
  • Hurricane wind tier: None
  • Tree-canopy density: Low — sparse canopy, standard open gutters adequate

New Mexico licensing & permits

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: New Mexico Construction Industries Division — GB-2 (General Building) (official site)
  • Permit: state or municipal building permit typical for gutter installation on new construction

How gutter installation costs vary in New Mexico

New Mexico's 14-inch annual rainfall is concentrated in the July-September North American Monsoon with intense afternoon thunderstorm cells that overwhelm unguttered residential eaves — Albuquerque, Santa Fe, and Las Cruces retrofit demand is driven by monsoon-driven foundation undermining and stucco staining on south- and west-facing eaves that bear the highest monsoon flow.

State-specific code or insurance rule: New Mexico Construction Industries Division issues GB-98 (Residential), GB-2 (General Building), and ES-3 (Roofing & Cladding) as separately tested classifications — and New Mexico is one of the few states where adobe and pueblo-style mud-plaster construction has its own residential code overlay, with the CID enforcing traditional canale (carved wooden roof drain) profiles in Santa Fe and Taos historic-district overlays alongside modern aluminum K-style systems.
