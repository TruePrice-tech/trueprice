Siding Replacement Cost in New Mexico (2026)

Re-siding a 1,500 sq ft home in New Mexico typically runs $9,000–$24,500, with three-coat hard-coat stucco the dominant residential cladding choice. High-desert solar irradiance across the Albuquerque-to-Las Cruces corridor at 5,000-7,000 feet elevation drives stucco's dominance over vinyl (which UV-degrades faster at altitude) and cedar (which checks and cups under intense solar exposure) — and northern New Mexico WUI overlay across Santa Fe, Los Alamos, and Sandoval counties has driven Class A ignition-resistant cladding adoption since the 2011 Las Conchas and 2022 Hermits Peak fires.

New Mexico climate & cladding drivers

  • IECC climate zone: 3B-4B-5B
  • Dominant residential cladding: Three-coat hard-coat stucco — dominant in arid and Mediterranean Sun Belt
  • Hurricane wind tier: Low wind exposure — sub-110 mph design wind

  • Salt-air corrosion zone: No salt-air corrosion exposure (interior continental)

New Mexico licensing & permits

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: New Mexico Construction Industries Division — GB-2 (General Building) or GB-98 (Residential) (official site)
  • Permit: state or municipal building permit required for full re-side

How siding replacement costs vary in New Mexico

High-desert solar irradiance across the Albuquerque-to-Las Cruces corridor at 5,000-7,000 feet elevation drives stucco's dominance over vinyl (which UV-degrades faster at altitude) and cedar (which checks and cups under intense solar exposure) — and northern New Mexico WUI overlay across Santa Fe, Los Alamos, and Sandoval counties has driven Class A ignition-resistant cladding adoption since the 2011 Las Conchas and 2022 Hermits Peak fires.

State-specific code or insurance rule: New Mexico Construction Industries Division issues GB-98 (Residential), GB-2 (General Building), and ES-3 (Roofing & Cladding) as separately tested classifications — and New Mexico is one of the few states where adobe and pueblo-style mud-plaster construction has its own residential code overlay, with the CID enforcing traditional hand-applied earthen plaster requirements on Santa Fe and Taos historic-district overlays alongside modern three-coat stucco standards.
