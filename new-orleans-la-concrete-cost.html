New Orleans concrete FAQ

How much does a concrete foundation cost?

A full basement foundation costs $15,000-$30,000. A slab foundation for a house costs $5,000-$15,000. A garage slab costs $2,500-$6,000. Price depends on size, depth, and soil conditions.

How long before I can drive on new concrete?

Wait 24-48 hours for foot traffic, 7 days for vehicle traffic, and 30 days for full cure. Avoid deicing salt for the first winter. New concrete reaches 90% strength in 7 days and full strength in 28 days.

What is the best time to pour concrete?

Pour when temperatures stay between 50-80F for at least 3 days. Spring and fall are ideal. Avoid pouring in rain, direct hot sun, or below freezing. Cold and heat both compromise cure quality.

How thick should a concrete driveway be?

Residential driveways should be 4 inches thick for cars and 5-6 inches for heavy vehicles like RVs. A 6-inch compacted gravel base underneath is essential. Thickened edges (6 inches) prevent cracking at the perimeter.

Is concrete or asphalt better for a driveway?

Concrete lasts 30+ years vs. 15-20 for asphalt. Concrete costs more upfront ($6-$12/sqft vs. $3-$6/sqft) but needs less maintenance. Asphalt needs resealing every 3-5 years and is softer in extreme heat.

concrete work in New Orleans, LA: local context

What drives concrete work cost in New Orleans. Trade wages in New Orleans run close to the national median. Price differences from one contractor to another here are driven more by overhead, efficiency, and material choices than by labor cost. New Orleans's concrete work costs are essentially average for the US market in our analysis.

Site-specific factors around New Orleans. New Orleans has local climate conditions that affect project scheduling and material choices. Concrete in New Orleans cures differently than in dry climates — high humidity slows evaporation, which actually improves curing strength but extends the timeline. The bigger concern is expansive clay soils common in this region, which cause heave and cracking. Proper sub-base preparation (4-6 inches of compacted gravel) is non-negotiable. Older properties in New Orleans (averaging 50 years) often have settling concrete that affects drainage. Re-pouring may require sub-grade correction (compaction, drainage tile) that new construction doesn't need. Budget 10-20% extra for site preparation on established properties.

Regulatory side of concrete work in New Orleans. Expect a permit requirement for concrete work work in New Orleans; check with the local building department before signing any contract.

Contractor market in New Orleans. At 378,715 people, New Orleans has enough trade professionals to give homeowners real choices. Three competitive quotes is the minimum for any project over $1,000. New Orleans has seen steady growth, which means a healthy supply of established contractors with multi-year track records.

Local factors: concrete work in New Orleans, LA

What local homes look like in New Orleans. With a median home age near 50 years, New Orleans straddles the line between mid-century and modern construction. concrete work contractors frequently encounter a mix of original and previously updated systems, which can complicate scope assessment. Get a thorough pre-work inspection.

Compliance and HOA notes. Few New Orleans properties fall under HOA governance, which simplifies concrete work project planning. You deal only with city building codes and permits, not architectural committees.

Local trade economics. New Orleans has moderate construction activity. Contractors are busy but accessible with 1-3 weeks' notice. Off-season quotes can run 8-15% below peak rates. New Orleans's large market has concrete contractors ranging from 1-person decorative specialists to large commercial outfits that also do residential. For standard flatwork (driveways, patios), mid-size companies with dedicated residential crews typically offer the best combination of pricing and quality.

Seasonal timing. New Orleans allows year-round concrete work, but the ideal window is spring and fall when temperatures support proper curing without the heat-related challenges of summer. Avoid scheduling large pours during the hottest months — early morning pours are the only reliable option.

By neighborhood. In New Orleans, the highest volume of concrete work work tends to come out of French Quarter, Garden District, Marigny, and Bywater — larger lots, older homes, and higher home values all drive replacement demand. Contractors familiar with these neighborhoods often already know the typical building stock, permit nuances, and HOA quirks.

Local geography. French Quarter, Jackson Square, City Park high water table and historic district restrictions make New Orleans uniquely complex; many projects require both city permits and HDLC approval These geographic realities subtly shape contractor pricing and availability in New Orleans.

What drives cost. Labor costs in New Orleans track within a few points of the national average for concrete work, so material selection and project scope are the bigger pricing levers for homeowners. The 50-year average home age in New Orleans means most concrete work projects encounter at least one behind-the-wall surprise. Experienced local contractors price this risk in; lowball bids from out-of-area contractors often don't.

Red flags to watch for. Any New Orleans contractor who asks for more than 30% upfront before materials are ordered is a red flag. Standard practice is 10-15% deposit, materials-on-delivery payment, and final payment on completion. Watch for concrete work quotes in New Orleans that lack line-item detail. A professional estimate breaks out labor, materials, permits, and cleanup separately. Lump-sum bids hide margin and make change orders impossible to evaluate. For older New Orleans homes (average 50 years), beware of concrete work quotes that don't mention code compliance. Modern codes have changed significantly since these homes were built — any work that triggers inspection should be priced with code upgrades included.
