New York Efficiency Targets: SEER and AFUE

Cooling efficiency target

16+ SEER

16 SEER is the sensible New York minimum because heating, not cooling, dominates your Con Edison bill. The 14-to-16 SEER jump delivers modest savings here; the 16-to-20 jump delivers almost none. Put the efficiency budget toward the furnace or a cold-climate heat pump instead.

Heating efficiency target

95%+ AFUE

New York's heavy heating load makes 95-96% AFUE the New York minimum on any new gas furnace. At Con Edison's $1.85/therm rate, the 80-to-96% AFUE jump saves $225-$420 annually in a typical New York home. Condensing furnaces vent through 2-3 inch PVC rather than the old metal flue, which is a plus if your intown home already has a compromised brick chimney but adds cost on a first-time conversion.

What's the SEER2 floor in New York? Federal floor for NY is 14 SEER2 under the DOE northern-region rule, but most New York homeowners should aim well above that because the incremental cost of higher SEER2 is modest. Any "14 SEER" number on a New York 2026 bid is either left-over old inventory or a labeling mix-up between SEER2 and legacy SEER; the AHRI listing at ahridirectory.org is the authoritative source.

Rebates available to New York homeowners. NYSERDA Clean Heat provides $2,000-$4,500 per ton for cold-climate heat pumps in Con Edison territory; rebates are administered by the installer and deducted from the invoice. 25C heat-pump credits ended with 2025 and have no effect on 2026 New York project taxes. IRA HEAR (up to $8,000 for income-qualified NY households) and IRA HOMES (up to $4,000-$8,000 performance-based) continue — administered through NY's designated energy office, applied by the installer, not via the homeowner's tax return. Ground-loop geothermal is a separate path in New York: 30% Section 25D credit, uncapped, runs through 2034.

New York HVAC Permitting and Inspections

Permit authority: NYC Department of Buildings (DOB). DOB Work Permit required for ductwork and gas; self-certified by licensed Master Plumber for the gas tie-in. NYC DOB also enforces the Local Law 97 emissions cap for larger buildings, which is nudging Manhattan co-ops toward VRF conversions.

New York-specific coverage. NYC Local Law 97 emissions caps on large residential buildings are pushing Manhattan co-op boards to favor heat-pump VRF conversions over legacy steam, which affects bid structure for anyone in a building over 25,000 square feet. A New York mechanical permit formally signs off on Manual J load calc, service electrical, gas pipe sizing if gas is involved, refrigerant line routing, condensate discharge, and NY's required duct-leakage testing. On top of that baseline, NYC Department of Buildings reviews condensate routing, combustion-air make-up, and line-set insulation as standard NY checklist items. NYC Department of Buildings also checks steam-to-gas boiler conversions in pre-war brownstones and ductless Mitsubishi mini-splits in Queens row houses are the dominant upgrade paths-specific details a generic Sun Belt tract-home inspector wouldn't recognize.

That New York permit fee is cheap insurance compared to what skipping it costs. Working without the NYC Department of Buildings permit nulls Carrier warranty rights, produces a resale-inspection flag, and transfers install liability from the contractor to the homeowner: a botched New York furnace install can vent CO through a cracked heat exchanger or undersized flue, and NYC Department of Buildings is the last real check before that goes live in your living room. A New York contractor's "we'll skip the permit" pitch mostly saves them from NYC Department of Buildings scrutiny, not you anything meaningful. Under NY law, permit liability stays with the contractor — avoid any pitch to pull the permit yourself as an owner-builder.

A2L changes how New York shops handle refrigerant. New York homeowners getting 2026 installs will see either R-454B (Honeywell) or R-32 (Daikin/Mitsubishi) on the nameplate, full stop. Pre-2025 R-410A stock in NY still exists but is on its way out through Carrier and similar distributor channels. Because A2L is class 2L (mildly flammable) and R-410A was class 1 (non-flammable), UL 60335-2-40 adds install requirements. The new requirements: charge-to-room-volume minimums, leak-detection sensors on heads above certain tonnages, and A2L-specific brazing procedures. NYC DOB does not equate to A2L certification; the New York shop you hire should produce A2L training documentation independently.

What to verify on any New York bid. New York install quality matters more than the nameplate. A premium Carrier unit installed sloppily loses to a mid-tier unit installed right in any New York home. Five craft items to insist on in New York: a Manual J tied to Con Edison's climate data, refrigerant charge dialed by live superheat/subcooling readings rather than weight, mastic-paste duct sealing (not tape — it fails in moderate conditions within three years), New York's heating-dominant load profile calls for 400 CFM per ton, adjusted for long heat-call duty cycles, and a signed commissioning sheet before you release final payment. Every New York bidder should walk you through all five; anyone who dismisses the question is telling you something.
