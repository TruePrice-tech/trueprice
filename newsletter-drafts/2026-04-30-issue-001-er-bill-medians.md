# Real Cost Briefing — Issue 001

**Welcome. You're reading the first issue of Real Cost Briefing.**

Today: emergency room bills.

---

## The headline number

A standard Level 3 emergency room visit (CPT code **99283** — described by CMS as "the most common ER visit") has a Medicare reimbursement rate of **$150**.

The same visit, billed to commercial insurance, typically lands between **$500 and $1,500**.

The same visit, when you're uninsured or the hospital bills you out-of-network, can hit **$2,500 to $5,000** before insurance adjustments.

That's a 17x to 33x spread on the same five-digit code, for the same 30 minutes of physician time, in the same building.

The spread isn't fraud. It's how American healthcare pricing works. But almost nobody explains it before you sign that consent form at intake.

---

## What the five ER levels actually mean

The five common ER CPT codes (99281 through 99285) are how the hospital tells the insurer how complex your visit was. Higher level = higher charge. Here's the spread, using Medicare rates as the floor and typical commercial pricing as the ceiling:

| CPT | Description | Medicare rate | Commercial range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 99281 | ER visit (L1) | $50 | $150–500 | Minor: simple suture, prescription |
| 99282 | ER visit (L2) | $95 | $300–900 | Low-moderate: sprain, mild infection |
| 99283 | ER visit (L3) | $150 | $500–1,500 | Most common: pain, fever, vomiting |
| 99284 | ER visit (L4) | $250 | $800–2,500 | Urgent: chest pain workup, fracture |
| 99285 | ER visit (L5) | $400 | $1,500–4,000 | Critical care evaluation |

Source: CMS Medicare Physician Fee Schedule 2026, RAND Hospital Pricing Index 2024, Woogoro pricing flywheel (50+ real ER bills).

**The thing the hospital won't tell you:** ER level coding has been the single most-disputed category in medical billing for the last decade. A 2018 RAND study found ~25% of ER bills are coded one level higher than the documentation justifies. That's "upcoding" and it's worth disputing.

---

## How to know if you got upcoded

If your bill shows **99284 or 99285** but the visit involved any of these, you have grounds to push back:
- You were in and out in under 30 minutes
- No imaging beyond a single X-ray
- No IV medication
- No specialist consult
- Discharge without admission within 2 hours

The discharge summary you got on paper should describe the level of complexity. If it says "fast-track" or "low-acuity" but the bill says L4, that's a coding mismatch you can dispute by writing to the billing department: **"I am requesting a coding review based on the discharge documentation."**

That single sentence, sent in writing, gets the bill re-reviewed in roughly 40% of cases according to the National Patient Advocate Foundation.

## What to do this week

If you have an ER bill from the last 12 months sitting in a drawer:

1. **Find the CPT code.** It's usually in the line items, 5 digits starting with 99.
2. **Look up the Medicare rate** for your state at [woogoro.com/medical-bill-analyzer](https://woogoro.com/medical-bill-analyzer) — paste the bill, get a fair-price benchmark in 10 seconds.
3. **If the charged amount is more than 3x Medicare,** that's a flag worth disputing. Write the billing department, request an itemized bill, ask for a coding review.

Patients who dispute ER bills successfully reduce the amount owed in roughly **half** of cases. The hospital expects this. The billing department has discretion. It costs you a stamp.
