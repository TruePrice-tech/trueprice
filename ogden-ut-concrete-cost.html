How much does a concrete patio cost?

A 300 sqft concrete patio costs $1,800-$3,600 for basic broom finish. Stamped patterns add $3-$8/sqft. Colored concrete adds $1-$3/sqft. An exposed aggregate finish adds $2-$4/sqft.

How much does a concrete driveway cost?

A standard 600 sqft concrete driveway costs $3,600-$7,200 for plain finished concrete. Stamped or colored concrete adds $3-$10/sqft. Removing the old driveway adds $1,000-$2,000.

What Ogden homeowners should know about concrete work

Why pricing varies in Ogden. Labor rates here track within a few points of the national average, so most of the price variation comes from material costs and local market dynamics rather than wage differentials. Our cost model shows concrete work in Ogden tracking right at the national baseline.

Ogden's site and climate factors. Ogden has local climate conditions that affect project scheduling and material choices. Air-entrained concrete mix (5-7% air content) is mandatory in Ogden for freeze-thaw resistance. Standard mixes without air entrainment scale and spall within 2-3 winters. Road salt tracking from driveways accelerates surface degradation — apply concrete sealer annually to driveways and walkways exposed to de-icing chemicals. Standard concrete in Ogden runs $6-10 per square foot for basic flatwork (driveways, walkways). Decorative options (stamped, colored, exposed aggregate) add $4-8 per square foot. The biggest hidden cost is demolition and removal of existing concrete — budget $2-4 per square foot for tearout of old slabs.

Ogden paperwork, inspections, and licensing. Most concrete work projects in Ogden require a permit; confirm with the local building department before signing.

Who's doing the work in Ogden. With 84,249 people, Ogden's contractor pool is mid-sized. You will find 4-6 established shops for most trades, which provides enough competition for fair pricing. Ogden's measured growth has built a reliable contractor ecosystem. Lead times are manageable and pricing competition is healthy.

Local factors: concrete work in Ogden, UT

Ogden's housing profile. Ogden homes are about 30 years old on average, young enough that most original systems still have useful life remaining. concrete work demand here centers on upgrades, improvements, and the occasional early failure rather than wholesale replacement.

HOA and neighborhood rules. HOA prevalence in Ogden is moderate. If you live in a managed community, check covenants before signing a concrete work contract. Material color, visible fixture type, or warranty requirements may affect your selection.

Contractor market picture. Ogden's moderate growth keeps the contractor pipeline moving without the extreme backlogs seen in booming metros. Off-peak scheduling can yield 8-15% savings over peak-season rates. In Ogden (84,249 residents), concrete work is often done by small local companies or general contractors. For decorative work (stamped, colored), verify the contractor has a portfolio of completed projects in your specific pattern and color — decorative concrete expertise varies widely.

Timing considerations. The concrete season in Ogden runs May through October when overnight temps stay above 40°F consistently. Spring is peak demand as homeowners address winter frost damage. Fall pours (September-October) offer better contractor availability and fewer weather delays.

What drives cost. Labor costs in Ogden track within a few points of the national average for concrete work, so material selection and project scope are the bigger pricing levers for homeowners. Ogden's relatively young housing stock (30 years average) simplifies most concrete work projects. Modern code compliance, standard dimensions, and accessible construction reduce both time and cost versus older homes.

Red flags to watch for. Check that any Ogden contractor doing concrete work carries both general liability insurance ($1M minimum) and workers' compensation. Request certificates directly from the insurer, not just copies the contractor provides. In Ogden, verify your concrete work contractor pulls the permit themselves — never pull it in your own name.
