Frequently asked plumbing questions

Can I do plumbing repairs myself?

Simple tasks like replacing a faucet cartridge or fixing a running toilet are DIY-friendly. Anything involving supply lines, gas connections, sewer work, or permits should be left to a licensed plumber.

Should I repair or replace old pipes?

If your home has galvanized steel or polybutylene pipes, full replacement is usually worthwhile. Copper or PEX in good condition can be repaired locally. A camera inspection reveals the true condition.

What is the average cost of a bathroom remodel plumbing rough-in in Orange?

A bathroom plumbing rough-in in Orange typically costs $1,500-$4,000 for new supply and drain lines. Moving fixtures from their original locations adds $500-$1,500 per fixture.

What causes low water pressure?

Common causes include corroded galvanized pipes, a partially closed main valve, mineral buildup in fixtures, a failing pressure regulator, or a hidden leak in the supply line.

Why does my water heater make popping noises?

Popping or rumbling sounds come from sediment buildup at the bottom of the tank. Flushing the tank annually prevents this and extends the heater's lifespan by 2-3 years.

Does Orange's hard water damage plumbing?

It does. The mineral content in Orange's water supply leaves calcium and magnesium deposits inside pipes, tanks, and fixtures. Water heaters lose 20-30% efficiency as scale builds on heating elements. Fixture aerators clog and valves stick. A whole-house softener ($1,500-$3,000 installed) prevents the buildup. Descale your water heater yearly to extend its life by 3-5 years.

What should a plumbing quote include in Orange?

A professional plumbing quote in Orange should itemize: labor hours, material costs and brands, permit fees if applicable, cleanup, and warranty terms. The quote should include the plumber's license number and proof of insurance. Get three written quotes for any job over $500 to ensure fair pricing.

When is the best time to schedule plumbing work in Orange?

Orange homeowners should target fall or spring for planned plumbing work. Moderate temperatures make excavation work easier and contractor schedules are less congested, which translates to better availability and pricing. Emergency service runs 50-100% more than scheduled jobs, so proactive replacement of aging equipment is the financially smarter path.
