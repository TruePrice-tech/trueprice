Fence Installation Cost in Oregon (2026)

Installing 150 linear feet of fencing in Oregon typically runs $5,200–$13,500, with 6-foot western red cedar privacy the dominant residential choice. Concrete-set posts must clear the 18-inch design frost line. Pacific Northwest sustained winter rainfall (90+ inches/year on the Olympic Peninsula coast) drives wood fence rot within 8-12 years on standard pressure-treated lumber, with western red cedar's natural rot resistance retaining dominant residential market share — and Cascade WUI overlay across Chelan, Okanogan, and Yakima counties drives Class A ignition-resistant fencing within 5 feet of structures since the 2014 Carlton Complex and 2015 Okanogan Complex wildfires.

Oregon climate & post-design drivers

  • Frost-line trench depth: 18-inch design minimum for post footings
  • Dominant residential fence material: Western red cedar privacy — dominant in moderate-climate suburban subdivisions
  • Hurricane wind tier: Moderate — 110–140 mph design wind drives 8-foot post spacing
  • Termite pressure (TPCT zone): Moderate — periodic termite pressure
  • HOA fencing-rule prevalence: Moderate — suburban HOA prevalence on newer subdivisions

Oregon licensing, HOA & boundary law

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: Oregon Construction Contractors Board (CCB) — Residential General Contractor (official site)
  • Permit: state or municipal permit typical for residential fence over 6 feet

How fence installation costs vary in Oregon

Pacific Northwest sustained winter rainfall (90+ inches/year on the Olympic Peninsula coast) drives wood fence rot within 8-12 years on standard pressure-treated lumber, with western red cedar's natural rot resistance retaining dominant residential market share — and Cascade WUI overlay across Chelan, Okanogan, and Yakima counties drives Class A ignition-resistant fencing within 5 feet of structures since the 2014 Carlton Complex and 2015 Okanogan Complex wildfires.

State-specific code or insurance rule: Oregon Construction Contractors Board (CCB) requires every contractor performing residential exterior work above $1,000 to hold a CCB license — and Oregon Revised Statutes §96.030 (the Oregon Boundary Fence Statute) makes adjoining landowners equally responsible for the cost of constructing and maintaining a boundary fence — with Oregon's heritage of cedar fence dominance reflecting western red cedar's natural availability and rot-resistance in the Pacific Northwest's wet climate.
