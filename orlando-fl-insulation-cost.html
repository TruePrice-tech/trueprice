Frequently asked insulation questions

What is the difference between open-cell and closed-cell spray foam?

Open-cell (R-3.7/inch) is cheaper, flexible, and better for interior walls. Closed-cell (R-6.5/inch) is rigid, waterproof, adds structural strength, and is required in flood zones and below-grade applications.

How much energy does insulation save?

Insulating an under-insulated attic to current code saves 15-25% on heating and cooling costs. In a home spending $2,000/year on energy, that is $300-$500 annual savings, paying back a $1,500-$2,500 project in 3-5 years.

Should I insulate my walls or attic first?

Attic insulation provides the best return since heat rises. Most homes lose 25-30% of heat through the attic. Wall insulation is second priority and harder to retrofit. Floor insulation over crawl spaces is third.

Orlando-area air sealing priorities

A typical Orlando 1980s CBS home pre-retrofit blower door reads 7-11 ACH50 because of unsealed top-plate penetrations where the wood truss bearing sits on the block wall, open bathroom fan housings, and duct leakage through attic-mounted air handlers pulling unconditioned 150F attic air. OUC and Duke Energy auditors prioritize sealing the air handler plenum and return boot connections with mastic (often a 20-30% leakage source alone), foaming the truss-to-block-wall bearing joint, and gasketing every recessed can with airtight IC-rated LED retrofit kits. Common Orlando Insulation Upsells and What They Actually Buy

The dominant Orlando upsell is closed-cell spray foam at the roof deck underside at $3.80-$5.50 per sqft to 'condition the attic,' which only makes sense when the air handler and ductwork are attic-mounted. For CBS homes with the HVAC in a garage closet, this buys almost nothing. A frequent bad upsell is 'insulating' CBS block walls by injecting loose-fill into the block cells, which Orlando Energy Raters have measured at less than R-2 improvement after thermal bridge accounting. The legitimate paired upgrade is duct replacement with R-8 insulated flex and a variable-speed 16+ SEER heat pump.

Orlando energy code snapshot: the basics (Zone 2A)

Orlando follows the Florida Building Code 7th Edition (2020) for Zone 2A: R-38 attic and R-13 walls. Florida Building Code is separate from IECC and has its own compliance path. OUC and Duke Energy service territories follow the same code but issue permits through different municipal portals (City of Orlando Permitting Services versus Orange County Building Safety). The Florida Building Code high-velocity hurricane zone provisions do not apply to the Orlando metro (those apply to Broward, Miami-Dade, and Palm Beach).

DOE recommended insulation levels for Zone 2A are attic R-30 to R-60 and walls R-13. Code minimums in Orlando are attic R-38 and walls R-13. Exceeding code by one R-tier typically returns within 4 to 7 years of utility savings in this market.

Moisture Control and Vapor Strategy for Orlando homeowners

Orlando's year-round cooling climate at 75-90% outdoor relative humidity drives a strict exterior-to-interior vapor flow pattern. Vapor retarders belong on the exterior side of the wall (the warm-in-summer side), not the interior. Kraft-faced batts installed with kraft facing the interior drywall are wrong in Orlando and produce mold within 18 months. Open-cell foam at the roof deck in unvented assemblies requires a Class III interior vapor retarder (latex paint) only. Poly sheeting is absolutely prohibited in Zone 2A because it creates a double vapor barrier that traps moisture from every afternoon thunderstorm.
