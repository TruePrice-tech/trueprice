What Orlando residents ask about landscaping

Do I need a permit for landscaping in Orlando?

Most landscaping in Orlando does not require permits. Exceptions include retaining walls over 4 feet, grading that changes drainage, connecting to city water for irrigation, and work near property lines.

How often should I replace mulch?

Replenish organic mulch annually (top off to 3 inches). Full replacement every 2-3 years prevents matting and fungal buildup. Rock and rubber mulch last 5-10 years but do not improve soil.

What is the cheapest way to landscape a yard?

Seed instead of sod, use native plants from local nurseries, mulch with free municipal compost, and phase the project over 2-3 seasons. A basic DIY front yard makeover can be done for $500-$1,500.

How much does mulch cost?

Bulk mulch costs $25-$50 per cubic yard delivered. Plan 3 inches deep and 1 yard covers 108 sqft. A 1,000 sqft bed needs about 10 yards ($250-$500). Professional installation adds $30-$50/yard.

Native plant palette near Orlando

Orlando's native palette centers on live oak, cabbage palm, sabal palmetto, and bald cypress for canopy, with coontie, muhly grass, sunshine mimosa, and blue-eyed grass for groundcover and accent. Florida-Friendly landscaping statute (Section 373.185 Florida Statutes) specifically limits HOA restrictions on native-plant installations, which gives Orlando homeowners legal protection for converting turf to native beds. Harry P. Leu Gardens in Orlando and Bok Tower Gardens in Lake Wales showcase the regional palette. Green Isle Gardens nursery and Biosphere Consulting supply most of the Central Florida native market. Hurricane preparation drives landscape decisions: canopy trees must be wind-rated, and specimen palms require specific pruning protocols before storm season.

Understanding soil conditions and amendments in Orlando

Orlando soil is deep Central Florida sand over Ocala limestone karst, with pH 6.5-7.5 and essentially zero organic content. Sandy soil drains so rapidly that organic amendment (composted pine bark, aged manure) and heavy mulching are mandatory to retain moisture for any ornamental planting. Winter Park lots near lakes sit on muck soil pockets that require entirely different treatment. University of Florida IFAS Extension soil testing ($8-$20) is the standard baseline. Reliable Peat and Mulch and Florida Silica Sand Supply provide most Central Florida amendments. The Floridan Aquifer source water at 180-280 ppm is hard enough to affect soil pH over time.

Understanding irrigation rules and rebates in Orlando

St. Johns River Water Management District restricts landscape watering to two days per week (odd/even address by day) with no watering between 10am and 4pm year-round. These restrictions apply to Orange, Seminole, and Osceola counties covering the entire Orlando metro. TCEQ-equivalent Florida licensing requires a Licensed Irrigation Contractor for any new system install. OUC and Duke Energy do not offer irrigation-specific rebates, but the Florida Water Star program certifies efficient landscape designs. Drip conversion is the path around day-of-week restrictions because drip systems are exempt from the watering schedule in most jurisdictions.

Orlando does not offer direct turf-replacement rebates like western cities, but the Florida-Friendly Landscaping program through UF IFAS Extension provides free design consultations and plant lists. St. Johns River Water Management District funds green-infrastructure projects in target watersheds. OUC's conservation program offers water-audit services. Dr. Phillips and Lake Nona master-planned community HOAs are increasingly accepting Florida-Friendly native landscapes under the state statutory protection. Orlando: lawn and turf choices

St. Augustine (Floratam, Palmetto, Sapphire, and Bitterblue varieties) dominates Orlando residential lawns because of its shade tolerance under live-oak canopy and recovery from summer-afternoon thunderstorm damage. Zoysia (Empire, JaMur) is gaining on newer Lake Nona and Dr. Phillips tract homes for drought tolerance. Bermuda is reserved for full-sun sports lawns. Bahia grass shows up on larger College Park lots as a low-maintenance alternative. Chinch bug (Blissus insularis) and gray leaf spot (Pyricularia grisea) control drive seasonal treatment schedules on St. Augustine turf across the metro.

Invasive pests and plants in Orlando

Brazilian pepper (Schinus terebinthifolia) is the dominant invasive plant in Orlando; Category I listing by Florida Exotic Pest Plant Council mandates removal on any cleared lot. Air potato vine (Dioscorea bulbifera) smothers native canopy in College Park and Winter Park. Invasive iguanas are an increasing concern in lakefront properties. Asian citrus psyllid affects citrus plantings (Orlando has a strong backyard-citrus tradition). Laurel wilt disease transmitted by the redbay ambrosia beetle threatens native swamp bay and avocado plantings. South American palm weevil is devastating Canary Island date palms across the metro.

HOA and design review throughout Orlando

Tree services and ordinances throughout Orlando

City of Orlando Urban Forestry Division manages public trees; private-lot tree removal over 6 inches DBH requires a Tree Removal Permit. Winter Park has its own tree-protection ordinance (some of the strictest in Central Florida) requiring arborist review and replacement planting. Licensed arborists through ISA charge $800-$3,500 per mature tree removal. Hurricane-season (June-November) emergency cleanup spikes pricing 40-60%. The post-Hurricane Ian (2022) canopy loss drove a wave of replanting that the Orlando Urban Forestry program subsidized through Trees for All.

Seasonal maintenance rhythm for Orlando homeowners

Orlando landscape maintenance runs year-round with no dormancy period. Warm-season grass peaks March through October with summer-thunderstorm damage repair as a constant task. Pre-emergent weed control applications happen late-January and late-September. Hurricane-season preparation (June 1 start) requires canopy pruning, palm frond removal, and hardscape securing by May 15. Post-hurricane cleanup creates episodic demand spikes. Winter-visitor tourist season (November-March) drives HOA enforcement of landscape appearance standards in Dr. Phillips and Lake Nona.

Orlando-area stormwater rules

St. Johns River Water Management District and South Florida Water Management District split jurisdiction in the southern Orlando metro. City of Orlando stormwater ordinance requires on-site detention for any project creating more than 4,400 sqft of impervious surface. Rain-garden and bioswale installations qualify for WMD cost-share. The extensive lake system (over 100 named lakes in the Orlando metro) drives nutrient-management rules that restrict fertilizer application within 10 feet of any water body under the Orange County Fertilizer Ordinance.

Common Orlando Landscaping Scams

The dominant Orlando landscaping scam is the post-hurricane door-to-door crew offering tree removal and debris cleanup for $1,000-$4,000 cash, demanding upfront payment, and disappearing. Florida DBPR licensing (Landscape Contractor through the Construction Industry Licensing Board) is the baseline verification. The DBPR license lookup confirms insurance status. A second scam is selling non-native tropical species as 'hurricane-resistant' at premium pricing when native live oaks and cabbage palms have superior wind resistance. Verify ISA certification and $1M general liability before cash changes hands.

Local market conditions. The Orlando metro's rapid expansion has created a seller's market for contractors. Orlando's large market supports full-service landscape design firms, installation-only crews, and maintenance companies.
