Orlando siding FAQ

How much does siding replacement cost in Orlando?

Siding replacement in Orlando costs $6,000-$18,000 for an average home. Vinyl is the most affordable ($4-$8/sqft installed), while fiber cement ($8-$14/sqft) and wood ($10-$18/sqft) cost more.

What is the most energy-efficient siding?

Insulated vinyl siding (with foam backing) adds R-2 to R-5 insulation. Fiber cement over rigid foam sheathing provides better thermal performance. The biggest energy gains come from fixing air leaks during installation.

What goes under siding?

From inside out: wall sheathing (plywood or OSB), a weather-resistant barrier (house wrap like Tyvek), then siding. Some installations add rigid foam insulation between the house wrap and siding.

Dominant siding materials within Orlando

Orlando siding choices center on stucco, fiber cement, and aluminum across neighborhoods like Winter Park and College Park. CBS block construction in older neighborhoods requires masonry repair rather than siding replacement.

Orlando's dominant siding material is stucco (scratch-brown-finish coat over concrete-block construction) on 60-70% of Central Florida residential, reflecting the region's post-1960 concrete-block-and-stucco building tradition. Fiber cement (James Hardie HardiePlank) appears on higher-end renovations in Winter Park, College Park, and Dr. Phillips where homeowners want lap-siding aesthetics over block-wall substrate. Vinyl siding is relatively uncommon in Orange County compared to Northern markets due to UV degradation, HOA prohibitions in Windermere and Lake Nona communities, and the dominance of block-wall construction. Wood clapboard survives on some 1920s-1940s College Park bungalows and Winter Park historic homes.

Understanding climate impact on siding in Orlando

Orlando siding faces intense UV, tropical moisture, hurricane wind loads, and salt-air corrosion in coastal zones. Moisture management behind siding is the primary failure pattern in Orlando.

Orlando is 50+ miles inland from Atlantic and Gulf coasts, so salt-air corrosion is not a primary concern. Orlando's corrosion challenges center on Central Florida's high water table, afternoon-thunderstorm humidity (50+ inches annual rainfall), and standing-water conditions around foundations in low-lying neighborhoods near Lake Apopka, Lake Conway, and the Butler Chain. Grade-level siding-to-foundation transitions require specific drainage detail and flashing to prevent moisture wicking. Galvanized steel fasteners perform adequately in Orlando's inland humid-subtropical climate for 15-20 years; stainless-steel provides longer service on persistently wet-site conditions.

HPB Certificate of Appropriateness is required for exterior alterations on contributing structures. Approved materials include period-appropriate wood clapboard matching original profile, stucco color-matching to documented original finish, and specific fiber cement profiles on non-contributing infill. Orlando and wall assembly details and window integration

Orlando warm-edge detailing on siding-to-window interface: continuous flashing with minimum 4-inch overlap at head, 6-inch at jamb, 2-inch at sill. Back-dam and sill pan under window rough sill (critical in Central Florida humidity and afternoon-thunderstorm wind-driven rain). Florida Building Code 7th Edition requires documented water-resistant flashing systems; self-adhered flashing tape and sill pan at every opening or final inspection fails. Stucco-to-window interface on CMU construction requires specific weep screed and drainage detail; failed weep screed is the #1 Orlando stucco moisture-intrusion source on 1970s-1990s block homes.

Orlando does not face meaningful cold-climate conditions. Warm-climate detailing drives assembly design: continuous air barrier behind siding prevents cooled-air infiltration from conditioned interior to exterior wall cavity (significant in Orlando's 9-month cooling season). Rain-screen drainage gap (1/4" to 3/8") between weather-resistive barrier and stucco or fiber cement allows drying after daily thunderstorm events during June-September rainy season. Radiant barrier on wall assembly reduces solar gain on west-facing elevations in cooling-dominated Central Florida. Termite barrier (both subterranean and drywood species in Orange County) at grade level is essential; wood siding at grade fails within 8-12 years without treatment.

Stucco moisture testing using a penetrating moisture meter identifies hidden CMU-cavity moisture before new finish application. Termite inspection (both subterranean and drywood species common in Orange County) of wood fascia and structural members is essential before installation. Siding upsells to accept and decline within Orlando
