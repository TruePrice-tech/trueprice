Gutter Installation Cost in Pennsylvania (2026)

Whole-house gutter installation in Pennsylvania typically runs $1,200–$4,500, with 5-inch K-style seamless aluminum the dominant residential profile and roughly 42 inches of annual precipitation driving sizing. Pennsylvania's 42-inch annual rainfall combined with Allegheny Plateau winter snow-load and Pocono ice-dam exposure on north-facing eaves drives near-universal heat-cable retrofit on long-tenure homes — and high mature-deciduous canopy across the Susquehanna and Delaware River valleys drives gutter-guard retrofit demand on Philadelphia, Lancaster, and Scranton suburbs within 2-3 years of original installation.

Pennsylvania rainfall & drainage drivers

  • Annual rainfall: 42 inches/yr — drives K-style sizing and downspout count
  • Dominant residential gutter profile: 5-inch K-style seamless aluminum — dominant low-rainfall retrofit profile
  • Ground snow load: 30 psf — drives gutter bracket spacing and ice-shield requirements
  • Ice-dam risk tier: High — sustained sub-freezing winter requires ice-and-water shield + heat-cable retrofit budget
  • Hurricane wind tier: None
  • Tree-canopy density: High — mature deciduous canopy drives gutter-guard demand and 2x annual cleaning frequency

Pennsylvania licensing & permits

  • License status: Statewide registration required (no exam)
  • License board: Pennsylvania Office of Attorney General — Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration (official site)
  • Permit: municipal building permit typical for gutter installation on new construction; Philadelphia and Pittsburgh separate inspection programs

How gutter installation costs vary in Pennsylvania

State-specific code or insurance rule: Pennsylvania Home Improvement Consumer Protection Act (HICPA, 73 P.S. §517) requires every contractor performing residential exterior work above $5,000 per year to register with the Office of Attorney General — and unique to Pennsylvania, the HICPA registration carries no skills examination but mandates a written contract with 16-point disclosures, prohibits any payment over 33% of contract value before substantial completion, and creates a private right of action for treble damages on HICPA violations.
