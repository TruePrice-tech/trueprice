Maricopa County's Department of Environmental Services requires permitted disposal of R-22 and R-410A refrigerant with a signed manifest; your contractor should include the disposal manifest number on the final invoice. The permit paperwork covers Manual J calculation, electrical service work, gas-piping size where applicable, refrigerant line-set path, condensate handling, and the duct-leakage testing AZ code specifies. Phoenix permits aren't optional red tape — they're leverage against bad installs. Skipping the permit in Phoenix voids Trane's warranty and any other manufacturer coverage, creates an MLS-listed defect at sale time, and sticks you with liability: a botched Phoenix AC install leaks refrigerant into the attic, drips condensate into the APS (Arizona Public Service)-side electrical panel, or fails its first pressure test during July; City of Phoenix Development Services catches those upstream of drywall closing back up. When a Phoenix contractor offers to save you the permit fee, the savings really accrue to them by dodging City of Phoenix Development Services review. The A2L rollout is reshaping Phoenix installs. Phoenix homeowners getting 2026 installs will see either R-454B (Honeywell) or R-32 (Daikin/Mitsubishi) on the nameplate, full stop. A2L sits in flammability class 2L versus class 1 for R-410A, so installation practice tightens under UL 60335-2-40. Requirements include volume-to-charge ratios, leak sensors on indoor equipment over specified tonnages, and brazing-joint protocols that depart from R-410A practice.
