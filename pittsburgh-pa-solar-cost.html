Pittsburgh solar FAQ

How does the federal solar tax credit work?

The Investment Tax Credit (ITC) lets you deduct 30% of your total solar installation cost from your federal taxes. It applies to purchased systems, not leased. There is no cap on the credit amount.

How many panels do I need?

Panel count depends on your electricity usage, panel wattage, and roof sun exposure. A typical home uses 20-30 panels (400W each) to offset 80-100% of electricity consumption.

How much does solar cost in Pittsburgh?

Most homeowners in Pittsburgh pay $15,000-$30,000 before incentives for a 6-10kW system. After the 30% federal tax credit, net cost drops to $10,500-$21,000.

Will solar panels damage my roof?

Properly installed panels do not damage roofs. Reputable installers use flashing and sealant at every penetration point. If your roof needs replacement within 5-10 years, do it before installing solar.

Does solar work in Pittsburgh's cold winters?

Pittsburgh homeowners are sometimes surprised to learn that solar panels produce electricity more efficiently in cold temperatures. While winter days are shorter, net metering banks your summer surplus as credit. Snow slides off angled panels quickly, usually within a day. Annual production is lower than desert climates, but stronger incentives and higher electricity rates in cold-climate markets often deliver comparable payback periods.

What solar incentives are available in Pittsburgh?

All Pittsburgh homeowners qualify for the 30% federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC), which has no dollar cap. Beyond that, check your state's solar incentive database (DSIRE) for state tax credits, utility rebates, Solar Renewable Energy Credits (SRECs), property tax exemptions, and net metering policies. These vary by state and utility and can reduce your net cost by an additional 10-30% beyond the federal credit.

When is the best time to install solar in Pittsburgh?

For Pittsburgh, schedule solar installation during late spring or summer. Weather cooperates for roof work and you maximize first-year production before winter. Keep in mind the full process (quoting, permitting, utility approval, installation) takes 6-12 weeks. Start 2-3 months before your target date. If tax-year timing matters, plan around the installation completion date since that triggers the ITC.

Utility rate and net metering structure within Pittsburgh

Duquesne Light residential solar operates under Pennsylvania's net metering framework (66 Pa. C.S. 2799.1) with 1-for-1 retail-rate credits for exported energy. Credits roll forward month-to-month with annual true-up. Duquesne Light's residential rate is the baseline. Pennsylvania Solar Renewable Energy Credits (PA SRECs) trade at $20-$45 per MWh through the PJM GATS trading platform, providing meaningful additional revenue that most other states in this group cannot match. Peoples Gas service is unrelated to solar economics. The PA SREC market makes Pittsburgh solar more financially attractive than the modest solar resource suggests.
