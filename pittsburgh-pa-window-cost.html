Common window replacement questions in Pittsburgh

What type of replacement window is best?

Vinyl frames offer the best value with low maintenance and good insulation. Fiberglass is more durable and paintable. Wood has the best aesthetics but needs ongoing maintenance. Aluminum is cheapest but conducts heat.

How much energy do new windows actually save?

Replacing single-pane windows saves 25-30% on heating and cooling costs. Upgrading from older double-pane to modern Low-E double-pane saves 10-15%. Payback takes 10-15 years through energy savings alone.

How much does window replacement cost in Pittsburgh?

Window replacement in Pittsburgh costs $400-$1,200 per window installed, depending on size, frame material, glass type, and accessibility. A whole-house project (10-15 windows) runs $5,000-$15,000.

What is the difference between full-frame and insert replacement?

Insert replacement fits a new window into the existing frame, is faster, and costs less. Full-frame replacement removes everything down to the studs, costs 30-50% more, but fixes hidden damage.

Licensing and code rules near Pittsburgh

Pittsburgh window permitting runs through the Department of Permits, Licenses, and Inspections (PLI). Pennsylvania HIC Act 132 registration is required for all residential contractors, and PLI verifies registration at permit issuance. The Pittsburgh Historic Review Commission (HRC) governs contributing structures in Allegheny West, Manchester, Mexican War Streets, North Side, and Deutschtown. HRC requires matching sash profiles, meeting rail dimensions, muntin width, and exterior color on contributing structures, and the review process requires physical samples of proposed replacement material. Wood or approved aluminum-clad wood with 7/8-inch simulated divided lites is the standard HRC-approved specification. Vinyl is categorically prohibited on HRC-reviewed primary elevations. EPA RRP (Renovation, Repair, and Painting) certification is mandatory on all pre-1978 homes, which covers the vast majority of Pittsburgh city housing stock. Pre-1978 Pittsburgh homes almost certainly have lead paint on original window sash, and RRP-certified lead-safe work practices add $150-$300 per window to the project cost but are non-negotiable under federal law.

Pittsburgh-specific window fraud patterns

Pittsburgh's most persistent window fraud targets the EPA RRP requirement on pre-1978 homes. A contractor offers a below-market bid by skipping lead-safe work practices (containment, HEPA vacuum, wet methods, clearance testing), which saves $150-$300 per window in labor but exposes the homeowner to EPA fines of up to $37,500 per day per violation and creates a lead-contaminated environment around the home's perimeter. Verify the contractor's EPA RRP firm certification at epa.gov/lead and confirm that the project supervisor holds a current EPA RRP Renovator certificate. The second Pittsburgh-specific scam involves PA HIC Act 132 violations: a contractor operates without HIC registration, which means the homeowner has no access to the PA Attorney General's recovery fund if the contractor defaults. Verify HIC registration at the Attorney General's website on the day of contract signing.

Pittsburgh-area window scam patterns and contractor verification
