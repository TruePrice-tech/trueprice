Questions Portland homeowners ask about insulation

Is blown-in insulation better than batts?

Blown-in fills gaps and voids that batts leave around wiring, pipes, and framing. It performs 15-25% better than batts in real-world conditions. Batts are easier to install in open walls during new construction.

Do I need a permit for insulation in Portland?

Most Portland jurisdictions do not require permits for adding insulation to existing spaces. Permits may be needed if the work involves removing drywall or altering vapor barriers. Check local codes.

What is a vapor barrier and do I need one?

A vapor barrier prevents moisture from passing through walls and ceilings. In cold climates, install on the warm (interior) side. In hot-humid climates, install on the exterior side. Closed-cell spray foam acts as its own vapor barrier.

Rebates and utility programs: a Portland guide

Energy Trust of Oregon pays $1,000-$3,500 for whole-house insulation and air sealing verified by a BPI Envelope Professional, and the program is fuel-agnostic (covers PGE electric and NW Natural gas customers equally). Common Portland Insulation Upsells and What They Actually Buy

Energy Code Snapshot near Portland (Zone 4C)

Portland follows Oregon Residential Specialty Code (based on IECC 2021 with Oregon amendments) for Zone 4C: R-49 attic, R-21 cavity plus R-5 continuous for walls in new construction. Oregon CCB license is required for all insulation work regardless of value. The Oregon Reach Code voluntary path targets 10% above prescriptive and qualifies for additional Energy Trust incentives. City of Portland Bureau of Development Services issues permits with 5-10 business day review for standard scope.

DOE recommended insulation levels for Zone 4C are attic R-49 to R-60 and walls R-13 to R-21. Code minimums in Portland are attic R-49 and walls R-13+5ci. Exceeding code by one R-tier typically returns within 4 to 7 years of utility savings in this market.

Portland moisture control and vapor strategy

Portland's Csb marine climate keeps relative humidity above 75% from October through May, making moisture management the primary insulation consideration rather than a secondary one. The Oregon Residential Specialty Code explicitly prohibits interior polyethylene sheeting in Zone 4C because it traps summer moisture. Exterior wrap must be a vapor-permeable WRB (Typar, Tyvek, or mineral wool). Crawlspace moisture is the number-one failure mode in Portland; the Energy Trust-approved encapsulation standard is a 20-mil vapor barrier over the soil, perimeter closed-cell foam at R-21, mechanical dehumidification sized for 60 pints per day, and positive-pressure air mixing from the HVAC system.
