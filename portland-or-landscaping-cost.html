Landscaping questions for Portland homeowners

What is xeriscaping?

Xeriscaping uses drought-tolerant plants, efficient irrigation, and permeable surfaces to minimize water use. It cuts outdoor water consumption 50-75% and is increasingly required in water-restricted regions.

Do I need a permit for landscaping in Portland?

Most landscaping in Portland does not require permits. Exceptions include retaining walls over 4 feet, grading that changes drainage, connecting to city water for irrigation, and work near property lines.

How often should I replace mulch?

Replenish organic mulch annually (top off to 3 inches). Full replacement every 2-3 years prevents matting and fungal buildup. Rock and rubber mulch last 5-10 years but do not improve soil.

Portland: native plant palette

Portland's Pacific Northwest native palette centers on Oregon grape (Mahonia aquifolium, the state flower), western sword fern, red flowering currant, salal, and Pacific rhododendron for understory beds, with Douglas fir, western red cedar, and vine maple for canopy. Portland's 30%+ urban canopy coverage is among the highest of any major US city, creating deeply shaded lots where native shade-tolerant species are essential. Portland Nursery on Division Street and Xera Plants in SE Portland specialize in PNW natives adapted to the Willamette Valley climate. Leach Botanical Garden and Hoyt Arboretum demonstrate the regional palette. Rain-garden native plantings use slough sedge, red-osier dogwood, and yellow monkey flower for wet-tolerant bioswale edges.

Portland and soil conditions and amendments

Portland soils are Vashon glacial till with dense Lawton clay lenses across the Puget-Willamette Lowland that drain poorly and compact under foot traffic. Portland Hills silt on the west side creates slope-instability issues that require geotechnical sign-off before any significant landscape grading. The persistent moisture (43 inches of rain, concentrated October-May) keeps pH acidic at 5.0-5.8 under the conifer canopy, making lime applications common before lawn establishment. Cedar Grove compost and Grimm's Fuel Company supply most amendments. Oregon State Extension soil testing ($25-$55) is the standard baseline. Alberta Arts bungalow lots commonly have 60+ years of organic accumulation that creates rich but poorly drained topsoil.

Irrigation rules and rebates for Portland homeowners

Portland Water Bureau does not mandate fixed residential watering-day schedules because the 43 inches of annual rainfall makes irrigation a June-through-September-only concern. Oregon Plumbing Code requires backflow prevention (ASSE 1013 RPZ) on all systems. Oregon CCB (Construction Contractors Board) license is required for irrigation installation; verify at ccb.oregon.gov. Smart-controller rebates are not offered by Portland Water Bureau but Energy Trust of Oregon provides incentives for integrated landscape-plus-efficiency packages. Water conservation is a minimal concern compared to California or Texas because of the abundant rainfall.

Portland Bureau of Environmental Services RainWise program pays up to $6/sqft of impervious area reduced through rain gardens, cisterns, and permeable paving, making it one of the most generous landscape-related rebate programs in the country. Energy Trust of Oregon provides separate incentives for integrated whole-property efficiency work. Metro Regional Government's Natural Gardening program provides education and design subsidies. Portland does not need turf-replacement rebates in the western-xeriscape sense because water scarcity is not a concern; the RainWise stormwater program serves as the primary landscape-incentive driver instead.

A Portland look at lawn and turf choices

Cool-season tall fescue and perennial ryegrass blends dominate Portland residential lawns. Red fescue (Creeping red, Chewings) handles shaded lots under the 30%+ canopy. No-mow fescue blends (Eco-Lawn, Pearl's Premium) are gaining in Alberta Arts and Sellwood sustainability-focused yards. Kentucky bluegrass is rare because Portland's persistent moisture promotes fungal disease on it. Warm-season grasses are not viable. Moss infiltration into lawns is a regional constant from October through May; ferrous-sulfate treatment is the standard remediation but many Portland homeowners deliberately cultivate moss as a low-maintenance alternative.

Portland: invasive pests and plants

English ivy (Hedera helix) is the dominant invasive plant in Portland; No Ivy League volunteer programs organize neighborhood removal events. Himalayan blackberry (Rubus armeniacus) overtakes unmaintained lots within two growing seasons. Garlic mustard (Alliaria petiolata) is spreading in Willamette Valley woodlands. Japanese knotweed (Reynoutria japonica) is established along the Willamette River corridor. Emerald ash borer was confirmed in Oregon in 2022 (Forest Grove); Oregon Department of Agriculture monitors spread. Spotted lanternfly has not yet reached Oregon but USDA monitors the I-5 corridor.

Portland: HOA and design review

Portland has relatively few HOA-controlled communities within city limits, but Lake Oswego, West Linn, and Tigard suburbs have master-planned communities with strict landscape requirements. Oregon does not have explicit statutory protection for xeriscape or native landscaping against HOA turf covenants the way California and Arizona do. Portland's Bureau of Environmental Services Critical Areas Ordinance adds environmental-review layers on any steep-slope, wetland, or stream-buffer parcel independent of HOA status. Alberta Arts and Sellwood have informal but peer-enforced native-garden character expectations.

Portland Tree Services and Ordinances

Portland Tree Code (Title 11 Trees) protects trees over 12 inches DBH on development sites and heritage trees over 24 inches DBH on all private property. Removal requires a City Forester permit and replacement planting or payment into the City Tree Fund. Licensed arborists through ISA charge $900-$3,800 per mature tree removal. The January 2021 ice storm that coated the metro in 1-2 inches of ice damaged tens of thousands of trees and created a 12-month backlog for arborist services. Douglas fir root rot (Armillaria) affects mature specimens across the metro and drives preventive-assessment demand.

Portland Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

Portland landscape maintenance runs year-round but the productive planting window is late-April through October. Spring cleanup in March includes moss removal from paths, hardscape, and roofs (a uniquely Portland seasonal task), early-season pruning, and pre-emergent moss treatment on lawns. Summer irrigation runs June through September only. Fall cleanup (October 15-December 15) includes leaf collection from the dense canopy and winter preparation. The wet season (October-May) creates steady moss-management and drainage-maintenance workload that drier markets do not face.

Stormwater rules across Portland

Portland Bureau of Environmental Services stormwater manual applies to any project creating over 500 sqft of impervious surface. The RainWise Cistern and Rain Garden program pays up to $6/sqft of impervious area reduced, one of the highest in the country. Portland's combined sewer overflow (CSO) zones prioritize rebate funding in specific watersheds. Eco-roofs (green roofs) receive floor-area-ratio bonuses in commercial zones that affect mixed-use residential projects. The Willamette River cleanup (Portland Harbor Superfund site) drives additional water-quality rules in river-adjacent parcels.

Common Portland Landscaping Scams

The dominant Portland landscaping scam is the post-ice-storm or post-windstorm door-to-door crew offering tree removal for $1,000-$4,000 cash, demanding upfront payment, and disappearing. Oregon CCB license (required under ORS 701) is the baseline verification; the CCB license lookup confirms bond and insurance status. Unlicensed landscape work over $1,000 is a misdemeanor. A second scam is selling invasive English ivy or Himalayan blackberry removal that only cuts stems without herbicide treatment, guaranteeing regrowth within one season.
