Soil conditions and amendments within Sacramento

Lawn and turf choices around Sacramento

Sacramento and invasive pests and plants

Red imported fire ants were confirmed in Sacramento County in 2022 and California Department of Food and Agriculture mandates reporting and treatment. Asian citrus psyllid affects the strong Sacramento backyard-citrus tradition. Emerald ash borer has not yet reached Sacramento but CDFA monitors. Yellow starthistle (Centaurea solstitialis) dominates invasive-weed management on undeveloped and edge lots. Sudden oak death (Phytophthora ramorum) threatens native valley oak populations. Shot hole borer (polyphagous) is monitored but not yet confirmed in the Sacramento metro.

HOA and design review for Sacramento homeowners

Elk Grove and Natomas master-planned communities enforce strict landscape requirements including approved plant lists, MWELO-compliant water budgets, and minimum canopy-tree counts. California Civil Code 4735 protects homeowners' right to drought-tolerant landscaping against HOA turf-required covenants. East Sacramento and Midtown have no formal HOAs but neighborhood associations enforce peer-reviewed character standards. Sacramento Preservation Commission adds landscape-review layers for designated historic districts.

Tree services and ordinances near Sacramento

Seasonal maintenance rhythm throughout Sacramento

Sacramento landscape maintenance runs year-round with a distinct summer-stress dormancy period for cool-season plants. Warm-season grasses green up in March and peak June-September. Pre-emergent weed control applications happen mid-February and mid-September. The extreme summer heat (60+ days above 100F) creates irrigation-management demands that require daily monitoring of smart-controller schedules. Winter (Tule) fog events (December-February) create cold-air pooling that kills tender tropicals at ground level while leaving nearby elevated plantings unharmed.

Common Sacramento Landscaping Scams

The dominant Sacramento landscaping scam is the door-to-door crew offering tree-trimming and palm-removal for $500-$2,000 cash after winter storms, demanding payment up front and disappearing. California CSLB licensing (C-27 landscape classification) is the baseline verification. The CSLB license lookup confirms bond and insurance status. Unlicensed work over $500 is a misdemeanor. A second scam is selling MWELO-compliant landscape plans that actually violate the water-budget calculations, leaving the homeowner with a failed city inspection. Verify CSLB license, $1M general liability, and request the MWELO compliance worksheet before signing.
