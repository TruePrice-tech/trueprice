What Sacramento residents ask about window replacement

Should I replace all windows at once?

Replacing all windows at once saves 10-15% on labor versus doing them individually. It also ensures uniform appearance and consistent energy performance throughout the home.

Do replacement windows increase home value?

New windows recover 65-75% of their cost at resale. They also improve curb appeal, reduce noise, and eliminate a common inspection concern for buyers.

What type of replacement window is best?

Vinyl frames offer the best value with low maintenance and good insulation. Fiberglass is more durable and paintable. Wood has the best aesthetics but needs ongoing maintenance. Aluminum is cheapest but conducts heat.

How much energy do new windows actually save?

Replacing single-pane windows saves 25-30% on heating and cooling costs. Upgrading from older double-pane to modern Low-E double-pane saves 10-15%. Payback takes 10-15 years through energy savings alone.

How much does window replacement cost in Sacramento?

Window replacement in Sacramento costs $400-$1,200 per window installed, depending on size, frame material, glass type, and accessibility. A whole-house project (10-15 windows) runs $5,000-$15,000.

Sacramento's energy performance spec

Sacramento is IECC zone 3B, a hot-dry inland climate where cooling drives 60-70% of annual residential energy use. SMUD (Sacramento Municipal Utility District) serves the city and inner suburbs while PG&E covers the outer metro. ENERGY STAR targets U-factor 0.30 and SHGC 0.25 for zone 3B, and California Title 24 Part 6 (2022 Energy Code) enforces SHGC 0.23 on west-facing glazing in new construction and 0.25 on residential alterations. SMUD's Home Performance Program pays $1-$3 per square foot of replaced glass area on qualified ENERGY STAR products, and PG&E's residential rebate adds a parallel credit for service-area homes. Stacking utility rebates with the federal 25C credit returns $75-$150 per window on a typical Sacramento whole-house replacement. Central Valley summer heat (30+ days above 100F) makes SHGC the dominant Sacramento glazing metric: uncoated west-facing glass on a July afternoon transmits roughly 230 BTU/hr per square foot, and spectrally-selective Low-E 366 drops that to roughly 55 BTU/hr.

Sacramento energy economics in the Central Valley reward balanced glazing because the inland climate produces genuine winter heating loads (unlike coastal California) plus severe summer cooling demand when temperatures exceed 105F for consecutive days in July and August. SMUD's Home Performance Program offers rebates of $50-$100 per qualified ENERGY STAR window, among the most generous municipal utility programs in California. California Title 24 Part 6 (2022 cycle) requires SHGC 0.23 on west-facing glazing in new construction and 0.40 on alterations within Climate Zone 12, verified at permit final by the Sacramento Regional Building Authority.

Sacramento-specific window fraud patterns

Sacramento's most common window fraud involves unlicensed contractors operating without the required CSLB C-17 or B license. California law requires the CSLB license number on all advertising, contracts, vehicles, and business cards. Check cslb.ca.gov on the day of contract signing: the database shows license status, expiration, bond amount, workers' compensation certificate, and complaint history. The second Sacramento-specific scam targets Title 24 compliance: a contractor installs windows that meet the NFRC label requirements but fails to pull the alteration permit or schedule the HERS verification, which means the homeowner cannot claim SMUD or PG&E rebates and has an unpermitted modification that surfaces during a future sale. Confirm the permit number and HERS rater assignment before the installation date.
