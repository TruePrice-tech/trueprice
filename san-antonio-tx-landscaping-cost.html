Landscaping questions for San Antonio homeowners

How much does mulch cost?

Bulk mulch costs $25-$50 per cubic yard delivered. Plan 3 inches deep and 1 yard covers 108 sqft. A 1,000 sqft bed needs about 10 yards ($250-$500). Professional installation adds $30-$50/yard.

How much does a sprinkler system cost?

A residential irrigation system costs $2,500-$5,000 for a quarter-acre lot. Smart controllers add $100-$300 and reduce water usage 20-40%. Drip irrigation zones for beds cost $500-$1,000 extra.

Should I hire a landscaper or landscape architect?

A landscaper handles installation and maintenance. A landscape architect designs complex projects and manages grading, drainage, and permits. For projects over $15,000 or involving structural elements, an architect is worthwhile.

How much does lawn installation cost?

Sod costs $1-$2 per square foot installed. Hydroseeding costs $0.05-$0.10/sqft. Traditional seeding costs $0.10-$0.25/sqft but takes 6-8 weeks to establish. A 5,000 sqft lawn costs $5,000-$10,000 in sod.

A San Antonio look at soil conditions and amendments

San Antonio soil sits on the Balcones Fault Zone where expansive Balcones clay meets Edwards limestone ledge rock. Clay Plasticity Index values reach 40-55 in Alamo Heights, producing 3-4 inches of seasonal vertical movement that cracks any rigid hardscape without proper base preparation. Stone Oak sits on thinner clay over limestone with depth to bedrock often under 12 inches. Texas A&M AgriLife Extension soil testing ($10-$25) is the standard. Garden-Ville compost and Nature's Mulch supply most amendments. Foundation-aware landscaping is critical: plants with aggressive root systems (live oak, Chinese tallow) must be placed at least 15 feet from foundations to avoid exacerbating clay shrink-swell.

A San Antonio look at irrigation rules and rebates

SAWS (San Antonio Water System) Stage II restrictions limit landscape watering to one day per week with specific hours, making San Antonio's irrigation rules among the strictest in the entire state. TCEQ Chapter 344 requires licensed irrigators (LI license) for any new system install. Drip-conversion rebates through SAWS pay $0.50/sqft for turf converted to drip-irrigated native beds. Edwards Aquifer Authority conservation programs add additional incentives for properties in the recharge zone. CPS Energy does not offer irrigation-specific rebates but SAWS WaterSaver rebates fill the gap.

SAWS WaterSaver Landscape Coupon program pays $100-$400 for qualifying drought-tolerant landscape conversions including turf removal, native plants, and efficient irrigation. The Edwards Aquifer Authority Drought Management Plan adds additional conservation incentives. CPS Energy SAVE program indirectly supports landscape efficiency through whole-house energy rebates. Texas A&M AgriLife Extension's Earth-Kind landscaping program provides free design guidance. SAWS Stage II restrictions make the turf-replacement economics more compelling than in most Texas cities because the water savings are immediate and significant.

Lawn and turf choices near San Antonio

Warm-season Bermuda (Tifway 419, Celebration) dominates San Antonio residential lawns because of heat and drought tolerance under SAWS Stage II restrictions. Buffalograss (Cody, Legacy) is the signature native alternative and qualifies for SAWS WaterSaver rebates. Zoysia (Empire, JaMur) handles shaded Alamo Heights lots. St. Augustine is used sparingly because of its higher water demand under SAWS restrictions. Cool-season fescue is not viable. The SAWS watering schedule effectively forces most new landscapes toward turf-replacement or low-water native installations.

San Antonio Invasive Pests and Plants

Red imported fire ants (Solenopsis invicta) dominate San Antonio yard pest management; broadcast bait applications are standard twice yearly. Oak wilt (Ceratocystis fagacearum) is particularly aggressive on Hill Country red oaks and live oaks; Texas Oak Wilt Qualified sanitation rules limit pruning to the November-January window. Crape myrtle bark scale is a growing ornamental concern. Giant cane (Arundo donax) along the San Antonio River and its tributaries is the dominant invasive plant. Ball moss (Tillandsia recurvata) is not technically invasive but weighs down native live oak branches and is a constant maintenance item.

HOA and design review across San Antonio

Stone Oak and the Rim master-planned communities enforce strict landscape requirements including approved plant palettes, minimum mulch depth, and irrigation-system certification. King William is a formal historic district under HDRC review. Alamo Heights is a separate municipality with its own landscape ordinance. Texas Property Code 202.007 protects drought-tolerant landscaping against HOA turf-required covenants, giving homeowners legal cover for native conversions. Monte Vista Conservation Society enforces informal character standards on the National Register district.

San Antonio: tree services and ordinances

City of San Antonio tree ordinance protects heritage trees over 19 inches DBH; removal requires a Development Services permit and substantial mitigation planting. Alamo Heights has a separate tree ordinance with different DBH thresholds. Licensed arborists through ISA charge $800-$3,500 per mature tree removal. Oak-wilt certified arborists follow Texas Oak Wilt Qualified Logger guidelines for containment-zone work. The February 2021 Winter Storm Uri freeze killed or damaged thousands of subtropical and tropical plantings, creating a wave of native-species replacement that the SAWS WaterSaver program subsidized.

Seasonal maintenance rhythm: San Antonio edition

San Antonio landscape maintenance runs year-round with peak growth March through October. Pre-emergent weed control applications happen mid-February and mid-September. SAWS Stage II watering restrictions require precise irrigation scheduling year-round. Oak pruning is restricted to the November-January window to avoid spreading oak wilt. The February 2021 Uri freeze and subsequent cold events drove many homeowners to replace frost-tender species (bougainvillea, plumbago, esperanza) with Hill Country native alternatives that tolerate occasional hard freezes.

Understanding stormwater rules in San Antonio

San Antonio River Authority and Edwards Aquifer Authority jointly regulate stormwater management across the metro. Properties in the Edwards Aquifer Recharge Zone face specific impervious-cover limits and water-quality protection requirements that add 3-5 weeks to any landscape permit. City of San Antonio stormwater ordinance requires detention for projects above threshold impervious-cover additions. Rain-garden and bioswale installations qualify for SARA cost-share. The Balcones clay soil's low permeability makes on-site detention sizing larger than equivalent projects in sandy-soil markets.
