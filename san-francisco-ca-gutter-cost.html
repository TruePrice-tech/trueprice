Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a gutter cost in San Francisco?

What is the cheapest gutter material?

Aluminum gutters are the most affordable option at $6-$12 per linear foot installed. Vinyl is comparable at $5-$11 per linear foot but less durable in extreme climates.

How long does a wood gutter last?

A well-maintained wood gutter typically lasts 15-20 years. Regular staining and sealing every 2-3 years can significantly extend its lifespan. Cedar and redwood last longer than pine.

How San Francisco's Rain Patterns Shape Gutter Design

24.6 inches
annual rainfall in San Francisco

San Francisco receives about 25 inches of rain per year, with nearly all of it concentrated between November and April. Summer is famously dry, often going 4-5 months without measurable rainfall, while atmospheric river events in winter can drop 3-6 inches over a few days. The fog drip that rolls in off the Pacific also keeps gutters damp far more often than rainfall totals alone suggest.

Sizing gutters for San Francisco rainfall

San Francisco rainfall frequently tops the 2 inches per hour that standard 5-inch K-style gutters can handle. The 6-inch upgrade delivers 40% more capacity for an extra $2-$3 per linear foot — a small premium that matters on Noe Valley, Mission, and Sunset rooflines during peak storms.

Downspout placement for San Francisco homes

The city's combined sewer system in neighborhoods like the Mission, Haight, and Inner Richmond means downspouts often tie directly into the sewer lateral, and SFPUC has been pushing homeowners toward green infrastructure disconnections where possible. Hillside homes in Bernal Heights, Twin Peaks, and Diamond Heights face steep-grade runoff concerns where poorly routed downspouts can undermine retaining walls and contribute to the serpentinite slope failures the city deals with every wet winter.

San Francisco homes typically need one downspout per 20-30 linear feet of gutter run. A standard 2x3-inch downspout handles about 600 sq ft of roof area; 3x4-inch oversized downspouts move substantially more water and earn their upcharge on larger roof planes common in Noe Valley, Mission, and Sunset.

No gutter guard system is actually maintenance-free, regardless of type. Even top-tier micro-mesh needs surface brushing every 1-2 years to clear fine pollen and dust buildup in San Francisco. Any San Francisco contractor pitching "never clean your gutters again" is overselling.

How San Francisco Winters Affect Your Gutters

Freeze risk level for San Francisco: low.

Hard freezes are rare in San Francisco proper, and ice dams are essentially unheard of at city elevation. Heated gutter cables have no practical purpose on a typical SF home and any contractor recommending them is upselling. The only cold-weather consideration is for homes above 800 feet in areas like Twin Peaks or Mt. Davidson where a few mornings per winter can dip below freezing.

Winter gutter basics in San Francisco

Ice is not the main concern in San Francisco, but proper gutter pitch — 1/4 inch of slope per 10 feet of run — still matters because standing water during a rare cold snap can crack seams and pop hangers even on mild-climate systems.

Red Flags in Gutter Quotes: What San Francisco Homeowners Should Watch For

These are the most common problems that surface on San Francisco gutter quotes.

5-inch gutters on a San Francisco home

At 24.6 inches of annual rain in San Francisco, a bid that defaults to 5-inch gutters without discussing Noe Valley, Mission, and Sunset roof area or peak rainfall rate is cutting corners. Roof planes over 1,500 sq ft draining to one run want 6-inch capacity in San Francisco's rain profile.

Wrong pitch or no pitch at all

Standard pitch is 1/4 inch per 10 feet of run toward the downspout. Flat gutters pool, attract mosquitoes, and overflow during San Francisco downpours. Test with a garden hose after install — pooling anywhere means the hanger heights need re-setting.

Missing drainage on San Francisco bids

Discharge routing is the half of a San Francisco gutter project contractors most often skip. Given 24.6 inches of San Francisco rainfall annually, dumping water at the foundation causes foundation movement, basement moisture, and yard erosion. A proper San Francisco bid addresses where the water goes, not just how it gets off the roof.

The San Francisco seamless bait-and-switch

Real seamless means the installer rolls the full gutter run on your San Francisco property from continuous coil stock; seams appear only at corners, outlets, and end caps. A "seamless" San Francisco bid that mentions joining factory-cut sections with splice connectors is sectional gutter at seamless pricing — the single most common install-side scam.
