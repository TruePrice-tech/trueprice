San Francisco Kitchen Stock and Typical Layouts

San Francisco kitchen stock runs from 1900s-1920s Victorian and Edwardian townhouses in Noe Valley and the Mission (80-120 sqft with shared-riser plumbing walls) through 1920s-40s Sunset and Richmond Marina-style row houses (90-130 sqft with back-stairs service kitchen) to 1960s-70s Marina District and Pacific Heights towers (80-130 sqft high-rise condo kitchens). Peninsula and East Bay suburbs (Daly City, Burlingame) have larger 150-220 sqft tract kitchens.

Victorian and Edwardian structural bearing walls run front-to-back on most SF townhouses and are load-bearing on nearly every interior wall. Opening a kitchen to the dining room requires a flush-beam LVL or steel with a licensed structural engineer letter. Condo and TIC unit boundaries restrict any wall removal that crosses to a neighboring unit. High-rise condo kitchens have plumbing slab-penetration rules that force sink locations to pre-existing risers. The Soft Story Ordinance retrofit schedule adds scheduling conflicts.

San Francisco Permits and Electrical Scope

San Francisco Department of Building Inspection (SFDBI) issues permits via Permit and Project Tracking System with 6-12 week turnaround on kitchen remodels. PG&E panel upgrades to 200A run $4,500-$9,500 because underground service relocation is common. California Title 24 Part 6 requires high-efficacy lighting and mandatory under-cabinet task lighting. San Francisco's All-Electric Ordinance effectively bans new gas hookups in kitchen remodels permitted after 2021; induction is not optional on new construction.

SFDBI runs 6-12 weeks on kitchen permits, longer with Historic Preservation Certificate of Appropriateness review. Oakland runs 4-8 weeks. Berkeley runs 6-10 weeks with strict green-building-ordinance review. Peninsula cities including San Mateo, Burlingame, and Palo Alto each run 4-8 weeks. Marin County (Mill Valley, Tiburon, Sausalito) runs 6-10 weeks. Historic district review through SF Planning adds 8-16 weeks on any exterior-visible work in designated districts.

San Francisco Countertop Trends and Local Stone Yards

Quartz (Caesarstone, Silestone) dominates SF condo and Victorian remodels because of the humidity and the co-op insurance pressure against porous stone. Slab yards include Da Vinci Marble in South SF, Cosmos Granite in Hayward, and M S International in Fremont. Natural marble remains the Pacific Heights and Presidio Heights luxury choice despite the etching. Sealed Carrara and Calacatta Gold show up in Nob Hill high-rise condos where the HOA approves stone uniformly.

San Francisco Plumbing, Water, and Rough-In

SF Public Utilities Commission delivers Hetch Hetchy water (45-80 ppm TDS), among the softest in the country, which is easier on fixtures than nearly any other major market. Victorian and Edwardian shared-riser plumbing means any sink relocation affects the neighboring unit and requires their consent in TIC and condo buildings. Master plumbers (CA CSLB-licensed, C-36 classification) charge $125-$180/hour. Soft Story Ordinance retrofits often overlap with kitchen projects in Marina and Mission District buildings.

SF kitchen remodels typically encounter a 40-50 gallon gas tank in a garage or basement utility closet. The All-Electric Ordinance increasingly pushes swaps to heat-pump water heaters on any significant permit. PG&E and BayREN rebates pay $1,000-$2,000 for heat-pump water heater swaps. Rheem ProTerra and AO Smith Voltex are common. Condo installations are tricky because of weight and vibration concerns on upper-floor units; specific condo bylaws often require ASHRAE-compliant mounting.
