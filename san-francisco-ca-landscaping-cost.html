Frequently asked landscaping questions

What should I look for in a contractor quote?

A professional quote breaks out labor, materials, permits, and cleanup separately. It includes a timeline, payment schedule, warranty terms, and the contractor's license number.

Do I need a permit for landscaping in San Francisco?

Most landscaping projects in San Francisco require a permit. Your contractor should pull the permit and schedule the inspection. Never pull permits in your own name.

What are common red flags in contractor quotes?

Red flags include: no line-item detail, same-day signing pressure, request for more than 30% upfront, no license or insurance verification, and verbal-only warranties.

When is the best time to schedule this work in San Francisco?

Off-peak seasons offer better pricing and availability. In San Francisco, scheduling during the shoulder season saves 10-20% versus peak-demand months.

San Francisco Native Plant Palette

Bay Area native palette centers on coast live oak, California buckeye, western redbud, California lilac (Ceanothus), western sword fern, coyote mint, and California poppy. Presidio native-plant nursery and the Regional Parks Botanic Garden in Tilden (Berkeley) are the regional references. Bay Natives Nursery in SF and Native Here Nursery in Berkeley supply most of the Bay Area native market. Fog-zone plantings use coast silk tassel, wax myrtle, and salal. Coastal Pacific Heights and Sea Cliff lean on shore pine and coast strawberry.

San Francisco Soil Conditions and Amendments

SF peninsula soils are Franciscan Complex serpentinite and sandstone with Bay Mud under Marina District and South Beach. Serpentine-soil chemistry (high magnesium, low calcium) limits non-native plantings on hillside parcels. Sonoma Compost and Grab N Grow amendments supply much of the Bay Area market. UC Marin Master Gardener soil testing ($30-$60) is the baseline. Pumice and lava-rock amendments address drainage on clay flats. Coastal fog delivers moisture year-round but also creates salt deposits.

San Francisco Irrigation Rules and Rebates

San Francisco Public Utilities Commission implements three-day-per-week residential watering with no daytime watering (10am-6pm prohibited). BAWSCA water-conservation agencies across the peninsula run similar programs. California Plumbing Code requires backflow prevention (ASSE 1013 RPZ) on all systems. SFPUC Water Conservation rebates pay $200-$750 for qualifying smart-controller installs. The BayREN residential program stacks with utility rebates. Dedicated irrigation meters qualify for reduced-rate commercial-irrigation pricing.

San Francisco Public Utilities Commission Lawn Be Gone rebate pays up to $3/sqft for turf-to-low-water conversions up to 2,000 sqft. BAWSCA member agencies (San Mateo, Burlingame, Hayward, Menlo Park) run coordinated programs with varying per-sqft amounts. East Bay Municipal Utility District (EBMUD) pays up to $3/sqft. Santa Clara Valley Water District pays similar amounts. Smart-controller rebates pay $200-$750 per qualifying device. Residential rain-barrel and cistern rebates pay up to $1,500.

San Francisco Lawn and Turf Choices

Cool-season fescue (dwarf tall fescue from Delta Bluegrass, Marathon II) and perennial ryegrass dominate SF residential lawns because year-round mild temperatures and cloud cover suit cool-season grass. Red fescue (Creeping red) is common on shaded Sunset and Richmond lots. No-mow fescue blends (Pearl's Premium, Eco-Lawn) are gaining in sustainability-focused Bernal Heights and Noe Valley yards. Kentucky bluegrass struggles because of fog-driven fungal disease. Warm-season grasses are not viable.

San Francisco Invasive Pests and Plants

Shot-hole borer (polyphagous and Kuroshio) affects Bay Area oaks, sycamores, and avocados. Sudden oak death (Phytophthora ramorum) is a regional constant that requires certified-sanitation removals. California oak moth affects hillside oak specimens. Glassy-winged sharpshooter (vector of Pierce's disease) threatens ornamental grape plantings. Cape ivy (Delairea odorata) and English ivy dominate hillside invasive-plant management. Spotted lanternfly has not yet reached California but CDFA monitors.

San Francisco HOA and Design Review

SF HOA restrictions are limited because most SF housing is co-op or condo rather than HOA-governed detached units. Peninsula and East Bay master-planned communities (Palo Alto Professorville, Hillsborough, Atherton) have strict informal character standards but limited formal HOA. California Civil Code 4735 protects drought-tolerant landscaping against any HOA turf-required covenant. Coastal Commission jurisdiction adds environmental-review layers on any Pacifica, Half Moon Bay, or shoreline parcel independent of HOA status.

San Francisco Tree Services and Ordinances

SF Urban Forestry Council manages street trees under the Public Works Code; homeowners cannot prune or remove them without Urban Forestry approval. Private-property trees designated as Significant or Landmark Trees are protected under the Urban Forestry Ordinance. Licensed arborists through ISA charge $900-$3,800 per mature tree removal. California tree-care licensing under the California Contractors State License Board (CSLB D-49) is the baseline verification. Peninsula communities (Hillsborough, Atherton) have stricter private-tree rules.

San Francisco Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

SF peninsula landscape maintenance runs year-round because mild temperatures eliminate seasonal dormancy for most plants. Winter pruning of deciduous fruit and roses happens December-February. Spring cleanup in March includes moss removal from paths and coastal salt-damage remediation. Summer irrigation runs May through October. Fall cleanup October-November. The October Indian summer window is the prime pour and planting season with minimal fog interference. Atmospheric-river storms December-March drive drainage maintenance.

San Francisco Stormwater Rules

SFPUC stormwater management requirements apply to projects over 2,500 sqft of impervious surface under the Stormwater Management Ordinance. The Better Roofs Ordinance mandates green or solar roofs on many new residential builds. BAWSCA regional stormwater coordination sets peninsula standards. Permeable pavers, green roofs, and rain gardens qualify for Stormwater Management Program rebates. Bioswales and rain gardens along RH-1 and RH-2 frontages qualify for landscape credits.

About landscaping in San Francisco, CA

Why pricing varies in San Francisco. Labor rates here run roughly 46% above the national average per BLS metro data, which is the single biggest driver of how this city's pricing differs from the national midpoint. For landscaping specifically, our cost-multiplier model puts San Francisco at roughly 43% above the national midpoint.

Local conditions. San Francisco has local climate conditions that affect project scheduling and material choices. San Francisco's mild, wet climate makes it one of the easiest landscaping environments in the country. Native rhododendrons, ferns, and conifers thrive with minimal intervention. The challenge is managing overgrowth — maintenance budgets should include seasonal pruning and weed control. Established properties in San Francisco (averaging 52 years) often have mature trees and existing landscape elements that a redesign should work around. Removing mature trees costs $500-2,000 each and eliminates decades of shade value. Design with existing assets, not against them.

Permits, codes, and licensing. Most landscaping projects in San Francisco require a permit; confirm with the local building department before signing.

Contractor market. As a major metro of 837,442 residents, the local contractor market is deep — expect 10+ legitimate bidders on any standard residential job. San Francisco has seen steady growth, which means a healthy supply of established contractors with multi-year track records.

San Francisco, CA landscaping: local considerations

Home stock in San Francisco. The typical San Francisco home is around 52 years old. Most systems are past their first-replacement cycle and approaching their second. Expect landscaping quotes to include mentions of upgraded electrical service, ductwork adjustments, or insulation retrofits that newer homes wouldn't need.

HOA & compliance. HOAs are common in San Francisco. For landscaping, factor in 2-4 weeks of HOA architectural review before starting, and collect pre-approval of materials in writing. HOA rejections after project start can be expensive.

Local market conditions. San Francisco has moderate construction activity. Contractors are busy but accessible with 1-3 weeks' notice. Off-season quotes (contractor-dependent) can run 8-15% below peak rates. San Francisco's large market supports full-service landscape design firms, installation-only crews, and maintenance companies. For projects over $8,000, a dedicated design firm with installation capability typically provides better results than splitting design and installation across separate companies.

Seasonal timing. San Francisco offers a longer landscaping season than cold-climate markets. The busiest period is March through June when spring planting demand peaks. Fall (October-December) is an excellent time for plantings, hardscape projects, and irrigation system installation at lower competition and often better pricing.

Neighborhood patterns. In San Francisco, the highest volume of landscaping work tends to come out of Noe Valley, Mission, Sunset, and Richmond — larger lots, older homes, and higher home values all drive replacement demand. Contractors familiar with these neighborhoods often already know the typical building stock, permit nuances, and HOA quirks.

Geography note. Golden Gate Bridge, Coit Tower, Mission Dolores Park 49 square miles of dense urban infill; staging, parking permits, and dumpster placement add 5-15 percent to most exterior projects These geographic realities subtly shape contractor pricing and availability in San Francisco.

What drives cost. The biggest factor in San Francisco landscaping project pricing is labor cost, running 46% above national benchmarks. For a full yard makeover, that premium alone accounts for $1840-3680 in additional cost. The 52-year average home age in San Francisco means most landscaping project projects encounter at least one behind-the-wall surprise. Experienced local contractors price this risk in; lowball bids from out-of-area contractors often don't.

Red flags to watch for. Watch for landscaping project quotes in San Francisco that lack line-item detail. A professional estimate breaks out labor, materials, permits, and cleanup separately. Lump-sum bids hide margin and make change orders impossible to evaluate. Check that any San Francisco contractor doing landscaping project carries both general liability insurance ($1M minimum) and workers' compensation. Request certificates directly from the insurer, not just copies the contractor provides. For older San Francisco homes (average 52 years), beware of landscaping project quotes that don't mention code compliance. Modern codes have changed significantly since these homes were built — any work that triggers inspection should be priced with code upgrades included.
