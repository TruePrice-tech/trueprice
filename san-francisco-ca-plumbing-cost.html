What San Francisco residents ask about plumbing

Why does my water heater make popping noises?

Popping or rumbling sounds come from sediment buildup at the bottom of the tank. Flushing the tank annually prevents this and extends the heater's lifespan by 2-3 years.

How do I know if I have a slab leak?

Signs include unexplained spikes in your water bill, warm spots on the floor, the sound of running water when fixtures are off, and cracks in the foundation or damp baseboards.

What is a backflow preventer and do I need one?

A backflow preventer stops contaminated water from flowing back into the clean supply. Most municipalities require them on irrigation systems and commercial properties. Some require annual testing.

How often should I have my plumbing inspected in San Francisco?

A yearly plumbing check in San Francisco prevents the kind of surprises that cost thousands. The priority items: water heater (8-12 year lifespan, check anode rod and sediment), braided steel supply lines under sinks (replace after 10 years regardless of appearance), and main sewer line condition. Camera sewer inspections ($150-$400) every 3-5 years are standard practice for older homes.

What should a plumbing quote include in San Francisco?

A professional plumbing quote in San Francisco should itemize: labor hours, material costs and brands, permit fees if applicable, cleanup, and warranty terms. The quote should include the plumber's license number and proof of insurance. Get three written quotes for any job over $500 to ensure fair pricing.

When is the best time to schedule plumbing work in San Francisco?

San Francisco homeowners should target late spring or early fall for planned plumbing work. Dry weather simplifies any excavation and contractor availability is better, which translates to better availability and pricing. Emergency service runs 50-100% more than scheduled jobs, so proactive replacement of aging equipment is the financially smarter path.

San Francisco: sewer infrastructure

SFPUC operates a combined sewer system across most of San Francisco, unique among major California cities. The Oceanside and Southeast Water Pollution Control Plants treat combined flow. The 1970s Wastewater Master Plan built the city's first-in-California combined-sewer treatment system, and the ongoing Sewer System Improvement Program (SSIP) replaces aging infrastructure. Backwater valves at the building trap plus sump pumps in low-lying ground-floor areas are essential, and SFPUC's Non-Potable Water Program supports graywater reuse.

SF's combined sewer system handles both sanitary and stormwater, requiring SSIP infrastructure investment. SFPUC's Stormwater Management Ordinance requires capture and infiltration for any new impervious work over 2,500 sqft. Atmospheric river events in January and February test the system annually. The Green Infrastructure Plan targets 25 percent impervious-area reduction citywide. King-tide flooding on the Embarcadero and Mission Creek drives sea-level-rise adaptation planning that affects low-lying plumbing design.
