San Jose concrete FAQ

How thick should a concrete driveway be?

Residential driveways should be 4 inches thick for cars and 5-6 inches for heavy vehicles like RVs. A 6-inch compacted gravel base underneath is essential. Thickened edges (6 inches) prevent cracking at the perimeter.

Is concrete or asphalt better for a driveway?

Concrete lasts 30+ years vs. 15-20 for asphalt. Concrete costs more upfront ($6-$12/sqft vs. $3-$6/sqft) but needs less maintenance. Asphalt needs resealing every 3-5 years and is softer in extreme heat.

Do I need a permit for concrete work in San Jose?

In San Jose, permits are typically required for driveways, sidewalks, and any concrete work near property lines or public right-of-way. Backyard patios may be exempt depending on size. Check local requirements.

What is stamped concrete?

Stamped concrete uses patterned mats pressed into wet concrete to mimic brick, stone, slate, or wood. It costs $12-$25/sqft installed. It requires resealing every 2-3 years to maintain appearance and prevent wear.

What causes concrete to crack?

Common causes: shrinkage during curing, frost heave from inadequate base prep, tree roots, heavy vehicle loads beyond design, poor control joint placement, and water pooling due to improper grading.

Climate Impact on San Jose Concrete

San Jose's Mediterranean climate is relatively gentle on residential concrete: the mild winters with rare frost events eliminate the freeze-thaw spalling that dominates cold-climate concrete maintenance. San Jose's Mediterranean climate is relatively gentle on residential concrete: the mild winters with rare frost events eliminate the freeze-thaw spalling that dominates cold-climate concrete maintenance. The primary climate-driven degradation is UV-accelerated surface chalking and color fading on decorative concrete (stamped, stained, exposed aggregate) because the Santa Cruz Mountains' UV intensity is higher than fog-shielded coastal cities. Standard broom-finish concrete performs well for 20-30 years with minimal climate-related maintenance. The September heat-dome events (105F+) can cause rapid moisture loss in fresh concrete if curing protocol is not followed, but these events are brief (7-14 days) and experienced South Bay contractors manage the schedule accordingly.

What local disaster informs your detailing? San Jose's primary concrete risk is seismic: the 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake (6.9M) cracked and displaced residential concrete across the South Bay, with the most severe damage in the liquefaction-prone areas of north San Jose and Japantown where Bay Mud amplified ground motion. Post-earthquake concrete inspection should include level verification (settling of one side relative to the other), crack-width measurement (cracks wider than 1/4 inch indicate structural displacement), and expansion-joint integrity assessment. The December 2022-January 2023 atmospheric-river flooding produced soil erosion beneath concrete slabs along the Coyote Creek floodplain, creating voids that manifest as settling and cracking months later.

Standard residential flatwork (driveways, patios, walkways under 30 inches above grade) typically requires an over-the-counter permit. California Title 24 interaction: any concrete work that affects the building envelope (new patio covers, garage floor modifications) may trigger energy-code compliance review. CalRecycle requires 65% demolition-debris diversion with disposal documentation at final inspection. Naglee Park Historic District may review visible hardscape changes on contributing properties.

When to Pour Concrete in San Jose

San Jose's concrete pouring season runs year-round with marine-layer moderation providing comfortable working conditions for most months. The optimal windows: March through June (stable weather, moderate temperatures, maximum daylight) and September through November (post-heat-dome, pre-rain). The September heat-dome period (105F+ for 7-14 days) restricts pours to early morning. December through February rain events create 1-3 week scheduling delays during the wettest months. The marine layer's morning fog does not typically delay concrete work because it burns off by 10-11am, allowing midday and afternoon pours during the dry season.
