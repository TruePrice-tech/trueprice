What San Jose residents ask about landscaping

How much does a retaining wall cost?

Retaining walls cost $20-$50 per square face foot for concrete block, $25-$60 for natural stone, and $15-$30 for timber. A 50 linear foot wall at 3 feet tall runs $3,000-$9,000 installed.

What is xeriscaping?

Xeriscaping uses drought-tolerant plants, efficient irrigation, and permeable surfaces to minimize water use. It cuts outdoor water consumption 50-75% and is increasingly required in water-restricted regions.

Do I need a permit for landscaping in San Jose?

Most landscaping in San Jose does not require permits. Exceptions include retaining walls over 4 feet, grading that changes drainage, connecting to city water for irrigation, and work near property lines.

How often should I replace mulch?

Replenish organic mulch annually (top off to 3 inches). Full replacement every 2-3 years prevents matting and fungal buildup. Rock and rubber mulch last 5-10 years but do not improve soil.

What is the cheapest way to landscape a yard?

Seed instead of sod, use native plants from local nurseries, mulch with free municipal compost, and phase the project over 2-3 seasons. A basic DIY front yard makeover can be done for $500-$1,500.

Native plant palette across San Jose

San Jose's Mediterranean climate supports one of the richest native-plant palettes in this group: California native oaks (valley oak, coast live oak), manzanita, ceanothus, California fuchsia, deer grass, and toyon thrive with zero supplemental irrigation once established. The Santa Clara Valley Water District's landscape-rebate program incentivizes turf conversion to drought-tolerant native plantings. Willow Glen and Rose Garden neighborhoods have mature heritage oak canopies that constrain planting plans because the valley oaks' shallow root systems compete for water and nutrients within 30-40 feet of the trunk. Soil conditions and amendments for San Jose homeowners

San Jose soil: Bay Mud and younger alluvium in north San Jose require organic amendment to improve drainage before planting. Almaden Valley's older alluvium is well-drained but nutrient-depleted from decades of residential use; 3-4 inches of compost tilled into the top 8 inches restores biological activity. Evergreen's Franciscan Complex expansive clay needs gypsum amendment and raised beds for plants sensitive to seasonal waterlogging. The Hetch Hetchy/local-blend moderate water hardness (100-150 ppm) is gentle on irrigation systems and plantings. Heritage oak root-zone protection (no grade changes, no compaction, no irrigation within the drip line) is a PBCE-enforced requirement on properties with protected oaks.

Irrigation rules and rebates: a San Jose guide

Santa Clara Valley Water District enforces permanent irrigation efficiency standards: smart controllers with rain sensors are mandatory on new installations, drip irrigation is required for all shrub and tree beds, and spray irrigation is restricted to turf areas with precipitation rates matched to application. Rotating-nozzle heads ($3-$5 each) reduce runoff on sloped properties. The District offers $2/sqft turf-conversion rebates (up to $6,000 per property). Water costs from San Jose Water Company ($0.008-$0.012/gallon tiered) make irrigation efficiency a significant financial motivator beyond regulatory compliance. The California Model Water Efficient Landscape Ordinance (MWELO) governs new and renovated landscapes over 500 sqft.

San Jose turf-conversion incentives: Santa Clara Valley Water District offers $2/sqft (up to $6,000) for residential turf removal and replacement with water-efficient landscape. BayREN provides complementary funding for whole-home water efficiency including landscape. California MWELO governs all new and renovated landscapes. The combined incentive stack makes turf conversion cost-neutral on projects over 500 sqft because the rebate covers 50-70% of the replacement planting cost. Willow Glen and Rose Garden have the highest conversion rates because the mature oak canopy makes turf maintenance difficult (oak-shade kills most turf grasses) and the native-plant alternative thrives under the oaks.

San Jose and lawn and turf choices

San Jose lawn: tall fescue and perennial ryegrass blends dominate traditional lawns. UC Verde buffalograss and hybrid bermuda serve the drought-tolerant lawn market. Turf alternatives (clover, creeping thyme, dymondia) are increasingly popular in Willow Glen and Naglee Park for their zero-to-low irrigation requirement. The Santa Clara Valley Water District's $2/sqft turf-removal rebate has driven significant lawn-to-native conversion since 2015. Artificial turf ($8-$14/sqft installed) has growing share in Cambrian Park and Almaden Valley for pet-run areas and front-yard aesthetic consistency. The California drought-consciousness culture makes any large-lawn installation increasingly difficult to justify aesthetically and economically.

Understanding invasive pests and plants in San Jose

San Jose invasive species: English ivy, French broom, and Himalayan blackberry are the most aggressive landscape invaders. Sudden Oak Death (Phytophthora ramorum) threatens the heritage coast live oak population -- avoid planting bay laurel (a host species) within 15 feet of oaks. Bark beetles stress drought-weakened oaks during dry years. Gophers and ground squirrels are the primary landscape pests throughout the Valley. Integrated pest management (IPM) is the standard approach; Santa Clara County provides free IPM consultation through the UC Master Gardener program.

San Jose: HOA and design review

San Jose HOA landscape restrictions: moderate in Cambrian Park and Almaden Valley master-planned communities. Common requirements: maintained front lawn or approved turf alternative, no dead/dying plants visible from street, maximum hedge height, tree-trimming at sidewalk and driveway clearance. Naglee Park Historic District may review visible landscape changes on contributing properties. California AB 2104 (2015) prohibits HOAs from fining homeowners for brown lawns during declared drought emergencies. The tech-affluent homeowner culture favors design-forward landscapes that meet HOA standards while expressing environmental values through native-plant and drought-tolerant choices.

San Jose's tree services and ordinances

San Jose tree service: heritage valley oaks and coast live oaks are protected under the City's tree-preservation ordinance. Removal of any protected tree requires PBCE permit and may require replacement planting at 2:1 ratio. Tree pruning (structural, clearance, deadwood) runs $300-$1,500 per tree depending on size and access. ISA-certified arborists ($200-$400 consultation) provide independent assessment versus tree-service companies who profit from removal. The heritage-oak canopy in Willow Glen and Rose Garden is the neighborhood's defining landscape asset; any landscape plan should design around existing oaks rather than remove them.

San Jose Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

San Jose seasonal landscape calendar: January-February dormant-season pruning of deciduous trees and shrubs. March-April spring planting (the primary planting window) after rain season and before summer heat. May-June transition to summer irrigation schedules. July-September reduced maintenance (drought-tolerant natives need no summer care). October-November fall planting (the secondary window, preferred for native trees and shrubs because fall-planted roots establish during the winter rains). December cleanup of fall leaf drop from heritage oaks and deciduous trees. Professional maintenance contracts: $200-$400/month for full-service including irrigation management.

Stormwater rules: San Jose edition

San Jose stormwater management through landscaping: Santa Clara Valley Water District requires all new and renovated landscapes to manage stormwater on-site where feasible. Rain gardens, bioswales, and permeable hardscape reduce runoff and may qualify for stormwater-fee credits. The December 2022-January 2023 atmospheric-river flooding demonstrated that landscape grading and drainage design must handle extreme precipitation events, not just average rainfall. Proper lot grading away from foundations prevents the soil saturation that drives foundation damage on the Bay Mud and expansive clay soils.
