What San Jose residents ask about painting

How many coats of paint do I need?

Two coats over primer is standard. Going from dark to light or light to dark may need a tinted primer plus two topcoats. Quality paint with good coverage sometimes looks fine in one coat but two is always more durable.

Do I need a permit to paint my house in San Jose?

Painting typically does not require a permit in San Jose. The exception is homes in historic districts where exterior color changes may need approval from a review board.

What paint sheen should I use?

Flat/matte hides imperfections (ceilings, low-traffic walls). Eggshell and satin work for most walls. Semi-gloss is ideal for trim, doors, and kitchens/baths. High-gloss is for accent trim and furniture.

How much does paint prep work cost?

Prep is 50-70% of the total job cost. Scraping, sanding, caulking, priming, and repairs can cost $1,500-$4,000 on an exterior. Skipping prep leads to peeling paint within 1-2 years.

San Jose Climate and Paint Durability

San Jose's Mediterranean climate delivers the most intense UV exposure in the Bay Area because the Santa Cruz Mountains block the Pacific marine layer that protects coastal cities. South and west exposures on Willow Glen Craftsman homes and Cambrian Park ranch houses fade paint 2-3 years faster than fog-shielded coastal properties 40 miles away. The dry summer-wet winter cycle concentrates moisture damage to the November-March period when sustained rain events penetrate any gaps in the paint film. Wildfire smoke events from Santa Cruz Mountains fires deposit fine ash on painted surfaces that embeds in the topcoat and accelerates yellowing if not cleaned within 30 days. The September heat-dome events (105F+) can blister fresh paint applied within the previous 30 days on south-facing surfaces.

San Jose's dry Mediterranean climate produces minimal mildew risk compared to humid markets like Baltimore or Orlando. North-facing surfaces under heavy tree canopy in Willow Glen and Rose Garden may develop mild biological growth during the November-March wet season, but standard power washing removes it without mildewcide treatment. The primary moisture concern is at the stucco-to-wood trim interface where differential expansion traps moisture: caulking these joints during paint prep prevents the peeling and wood-rot that develops over 3-5 years of wet-season moisture infiltration. Irrigation overspray on stucco walls causes efflorescence (white mineral deposits) that must be treated with dilute muriatic acid before painting.

San Jose Permits and Lead Paint Rules

San Jose does not require permits for exterior painting unless the project involves structural repair (siding replacement, wood-rot remediation, or lead-abatement scope). CSLB C-33 painting license is mandatory for California painting contractors; verify at cslb.ca.gov. EPA RRP (Renovation, Repair, and Painting) Rule compliance is mandatory on pre-1978 homes for any work disturbing more than 6 sqft of painted surface per room (exterior: per side). RRP certification adds $500-$1,500 to the project cost for containment, documentation, and disposal. CalRecycle paint-debris disposal requirements apply to all paint projects generating hazardous waste.

San Jose Seasonal Painting Windows

The painting season in San Jose runs year-round due to the Mediterranean climate, but optimal windows differ by scope: exterior painting is best October through May when temperatures stay below 90F and UV intensity is moderate enough for proper curing. June through September exterior work requires early-morning application (before 10am) on south and west exposures because direct sun above 90F causes latex to skin over before bonding, producing peeling within 6-12 months. The September heat-dome period (105F+ for 7-14 days) creates a complete blackout window for exterior paint application. Interior painting proceeds year-round without climate constraints. Scheduling 3-4 weeks ahead is standard for quality contractors during the October-May peak season.

San Jose Common Contractor Upsells

San Jose painting upsells worth accepting: caulking at all trim joints and window perimeters ($400-$900, essential for the dry-wet cycling), primer coat on all color changes and bare-wood areas ($600-$1,500, extends finish-coat adhesion by 3-5 years), and power washing at 2,000-2,500 PSI ($300-$600, removes the accumulated UV-degraded surface and ensures adhesion). Upsells to decline: "premium" clear-coat topcoat over the finish (minimal value on quality acrylic latex), spray-applied finish without back-brushing (looks smooth but lacks the mechanical adhesion that brush and roller provide), and "10-year" warranty claims from contractors with less than 5 years in the Bay Area market.
