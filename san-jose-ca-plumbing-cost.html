Questions San Jose homeowners ask about plumbing

How long do plumbing fixtures typically last?

Faucets last 15-20 years, toilets 25-50 years, water heaters 8-12 years (tank) or 15-20 years (tankless), and supply lines should be replaced every 20-25 years.

What is the difference between copper and PEX piping?

Copper lasts 50+ years, resists bacteria, and has proven durability. PEX costs 60% less, installs faster, resists freezing better, and requires fewer joints. Both are code-compliant.

Is trenchless sewer repair worth the extra cost?

Trenchless repair costs 20-40% more but avoids excavating your yard, driveway, or landscaping. It is faster and causes less property damage, often making it cheaper overall.

How much does it cost to replace a water heater?

A standard 50-gallon tank water heater runs $1,200-$2,500 installed. Tankless units cost $2,500-$5,000 but last roughly twice as long and cut energy bills 20-30%.

How often should I have my plumbing inspected in San Jose?

An annual plumbing inspection catches small issues before they become emergencies. In San Jose, pay special attention to water heater condition (they last 8-12 years), supply line connections under sinks (especially braided steel lines older than 10 years), and the main sewer line. A sewer camera inspection ($150-$400) every 3-5 years is worthwhile for homes older than 20 years.

What should a plumbing quote include in San Jose?

Every San Jose plumbing quote should break down: labor, materials with brand names, permit fees where required, disposal, and warranty coverage. Verify the plumber's license number and insurance are documented. For any job over $500, three written quotes with identical scope give you a reliable pricing baseline.

When is the best time to schedule plumbing work in San Jose?

Plan your San Jose plumbing projects for late spring or early fall. Dry weather simplifies any excavation and contractor availability is better, and you avoid the emergency premium that adds 50-100% to the bill. Water heaters, supply lines, and fixtures nearing end-of-life are worth replacing on your schedule rather than theirs.

San Jose Water Utility and Supply

San Jose Water Company (SJWC), a privately owned utility serving most of the South Bay, sources water from the Santa Clara Valley Water District's blend of Hetch Hetchy Aqueduct imported Sierra Nevada surface water and local groundwater from the Santa Clara Subbasin. The blended supply delivers moderate hardness at 100-150 ppm, gentler on plumbing infrastructure than the hard-water markets of Texas and the desert Southwest. SJWC rates run among the highest in the nation at $0.008-$0.012 per gallon for Tier 1 residential, which makes leak detection and water-efficiency upgrades higher-ROI investments in San Jose than in low-rate markets. The drought-driven mandatory conservation period (2014-2017) permanently changed the Silicon Valley water culture: low-flow fixtures, tankless water heaters, and greywater systems are common specification items rather than premium upsells.

The Hetch Hetchy blend softens seasonal variation: winter imported water runs 60-80 ppm while summer groundwater-dominant supply reaches 120-150 ppm. This moderate hardness is kind to standard plumbing fixtures and tank water heaters, which generally reach their full rated lifespan without aggressive descaling. Tankless water heaters still benefit from annual flush-and-descale maintenance at $150-$250 because the intermittent higher-hardness summer supply deposits minerals in the heat exchanger over time. Copper supply lines installed in the 1960s-70s ranch-house belt (Cambrian Park, Almaden Valley, Evergreen) are approaching the 50-60 year pinhole-leak threshold.

Sewer infrastructure around San Jose

San Jose operates its own wastewater collection system feeding the San Jose-Santa Clara Regional Wastewater Facility, the largest tertiary treatment plant west of the Mississippi. The city's aggressive I&I (inflow and infiltration) reduction program means sewer lateral condition matters during permit review: PBCE may require sewer-lateral camera inspection on homes built before 1970 as a condition of major plumbing permits. Willow Glen and Naglee Park homes with original vitrified-clay laterals from the 1920s-40s are the highest-risk segment for root intrusion and joint separation. PVC lateral replacement through the lateral from the house to the city main runs $4,000-$8,000 in San Jose depending on depth and length.

San Jose's stormwater runs through a separate system from sanitary sewer, discharging into Coyote Creek, Guadalupe River, and ultimately the Bay. The December 2022-January 2023 atmospheric-river flooding demonstrated that Coyote Creek overflow produces widespread ground-level flooding across downtown, Japantown, and the Monterey Road corridor that can overwhelm sanitary-sewer connections through backflow. Backflow prevention valves ($300-$600 installed) are essential in the Coyote Creek floodplain. Sump pumps are rare in San Jose because most homes lack basements, but crawlspace drainage on Willow Glen and Naglee Park homes becomes critical during heavy rain events.

Water heater culture: a San Jose guide

Silicon Valley's high electricity rates ($0.30+/kWh from PG&E/SJCE) and gas rates ($1.75/therm) make water-heater operating cost a significant homeowner consideration. Tankless gas water heaters (Rinnai, Navien, Noritz) dominate the upgrade market in Cambrian Park and Almaden Valley because the continuous hot-water supply and 20+ year lifespan justify the $3,000-$4,500 installed cost versus $1,200-$1,800 for standard tanks. Heat-pump water heaters (Rheem, Bradford White) are gaining share driven by BayREN and SJCE electrification incentives. The California energy-code mandate for solar-ready water heating on new construction has created a hybrid-heat-pump water heater market that did not exist before 2020.

San Jose freeze risk and winterization: overview

San Jose's mild winters (rarely below 35F) create minimal freeze risk for interior plumbing, but exposed outdoor pipes in Almaden Valley and the Evergreen foothills can freeze during the handful of overnight frost events each winter. Standard winterization: insulate all outdoor spigots and exposed supply lines in unconditioned garages. The low freeze risk means San Jose plumbers rarely handle freeze-burst emergencies, which creates a skill gap versus cold-climate markets: if a rare freeze event does occur, the response queue fills quickly because the local trade has limited surge capacity for freeze-related work.

Emergency vs Scheduled Plumbing near San Jose

San Jose plumbing emergencies (burst pipes, sewer backups, water heater failures) run $200-$500 for the service call plus parts and labor. Multiple 24/7 services operate in the South Bay, but Silicon Valley's high labor rates mean emergency calls run 25-35% above national averages. The most common emergency calls are sewer-lateral root blockages in Willow Glen and Naglee Park (tree-root season peaks in late summer), water-heater tank failures (average 10-12 year tank life in the moderate-hardness water), and kitchen drain blockages in older homes with undersized 1.5-inch drain lines that current code sizes at 2 inches.

San Jose's wells and septic

San Jose is fully served by SJWC municipal water and the city's sanitary sewer system. There are no residential wells or septic systems within city limits. Outlying areas of unincorporated Santa Clara County (south of Bailey Avenue, east of the Evergreen foothills) may have well water and septic, but these fall outside San Jose's permit jurisdiction. The Hetch Hetchy municipal supply is among the highest-quality municipal water sources in the United States, requiring minimal treatment.
