Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a gutter cost in Seattle?

Most homeowners in Seattle pay between $1,150 and $1,750 for gutter installation, depending on material and yard size.

What is the cheapest gutter material?

Aluminum gutters are the most affordable option at $6-$12 per linear foot installed. Vinyl is comparable at $5-$11 per linear foot but less durable in extreme climates.

How long does a wood gutter last?

A well-maintained wood gutter typically lasts 15-20 years. Regular staining and sealing every 2-3 years can significantly extend its lifespan. Cedar and redwood last longer than pine.

Seattle Rainfall and Gutter Drainage

37.5 inches
annual rainfall in Seattle

Seattle receives about 37-38 inches of rain per year, but the perception of constant rain comes from the 150+ days of measurable precipitation. Seattle's rain is typically light and steady rather than intense. However, atmospheric river events in fall and winter can deliver heavy rain over multiple days, and these sustained events test gutter capacity more than brief thunderstorms. The constant moisture means gutters in Seattle are wet for 6+ months of the year.

Seattle-specific gutter sizing

Standard 5-inch K-style gutters move about 1.2 gallons per second, adequate for moderate rainfall up to 2 inches per hour. Seattle rainfall intensity routinely exceeds that threshold, so 6-inch oversized gutters with 40% more capacity are worth the $2-$3 per linear foot upcharge in Capitol Hill, Ballard, and Fremont.

Downspout capacity and placement

Seattle's hilly terrain and glacial till soil create complex drainage requirements. Many homes on hillside lots require downspouts connected to tightline drainage systems that route water to the street rather than allowing surface discharge, which can contribute to the landslide risk that affects many Seattle neighborhoods.

Rule of thumb for Seattle: one downspout per 20-30 linear feet of gutter. Each 2x3-inch downspout drains roughly 600 sq ft; step up to 3x4-inch on Capitol Hill, Ballard, and Fremont homes with large roof planes or steep pitches that concentrate runoff.

Gutter Guard Analysis for Seattle

Seattle's tree canopy is very heavy; the prevalent species are Douglas fir, western red cedar, big leaf maple, alder, birch. Guard performance hinges on local debris type, and Capitol Hill, Ballard, and Fremont homes generally share the same mix.

Primary debris types in Seattle

Cedar fronds and bark strips year-round, Douglas fir needles, massive big leaf maple leaf drop in October-November, moss growth inside gutters, and alder catkins in spring.

Recommended guard style

Micro-mesh guards are the clear winner in Seattle because they prevent moss growth inside the gutter channel (a major Seattle-specific problem) while handling the mix of needles, fronds, and large maple leaves; reverse-curve guards allow moss to grow on the gutter surface in Seattle's wet climate.

Seattle guard pricing tiers

Expect $7-$15/LF for micro-mesh in Seattle, $4-$8/LF for screen, $10-$18/LF for reverse-curve, $2-$5/LF for foam (don't). National-brand pricing from LeafFilter or Gutter Helmet lands $15-$35/LF with warranty bundled; a local Seattle installer offering the same micro-mesh quality typically beats that by 30-50%.

Every gutter guard needs some maintenance — even the best micro-mesh products in Seattle need periodic surface brushing for pollen and dust every 1-2 years. "Never clean your gutters again" is marketing, not a realistic promise in Seattle.

Winter Gutter Concerns in Seattle

Freeze risk level for Seattle: low.

Seattle rarely experiences hard freezes, but the occasional cold snap (like December 2022) can freeze gutters for several days. The bigger issue is the sustained weight of wet debris and standing water in gutters that are not draining properly. Heated cables are unnecessary for almost all Seattle homes. The exception is homes at higher elevations near the Cascades foothills where overnight freezes are more common from December through February.

What Seattle homeowners should know

Seattle rarely produces ice-dam conditions. The detail that still matters: correct 1/4-inch-per-10-foot pitch so standing water doesn't pool and freeze during the occasional hard night.

Seattle DIY vs. hired-help tradeoff

DIY makes sense on single-story Seattle homes with flat ground for the ladder. Two-story Capitol Hill, Ballard, and Fremont homes, sloped yards, and complex rooflines should go pro — US ladder-fall stats make the $150 cleaning a bargain.

What to Watch for in Seattle Gutter Bids

Here are the Seattle gutter-bid patterns that burn homeowners most often.

Bidding Seattle roofs without rainfall context

Seattle receives 37.5 inches of rain annually. A contractor bidding standard 5-inch gutters without mentioning Seattle rainfall intensity or your specific roof area is sizing for convenience, not performance. Homes in Capitol Hill, Ballard, and Fremont with roof areas over 1,500 sq ft draining to a single run should default to 6-inch gutters.

Seattle gutter slope problems

Gutters should slope toward downspouts at about 1/4 inch per 10 feet of run. Flat gutters pool water, breed mosquitoes, and overflow in storms. After install, run a simple Seattle water test: pour water at the high end and confirm steady flow to the downspout with no pooling. If water stands, pitch needs correction.

No splash blocks or downspout extensions

Seattle downspouts need splash blocks, extensions, or buried drain tile — without them, 37.5 inches of annual rain lands at the foundation each year. Foundation problems, wet basements, and landscape erosion follow. Seattle bids that ignore discharge routing are missing half the job.

"Seamless" that actually has seams

Real seamless means the installer rolls the full gutter run on your Seattle property from continuous coil stock; seams appear only at corners, outlets, and end caps. A "seamless" Seattle bid that mentions joining factory-cut sections with splice connectors is sectional gutter at seamless pricing — the single most common install-side scam.

Best Time to Install Gutters in Seattle

Best months

June through September (the dry window)

Seattle's brief dry season (July-September) is the ideal window for gutter installation. Contractors prefer working in dry conditions, sealant cures properly, and you can test the new system with garden hose flow before the rains arrive. Fall and winter installation is possible but expect 10-20% higher labor costs for wet-weather work.

Peak pricing / low availability

October through February (wet season makes installation difficult and demand for repairs peaks)

Expect 10-20% higher labor costs and longer lead times during Seattle peak season. Emergency gutter repairs after Seattle storms command premium pricing regardless of month.
