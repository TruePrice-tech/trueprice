What SEER and AFUE to Aim For in Seattle

Cooling efficiency target

16+ SEER

16 SEER is the sensible Seattle minimum because heating, not cooling, dominates your Seattle City Light (electric) bill. The 14-to-16 SEER jump delivers modest savings here; the 16-to-20 jump delivers almost none. Put the efficiency budget toward the furnace or a cold-climate heat pump instead.

Heating efficiency target

92%+ AFUE

Seattle's heating season is meaningful but not extreme, so 90-92% AFUE is the sensible sweet spot. Permits, Codes, and Inspections for Seattle HVAC

Permit authority: Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections (SDCI). SDCI issues mechanical permits through its Accela portal; Washington State requires an HVAC/R 06A specialty contractor license that is distinct from the general contractor license, and Seattle additionally enforces its Energy Code Amendment that exceeds the Washington State Energy Code for new equipment.

Seattle permit review checklist. Seattle Green Building Code requires a commissioning checklist for new heat pump installations and City Light interconnection for any system drawing more than specified residential service amperage; commissioning documentation ties to the electrical permit not the mechanical permit. The Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections mechanical permit review covers Manual J sizing, electrical tie-in, gas line sizing if applicable, line-set routing, the condensate path, and whatever duct-leakage testing WA specifies. On top of that baseline, Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections reviews condensate routing, combustion-air make-up, and line-set insulation as standard WA checklist items. Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections verifies ductless mini-split heat pumps on craftsman bungalows in Ballard, Wallingford, and Green Lake where there is no existing ductwork-specific items that generic national HVAC templates miss.

Pulling the Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections permit is always the right call in Seattle. Skipping the permit in Seattle voids Mitsubishi's warranty and any other manufacturer coverage, creates an MLS-listed defect at sale time, and sticks you with liability: a botched Seattle furnace install can vent CO through a cracked heat exchanger or undersized flue, and Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections is the last real check before that goes live in your living room. A Seattle contractor's "we'll skip the permit" pitch mostly saves them from Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections scrutiny, not you anything meaningful. Permit-holder liability belongs to the installer under WA law; don't let them transfer it via an owner-builder filing.

A2L refrigerant arrives in Seattle. Every 2026 residential AC or heat pump going into a Seattle home runs R-454B (Honeywell's Solstice N41) or R-32 (the Daikin and Mitsubishi preference) under the AIM Act phase-down. Mitsubishi distribution in WA still moves residual pre-2025 R-410A stock, but that pipeline is closing fast. A2L sits in flammability class 2L versus class 1 for R-410A, so installation practice tightens under UL 60335-2-40. The new requirements: charge-to-room-volume minimums, leak-detection sensors on heads above certain tonnages, and A2L-specific brazing procedures. WA licensing does not equate to A2L certification; the Seattle shop you hire should produce A2L training documentation independently.
