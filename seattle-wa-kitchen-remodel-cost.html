Seattle kitchen remodel FAQ

Should I refinish or replace my cabinets?

Refinish if the cabinet boxes are solid wood and structurally sound. Replace if they are particleboard, water-damaged, or you want to change the layout. Refinishing costs 30-50% of new cabinets.

How much do new kitchen appliances cost?

A mid-range appliance package (range, refrigerator, dishwasher, microwave) costs $3,000-$6,000. Premium brands run $8,000-$15,000+. Buy during holiday sales for 20-40% savings.

What is the most expensive part of a kitchen remodel?

Cabinets are typically the largest single expense at 30-40% of the total budget. Countertops are second at 10-15%, followed by labor, appliances, and flooring.

Kitchen stock and typical layouts near Seattle

Seattle kitchen stock runs from 1920s Ballard, Fremont, and Queen Anne craftsman bungalows (90-130 sqft with original pantry) through 1950s Maple Leaf and View Ridge mid-century ranches (150-210 sqft) to 2000s tract townhouses in Columbia City and Rainier Valley (140-220 sqft with vertically stacked layouts). Magnolia mid-century post-and-beam kitchens have exposed-beam constraints. Capitol Hill Victorian townhouses have stairs immediately adjacent to the kitchen that complicate expansion plans.

Seattle's hilly terrain means many kitchens sit on daylight-basement or split-level floorplans with limited hood-vent exterior options. Tight 3,000-4,000 sqft lot sizes in Fremont and Ballard constrain kitchen addition even where setbacks technically allow. SDCI Director's Rule 11-2019 caps impervious surface on SF5000 lots at 35 percent and any hardscape tied to a kitchen expansion must fit the overall lot-coverage budget. Critical Areas Ordinance overlays affect steep-slope parcels in West Seattle and Magnolia.

Understanding permits and electrical scope in Seattle

Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections (SDCI) issues permits via ACCELA with 4-8 week turnaround on kitchen remodels. Seattle City Light panel upgrades to 200A run $3,500-$6,500; overhead service conversion to underground is common in older neighborhoods. Washington adopted the 2020 NEC and requires AFCI/GFCI combo protection on kitchen receptacles. Seattle Energy Code requires induction rough-in and heat-pump-ready electrical on any new kitchen permit filed after 2023 per the Building Emissions Performance Standard.

SDCI ACCELA runs 4-8 weeks on kitchen permits, longer than most major cities. Bellevue runs 4-6 weeks. Kirkland and Redmond are typically faster. Island County, Kitsap, and Snohomish County permits for unincorporated areas have varying turnaround. Historic district review through the Seattle Landmarks Preservation Board adds 6-12 weeks on any exterior-visible work in Pioneer Square, Columbia City, International District, or the Pike Place Market Historical District.

Seattle: plumbing, water, and rough-in

Seattle Public Utilities delivers soft water (45-80 ppm TDS) from Cedar and Tolt River watersheds, one of the softest major-metro waters in the country. Crawlspace foundations on Ballard and Fremont bungalows allow straightforward plumbing relocation. Master plumbers (WA L&I-licensed) charge $110-$160/hour. The Washington Plumbing Code amends the UPC; expansion tanks are required on any replacement water heater. Galvanized-to-copper transitions are common in 1920s-40s houses and must be replaced during any plumbing rough-in.

Seattle kitchen remodels typically encounter a gas or electric tank in a basement utility room. Puget Sound Energy rebates pay $1,200-$1,500 for heat-pump water heater swaps. Rheem ProTerra and AO Smith Voltex are common. Seattle's mild climate makes basement heat-pump water heaters highly efficient year-round, and the Seattle Energy Code requires heat-pump-ready electrical rough-in on any kitchen remodel. Tankless gas is less common than most markets because of the city's electrification push.
