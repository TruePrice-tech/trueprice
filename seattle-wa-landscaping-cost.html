Seattle landscaping: answers to common questions

What is the cheapest way to landscape a yard?

Seed instead of sod, use native plants from local nurseries, mulch with free municipal compost, and phase the project over 2-3 seasons. A basic DIY front yard makeover can be done for $500-$1,500.

How much does mulch cost?

Bulk mulch costs $25-$50 per cubic yard delivered. Plan 3 inches deep and 1 yard covers 108 sqft. A 1,000 sqft bed needs about 10 yards ($250-$500). Professional installation adds $30-$50/yard.

How much does a sprinkler system cost?

A residential irrigation system costs $2,500-$5,000 for a quarter-acre lot. Smart controllers add $100-$300 and reduce water usage 20-40%. Drip irrigation zones for beds cost $500-$1,000 extra.

How much should I pay upfront?

Never pay more than 10-15% as a deposit before work begins. Standard payment structure: deposit, progress payment when materials arrive, final payment on completion.

What should I look for in a contractor quote?

A professional quote breaks out labor, materials, permits, and cleanup separately. It includes a timeline, payment schedule, warranty terms, and the contractor's license number.

Most landscaping projects in Seattle require a permit. Your contractor should pull the permit and schedule the inspection. Never pull permits in your own name.

Seattle's soil conditions and amendments

Seattle soils are Vashon glacial till with Lawton clay lenses across the Puget Lowland. Hillside lots on unstable Duwamish fill require geotechnical sign-off. Cedar Grove compost and GroCo biosolid amendments dominate the Seattle market. WSU Puyallup Research Center soil testing ($40-$90) provides the baseline. The wet climate means pH trends acidic (5.0-6.0) under conifer canopy, and lime applications are common before lawn establishment. Pumice and horticultural sand amendments address drainage issues.

Understanding irrigation rules and rebates in Seattle

Seattle Public Utilities does not mandate fixed residential watering-day schedules but requires backflow prevention (ASSE 1013 RPZ) on all systems per Washington State Plumbing Code. WSDOH certifies backflow assembly testers (BAT certification). Cascade Water Alliance and South Bainbridge Water provide separate rebate programs. Seattle City Light does not offer irrigation-specific rebates but Savings Within Reach program rebates heat-pump-irrigation combinations. Peak hours (10am-4pm) watering is actively discouraged.

Seattle RainWise rebate program pays up to $6/sqft of impervious area reduced through rain gardens, cisterns, and permeable paving. Savings Within Reach and Cascade Water Alliance rebates stack for qualifying conversions. The King County Natural Yard Care program provides education and design subsidies. Puget Sound Energy does not offer direct turf-replacement rebates. Smart-controller rebates through Cascade Water Alliance pay $50-$150. Residential rain-barrel rebates pay up to $150.

Seattle Lawn and Turf Choices

Cool-season tall fescue and perennial ryegrass blends dominate Seattle residential lawns. Red fescue (Creeping red fescue, Chewings fescue) is common on shaded lots. No-mow fescue blends (Eco-Lawn, Pearl's Premium) are gaining in Ballard and Phinney Ridge sustainability-focused yards. Kentucky bluegrass is rare because the persistent moisture promotes fungal disease on it. Warm-season grasses are not viable. Moss infiltration into lawns is a regional constant; ferrous-sulfate treatment is the standard remediation.

A Seattle look at invasive pests and plants

Emerald ash borer was confirmed in Washington in 2022 (Forest Grove, Oregon spread); Washington State Department of Agriculture monitors. Hemlock woolly adelgid affects western hemlock plantings in the South Sound. Spotted lanternfly has not yet reached Washington but WSDA monitors. Japanese beetle (Popillia japonica) is established in Sunnyside WA and spreading. English ivy (Hedera helix) and Himalayan blackberry (Rubus armeniacus) dominate invasive-plant management. Garlic mustard infestations are growing in King County.

A Seattle look at HOA and design review

Seattle HOA restrictions are relatively rare in the city proper but common in Eastside master-planned communities (Bear Creek, Newcastle, Trilogy). Washington's Homeowners Association Act (RCW 64.38) provides no explicit xeriscape protection like California and Arizona do. King County unincorporated-area HOAs vary widely. SDCI's Critical Areas Ordinance adds environmental-review layers on any steep-slope, wetland, or stream-buffer parcel independent of HOA status. Ballard and Phinney Ridge have informal peer-enforced character.

Tree services and ordinances for Seattle homeowners

Seattle Tree Protection Ordinance (SMC 25.11) designates exceptional trees and protects them under SDCI review; non-exceptional trees over 6 inches DBH on development sites still require permits for removal. Licensed arborists through ISA charge $900-$3,800 per mature tree removal. Windstorm cleanup (2006 Hanukkah Eve storm, 2021 atmospheric river) spikes pricing 40-60 percent. Douglas fir root rot (Armillaria) affects mature trees across the metro. PNW Tree Risk Assessment Qualified (TRAQ) credentials matter locally.

Seasonal maintenance rhythm for Seattle homeowners

Seattle landscape maintenance runs year-round but the productive window is late-April through October. Spring cleanup in March includes moss removal from paths and roofs, early-season pruning, and pre-emergent moss treatment on lawns. Summer irrigation runs June through September. Fall cleanup (October 15-December 15) includes leaf collection and sprinkler blow-out. Winter pruning of deciduous fruit trees and roses happens January-February. The wet season (October-May) creates steady moss-management workload.

Stormwater rules: a Seattle guide

SDCI Stormwater Management Manual applies to any project creating over 2,000 sqft of impervious surface. The RainWise Cistern and Rain Garden program pays up to $6/sqft of impervious area reduced. King County Clean Water Healthy Habitat bond funds additional green-stormwater infrastructure. Seattle's combined sewer overflow (CSO) zones prioritize rebate funding in specific watersheds. The Environmentally Critical Areas Ordinance layers further rules on steep-slope, wetland, and stream-adjacent parcels.
