Frequently Asked Questions

How much do solar panels cost in Seattle?

Most homeowners in Seattle pay between $16,320 and $79,310 before the 30% federal tax credit. After the credit, costs drop significantly.

Is the 30% federal solar tax credit still available?

Yes. The federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) provides a 30% tax credit on the total cost of your solar installation through 2032. It drops to 26% in 2033 and 22% in 2034.

How long does solar installation take?

Physical installation typically takes 1-3 days. However, the full process from contract signing to power-on can take 2-3 months due to permitting and utility interconnection.

Solar Economics in Seattle: Payback and Savings

The financial case for solar in Seattle depends on three variables: sun hours, electricity rates, and incentive structure. Here is how they stack up.

Sun hours and production. Seattle averages 3.6 peak sun hours per day, which translates to roughly 10,512 kWh of annual production from a typical 8 kW system. Seattle solar production is the most seasonal of any major US city: ~6.0 kWh/kW/day in June-July versus ~1.2 kWh/kW/day in December when cloud cover and short days combine. Over 70% of annual production happens between April and September.

Electricity cost offset. At WA's average residential rate of ~$0.12/kWh, an 8 kW system offsets approximately $1,261 in electricity per year. Your actual savings depend on your usage pattern, time-of-use rate structure, and how much energy you export versus consume directly.

Net metering. Washington State mandates net metering. Seattle City Light customers receive full retail rate credits for exported energy, with credits rolling over month-to-month. Seattle City Light is already ~90% carbon-free (hydropower), so the environmental argument for solar is more about resilience than carbon reduction.

Payback period. With the 30% federal ITC and WA's incentive structure, most Seattle homeowners see a payback period of 10-13 years. After payback, your system produces essentially free electricity for the remaining 15-20 years of its warranted life. A well-sized system in Seattle can generate $40,000-$80,000 in lifetime electricity savings.

Solar Incentives and Tax Credits for Seattle Homeowners

Federal Solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC) - 30%

The federal ITC under IRC Section 25D provides a 30% tax credit on the total cost of your solar installation, including panels, inverters, battery storage, wiring, and installation labor. This credit is available through 2032, then drops to 26% in 2033 and 22% in 2034. This is a dollar-for-dollar tax credit, not a deduction. If you owe $8,000 in federal taxes and your credit is $7,000, your tax bill drops to $1,000. Unused credits can be carried forward to future tax years.

Important distinction: The IRA Section 25C heat pump and energy efficiency credit expired in December 2025. The Section 25D solar and battery credit remains fully active through 2034. Do not confuse these two programs when planning your installation.

WA state incentives. Washington State's solar incentive program expired in 2021. The state offers a sales tax exemption for solar installations (worth 10% given WA's 10.25% Seattle rate) and exempts solar from property tax. No state tax credit.

Utility incentives. Seattle City Light does not currently offer a solar rebate program. The sales tax exemption is the primary state-level benefit.

Property tax exemption. Washington exempts solar installations from the state and local sales tax (saving roughly 10% in Seattle's tax jurisdiction). No separate property tax exemption exists.

Stacking incentives. The federal ITC applies to the gross system cost before any state rebates are deducted. State credits and utility rebates stack on top of the federal credit. For a $24,000 system in Seattle, the effective cost after all available incentives can drop to $14,000-$16,000.

Climate and Sun Exposure in Seattle

Peak sun hours. Seattle receives an average of 3.6 peak sun hours per day, which is below the national average but still economically viable with favorable incentives. For context, the US average is about 4.5 peak sun hours.

Seasonal production. Seattle solar production is the most seasonal of any major US city: ~6.0 kWh/kW/day in June-July versus ~1.2 kWh/kW/day in December when cloud cover and short days combine. Over 70% of annual production happens between April and September. This seasonal swing affects cash flow: summer months may generate credits that offset winter shortfalls, depending on your utility's net metering policy.

Cloud cover. Seattle averages 70% cloud cover annually, the highest of any major US metro. However, June through September is remarkably clear and dry, and the long summer days (16+ hours of daylight at solstice) compensate significantly. Modern solar panels still produce 10-25% of their rated output on overcast days through diffuse radiation, but cloud cover is factored into the peak sun hours figure above.

Temperature effects. Solar panels are rated at 25C (77F) and lose roughly 0.3-0.4% efficiency per degree above that temperature. In Seattle, summer heat reduces panel efficiency modestly during peak afternoon hours, but the temperature effect is manageable and already factored into production estimates from reputable installers.

HOA Rules and Solar Permitting in Seattle

Solar access law. Washington State RCW 64.38.055 prohibits HOAs from banning solar installations. HOAs may require panels to be parallel to the roofline and may have color requirements, but cannot prevent installation.

Permitting. Seattle DCI (Department of Construction and Inspections) requires permits for all solar installations. Expect 2-6 weeks depending on structural requirements. Systems on older homes (pre-1960) may require structural engineering review. Your installer should handle all permitting as part of the contract. If a company asks you to pull your own permits, that is a yellow flag. The permit holder is legally responsible for code compliance.

Building code. Washington State Energy Code requires specific underlayment ratings; moss treatment is recommended every 2-3 years. Solar installations must comply with the National Electrical Code (NEC) Article 690, which governs photovoltaic systems. Key requirements include rapid shutdown capability (required since NEC 2017), proper conductor sizing, and equipment grounding. Your installer should be intimately familiar with these requirements.

Inspection process. After installation, expect two inspections before your system can generate power: a building/structural inspection and an electrical inspection. Once both pass, your utility will install a bidirectional meter (or reprogram your existing smart meter) and authorize interconnection. The full process from installation completion to power-on typically takes 2-6 weeks, with utility interconnection being the bottleneck.

Grid Connection and Net Metering in Seattle

Your utility. Most Seattle homeowners are served by Seattle City Light (a public utility). Confirm your utility provider before evaluating solar proposals, because net metering terms vary significantly between utilities even within the same city.

Net metering details. Washington State mandates net metering. Seattle City Light customers receive full retail rate credits for exported energy, with credits rolling over month-to-month. Seattle City Light is already ~90% carbon-free (hydropower), so the environmental argument for solar is more about resilience than carbon reduction.

Interconnection process. After your system passes final inspection, your installer submits an interconnection application to Seattle City Light. The utility reviews the application, may inspect the meter connection, and then authorizes Permission to Operate (PTO). Do not energize your system before receiving PTO. Operating without utility authorization can damage equipment, void warranties, and create liability issues.

System sizing strategy. With Seattle's net metering structure, sizing your system to offset 90-100% of your annual usage typically provides the best return. Your installer should model your specific usage pattern and utility rate to optimize system size.

Solar Scams and Red Flags in Seattle

Solar is a legitimate investment, but the industry has its share of bad actors. Here is what Seattle homeowners need to watch for.

Solar lease vs. purchase traps

Leasing companies offer "$0 down solar" and handle everything, but the homeowner does not own the system, does not receive the federal tax credit (the leasing company claims it), and is locked into 20-25 year contracts with escalating payments (typically 2-3% annually). After 20 years, a leased system can cost more than the electricity it replaces. In Seattle, the strong contractor market means purchase financing at competitive rates is readily available. Own your panels.

Inflated production estimates

Some installers inflate production projections by 15-25% to make the payback math look better. Ask for production estimates using PVWatts (the NREL tool) or similar validated modeling software, and verify the shading analysis uses actual site data, not assumptions. In Seattle, your system should produce roughly 3.6 kWh per installed kW per day on an annual average basis. If an installer promises significantly more, demand documentation.

Pressure sales tactics and one-day offers

Legitimate solar installers do not pressure you to sign today. Any "this price is only available if you sign now" pitch is a high-pressure sales tactic designed to prevent you from comparing bids. The solar investment tax credit is not going anywhere until 2033. Material costs are not spiking tomorrow. Get at least three quotes and take your time.

"Free solar" door-knockers

Door-to-door solar salespeople (especially common in Seattle during summer months) often work on commission and represent lease or PPA (Power Purchase Agreement) products. They may show you a monthly payment lower than your current electric bill, but the contract locks you in for 20-25 years, includes escalators, and can complicate home sales. The panels they install are usually the cheapest available to maximize their margin. If someone knocks on your door offering free solar, close the door and do your own research.

Unrealistic battery claims

Battery storage (Tesla Powerwall, Enphase IQ, etc.) is a real product with genuine benefits for backup power, but some installers oversell battery as a money-making investment. In most WA rate structures, battery storage alone does not improve the financial payback of a solar installation unless you are on a time-of-use rate with large peak/off-peak spreads. Buy battery for power security during outages, not as a financial investment.

Best Time to Install Solar in Seattle

Best months to install

February-April

Installing before the dry season (June-September) captures the entire high-production window. Scheduling during the rainy season also means better contractor availability and potentially lower pricing.

Why timing matters

Maximize first-year production

Solar panels start generating returns the day they are activated. Installing 2-3 months before the peak production season means your first 12 months of ownership capture the maximum possible production. In Seattle, peak production runs from May through September, so having your system operational by April or May is the ideal target.

The full timeline. Plan for 2-3 months from contract signing to power-on. This includes site assessment (1-2 weeks), design and engineering (1-2 weeks), permitting (2-4 weeks), physical installation (1-3 days), inspection (1-2 weeks), and utility interconnection (2-4 weeks). Working backward from your target go-live date helps you choose the right signing date.
