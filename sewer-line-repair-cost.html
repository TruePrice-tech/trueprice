CIPP epoxy lining averages $80 to $250 per foot. Excavation runs $50 to $200 per foot before landscape and concrete restoration adds another $1,000 to $5,000. Spot Repair vs Full Replacement: When Each Makes Sense

The single most important number on a sewer quote is whether you need a spot repair or a full replacement. Picking the wrong one wastes thousands.

  • Spot repair ($1,000-$4,000): A targeted dig at a single problem point. The plumber excavates a 4 to 8 foot section, removes the failed joint or root-invaded fitting, replaces with new PVC or HDPE, and backfills. Best for a single offset joint, isolated root intrusion, or one fractured section in otherwise sound pipe. Decision is made by the camera footage.
  • Full replacement ($3,000-$25,000): The whole lateral is replaced from house to main. Right call when the camera shows multiple offsets, scaled cast iron throughout, recurring bellies, or the pipe is Orangeburg (1948 to 1972 era pressed-fiber pipe that delaminates and collapses). Also right call when more than 30% of the line shows damage.
  • Liner-only rehab (CIPP, $80-$250/ft): Inflate an epoxy-impregnated liner inside the existing pipe and cure with steam, hot water, or UV. Only works if the host pipe is structurally sound (no collapses, no full breaks). 50-year design life. Best for cast-iron lines with scale and pinholes.
  • Decision rule: get the camera footage in writing. If a plumber recommends full replacement without showing video that justifies it, get a second opinion. The margin difference between $3,000 and $20,000 is the most aggressive upsell territory in residential plumbing.

Trenchless Methods: Pipe Bursting, CIPP Lining, Slip Lining

Trenchless replaces or rehabilitates the pipe without digging the full length. Three methods, each with strict applicability rules.

  • Pipe bursting ($80-$200 per foot): A pneumatic or hydraulic bursting head is pulled through the old pipe by a winch on the receiving end. As it advances, it splits the old pipe outward and pulls new HDPE pipe in behind it. Result: a brand-new pipe of equivalent or larger diameter (typically 4 inch). Works on clay, cast iron, and Orangeburg. Will not work on collapsed sections (the head cannot pass) or where utilities are crossing the path. Requires a pit at each end (typically 4 by 4 feet at the house cleanout and 4 by 4 feet at the city main connection).
  • CIPP pipe lining ($80-$250 per foot): A felt or fiberglass tube saturated with epoxy resin is inverted or pulled into the existing pipe, then inflated and cured. Result is a smooth, jointless liner inside the host pipe. Reduces inside diameter by 1/8 to 1/4 inch (negligible for sewer flow). Works only if the host pipe is structurally sound; will not bridge a collapse. Best for cast iron with scale and pinholes, or clay with hairline cracks but intact joints.
  • Slip lining ($60-$180 per foot): An older trenchless method. A smaller-diameter HDPE liner is pulled inside the existing pipe and grouted into the annular space. Reduces inside diameter (4 inch becomes ~3 inch). Mostly superseded by CIPP for residential.
  • What trenchless cannot do: cannot fix a fully collapsed section. Cannot bridge a missing pipe section. Cannot work where the lateral path has utility crossings (gas, electric) without re-routing. Cannot install where the city does not allow the method (a few municipalities still require open trench for tap connection).

The cost premium of trenchless over straight excavation is often offset by avoiding $2,000 to $5,000 in landscape and hardscape restoration. On a typical urban or suburban lot, trenchless is the cheaper total job.

Traditional Excavation: When Open Trench Is the Right Call

Some lateral repairs still require open-trench excavation. Knowing when, and what restoration costs to expect, prevents sticker shock.

  • When excavation wins: severely collapsed pipe (cannot pass a bursting head or liner), Orangeburg pipe with multiple voids, lateral path that conflicts with gas or electric service, very shallow lines (under 3 feet, where digging is cheap), or localized spot repair where trenchless setup cost would exceed the dig.
  • Per-foot cost ($50-$200): Lower end is shallow, sandy soil. Upper end is deep (8+ feet), rocky or clay soil, dewatered conditions, or work near foundation footings.
  • Depth pricing: Lateral depth doubles the labor and equipment time. A 4 foot trench is a backhoe job; a 10 foot trench needs shoring, OSHA-compliant trench boxes, and often a vac truck. Plan on +30 to +50% per foot of additional depth past 5 feet.
  • Restoration costs: Landscape (sod, plants, sprinklers) $500 to $5,000. Driveway concrete cuts $5 to $15 per square foot, plus $7 to $15 per square foot to repour. Sidewalk concrete cuts may require city permit and matching panel replacement, $200 to $600 per panel. Asphalt patching $4 to $8 per square foot.
  • Indoor disruption: Basement slab cuts add $500 to $2,000 if the lateral runs under the slab to a basement bathroom or floor drain.

Depth and Length Pricing: How Quotes Get Built

Sewer lateral pricing breaks down into linear footage, depth, and pit count. Understanding the math helps you sanity-check the quote.

  • Linear footage: Most residential laterals run 40 to 100 feet from house cleanout to city main. Older urban homes (deep lots) can run 80 to 150 feet. The plumber should provide the actual measurement after camera and locator work.
  • Depth bands: 0 to 4 feet is "shallow" (sun-belt slab homes). 4 to 7 feet is "standard" (most U.S. residential). 7 feet and deeper is "deep" (cold-climate basement homes, some hilly properties). Each band roughly adds $20 to $50 per foot to the open-trench cost.
  • Pit count for trenchless: Pipe bursting and CIPP need a pit at each end, plus an additional pit at any sharp bend or branch tie-in. A simple 60 foot lateral may need only 2 pits. A complex lateral with cleanouts and branches may need 3 to 4 pits, each adding $500 to $1,500 to the quote.
  • Tap connection: Where your lateral meets the city main is the trickiest joint. Some cities require their own crew to make the final tap (city tap fee $500 to $3,000). Others allow the contractor to do it under inspection. Check the line item on the quote.
  • Permits: Sewer permits range from $150 to $1,000 depending on city. Many cities also require a pre-work and post-work video sent to the inspector. The plumber pulls the permit; if they ask you to pull it, that is a licensing red flag.

Root Removal: Foam, Cutting, or Replacement?

Tree roots are the dominant cause of residential sewer failures. The treatment ladder runs from cheap maintenance to permanent fix.

  • Foaming root treatment ($200-$400 per visit): A herbicide foam (typically diquat or metam-sodium) is poured into the line. Foam expands to fill the pipe, killing roots on contact. Roots regrow but the kill lasts 12 to 24 months. Best as maintenance after a successful hydro-jet cleanout.
  • Mechanical root cutting (snake or hydro-jet, $400-$900): Snake or 4,000 PSI hydro-jet cuts roots back to the pipe wall. Does not kill the roots; they regrow in 6 to 18 months. Required first step before any spot repair or full replacement quote.
  • Spot repair ($1,500-$5,000): Excavate the single root-invaded joint, replace with watertight rubber-gasketed PVC or HDPE coupling. Permanent fix for the affected joint but does not prevent future invasion at other joints.
  • Full lateral replacement: Trenchless pipe-bursting with HDPE eliminates joints (HDPE is fused, not gasketed), so root re-entry is essentially impossible. The most permanent fix. CIPP also eliminates root entry by sealing the joints with the liner.
  • What does not work: copper sulfate (Roebic and similar) is sold for root prevention but rarely effective. Anecdotal at best, and corrosive to old galvanized lines.

City vs Homeowner Responsibility: Who Owns the Lateral?

The most expensive misunderstanding in sewer work is who owns which piece of pipe. The split varies by city.

  • Most common (40+ states): Homeowner owns the lateral from the house to the connection at the city main. The city owns and maintains the main itself. Damage anywhere on the lateral, including under the street to the main, is the homeowner's bill.
  • Property-line split (less common): Cities like Cleveland and Sacramento have historically owned the lateral from the property line to the main, leaving the homeowner only on the hook for the segment inside the property line. Verify the current rule with your city; these have shifted.
  • Centerline split: Some east-coast cities split at the centerline of the street. Check your municipal code.
  • Cost-share programs: A growing number of cities (Madison, Milwaukee, parts of California) offer 25 to 50% cost-share or low-interest loans for the public-right-of-way portion of a homeowner replacement. Check before you sign.
  • Service line endorsement on insurance: A $30 to $80 per year insurance endorsement covers many causes of lateral failure up to $10,000 to $15,000 per claim. If you do not have this rider and your home is over 30 years old, add it before the next renewal.
  • Contractor-caused damage: If a utility contractor's backhoe (gas company, fiber install) cracks your lateral, their general liability insurance pays. Get the company name, the call-before-you-dig (811) ticket, and document with photos.

Hidden Sewer Line Costs Most Homeowners Miss

  • Landscape restoration ($500-$5,000): Sod, plants, sprinklers, irrigation timers. Rarely included in the plumber's quote. Can rival the pipe cost on a heavily landscaped lot.
  • Driveway and sidewalk concrete cuts ($5-$15 per square foot): Plus repour at $7 to $15 per square foot. Sidewalk cuts may require city permit and matching panel replacement.
  • Asphalt restoration ($4-$8 per square foot): For laterals running under driveways or streets.
  • Permits ($150-$1,000): Sewer permits vary widely by city. Should appear on the quote. Plumber pulls; never homeowner.
  • City tap or connection fee ($500-$3,000): Some cities require their own crew to make the final connection at the main. Separate line item.
  • Pre and post video for inspection ($150-$400): Some cities require a video-of-record before and after work. Plumber should include but verify.
  • Portable toilet rental ($150-$300/week): For multi-day excavation jobs where the bathroom is unusable. Often forgotten.
  • Sewer cleanout install if missing ($300-$1,500): Required for trenchless access. Older homes often lack one.
  • Indoor demolition for cleanout access: If the cleanout is behind drywall or under finished flooring, opening access can add $300 to $1,200 in patching.
  • Backflow preventer ($200-$700): Some cities now require a backflow preventer (check valve) on new lateral installs to protect against city main backups.

Sewer Line Quote Red Flags (2026)

  • No camera inspection before quoting: The single biggest red flag. Without video, the plumber cannot honestly diagnose. A "we'll know once we dig" approach gives the contractor unlimited scope.
  • "We have to dig the whole yard" without offering trenchless: Trenchless options exist for nearly every residential lateral. A plumber who only offers excavation may be selling unneeded landscape repair as part of the upsell.
  • Scope creep mid-job: Mid-excavation, the contractor "discovers" the line is worse than expected and the price doubles. Demand a written change order signed before any additional work, with photo documentation of the new condition.
  • License gap: Sewer requires a state plumbing license, and some states require an additional sewer or trenchless endorsement. Verify with the state board.
  • No permit pulled: Sewer permits are required in nearly every jurisdiction. Skipping is a code violation that can void homeowners insurance and complicate a future home sale.
  • Cash-only or 100% deposit: Legitimate plumbers take card or check, and structure deposits at 25 to 50% with progress payments. 100% up front is a fraud risk.
  • Same-day pressure to sign on a $15K+ job: Reputable plumbers hold pricing 30 days. Same-day urgency on a five-figure repair is a sales tactic.
  • Refusing to email the camera video: You paid for the inspection. The video is your record.
  • Ambiguous "linear feet" billing: Quote should state actual measured length and per-foot rate. Vague "approximately 80 feet" turns into 110 billed feet without verification.
  • City-side work without checking responsibility: Paying for the city's portion of the lateral when the city owns it. Always call public works before signing.

How to Get the Best Sewer Line Repair Quote

  1. Get a camera inspection first. $300 to $700. The camera is the only honest diagnostic. Demand the video file (MP4) and a written report listing footage, depth, and pipe material.
  2. Get 2-3 written quotes including trenchless options. At least one quote should include pipe bursting and CIPP. A plumber who only quotes excavation may be steering toward unneeded landscape repair.
  3. Verify city vs homeowner responsibility. Call public works before paying. If the damage is at or past the curb, the city may own it or may offer cost-share.
  4. Itemize landscape, concrete, and restoration. The plumber's quote should be either inclusive (rare) or explicitly silent with a note that restoration is by others. Get separate restoration quotes from a landscape or concrete trade.
  5. Confirm permit and inspection scope. Permit fee on the quote, plumber pulls. Pre and post video to inspector if your city requires.
  6. Check the warranty. Trenchless pipe bursting and CIPP carry 25 to 50 year manufacturer warranties on the pipe or liner, plus 1 to 2 year labor. Excavation 1 year labor minimum. Get exclusions in writing.
  7. Pay schedule sanity check. 25 to 50% deposit, progress payments at milestones, final 10 to 20% on inspection sign-off. Anything front-loaded above 50% is a red flag.

Sewer Line Repair FAQ

How much does sewer line repair cost in 2026?

Sewer line repair cost depends on whether you need a spot fix or a full lateral replacement. Spot repair runs $1,000 to $4,000 for a single offset joint or root intrusion. Full lateral replacement runs $3,000 to $25,000 for a typical 50 to 100 foot residential lateral. Trenchless pipe-bursting averages $80 to $200 per linear foot. CIPP pipe lining averages $80 to $250 per foot. Traditional excavation runs $50 to $200 per foot before landscape and concrete restoration.

What is trenchless sewer repair and how much does it cost?

Trenchless sewer repair is any method that replaces or rehabilitates the pipe without digging the full trench. Two main methods: pipe bursting ($80 to $200 per foot) pulls a bursting head through the old pipe while feeding new HDPE in behind it; CIPP (cured-in-place pipe lining, $80 to $250 per foot) inflates an epoxy-impregnated liner inside the existing pipe and cures it in place. Both require entry and exit pits but no full trench. Best when landscape, hardscape, or driveway would otherwise be destroyed.

Who is responsible for the sewer line, the homeowner or the city?

In nearly every U.S. jurisdiction, the homeowner is responsible for the sewer lateral from the house to the connection at the city main. The city owns and maintains the main itself. Some cities (Cleveland, Sacramento, Boston, parts of Florida) own from the property line to the main; others put the homeowner on the hook all the way to the centerline of the street. Check your municipal code before paying for any work, especially if the damage is at or past the curb. Some cities offer 50% cost-share programs for the public-side portion.

How do I know if I need spot repair or full replacement?

The decision rests entirely on the camera inspection ($300 to $700). Single offset joint, root intrusion at one fitting, or one fractured section: spot repair, $1,000 to $4,000. Multiple offsets, scaled cast iron throughout, repeated bellies, or Orangeburg pipe (1948 to 1972 era fiber pipe that fails): full replacement. Never accept a full-replacement recommendation without seeing the camera footage. Demand the video file and a written report of footage and depth where each issue is.

How long does sewer line replacement take?

Trenchless pipe bursting on a typical 60 to 80 foot residential lateral takes 1 to 2 days plus 1 day for permits and inspection. Trenchless CIPP lining takes 1 day plus prep and inspection. Traditional excavation takes 2 to 5 days for the dig and replacement, plus another 1 to 4 weeks for landscape and concrete restoration. Plan on 1 day of no plumbing use, 2 to 3 days for full disruption with excavation, and budget for portable toilet rental on multi-day jobs ($150 to $300 per week).

Does homeowners insurance cover sewer line damage?

Standard homeowners insurance does not cover sewer line repair. Most policies exclude damage from gradual wear, root intrusion, and ground movement. A separate service line endorsement ($30 to $80 per year) covers many of these causes up to $10,000 to $15,000. Sewer backup coverage is also a separate endorsement and pays for water damage inside the home, not for the pipe repair itself. Verify both endorsements with your carrier before assuming coverage. Damage from a sudden, accidental cause (a contractor's backhoe) may be covered by their liability.
