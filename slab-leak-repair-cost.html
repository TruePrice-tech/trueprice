How to Know if You Have a Slab Leak

Slab leaks are sneaky because the leak itself is invisible (under the concrete) and the symptoms can mimic other issues. The five most reliable signs in 2026.

  • High water bill with no usage change: The earliest sign. A 30 to 100 percent bill increase month-over-month with no irrigation or guest changes is the strongest single indicator. Walk the meter with all water off; if the dial spins, you have a leak.
  • Warm spots on the floor: Hot-water lines run warmer than the surrounding slab. A localized warm patch in the floor (especially in a hallway or kitchen) is a classic hot-side slab leak. Cold-side leaks do not produce this symptom.
  • Sound of running water with everything off: A faint hiss or running-water sound near a wall or floor when all fixtures are off. Easier to hear at night.
  • Mildew or musty smell: Slow leaks under finished flooring breed mold. Smell often precedes visible damage by weeks.
  • Cracks in slab or shifting floors: Severe long-term leaks erode the soil under the slab and cause settlement. Cracks in tile, doors that suddenly stick, or hairline slab cracks are late-stage signs.
  • Low water pressure at fixtures: A pinhole leak diverts pressure. Sudden pressure drop at one or two fixtures may indicate a leak upstream.
  • Discoloration on baseboards or walls: Water wicks up wall plates and produces a dark line at the bottom 6 to 12 inches of drywall.

If you see any two of the above, get detection scheduled within the week. Slab leaks worsen exponentially: a $1,500 spot repair in week one becomes a $6,000 reroute plus $3,000 in mold remediation by week eight.

Detection Methods: Acoustic, Infrared, Electronic Line Tracing

Before any repair, the leak's exact location must be confirmed. Three detection methods are standard, and a reputable plumber uses two before jackhammering.

  • Acoustic detection ($150-$300): Sensitive ground microphones listen for the high-frequency hiss of pressurized water escaping. Most accurate on hot-side leaks (the temperature differential creates a clearer signal). Requires a quiet environment; appliances and HVAC must be off during detection.
  • Infrared / thermal imaging ($200-$400): A FLIR or similar thermal camera scans the slab for temperature differentials. Hot-water leaks show as warm bands on the floor. Cold-water leaks are harder to find this way unless they are large.
  • Electronic line tracing ($200-$500): A signal transmitter clamps onto the metallic pipe (works on copper, not PEX). A handheld receiver follows the signal above ground, mapping the pipe's path. Combined with pressure isolation testing, this pinpoints the leak within 1 to 2 feet.
  • Pressure isolation testing: The plumber isolates sections of the system with shut-off valves to confirm which run holds pressure and which does not. Often free as part of the diagnostic call.
  • Helium tracer gas (rare residential): For tricky cold-water leaks where acoustic and infrared fail. Helium is forced into the pipe; sensors detect where it escapes. Used mainly on commercial slabs.

A complete detection report includes the leak's depth, the exact location marked on the slab, the pipe run identified (kitchen hot, master bath cold), and whether one or multiple leak points exist. If a plumber wants to start jackhammering without this report, get a second opinion.

Spot Repair vs Reroute vs Repipe Overhead

Once detection confirms the leak, the plumber should present three repair tiers. Picking the right tier saves thousands and prevents wasted work.

  • Spot repair ($1,000-$4,000): Jackhammer a 1 to 4 square foot section through the slab, expose the failed pipe, cut and replace the section with copper or PEX, pressure test, patch the slab with concrete, restore the floor finish. 1 to 2 day job. Right call for a first-time leak in an otherwise sound system. Does not prevent the next leak.
  • Reroute around slab ($2,500-$8,000): Abandon the slab section. Run new PEX or copper from the manifold or fixture, up the wall, through the attic, and back down to the destination fixture. Pressure-tested before drywall closes. 2 to 3 day job. Right call for a second leak in the same house, or for an isolated supply run that has shown problems. The abandoned slab pipe is sealed and left in place.
  • Whole-house repipe overhead ($4,000-$15,000): Abandon all under-slab plumbing. Run new PEX (standard) or copper from a centralized manifold, through the attic, and down walls to every fixture. Drywall opened at every fixture. 3 to 5 day job. Right call when the home has had 2+ slab leaks or the original pipe is failing systemically (typically copper laid in 1960s-1980s in soft-water regions, or polybutylene from 1978 to 1995). The most disruptive but most permanent fix.
  • Pipe lining or pipe coating (Newer alternative, $1,500-$4,500 per run): An epoxy coating is pulled through the existing pipe and cured. Works for pinhole-stage copper before the wall is fully eroded. Less common but growing. Not a fix for severely deteriorated pipe.

Decision rule by leak count: 1st leak, spot repair (assume bad luck). 2nd leak in 2 years, reroute or start considering repipe. 3rd leak ever, repipe overhead. Each leak past the first has a higher probability of more leaks soon, especially in soft-water regions and homes with copper laid in the 1960s-1980s.

Copper Pinholes vs Polybutylene Failure: Material Matters

The pipe material under your slab determines both the failure mode and the right fix.

  • Copper (post-1960s): Most common slab plumbing in homes built 1960 to 2000. Fails from inside-out pinhole erosion in soft, acidic, or aggressive water. Hot-side fails first because higher temperature accelerates corrosion. Lifespan 25 to 40 years in aggressive water, 50+ in neutral water. Spot repair is viable on first leak. Multiple leaks signal the whole copper system is approaching end of life.
  • Polybutylene (1978-1995): Gray plastic pipe with copper or brass fittings. Reacts with chlorinated municipal water and embrittles, then fails at the fittings. Every poly-plumbed home is on borrowed time and is in scope for the original class-action settlement (now closed but the underlying material remains in millions of homes). If you have polybutylene, repipe is the only sensible option, not spot repair.
  • Galvanized steel (pre-1960): Rare under slab but exists. Corrodes from inside out. Spot repair almost never makes sense; the rest of the system is failing too.
  • CPVC (1980s-present): Less common under slab. Brittle with age, fails at fittings. Spot repair viable.
  • PEX (modern): Rare under slab in older homes; common in new construction sleeved through conduit. Failures are typically from fitting issues, not pipe material. Spot repair is straightforward.

Insurance Coverage: Sudden vs Gradual

The single most important insurance detail on a slab leak: most policies cover the resulting water damage but not the pipe repair itself. Reading the policy correctly can shift coverage by tens of thousands of dollars.

  • Water damage from sudden leak (usually covered): Drying ($1,000 to $4,000), mold remediation ($1,500 to $6,000), flooring repair (tile, hardwood, carpet replacement), drywall and paint. Standard HO-3 policy covers up to your policy limit minus deductible.
  • Slab access cost (varies): Jackhammering through the slab to reach the leak. Many carriers cover this as "access" in the loss; others exclude it. Read the policy or call before the work.
  • Pipe repair itself (usually NOT covered): The plumber's bill to replace the failed pipe section is excluded as "wear and tear" or "gradual." Service line endorsement ($30 to $80 per year) covers many causes up to $10,000 to $15,000.
  • Gradual or hidden leaks (often excluded): A leak that has been dripping for months and is "discovered" during a high water bill check is sometimes excluded as gradual. Document the discovery date and the meter reading at first symptom.
  • Foundation movement from leak (sometimes covered): If the leak undermined soil and caused slab settlement, foundation repair coverage depends heavily on policy wording.
  • Steps to maximize coverage: file the claim immediately at first detection (carriers dispute late filings), get the detection report in writing with the date of discovery, photograph the scene before repair begins, save all receipts including detection, and keep the failed pipe section for the adjuster.

Always check your policy's slab leak language and any service line endorsement before paying out of pocket. Two homeowners with the same leak and the same carrier can have $0 to $25,000 in coverage difference based on policy add-ons.

Hidden Slab Leak Costs Most Homeowners Miss

  • Floor finish repair ($500-$5,000+): Tile, hardwood, carpet, vinyl plank. Spot repair through tile or hardwood is rarely a clean patch. Tile match on a 15-year-old floor is often impossible; whole-room re-tile may be needed.
  • Drywall and paint after wall access ($300-$1,500): Reroute and repipe overhead require opening walls at every fixture and turn. Patching is usually a separate trade.
  • Mold remediation if leak was slow ($1,500-$6,000): Slow leaks under flooring breed mold within 48 to 72 hours. If the leak ran weeks before discovery, expect remediation. Insurance often covers this.
  • Water damage drying ($1,000-$4,000): Industrial dehumidifiers and air movers run 3 to 7 days. Required before drywall and flooring restoration. Insurance often covers.
  • Jackhammering finished concrete patios or basement floors ($300-$1,500 add-on): Standard slab is straightforward. Stamped, stained, or tiled concrete adds finishing cost.
  • Furniture and appliance moving ($200-$800): Spot repairs in finished spaces require clearing the work area. Some plumbers include; some bill separately.
  • Permit fee ($75-$300): Required in most jurisdictions for slab penetration and pipe work. Plumber pulls.
  • Detection that comes back inconclusive: A second detection visit ($200 to $500) if the first method does not pinpoint. Rare but happens with cold-side leaks.
  • Hot water heater inspection: A failing slab leak on the hot side often degrades the water heater anode and lining. Add $100 to $200 inspection if the heater is over 5 years old.
  • Insurance deductible: $500 to $2,500 typical. Counts against the total recovery.

Slab Leak Repair Red Flags (2026)

  • Skipping detection and going straight to jackhammer: The biggest red flag in slab leak work. Without acoustic, infrared, or electronic detection, the plumber is guessing at the leak location. Demand a written detection report before any slab penetration.
  • "We have to repipe the whole house" as first recommendation: A repipe is rarely the right answer on a first-time leak. Plumbers who skip spot-repair and reroute options to push the highest-margin job are upselling. Get a second opinion.
  • No plumbing license or no detection equipment: Slab leak detection requires specialized equipment (FLIR cameras, acoustic detectors, line tracers). General handymen and unlicensed contractors should not be working slab leaks.
  • Ambiguous restoration scope: "We'll patch the floor" with no detail. Demand line items: tile or hardwood match, drywall closure, paint match, who handles each.
  • Same-day pressure to sign on a repipe: Repipe quotes hold for 30 days at reputable plumbers. Same-day urgency on a $10K+ job is a sales tactic.
  • No permit pulled: Slab penetration requires a plumbing permit in most jurisdictions. Skipping is a code violation that can void insurance.
  • 100% deposit or cash-only: Reputable plumbers structure 25 to 50% deposit with progress payments.
  • License missing from the quote: Active state plumbing license number is non-negotiable. Verify with the state board.
  • Refusing to provide the detection report in writing: You paid for the inspection. The report is your record and your insurance documentation.
  • Below-market quote (30%+ under others): Usually missing scope, restoration, permits, or proper licensing. The "savings" gets back-billed mid-job.

How to Get the Best Slab Leak Repair Quote

  1. Confirm the leak with the meter. Free, takes 10 minutes. Shut off all interior fixtures and irrigation. Watch the meter for 5 minutes. Spinning dial = leak.
  2. Get a detection inspection. $200 to $500. Acoustic plus infrared, or acoustic plus electronic line tracing. Get a written report with the leak location and depth.
  3. Get 2-3 written quotes for spot repair, reroute, and repipe. Each method has different upfront and lifetime cost. A plumber who jumps straight to repipe without offering spot repair is upselling.
  4. File the insurance claim before major repair. Document the leak with photos and the detection report at first discovery. Carriers dispute claims that wait until after extensive demo.
  5. Confirm flooring restoration scope. Spot repair through finish tile or hardwood is rarely a clean patch. Get a written restoration plan.
  6. Verify license and insurance. Active state plumbing license number on the quote, GL and workers comp certificates available on request.
  7. Check the warranty. Spot repair: 1 year labor minimum, 5 to 25 year manufacturer on PEX. Repipe overhead: 25 year manufacturer plus 1 to 5 year labor. Get exclusions in writing.

Slab Leak Repair FAQ

How much does slab leak repair cost in 2026?

Slab leak repair costs $1,000 to $4,000 for a spot repair (jackhammer through the slab, replace the leaking section, patch). Rerouting around the slab through walls or attic runs $2,500 to $8,000. A whole-house repipe overhead through walls and attic runs $4,000 to $15,000. Detection alone runs $200 to $500. The right method depends on how many leaks the home has had, the pipe material, and whether you have a finished concrete slab or finish flooring above.

How do I know if I have a slab leak?

The five most reliable signs of a slab leak: a sudden 30 to 100 percent jump in your water bill with no usage change; warm spots on the floor (hot-side leak warming the slab from below); the sound of running water when no fixtures are on; mildew or musty smell rising through carpet or hardwood; and visible cracks or moisture stains in the slab or baseboards. Walking the meter with all water off is the fastest free check: if it spins, you have a leak somewhere. Whole-home leak detection runs $200 to $500 and pinpoints the source in 1 to 2 hours.

What causes a slab leak?

In post-1960s homes, the dominant cause is copper pinhole leaks from soft, acidic, or aggressive water that erodes the inside of the pipe over 20 to 40 years. Copper laid directly in concrete with no protective sleeve is most vulnerable. Polybutylene plumbing (1978 to 1995) fails from chlorinated water reactions and is now in class-action removal. Galvanized lines under slabs (mostly pre-1960) corrode from inside out. Foundation movement and seismic events also crack pipes at fittings. Hot-water lines fail before cold because higher temperature accelerates corrosion.

Spot repair vs reroute vs repipe: which is right for my house?

Spot repair ($1,000 to $4,000) is right for a single first-time leak in a home where the rest of the system is sound. It is fastest but does not prevent the next leak. Reroute ($2,500 to $8,000) abandons the slab section and runs new PEX or copper through walls or attic to reach the affected fixture. Best for the second leak in the same house when the rest of the system is still serviceable. Repipe overhead ($4,000 to $15,000) abandons all slab plumbing and runs new lines through walls and attic to every fixture. Best when the home has had 2+ slab leaks or the original pipe is failing systemically.

Does homeowners insurance cover slab leaks?

Standard homeowners insurance covers sudden, accidental water damage from a slab leak (drying, mold remediation, flooring repair) but does not cover the pipe repair itself. Coverage for the access (jackhammering through the slab) varies by carrier and is often partially covered. Gradual leaks, slow drips, and damage from corrosion are usually excluded. Document the leak with detection reports and photos at first discovery. File the claim before extensive repairs to avoid disputes about cause. Read the slab-leak-specific exclusions on your policy; some carriers require a service line endorsement for pipe repair coverage.

How much does slab leak detection cost?

Slab leak detection runs $200 to $500 as a standalone service in 2026. Acoustic detection ($150 to $300) uses sensitive microphones to listen for the leak's sound through the slab. Infrared detection ($200 to $400) uses thermal imaging to find temperature differentials from a hot-water leak warming the slab. Electronic line tracing ($200 to $500) energizes the pipe and uses a receiver to map its path. A reputable plumber uses two methods to confirm before jackhammering. If a plumber proposes drilling without detection, get a second opinion.
