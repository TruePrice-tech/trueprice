Landscaping questions for St Louis homeowners

How much does mulch cost?

Bulk mulch costs $25-$50 per cubic yard delivered. Plan 3 inches deep and 1 yard covers 108 sqft. A 1,000 sqft bed needs about 10 yards ($250-$500). Professional installation adds $30-$50/yard.

How much does a sprinkler system cost?

A residential irrigation system costs $2,500-$5,000 for a quarter-acre lot. Smart controllers add $100-$300 and reduce water usage 20-40%. Drip irrigation zones for beds cost $500-$1,000 extra.

Should I hire a landscaper or landscape architect?

A landscaper handles installation and maintenance. A landscape architect designs complex projects and manages grading, drainage, and permits. For projects over $15,000 or involving structural elements, an architect is worthwhile.

How much does lawn installation cost?

Sod costs $1-$2 per square foot installed. Hydroseeding costs $0.05-$0.10/sqft. Traditional seeding costs $0.10-$0.25/sqft but takes 6-8 weeks to establish. A 5,000 sqft lawn costs $5,000-$10,000 in sod.

St. Louis Soil Conditions and Amendments

St. Louis soil varies dramatically across the city/county split: Central West End sits on windblown loess over Mississippian limestone with pH 7.0-7.5, while Soulard's river-bottom alluvium runs more acidic at 6.0-6.5. The Hill neighborhood's heavy clay requires gypsum amendment before any ornamental planting. Urban yards in North City commonly contain coal-clinker fill from pre-1950 heating systems and lead contamination from decades of industrial emissions. University of Missouri Extension soil testing ($15-$35) is the standard starting point. Missouri Organic Recycling and Soil Service Garden Center supply most amendments.

St. Louis Lawn and Turf Choices

Cool-season tall fescue blends (Rebel, Millennium, and Crossfire II from local sod farms) dominate St. Louis residential lawns because of shade tolerance under the dense canopy of Central West End and Soulard street trees. Kentucky bluegrass is used on sunny Clayton and Kirkwood tracts. Zoysia (Zenith, Empire) is gaining on southern-exposure lots but the 60 freeze-thaw cycles create winter-brown periods that last November through April. The transition-zone position of St. Louis (straddling USDA Zones 6b and 7a) makes lawn-grass selection more complex than purely warm or cool markets.

St. Louis Invasive Pests and Plants

Emerald ash borer has killed virtually every mature ash across the St. Louis metro; ongoing removals dominate arborist work. Spotted lanternfly was confirmed in Missouri in 2024 and the Missouri Department of Agriculture mandates reporting. Japanese beetle (Popillia japonica) is a persistent turf and ornamental pest. Bush honeysuckle (Lonicera maackii) dominates invasive-plant management and has overtaken understory in many Central West End and Shaw lots. Callery pear (Pyrus calleryana) removal is a specific regional priority because it crowds out native dogwood and redbud.

St. Louis Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

St. Louis landscape maintenance runs March 15 through November 15, with the 60 freeze-thaw cycles compressing the spring-cleanup window into a 3-week rush. Spring cleanup includes winter-salt-damaged perennial replacement, lawn dethatching, and crabapple and pear pruning. Summer irrigation runs May through September. Fall cleanup (October 15-December 1) includes leaf collection from the dense Central West End canopy and sprinkler blow-out. Winter snow and ice management adds $500-$2,000 season-long for larger Clayton and Kirkwood properties.
