Roofing questions for Stockton homeowners

Should I repair or replace my roof?

If damage covers less than 30% of the roof and the roof is under 15 years old, repair is usually sufficient. Beyond that, replacement provides better long-term value.

Should I choose the lowest roofing quote?

Not always. Lower bids sometimes exclude flashing, ventilation upgrades, or disposal costs that appear later as change orders. Compare scope, not just price.

How long does a roof replacement take?

Most residential roofs take 1-3 days to replace. Complex roofs with multiple layers, steep pitch, or extensive damage can take 4-7 days.

What is the most affordable roofing option?

Three-tab asphalt shingles are the most affordable at $3-5 per square foot installed. Architectural shingles cost $4-7/sqft but last 10-15 years longer, making them a better value over time.

How much does a new roof cost in Stockton?

Most homeowners in Stockton pay between $8,000 and $16,000 for a new roof, depending on size, material, and complexity.

How long does a roof last in Stockton?

Expect architectural asphalt shingles to last 20-25 years in Stockton, with 3-tab shingles at 15-20 years, metal roofing at 40-60, and tile at 50+. The biggest lifespan variable here is UV exposure since it accelerates granule loss and material breakdown. Adequate attic ventilation and lighter-colored materials both help counteract this.

Stockton permits: required to replace my roof?

Most Stockton jurisdictions require a permit for a complete roof replacement. This is the contractor's responsibility to obtain before work starts. Skipping permits creates real risks: insurance coverage gaps, code violation liability, and mandatory disclosure when selling.

When is the best time to replace a roof in Stockton?

The optimal window for roof replacement in Stockton is fall or early spring, summer surface temperatures make installation difficult and can affect adhesive curing. Scheduling during off-peak periods can save 10-15% on labor costs since contractors have more availability and may offer competitive pricing to fill their calendar. Avoid scheduling immediately after a major storm event when contractor demand (and prices) spike.
