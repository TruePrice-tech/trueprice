Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a paver patio cost in Tampa?

How long does a paver patio last?

A properly installed paver patio can last 25-50 years with minimal maintenance. Pavers can be individually replaced if damaged, making them a long-lasting investment.

Do I need a permit for landscaping work?

Permits are typically required for retaining walls over 4 feet tall, major grading changes, and drainage work that affects neighboring properties. Check with your local building department in Tampa.

Tampa Native Plant Palette

Tampa Bay native palette centers on live oak, sand live oak, sabal palm, gumbo limbo, American beautyberry, firebush, and coontie. Florida Botanical Gardens in Largo and the University of South Florida Botanical Gardens are the regional references. Wilcox Nursery in Largo and Florida Native Plants supply most of the Tampa Bay native market. Coastal plantings use sea oats, sea grape, and saw palmetto. Inland plantings in Pasco and Hillsborough use muhly grass, fakahatchee grass, and blanketflower.

Tampa Soil Conditions and Amendments

Tampa Bay soils are sandy-marl over limestone with karst sinkhole risk in parts of Pasco and Hillsborough. Organic mangrove muck on coastal parcels requires over-excavation. Kraft Compost and Heart's Desire Organics supply most of the Tampa Bay amendment market. University of Florida IFAS Extension soil testing ($15-$45) provides the baseline. High pH (7.5-8.5) and rocky underlay cause iron chlorosis on non-native plantings; sulfur and iron chelate applications are common. Peat-moss amendments address sandy drainage.

Tampa Irrigation Rules and Rebates

Southwest Florida Water Management District (SWFWMD) implements year-round residential watering restrictions: one day per week in winter, two days per week in summer, no daytime watering. Tampa Bay Water wholesale coordinates regional supply. Hillsborough, Pasco, Pinellas, and Manatee counties enforce SWFWMD rules. Florida Department of Environmental Protection irrigation codes apply. Backflow prevention (ASSE 1013 RPZ) is mandatory. Tampa Water Department smart-controller rebates pay $50-$150 per device.

Tampa Bay Water and SWFWMD do not offer direct turf-replacement rebates the way western cities do, but Florida Friendly Landscapes program subsidizes native-plant conversions through UF IFAS Extension. Tampa Lawn and Turf Choices

Warm-season St Augustine (Floratam dominates, Palmetto, Seville, Captiva varieties) is the Tampa Bay residential default. Zoysia (Empire, Zeon, JaMur) is gaining on newer Wesley Chapel and Riverview tracts. Bahia grass appears on some Pasco rural-feel lots. Bermuda is used for sunny sports-use and coastal-sand lawns. Cool-season grasses are not viable. Chinch bug and gray leaf spot drive seasonal treatment on St Augustine. Sod-webworm outbreaks spike late-summer applications.

Tampa Invasive Pests and Plants

Rugose spiraling whitefly is the dominant Tampa Bay ornamental pest, defoliating black olive and gumbo limbo. Red imported fire ants are pervasive. Asian citrus psyllid (HLB vector) requires quarantine compliance. Laurel wilt disease kills avocado and red bay plantings. Mole crickets attack Bahia grass lawns. Crape myrtle bark scale affects the iconic street tree. Burmese python has less effect on urban Tampa Bay than on Everglades-adjacent Miami but is monitored. Cuban treefrog and brown anole are established.

Tampa HOA and Design Review

Tampa Bay HOA restrictions are common in FishHawk Ranch, Westchase, Tampa Palms, and other master-planned communities. Florida Statute 373.185 protects Florida-friendly landscaping against HOA turf-required covenants. Davis Islands and Bayshore have informal but peer-enforced character standards. Historic district review in Hyde Park, Seminole Heights, and Ybor City adds architectural-review layers on exterior-visible landscape elements. Avila and Cheval gated communities have private architectural committees with tree-preservation and color-palette rules.

Tampa Tree Services and Ordinances

Tampa Tree Protection Ordinance (Chapter 27 of Code of Ordinances) protects grand trees (over 34-inch DBH) and specimen species. Hillsborough County, St Pete, and Pinellas County have parallel ordinances. Licensed arborists through ISA and FCHP (Florida Certified Horticulture Professional) charge $800-$3,500 per mature tree removal. Hurricane cleanup (Ian 2022, Idalia 2023) spikes pricing 50-80 percent. Palm trimming dominates typical seasonal workload; sabal palm, queen palm, and Washingtonia are the main species.

Tampa Seasonal Maintenance Rhythm

Tampa Bay landscape maintenance runs year-round. Warm-season grasses green up March-April and peak through October. Pre-emergent applications happen January and July. Hurricane-season staging (June-November) creates episodic workload spikes. Cold-front protection for tropical plantings during rare December-February freeze events drove many homeowners to replace frost-tender species after the 2010 and 2022 freezes. Palm trimming and pine-needle cleanup are the dominant ongoing tasks.

Tampa Stormwater Rules

SWFWMD Environmental Resource Permits apply to residential projects creating impervious-surface impact above thresholds. Hillsborough County Stormwater Management and St Pete Stormwater work with FDEP on regional coordination. Permeable pavers, rain gardens, and bioretention qualify for Florida Water Star new-construction certification. Flood Zone A parcels in South Tampa, Davis Islands, and Apollo Beach require freeboard-elevation compliance on any substantial improvement. Sinkhole Investigation Zone parcels require additional review.

Local conditions. Tampa has local climate conditions that affect project scheduling and material choices. Native and drought-tolerant species (yaupon, muhly grass, lantana) cut irrigation costs and survive both flood and drought.

Contractor market. With a population of 352,957, the local market has enough contractors to drive real competition on price, but you should still get three quotes. Tampa's rapid recent growth has tightened the contractor market — expect longer lead times and less negotiating room than smaller markets.
