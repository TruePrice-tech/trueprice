Attic Insulation Upgrade Cost in Texas (2026)

Attic insulation upgrades in Texas typically run $1,500–$5,400 for a 1,500 sq ft attic upgrade R-30 to R-49 blown cellulose, with blown fiberglass loose-fill the dominant residential retrofit material at the IECC R-38 attic minimum. Texas's combination of 3,000 annual cooling degree days, 75%+ humidity in East Texas, and dramatic regional climate variation from Houston Gulf coastal humid (1A) to El Paso arid (4B) drives state-specific insulation specifications — Houston, Dallas, San Antonio, and Austin support cooling-dominant attic systems with radiant-barrier sheathing, while West Texas El Paso, Lubbock, and Amarillo additionally require closed-cell spray foam at the roofline to manage extreme summer attic temperatures above 160°F and the longer winter heating season.

Texas energy code & climate-load drivers

  • IECC climate zone: 1A-2A-2B-3A-3B-4B
  • Required attic R-value: R-38 (IECC 2021 prescriptive minimum)
  • Required wall R-value: R-13 (above-grade wood-frame)
  • Adopted energy code basis: IECC 2015 — older cycle, R-49 attic + R-20 wall (cold) or R-38 (warm)
  • Stretch code available: No — IECC base code only
  • Annual heating + cooling load: 1,900 HDD / 3,000 CDD
  • Vapor barrier requirement: No statewide vapor retarder rule — climate zone determines
  • Dominant attic insulation material: Blown fiberglass loose-fill — dominant in cooling-dominant Sun Belt
  • IRA HOMES + HEER rebate status: Planning — IRA implementation plan filed with DOE, rebates pending state legislature

Texas licensing, rebates & permits

  • License status: No statewide trade license
  • License board: No statewide insulation license; State Energy Conservation Office (SECO) administers IRA HOMES + HEER (official site)
  • Permit: permit required for attic insulation retrofit per Texas Energy Conservation Code; municipal contractor registration required in Houston, Dallas, San Antonio, Austin, Fort Worth on residential exterior contracting

How attic insulation upgrade costs vary in Texas

State-specific code or insurance rule: Texas Government Code §447.004 imposes the Texas Energy Conservation Code (IECC 2015 base with state amendments) statewide — Texas was the last major state to adopt residential energy code statewide, and the Texas State Energy Conservation Office (SECO) under the Comptroller is the IRA HOMES + HEER administrator with one of the largest IRA rebate allocations in the country at $689 million combined HOMES + HEER, providing residential insulation rebates layered with utility-funded Oncor, CenterPoint Energy, and CPS Energy weatherization programs.
