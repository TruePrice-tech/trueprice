Landscape Installation Cost in Texas (2026)

Landscape installation in Texas typically runs $4,400–$18,600 for a front-and-back-yard residential landscape install, with cool- or warm-season turf-dominant front-yard design as the dominant residential approach across USDA hardiness zone 6a-10a. Texas's dramatic east-west climate variation from East Texas piney woods (52-inch annual rainfall, 8a hardiness) to Trans-Pecos arid (8-inch annual rainfall, 7a-8a hardiness) drives state-specific residential landscape design — Houston and Beaumont support St. Augustinegrass turf with Gulf-coastal humidity, Dallas-Fort Worth supports Bermuda and zoysia with regular irrigation, while West Texas El Paso, Lubbock, and Amarillo restrict residential turf to drought-tolerant buffalograss native cultivars and desert-adapted xeriscape palettes.

Texas climate, water & plant-palette drivers

  • USDA hardiness zone: 6a-10a — drives plant-palette selection and frost-survival ratings
  • Annual rainfall: 30 inches/yr — drives irrigation system sizing
  • Growing season: 240 frost-free days per year
  • Drought tier: Moderate — periodic drought, advisory water-saving guidelines
  • Irrigation dependency: High — 40-60% of residential outdoor water for irrigation
  • Frost-line trench depth: 6-inch design minimum for irrigation backflow + bed prep
  • Dominant residential aesthetic: Turf-dominant — cool- or warm-season residential lawn as primary front-yard ground cover
  • Wildfire defensible-space rule: No WUI defensible-space requirement

Texas licensing, pesticide & permits

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: Texas Department of Agriculture Pesticide Programs + Texas Department of Licensing & Regulation Landscape Architect (official site)
  • Permit: no permit required for landscape install; TDA Commercial Pesticide Applicator required for any chemical lawn care; HOA approval typical in deed-restricted communities

How landscape installation costs vary in Texas

Texas's dramatic east-west climate variation from East Texas piney woods (52-inch annual rainfall, 8a hardiness) to Trans-Pecos arid (8-inch annual rainfall, 7a-8a hardiness) drives state-specific residential landscape design — Houston and Beaumont support St. Augustinegrass turf with Gulf-coastal humidity, Dallas-Fort Worth supports Bermuda and zoysia with regular irrigation, while West Texas El Paso, Lubbock, and Amarillo restrict residential turf to drought-tolerant buffalograss native cultivars and desert-adapted xeriscape palettes.

State-specific code or insurance rule: Texas HB 645 (2013) grants every Texas homeowner the statutory right to install drought-tolerant native plants and xeriscape regardless of HOA covenants, restrictions, or deed-recorded landscape requirements — overriding HOA mandates in deed-restricted communities and forcing every Texas HOA architectural review committee to approve native xeriscape applications meeting Texas Department of Agriculture-published low-water-use plant lists, the strongest residential xeriscape-protection statute in the country alongside Florida's FFL Act.
