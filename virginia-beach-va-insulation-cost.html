Frequently asked insulation questions

Can I add insulation over existing insulation?

Yes, in most cases. Adding blown-in cellulose or unfaced fiberglass over existing attic insulation is common and effective. Never compress existing insulation and do not add a vapor barrier on top of existing insulation.

Should I remove old insulation before adding new?

Remove if the existing insulation is wet, moldy, pest-contaminated, or vermiculite (potential asbestos). Otherwise, add new insulation on top. Removal costs $1-$2/sqft and is usually unnecessary.

What is the difference between open-cell and closed-cell spray foam?

Open-cell (R-3.7/inch) is cheaper, flexible, and better for interior walls. Closed-cell (R-6.5/inch) is rigid, waterproof, adds structural strength, and is required in flood zones and below-grade applications.

How much energy does insulation save?

Insulating an under-insulated attic to current code saves 15-25% on heating and cooling costs. In a home spending $2,000/year on energy, that is $300-$500 annual savings, paying back a $1,500-$2,500 project in 3-5 years.

Virginia Beach-area energy code snapshot (Zone 4A)

Virginia Beach energy code: Virginia has adopted the 2018 IECC with amendments. IECC Zone 4A requires R-49 attic, R-13+5ci (or R-20) wall, and R-10 crawlspace-wall insulation for new construction. Existing homes are grandfathered until renovation triggers compliance. Dominion Energy's rebate programs provide financial incentive that supplements the code's retrofit expectations. The FEMA flood-zone at the Oceanfront and Sandbridge may affect insulation placement on ground-level assemblies that could be submerged during flood events.

DOE recommended insulation levels for Zone 4A are attic R-49 to R-60 and walls R-13 to R-21. Code minimums in Virginia Beach are attic R-49 and walls R-13+5ci. Exceeding code by one R-tier typically returns within 4 to 7 years of utility savings in this market.

Virginia Beach-area moisture control and vapor strategy

Virginia Beach moisture management is the most crawlspace-intensive among these metros: the 2-6 foot water table, combined with the Chesapeake Bay maritime humidity, creates a perpetual moisture challenge for the crawlspace-foundation homes prevalent in Great Neck, Thalia, and Kempsville. Crawlspace encapsulation (20-mil reinforced poly vapor barrier on soil, sealed to foundation walls, with dehumidifier and sump pump) is the standard of care before adding floor insulation. Unencapsulated crawlspaces with insulation produce the worst possible outcome: the insulation traps moisture against the floor joists, accelerating the wood rot and mold growth that an uninsulated but ventilated crawlspace would have avoided. The crawlspace-encapsulation-first sequence is non-negotiable for any Virginia Beach floor-insulation project.
