West Des Moines garage door: answers to common questions

How wide is a standard garage door?

A single-car door is 8-9 feet wide. A double-car door is 16 feet wide. Heights are typically 7-8 feet. Custom sizes are available but cost 20-40% more than standard dimensions.

Do I need a permit for a garage door in West Des Moines?

Garage door replacement in West Des Moines typically does not require a permit if you are not changing the opening size. If you widen or add a new opening, a structural permit is required.

What causes a garage door to be noisy?

Common causes: worn rollers (replace nylon for steel), dry hinges (lubricate with silicone spray), loose hardware, a chain-drive opener, or worn spring coils. A tune-up costs $100-$200.

What is the best type of garage door?

Insulated steel with polyurethane foam offers the best balance of durability, energy efficiency, and cost. R-value of 12-18 keeps the garage 10-20 degrees closer to house temperature.

What R-value garage door do I need in West Des Moines?

West Des Moines's winters demand serious insulation. An R-12 to R-16 door is the baseline for attached garages, with R-16 to R-18 recommended if you use the space as a workshop or heat it. The $200-$500 upgrade cost pays back through lower heating bills in rooms adjacent to the garage within 3-5 years.

Should I repair or replace my garage door in West Des Moines?

Repair makes financial sense for isolated damage: a broken spring, one or two panels, or an opener malfunction. Replace when the door exceeds 15 years, replacement panels are unavailable, you want insulation you do not currently have, or you are preparing to sell. Full replacement ($1,200-$3,000) recovers 90-100% at resale.

What's the best season to replace a garage door in West Des Moines?

West Des Moines homeowners get the best pricing and selection by scheduling garage door replacement during late spring or early fall. Moderate temperatures allow proper weatherstrip sealing and caulk curing, and you can save 5-10% versus peak season pricing. Spring demand surges from cold-weather spring failures, so plan ahead to avoid the rush.
