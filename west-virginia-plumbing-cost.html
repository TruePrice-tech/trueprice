Plumbing & Repipe Cost in West Virginia (2026)

Plumbing repipe in West Virginia typically runs $4,400–$11,400 for a whole-house 2-bath PEX repipe, with PEX-A or PEX-B cross-linked polyethylene the dominant retrofit choice and the International Plumbing Code (IPC) as the adopted enforcement basis. Buried service-line trench depth must clear the 30-inch design frost line. Acid mine drainage from abandoned coal mines across southern West Virginia and the Tygart Valley delivers surface and shallow-aquifer water at pH 3-4 with high iron, aluminum, and sulfate content — neutralization-filtration is core to any AMD-affected residential plumbing scope, and high-elevation Allegheny ridge counties with 30+ inch frost depths force exposed-riser heat-tape on every crawlspace retrofit above 2,500 ft elevation.

West Virginia water-quality & freeze drivers

  • IECC climate zone: 4A-5A — drives freeze-protection scope on supply lines
  • Frost-line trench depth: 30-inch design minimum for buried water service
  • Water hardness profile: Moderate (3.5–7 grains/gallon) — periodic appliance descaling recommended
  • Dominant retrofit supply material: PEX-A or PEX-B cross-linked polyethylene — dominant on whole-house repipes since 2005
  • Adopted plumbing code basis: International Plumbing Code (IPC) — ICC model adopted statewide
  • Lead service line risk tier: Moderate — partial pre-1986 service line inventory under utility review

West Virginia licensing & permits

  • License status: Statewide license required
  • License board: West Virginia Division of Labor — Plumber Licensure (PL-01 through PL-05 tiers) (official site)
  • Permit: municipal or county plumbing permit; on-site sewage through WV Bureau for Public Health

How plumbing and repipe costs vary in West Virginia

State-specific code or insurance rule: West Virginia Code §21-14 created a five-tier plumbing-licensure ladder under the Division of Labor — PL-01 Apprentice through PL-05 Master Plumber with Plumbers in Charge of Work — finer-grained than most Eastern states' three-tier structure, and the WV Bureau for Public Health separately permits every on-site sewage system under 64CSR9 regulating septic, mound, and aerobic-treatment systems statewide.
