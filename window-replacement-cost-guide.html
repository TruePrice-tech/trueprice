Window Replacement Cost Guide

Replacing windows is one of the best investments you can make in comfort, energy efficiency, and curb appeal. But prices vary significantly depending on the frame material, glass package, window style, and labor market in your area. This 2026 guide covers average costs for every major window type, breaks down where your money goes, and gives you practical strategies for getting the best value on your project.

Factors That Affect Your Cost

  • Window size and style. Standard double-hung windows are the most affordable. Casement, awning, sliding, and picture windows cost 10–20% more. Bay and bow windows can cost 3–5x a standard unit because they require structural support and custom framing.
  • Glass package. Double-pane Low-E glass is standard. Upgrading to triple-pane adds $100–$200 per window but significantly improves insulation and noise reduction. Argon or krypton gas fills between panes add another $30–$75 per window.
  • Number of windows. Most contractors offer per-window discounts on larger projects. Replacing 15–20 windows at once typically costs 10–15% less per unit than replacing 3–5.
  • Retrofit vs. full-frame. Retrofit (insert) installation fits a new window into the existing frame and costs less. Full-frame replacement removes the entire frame down to the studs, costs more, but is necessary when frames are rotted or damaged.
  • Lead paint. Homes built before 1978 may require EPA-certified lead-safe work practices, which can add $50–$150 per window in labor and disposal costs.
  • Region. Labor rates in the Northeast and Pacific West run 15–25% above national averages. The Midwest and South Central regions tend to fall at or below baseline.

How to Save Money on Window Replacement

Saving on windows does not mean choosing the cheapest product. It means getting the most value per dollar.

  • Get at least three quotes. Three bids give you enough data to spot outliers. Make sure each quote specifies the exact window brand, model, and glass package so you are comparing equal products.
  • Replace all windows at once. Per-window costs drop significantly on whole-house projects. You also avoid mismatched windows that can hurt resale value.
  • Claim energy efficiency rebates. The federal energy efficiency tax credit covers up to $600 per year for qualifying ENERGY STAR windows. Many utilities also offer rebates of $25–$75 per window.
  • Choose retrofit installation when possible. If your existing frames are in good condition, retrofit installation saves $100–$300 per window compared to full-frame replacement.
  • Schedule in the off-season. Late fall and winter are slower periods for window contractors in most markets. You may be able to negotiate 5–10% off for scheduling flexibility.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to replace all windows in a house?

For a typical home with 10–15 windows, expect $5,000–$15,000 with vinyl frames and $10,000–$25,000 with fiberglass or wood. The exact price depends on window size, glass package, frame material, and your local labor market.

Are vinyl windows worth it?

Vinyl windows offer the best value for most homeowners. They are affordable, energy efficient, and require almost no maintenance. Modern vinyl windows have come a long way in appearance and durability. For most homes, they are the practical choice.

How long do replacement windows last?

Vinyl windows typically last 20–30 years. Fiberglass and wood windows can last 30–50 years with proper maintenance. The seal on double-pane glass usually lasts 15–25 years before fogging may occur.

Do new windows really save on energy bills?

Yes. Replacing single-pane windows with double-pane Low-E windows can reduce heating and cooling costs by 10–25%, depending on your climate. Triple-pane windows provide even greater savings in cold climates. Most homeowners see a payback period of 5–10 years on energy savings alone.

Should I replace windows before selling my house?

If your windows are visibly dated, foggy, or drafty, replacing them can improve curb appeal and remove a negotiation point for buyers. Studies show window replacement recoups 60–70% of cost at resale. If your windows are in decent shape, the investment may not pay off before a sale.

What is the difference between retrofit and full-frame window replacement?

Retrofit (insert) installation fits a new window into your existing frame. It costs less and causes less disruption. Full-frame replacement removes the entire window down to the rough opening. It costs more but is necessary when frames are rotted, out of square, or you want to change the window size.
